Today’s Stratechery Update interview is, from one perspective, another installment of the Stratechery Founder series; as a reminder, one of the challenges in covering startups is the lack of available data. My solution is to go in the opposite direction and interview founders directly, letting them give their subjective overview of their companies, while pressing them on their business model, background, and long-term potential.

Another area of great interest to me, though, are new business models enabled by the Internet that are not necessarily scalable software companies. Obviously independent publishing is one of these, and Stratechery is an example of the sort of business that is possible because of the Internet, even if it is not the sort of tech company that I normally cover.

DNVR is another example in the Stratechery vein. I first met Adam Mares, the co-founder of DNVR, last year, and within five minutes of talking with him I told him I would love to have him on for a Stratechery Interview. DNVR has created a new kind of publishing business that includes text, podcasts, video, and, most surprisingly, an actual sports bar; I think it’s brilliant. And, given that the aforementioned NBA Finals include the Denver Nuggets, this seemed like the perfect week to have Mares on.

This interview, though, is not about basketball (although we spend a couple of minutes on it at the end): rather, it is an examination of a business that, if you step back, has started to recreate the geographic-driven differentiation that undergirded the media businesses of old, but instead of being differentiated by owning printing presses and broadcast spectrum, DNVR differentiates itself with focus and community-building. To that end we get into Mares’ background and how DNVR came to be, why podcast advertising — including gambling — work so well for their model, and whether it can be replicated in other cities.

Mares and Jokić

Adam Mares , welcome to Stratechery.

AM: Thanks for having me on, Ben.

As the most well known Denver Nuggets podcaster , you are doing quite the tour of shows this week, so you can add Stratechery to the list. Stratechery, though is not a basketball site — you have to go to @NoTechBen for my deranged takes as far as that goes — but rather a site about strategy and tech. So why do I have a Denver Nuggets podcaster on?

Well, one of the things that I’m most interested in and passionate about are the new possibilities and new business models enabled by tech and in that regard, you’ve built something extraordinary , which I want to get into the details of, and the fact that the Denver Nuggets are about to start the NBA Finals is a perfect time to do that. It’s funny, I suspect listeners think I’m coming up for an excuse to talk basketball, but actually as you’ll recall the first time I met you, within five minutes, I’m like, “You have to come on Stratechery sometime, I want to talk about this”, so here you are.

You’re well positioned to do that. We’ll see what happens in the Finals. I do have a couple questions for you in a little bit, but this is a Nuggets team that’s very well set up for a nice run here. The Avs won last year — yeah, it makes a lot of sense, maybe you’re going to be in the right place at the right time.

AM: Coach Prime as well , which is like the most interesting person in sports. His arrival in Boulder completely changed how CU [Colorado University] is covered. So yeah, another stroke of luck.

Well, let’s start with you though. What is your background? Where did you grow up? Are you from Denver originally? How’d you get into sports and podcasting, and Nikola Jokić mythology making? Today is Adam Mares’ mythology making, so tell me about your background.

AM: Oh, man, I’m going to embellish all of this. I’m 6’5″, I’ve always been a basketball player, I was always tall and gravitated towards basketball, and I was also always a writer. I actually wrote school plays back in middle school, I was one of those kids that was just doing the school newspaper, writing plays, writing sketches and skits and different things. So in a lot of ways, this is the confluence of who I am at my core, a sports enthusiast and a storyteller.

But my background, I’m from Denver. After college, I moved to Hollywood to give that a go, trying to work in Hollywood as a writer. I spent a decade as a real estate investor in Colorado, a complete left turn from the creative fields, and I had a successful decade run doing something I didn’t necessarily love but was in the right place at the right time and that afforded me to spend the last ten years pursuing a passion, which was getting back into writing. So I bounced around a little bit, but like I said, at my core, I’m a basketball fan, analyst and storyteller and that’s really what I’m doing now.

As far as this current era, this was just starting out a team-centered blog, you were the Denver Nuggets guy.

AM: That part’s actually funny, because I wasn’t trying to get out of real estate at the time. In 2013 or so, Internet blogging was new still, especially sports blogging, social media was very new and I was on Twitter, and I was just on Reddit and different places, and I was just talking hoops. Of course, I come from a basketball background so I’m able to provide a little bit more context and insight, and then in 2015 when I really started to do this, in earnest, I went to [Summer League] and I watched Nikola Jokić’s first game in Summer League. I met a bunch of my favorite writers there and spent a week with them and I walked away from that week thinking, “I can do this”. Meeting the other people who I had a lot of respect for, I thought, I have a perspective that a lot of people don’t and the ability to tell stories, and I’m thinking, “I can be good at this”. So after that Summer League in 2015, I really started to pursue it full time.

I got a full-time credential for the first time this 2015-16 season, so yeah it was at the start of Jokić’s career, it was the start of my career as well, doing it independently. Got picked up, wrote for Vice for a while, bounced around, did a couple of things, SB Nation, did that. But in 2019, that’s when I came together with a group of other writers here in Denver and formed a new company, DNVR . And obviously, the last three years have been that.

Well, we are going to dive into DNVR, that is going to be the focus here with the Nuggets as the sideshow. But it’s interesting because I am not a basketball blogger, but there is a nice symmetry to the fact that the Bucks drafted Giannis [Antetokounmpo] in 2013 when I started Stratechery . One of the things that happens when your passion becomes your job — tech was my passion for many, many years, and then it was my job — is you need a new thing to do when you’re not doing your job. It was perfect that basketball could be that outlet at the perfect time when there was the perfect person to cheer for on my favorite team, so I definitely can appreciate that. Again, this is your job as opposed to being your thing, but I could appreciate that point-of-view of right place, right time as far as your life circumstances are involved.

AM: And honestly, I might not be doing this if it weren’t for Jokić. The Nuggets have spent several periods sometimes in the desert, wandering the desert, trying to figure out who they are. If I would’ve started at one of those moments, I might’ve run out of passion or just never got off the ground because they were too boring to talk about, there isn’t an audience for it. With everything, there’s a little bit of a stroke of luck. Nikola Jokić arrived, and almost immediately he became a very interesting story, and at a moment in time when nobody was paying attention. So it allowed me to be the one that was telling the stories early on and growing the following in those early days, so then by the time everybody else started to realize that, I was well positioned.

A challenge here is we have a lot of people that probably don’t watch basketball, don’t watch sports, don’t even know who Nikola Jokić is. So big picture — he is this big Serbian center who might have been more conventional, maybe twenty, thirty years ago, but much more unconventional in today’s NBA, which is spaced out where you have all this shooting and athleticism; he’s this big lumbering big guy with unbelievable touch, unbelievable skill, has just taken the league by storm in many respects, and he has led the Nuggets — they just defeated the Lakers in the Conference Finals, we are waiting to see who they will face as at the time we are recording [Editor’s Note: They will be playing the Miami Heat ] — and is really in a market that has been almost completely ignored, both nationally and also locally to a certain extent. Again, there is a really interesting business story we’re going to get to, but there is probably some important sports context that people listening do need to understand.

I remember when it played some of the world’s best Go players, one of the things it did was it played the game in a way that felt bizarre that nobody had ever seen before. At first they were like, “Did we break it? Is something wrong? Is it doing something wrong?”. But as the game played out, you realized, “Oh, no, it’s playing a new strategy that we could not have conceived, that nobody had played”.

To me, I think Jokić is like that. When you say conventional thirty years ago, I’m a lifelong basketball fan, and I love the study of basketball, I love the X’s and O’s — Jokić to me has reinvented the game or opened up a new pathway to where now that he’s playing it you go, “Wow, I never thought to play the game that way”, and he’s excelled at it. So for me that’s part of the charm is that he’s charting new territory about how the game of basketball can be played.

Look at that, what an A+ tech analogy that makes perfect sense! It’s a fantastic way to describe it.

DNVR

Let’s talk about DNVR. There are three ways to talk about DNVR: (1) what is what it was, your vision and goal when you started it; (2) what it is today, and (3) what it will be. I think the first one’s the obvious way to start. You were in real estate, then you started being a sports blogger, you’re in that world, you have a passion for writing, but what came out of this DNVR is it’s not just a blog. What is it, and what was your idea when you got started?

AM: Well, first of all, I have to give credit. I’m only one of the partners there, and there’s a lot of talented people so I don’t want to make it sound like this is my thing that I invented, it’s a group of collaborators that came together.

But this is how I’ll tell the story. I was lucky when I told you I didn’t have a background in journalism or anything like that, I’m coming from real estate and I decide I want to do this as a hobby and then pursue it. I think, in many ways, I was lucky because I don’t carry any of the baggage of what sports journalism is supposed to be, and I say that to say that I was covering it in a unique way with some X’s and O’s and different things, but I was also doing watch parties. I think our first watch party was actually Nikola Jokić’s first game, we billed it as Emmanuel Mudiay’s first game because we thought that was the story, so come down and watch Mudiay!

I forgot about Mudiay, that’s right.

AM: So everybody’s excited for that, but turns out it was also Jokić’s debut, and we grew those over time. Eventually, we became partners — me and a couple of other people became partners with the Denver Nuggets. We were throwing the official Denver Nuggets parties, that’s how big these had gotten. We started selling merchandise like novelty shirts. The way I would talk in the podcast and interact with people, we’d create language, you’d create little nicknames and different things like that, we turned them into t-shirts. I partnered with one of the partners at DNVR, and one of the geniuses behind it, his name is Eric Wedum , and I partnered with him to start making some novelty t-shirts, and we were selling them and they became a big hit.

Meanwhile, there was another company called BSN, Brandon Spano Network, who had started up trying to be, I would say, something like The Athletic, but for Denver. They were writing a little bit more formal, trying to be journalists, but for the Internet — so blogging, but trying to be more professional, more buttoned up. Their model had had certain levels of successes and evolved over time.

That was a subscription model for them?

AM: It started out as a non-subscription model, but it evolved into a subscription model, mostly written articles. I was doing podcasts five days a week, they started doing podcasts for all Denver sports, five days a week, that became part of their model and then yes, went behind a paywall. Well over time, they’re looking at what’s happening over here. They see the t-shirt sales, they start doing t-shirt sales and scaling it up in a big way like, a company. Me, it’s just me and a buddy making t-shirts, selling them. They start scaling them up, they start doing watch parties, scaling them up. Eventually, we come together in the summer of 2019. We had actually been talking, Brandon Spano and myself had been talking a few times. He’d say, “Hey, come over here,” but it didn’t feel like the right time yet.

Then the summer of 2019, we came together, Brandon Spano, a guy named Ryan Koenigsberg , Eric Wedum, who I mentioned was my partner, and then myself and we came together and said, “Hey, let’s combine these two models. What we’re doing creatively, the voice we speak with, we speak as fans, we’re not afraid to admit that we’re rooting for our team.” The traditional journalism stuff, we throw that to the side saying, “No, we’re going to cover these with passion, with depth, with honesty and authenticity. We’re going to make money off of merchandise. We’re going to do watch parties, we’re going to do podcasts every day.” Eventually, it became live.

We came together and said, “Let’s do this, and then we will copy this model that we had been doing, do it for all Denver sports, and then let’s scale it up like a real company. Let’s not just try to sell a 100 or 200 shirts, let’s try to sell several thousand shirts. Let’s not try to do a watch party every now and then, let’s try to do watch parties all the time and make it the place that if you want to go somewhere and cheer on the Nuggets or the Broncos or whatever with other people, DNVR is going to be the place.” So that was the union, that was the partnership. It exploded right away. Right off the bat, it became the thing in Denver.

So one of the most interesting parts of this model is, it’s not often you’re talking to another podcaster or another writer and say, “Tell me about the bar .” But I think in many respects, from my perspective and what I know about your model, I feel like the bar is a symbol of what DNVR is. Is that a fair way to think about it? I think you’re driving towards that with the watch party idea. But how does that fit in? When did that come about? Is my sense right that that is an angle to understand what you’re doing?

AM: Well, so I should say one more core thing about our model, and that is we always say that our voice is going to be from the crowd, not the stage, or from the pew, not the pulpit. And what I mean by that is, I think traditional media is talking to their audience, not with them. It’s like, “Hey, I have the microphone and I’m going to tell you what it is, and you’re going to listen to that.” Our model from the very beginning was, we want this to be a thing where we are speaking on the level of our audience, not trying to act above them. We feel the same emotions. When things are great, it’s great for us, we’re all sharing in it. When things are bad, it’s like, “Hey, this sucks for all of us” and then it’s very interactive. We would always be trying to speak with people, allow them to come on the show and do different things, so it was very interactive.

And if you think about, our watch parties were a physical manifestation of the voice we had cultivated online. Online, it was a conversation so that people felt, I think the way that sports radio was really in the ’90s and early 2000s, where people would feel like, “This is my show, call in”, you connect with the host or what have you. We tried to do that, only even bigger, where people really feel like we’re their friend. But we have people meet us all the time for the first time and say, “I feel like I know you guys, I feel like we go way back”. So, the watch parties became the physical manifestation of that.

Then in early 2020, we’d actually discussed this as an opportunity, we didn’t think it would come so quick, it was basically six months into our existence and an opportunity came to us. A bar owner had said, “Hey, I’ve got this bar, it’s not doing very well, what if we rebranded it?” Our branding was so good, Eric Wedam is in charge of our branding, he’s created a visual brand and a bunch of things for us that really connects with our audience. He said, “What if we called it the DNVR bar, you guys came here and put your studio in it and every watch party, instead of going to different bars around town, every watch party happened at this bar?” Perfect idea, physical manifestation of our online community. You talk online all day, come and meet people in person all day, including the podcast host.

It was great other than we launched that other than we launched that on Friday the 13th, March 13th, 2020, which was two days after the NBA suspended play and two days before bars were ordered to be closed across the country.

You made it though!

AM: We made it.

Business Model

What’s interesting is there’s all these bits about partnerships and that sort of thing, was this an evolution of what DNVR was a company? So DNVR was this union of you and this other organization, and then you’ve built up from them, but then there’s the ALLCITY Network . You can definitely see the transition of these concepts, you talk about your own t-shirts to strong branding, there’s a through line there. You talk about the aspects of covering it being like a fan, watch parties, to owning your own bar. What was the transition in terms of the business side of this?

AM: We opened up the bar, we made it through the pandemic, and things are going pretty well after that. And by the way, we were forced to get creative through the pandemic as well. We were doing our podcasts sometimes live, but mostly they were just recorded and put up. During the pandemic, there was no sports to talk about — we’re a sports company, how do you survive? We started doing daily live shows that were themed to the different sports we were covering. But to be honest with you, they were games, they were interactive. We have to talk about something every day for an hour to make sure our advertisement duties are fulfilled and that’s when our company I think really figured out what we were doing live like a television show, but instead it’s on YouTube , just doing live shows every day that were interactive and different things.

Once the pandemic ended and once we got through, I should say the harsher parts of it, then you get to the point where you say, “DNVR is now a successful company”. We’re selling merch, we got podcasts, our advertisers — if I’ll say one other reason I think that we’ve been so successful, I always say that we go really deep with our audience, not necessarily wide. If you think about ESPN, they try to cast the widest net, “How do we capture the biggest audience”? But there’s no depth to it, people don’t identify with it. With us, our people get tattoos of our logo, our people will take pictures of them drinking the beer at Breckenridge Brewery, the sponsor of our company. They take pictures of it like, “Hey, guys, look what I’m doing.” Our audience, we convert so well with our advertisers and it’s a product of how we talk to our audience, how we interact and engage with them, that’s why we’ve been so successful.

So, now you have the bar, you’ve made it through the pandemic, you have the live shows, the merch is rolling, everything’s rolling. And it’s basically a rocket ship that’s just on autopilot now. We always knew we wanted to copy the model and go to other markets and I think it was in 2021 when we were finally ready to say, let’s try out Phoenix . We had a great group of people, it was regionally close, we thought that was important. They had sports gambling, which was important, because obviously it opens up a whole new revenue stream for you with advertising dollars, and we copied the model and tried it again in Phoenix with similar branding and a whole new cast and learned a lot from that experience.

I want to get into actually both those points. Let’s talk about the business model specifically — is your primary driver ads?

AM: Roughly 75% of our revenue comes from advertisements.

That’s obviously much higher margin than t-shirts, where you have to actually make the t-shirts and sell them, and you have to run a bar and pay a staff. It makes it up in advertising, that makes sense. I am curious about the role of gambling in this, because that’s almost a right place, right time aspect as well. As I understand it, you actually received an investment from, was it FanDuel?

AM: DraftKings has been our big sponsor.

Got it. So, are they actually a partner in this, or is just that they’re sponsoring everything and so it’s a super obvious connection there?

AM: Well, we’ve actually had different partners in different markets. When we launched Chicago CHGO , PointsBet was the gambling partner there. So, DraftKings, there are some people affiliated with DraftKings who have become investors separate of DraftKings, but DraftKings was one that really early on took a gamble on us when sports gambling went legal in Denver.

No pun intended.

AM: (laughing) Yeah, right. They took a chance on us and we converted immediately for them, and I think it was immediate where they said, “Oh, wow, this is our best horse, we made the right bet here.” And then obviously, followed us to Phoenix.

So what’s the mix then between gambling being a big driver, you mentioned you have a brewery sponsor that is your beer, is there a big local advertising play that goes into this, or how does your advertising sales work? Is it you have this one key sponsor or do you have a big team that’s fleshing that out?

AM: We have a big team, and again, it’s evolved over time. In the early days of DNVR back in 2019, one thing I love that we did, because we were a smaller company, we were all getting paid less, there weren’t as many people, there wasn’t as much overhead, we had a bunch of local partners, hyper-local partners, little pizza joints and dispensaries and things, people that weren’t paying a whole lot of money. But one of the things I liked about it was we partnered with a lot of companies that are very Colorado, and that’s true to this day, Breckenridge Brewery being one of them. And I think that it helped because we wanted to be the voice of Colorado, one of our slogans is “We Are Denver”. Early on, we had these small partners, but they were all people that were like, “Oh, yeah, that’s so Denver that you got Illegal Pete’s. It’s so Denver that you got Beau Jo’s or Breckenridge Brewery.”

But as we’ve grown, as the company has gotten bigger, we’ve put a corporate infrastructure in place with a great sales team that absolutely crushes and as I told you, our numbers, it’s an easy sell when you can take to them and say, “Hey, this is something that people aren’t just hearing and it’s name recognition and repetition”, it’s “No, being associated with this can be a really positive thing”. Now we’ve gained a lot bigger partnerships, with even bigger ones coming online here later on this year, national brands.

Local Online

There’s a broader theme here that is really interesting to me, which is you go back to the idea of the Internet, and Stratechery is a manifestation of this. I can sit in Taiwan and I can write something and have subscribers in 100 different countries around the world and it’s like the extreme on the, “You can reach anywhere, the whole world’s your market sort of bit”.

What I’m hearing from you, and what I think what is so intriguing to me about DNVR, is back in the day you made money by having a geographic monopoly, like The Denver Post or whatever it might be, and that went away with the Internet, when you were suddenly facing all this competition. There’s an aspect where DNVR recreated that geographic boundary, but they did it not by owning printing presses, but by just being like, “Look, if you actually care about Denver, this is the place to go”. You can care about Denver from Taiwan or you can care about Denver from Australia, or from Serbia is probably the better example, but you are effectively local and leveraging that, and that’s actually your differentiation. It’s the same differentiation as before, it’s just manifested very differently.

AM: I love that insight and it’s 100% accurate, so much so that we get a lot of people who fly in from out of the country who’ve never been to the US, let alone Colorado, but they come to Colorado and their first stop is the DNVR Bar, because what we’re doing is we are covering the Denver Nuggets and Broncos and all these things, but it’s also a love letter to the City of Denver and to the State of Colorado. We are speaking with passion, we’re all people that have a deep connection to the city, and that comes through in our coverage, whether it’s intentional or not. So all these people that listen to our show every day and connect and identify with us, they’re also hearing this passion we have for our state and for our city and that’s why I think it becomes hyper-local. We effectively in their minds own the city and so to come to visit us is to get the true Colorado experience.

Well I would go a step further, and this is where I think the DNVR model is interesting, broadly speaking, which is that DNVR is one of the best examples I’ve ever seen of using the Internet to build a community. It is the DNVR community, and it has aspects that manifest in the real world, this is where the bar is super interesting, and having that anchor in the real world. But at the end of the day, it’s a community and people become attached to Colorado, I would say, not because you are praising Colorado, but because they love DNVR and by extension, this is why the advertising model is so effective, because you are extending your feeling of belonging that comes from being a part of the DNVR community to products, to a location, and it’s flowing in the opposite direction.

AM: That’s 100% accurate, that’s exactly how this whole thing works, and you’re right that I said earlier, the bar is the physical manifestation of what we’ve built as a company, but even if nobody ever comes to the bar, they still feel 99% of the experience, because of all the things that you’re saying. They feel a part of something and that something is DNVR, that something is the Nuggets, that city is Denver, it’s all of those things.

Mediums and Bits

So, you mentioned you started out as a writer and then obviously you’ve become a huge podcaster, doing the daily podcast. I always love after a Nuggets win, it’s the Winner’s Lounge , after a loss it’s the Loser’s Lounge, even though it’s the same product; I just enjoy that articulation of it. You’ve done video — actually, you did this incredible trip to Serbia documenting Jokić’s background and the history of basketball in Central Europe, in the former Yugoslavia in particular.

Is this sort of a, “Look, it’s all digital, it’s all bits, we’re going to do it all”, or is there a hierarchy of drivers of what actually builds this community, makes the difference? Or does this bit about involving your fans and being a fan with them, does that work across all mediums?

AM: It’s a good question. I told you most of our revenue comes from advertising and most of our advertising comes from our live shows, our podcasts. So, for me personally, the hierarchy is, of course, our podcast product and our live post-game show product, because that’s where the money’s coming in.

But we do written articles, we have written content with some of the best writers in all Denver, some of the best sports writers and that’s an important part of our model, not necessarily because it’s a revenue driver. Trying to get subscribers, we have a good subscriber base, but it’s so hard. As you’ve seen across the country, it’s so hard to get a big enough local base of subscribers to carry, if we were just doing written content on the Nuggets, it’d be so hard. However, the written content brings us legitimacy and that’s an important piece of this. Even if financially if we want to write articles on every game, we want to be covering them doing features, and we do all of those things, the return on that is probably not worth the time investment. But it is worth it in terms of, “Hey, this is an important part of journalism, this is an important part of covering a team”. And having that as our backbone, again, allows us to do all the fun and silly stuff and all the other stuff that brings in all the money. So, that’s a big part of it, obviously the podcast —

I want to jump into that point, on two points. So, number one, that resonates with me personally because as I do more and more podcasting , it’s like, well, this podcasting gig’s pretty easy, I could definitely see myself doing this. But the number one reason to do the writing, beyond the fact that was the core of the Stratechery business model, and obviously by and large, pays the bills, even though most people now listen instead of read interestingly enough. For me personally, the writing makes you think and there’s that Bezos quote about all the details are between the bullets on the PowerPoint slide, that’s why he wanted people to write stuff out. That certainly is the case for me personally, that’s number one.

But number two, and this is the other reason why I’m delighted to talk to you, when Grantland shuttered , I wrote a piece about how ESPN had completely missed the business model, because they didn’t realize that different mediums serve different roles. And in that case, I was talking about how text is easier to spread, it’s easier to go viral and a podcast, you develop this deep audience that listens habitually, it is great for advertising.
