Quote of the Week
“So how was everyone’s night? :)”
— the @Peacock Twitter/X feed, trolling Netflix on Saturday morning for Friday’s glitch-plagued broadcast of the Jake Paul - Mike Tyson fight.

A little more on this…

Netflix C.T.O. Elizabeth Stone attempted some damage control this weekend, emailing employees that the widespread tech issues were “tackled brilliantly by prioritizing stability of the stream for the majority of viewers.” Okay… a “majority of viewers” is fine for what was essentially a silly stunt featuring a 58-year-old boxer. But if Netflix glitches that badly during a Christmas Day NFL game with playoff implications (not to mention a Beyoncé halftime performance), Roger Goodell will personally make Stone disappear faster than she can say “ Colin Kaepernick .”

Still, if there’s a larger takeaway from Paul v. Tyson , it’s that Netflix is now among the most powerful platforms in sports. The supposed 60 million global household audience number hasn’t been verified by Nielsen or another third-party service, but if the actual viewership is even close to that, there’s little reason for the major leagues to not be in business with Netflix, to the extent Netflix is willing to pay the big rights fees. I suspect that will be the next frontier in deal talks: Netflix wanting a discount for the worldwide audience it delivers. For some leagues, a haircut might be worth the exposure.

Now a few smaller items from last night’s Governors Awards, always a chatty affair…
  • Could Clint get Riseboroughed? : I talked to two filmmakers who had seen Guillermo del Toro ’s social media comments about Clint Eastwood ’s Juror #2 , possibly the legend’s final film. “Why was this not released wide in the states?” del Toro posted last week, praising the well-reviewed film and criticizing Warner Bros.’ tiny theatrical play with zero marketing ahead of a planned dump on Max in late December. G.D.T. is not only a best picture Oscar winner, he’s also one of those Academy influencers who gets super involved in the awards season and champions his faves to other voters. Could this be the start of a movement among directors to net the 94-year-old Eastwood one last Oscar nomination?

    It’s a long shot, especially if Clint doesn’t engage at least a little with voters, though Warners says industry screenings for the film will be announced this week, writer Jonathan Abrams recently spoke at a WGA event, and star Nicholas Hoult was stumping among the awards hopefuls at the Governors Awards (though he’s also got Nosferatu coming soon). But as we saw with To Leslie star Andrea Riseborough and her rogue support from actresses two seasons ago, such “grassroots” campaigns are possible in the social media age. Maybe Clint should hire Riseborough’s manager, Jason Weinberg , as a campaign consultant.

  • Disney’s Oscars insurance policy : Near the A table, I bumped into Academy C.E.O. Bill Kramer chatting with Debra OConnell , the Disney/ABC executive who, along with Bob Iger and Dana Walden , will decide whether to renew the broadcast deal for the Oscars. Bill and Deb didn’t want to give me an update on those early talks, but I imagine Disney’s recent big-ticket deal to take the Grammys from CBS for the next 10 years gives OConnell some insurance in case the Academy balks at a decrease in the current $90 million-plus annual arrangement (with givebacks when ratings don’t deliver) and wants to test the market for the top-rated awards show.

    Incidentally, that Grammys deal has been reported as everywhere from “more than $500 million” to as much as $600 million or even more. I’ve heard a lot of conflicting numbers—one source told me it’s $85 million a year by the end of the deal, and that Netflix tapped out at $75 million—though a Disney source counters that it’s much lower. Plus, it’s global, inclusive of TV and streaming, and with Grammys-branded specials tacked on, so it’s hard to compare the price to other current deals. And if Disney ponied up for the Oscars and Grammys, its ad sales team could sell both as a package and potentially float both boats higher. Since the current Academy deal ends in 2028, a big test will come in 2027, when ABC will have the Oscars, Grammys, and the Super Bowl. A triple-boondoggle to dangle in front of advertisers.

    Of course, the Oscars ratings have been in decline, despite the small uptick last year with Barbenheimer. Kramer and the other Academy people I talked to were high on the recently announced host Conan O’Brien , who I agree will do a great job, even if he doesn’t move the needle ratings-wise. (To that end, the Academy is praying Wicked scores a ton of nominations.) In a sign of the times, O’Brien’s WME reps pitched him using data from his podcast and from this spring’s all-timer Hot Ones appearance, plus his general popularity online while not being politically polarizing. That last part is seen as key for comic hosts now, especially when conversations with four-quadrant stars Will Ferrell , Ryan Reynolds and Hugh Jackman , and others went nowhere, even after the Academy pitched a lighter load of Oscars duties.

    More importantly, the Oscars still don’t even stream live on Disney+ or Hulu in this country, thanks to TV affiliate deals. That’s gotta be the first deal point for the Academy, and if I were OConnell, I’d be willing to overpay in exchange for the Academy limiting the number of Oscars presented on the live broadcast to either 15 or 20 awards (not the current 23, soon to be 24 with the addition of casting). That’s the only way to save the 55-member board of governors from themselves and force them to produce a better show that more people actually want to watch. A show like, say, the Grammys.

  • Apple wants help with movies : Apple’s film chief Matt Dentler was there last night, but I didn’t have a chance to ask him what he thought of the company actively looking for a senior executive to work alongside him. Readers know Apple services chief Eddy Cue has been reevaluating the original movies initiative after misguided bets on Matthew Vaughn ( Argylle ), Greg Berlanti ( Fly Me to the Moon ), Jon Watts ( Wolfs ), and others led to box office misfires and bad press. Apple’s not seeking a Dentler replacement, I’m told, just a “strong number two” (scare quotes mine), who will likely have more big-budget producing experience than Dentler, an indie sales guy and film festival programmer before getting the big job.

Now, Scott Mendelson on the lessons of this weekend’s theatrical bomb…
‘Red One’ and the New Arithmetic of Flops
The Rock’s kidnapped Santa caper sure looks like a $253 million Christmas catastrophe for Amazon MGM Studios. It also represents the peril of a certain kind of genre and a limit to its star’s bankability, too.
SCOTT MENDELSON
It’s easy to dunk on Red One , the early-Christmas-season film about a North Pole security officer ( Dwayne Johnson ) recruiting a mercenary ( Chris Evans ) to help track down a kidnapped and uncomfortably buff Santa Claus ( J.K. Simmons ). The Amazon MGM Studios action-comedy cost a reported $253 million to produce, not including P&A. Its Friday-Sunday debut netted $32 million, while the film earned just $50 million in two weeks of overseas theatrical release. By most conventional theatrical measures, this is a catastrophe.

But the true verdict on the film is slightly more nuanced. Red One actually notched a solid opening for an original theatrical release, in line with other moderate successes from the past four years like The Lost City , Free Guy , and If . It also opened in line with other Rock vehicles, such as the $36 million launches of Central Intelligence , Jumanji: Welcome to the Jungle , and Rampage . However, all of the aforementioned cost closer to $100 million than $200 million to produce.

Also, with an A– from CinemaScore and a surprisingly robust 2.93x Friday-to-weekend multiplier, Red One might yet enjoy the longer-tail success of other Christmas-themed movies released before Thanksgiving. If Red One performs like The Santa Clause 2 ($129 million after a $29 million debut in 2006), it could end up with around $142 million in North America. Meanwhile, Red One is looking at an overseas total—courtesy of distributor-for-hire Warner Bros.—of around $90 million. So, if you add in the international gross, the catastrophe may be downgraded to mid-range disaster before it eventually heads over to Prime Video.
The Hybrid Model
But we can’t give Red One too much of a pass. It’s still a $250 million movie released in a theatrical environment where even a $100 million-budgeted original is considered a major roll of the dice. Yes, the film was greenlit and budgeted for streaming before Amazon acquired MGM, and the theatrical release was ultimately considered additive to its economics. And, yes , $125 million domestic is considered a solid run for a live-action original in the current ecosystem. At the end of the day, Red One is likely to surpass the global grosses of recent Apple TV+ originals like Killers of the Flower Moon ($158 million) and Argylle ($96 million), as well as films that were initially intended for HBO Max, like Blue Beetle ($131 million).

Ironically, a $125 million–$150 million domestic finish might have been an unvarnished domestic win for MGM, even with soft overseas numbers, had production costs been on par with the Johnson’s successful 2010s vehicles. During his peak butts-in-seats era, the $80 million–$130 million-budgeted movies— Journey 2: The Mysterious Island , San Andreas , or Rampage— were massive hits, with $350 million–$475 million global cumes. So, why did Red One cost $253 million? Well, partly because the film was budgeted for streaming, meaning A-level talent got fatter paychecks upfront in lieu of performance-based box office bonuses. While it may be unfair to punish Amazon for a streaming-intended release not living up to a theatrical revenue-based profitability model, it’s also the pool they chose to swim in.
The Rock Hits the Ceiling
Red One is just the latest example of the industry overestimating Johnson’s bankability. Folks will show up for him to a point, but his appeal is limited unless it’s a Fast Saga sequel or a film becomes an unto-itself sensation like Sony’s Jumanji sequels. And that limit, of course, should be reflected in the cost. Neither the $90 million Jumanji: Welcome to the Jungle nor the $120 million Jumanji: The Next Level was so expensive to produce that their respective $962 million and $800 million worldwide cumes were required to turn a profit. And the fact that Red One was made for Amazon only made it more expensive, given Johnson’s rumored $50 million-plus buyout.

Maybe the critical lesson going forward—beyond the fact that Dwayne Johnson should make movies that can be theatrical successes with even $350 million in worldwide receipts—is that streaming originals should be budgeted like theatrical originals when they debut in theaters. That often means less money for talent upfront and more money spent on the movie itself. Netflix has already hinted at this change. And it does seem distasteful for streamers that willingly overpaid for A-level talent to try and make the couch competitive with theatrical stadium seating to then return to the old rules after changing the game. But, as demonstrated by Beetlejuice Beetlejuice (which went from a $147 million streaming title to a $100 million theatrical blockbuster that grossed $451 million worldwide), sometimes old school is the better school.

Nonetheless, all the caveats, disclaimers, and asterisks aside, Red One is still a $250 million movie that typifies a pattern of trying to turn almost any marquee character or brand into a franchise-friendly action-fantasy movie. This idea makes no more commercial sense in 2024 than it did in 1985 with Santa Claus: The Movie or 2013 with Jack the Giant Slayer . It’s one thing to offer the benefit of the doubt to the kind of non-franchise, star-driven, adult-skewing genre film that Hollywood used to make before I.P. took over. It’s another to bend over backward when tech companies and alleged disruptors make the same mistakes as their Hollywood rivals.
The Feedback
My Thursday analysis of Disney’s bullish guidance in streaming attracted a few contrarian takes. Some examples…

“Your newsletter seems to be calling this as the moment that Disney’s steaming has turned the corner. Maybe. But I’d only note that their entertainment D.T.C. profit margin is in the low single digits, and their direct-to-consumer business is still $11 billion-plus in the red, not including the billions more they might be on the hook for once the haggling over the final value of Hulu is worked out. In other words, streaming is a long way from being a consistently good business for Disney. Might be someday, but Netflix has a huge head start and Disney has been running with sandbags since the beginning. It’s really only a good business for Netflix so far.” — A journalist

﻿“Not sure if it’s fair to say the right streaming strategy is ‘more modest, narrowly tailored content investment, aggressive windowing, and third-party licensing’ when the most profitable streamer does the exact opposite of all those things.” — An executive

“One of the issues with Disney financials is that it’s masking a shit ton of streaming costs in its TV business—all Disney Channel/Disney Junior production and localization budgets, for example. Easy to post streaming profits in this scenario…” — Another executive
