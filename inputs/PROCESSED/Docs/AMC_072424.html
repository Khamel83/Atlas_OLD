🔥AMC!🔥

The Bear season three. The Acolyte. The Boys season four. Cobra Kai season six. Hit Man. House of the Dragon. What do all of these things have in common?

None of them are at a multiplex near you.

Indeed, unless you have kids old enough to revel in the delights of Despicable Me 4 or Inside Out 2 — both of which have had very successful summer runs — there’s been little compelling fare to draw you away from your television to the local AMC Entertainment Holdings Inc. ($AMC) or Regal theater.

Which begs the question: are movie theaters f*cked? Let’s take stock of the summer.

Chris Hemsworth and Anya Taylor-Joy’s Mad Max prequel, Furiosa, kickstarted the summer box office season with quite a crash and burn. Furiosa made only ~$31mm over the long Memorial Day weekend, which was “…the worst it’s been in nearly three decades.”

Folks blamed prequel-itis for the poor performance, noting that the movie had good critical reviews and audience scores. But that excuse wasn’t enough to quell the doomslayers. From The Wrap:

This dramatic a low could suggest a real tipping point for the theatrical movie business. What are the implications for the future of a theatrical business when Memorial Day, with well-reviewed movies, hit a 26-year low? And having chosen to embrace streaming while producing movies for theaters, what changes might studios need to make to adjust to the new reality?

Industry insiders told TheWrap that the long-term implications of such a poor weekend are still difficult to discern, given that the 2024 box office data is muddled by last year’s WGA and SAG-AFTRA strikes. The strike-induced production lag has prolonged inconsistent grosses caused by inconsistent output of films for theaters since they reopened.

“I don’t think this is an inflection point where we say, ‘Oh, if the pandemic didn’t finish it off, the strikes will,’” said Comscore analyst Paul Dergarabedian. “This is a slump that I think everyone in Hollywood saw coming when the strikes went on for weeks on end. So many factors came together to disassemble any hope of a really strong Memorial Day weekend.”

The article goes on to cite the following, among other things, for the dismal box office: (i) the Hollywood strikes depleting inventory at worst and delaying films at best; (ii) no Marvel tentpole to start the summer season; and (iii) more selective audiences.* Why go see substandard fare at the theater when you can just watch the Hawk Tuah girl on repeat 100x? Or watch the best political drama of all time: the ‘24 presidential election! Selective indeed!!

In its second week, Furiosa all-but-disappeared, losing to The Garfield Movie in what amounted to a ghastly box office weekend. Per Variety:

Though “Garfield” gets first-place bragging rights, it’s another terrible weekend at the box office with ticket sales a staggering 65% behind the same three-day period in 2023. As a result, overall revenues remain 24% behind last year and more than 40% below pre-pandemic times. In other words, Hollywood and movie theaters desperately need some hits on the horizon to help shrink the gap.

The movie was all but gone only three weeks into its release:

Luckily, Will Smith’s new Bad Boys feature slapped (see what we did there?). Three weeks after Furiosa’s stinkfest, Smith and Martin Lawrence’s latest body cop entry surprised to the upside with a $56mm domestic opening and $104.6mm globally. In two weeks, it rode to $187mm which, on one hand, is not too shabby, but on the other hand, isn’t a monster success a la Barbie.

Speaking of Barbie, a week later Inside Out 2 crushed expectations, earning $155mm domestically and $140mm globally, “…the biggest opening of all time for an animated film in like-for-like markets when accounting for exchange rates.” Per The Hollywood Reporter:

Heading into the weekend, the $200 million sequel was tracking for a North American debut in the $90 million range, in line with the first film, which went on to earn nearly $860 million globally. Instead, Inside Out 2 is the first movie since Barbie almost a year ago to cross $100 million in its launch, a symbol of just how beleaguered the box office has been so far in 2024 due to strike-related delays and a precipitous downturn in moviegoing. (Barbie opened to $162 million domestically. And, like Barbie, Inside Out 2 has every chance of joining the billion-dollar box office club.) (emphasis added)

In contrast to the stupendously bad Memorial Day, the combo of the family-oriented animated Pixar film and Will Smith led to a Father’s Day weekend that tracked 30% higher than Father’s Day ‘23. AMC’s CEO, Adam Aron, seized the opportunity to take a victory lap, of sorts:

As the summer progressed, Mr. Aron — and his debtholders, lol, more on this in a moment 😉 — had more to get excited about. A Quiet Place: Day One burst out of the gate with a $53mm box office the week after Father’s Day, all but destroying the thesis that Furiosa’s performance sucked sh*t because of prequel-itis. And, yet, on the same weekend, Kevin Costner’s first part of a planned four-part Western frontier saga got absolutely annihilated with a pathetic $11mm in receipts — despite the actor bankrolling the film in large part himself and reportedly mortgaging certain of his personal real property to do so (personal bankruptcy coming, lol?). A Quiet Place: Day One, however, ended up surpassing $115mm.

From there things turned back to animation. Despicable Me 4 came out and took only two weeks to surpass $200mm in box office receipts, taking the baton from Inside Out 2, carrying the first half of July, including Independence Day weekend. Per Sherwood:

It became the fastest animated movie in history to cross the $1 billion threshold at the global box office, before going on to become the highest grossing Pixar film ever, and the 4th biggest animated movie of all time. (emphasis in original)

Impressive. Mr. Aron was, yet again, jumping out of his pants (see what we did there!?).

But animation alone won’t get Mr. Aron the revenue he needs to satisfy debtholders. Which gets us back to this dude:

Homeboy — that’s Glen Powell for those unaware — went from being a Val Kilmer wannabe and second tier character in Top Gun: Maverick to leading Twisters to over $80mm the weekend of July 19, 2024. This is, as The Hollywood Reporter noted, a “…far better than expected…” opening:

Heading into the weekend, tracking services had Twisters starting off with $40 million to $50 million domestically. Some distributors believed it would go higher, but no one predicted $70 million-plus, much less $80 million.

Next up: Deadpool & Wolverine. Many predict it could become the highest grossing R-rated movie of all time. 🤯

But, from there, the slate thins considerably through summer’s end. The year’s final tally may very well depend on how gaga audiences go for Lady Gaga in October.

What’s The Frequency, Kenneth? - Remastered

R.E.M.

While Mr. Aron pumps optimism through X’s technological veins — spending the last several months responding to the doom and gloom with unbridled hope and poo poo’ing the naysayers — we reckon he spent the early part of the summer worrying in private. People just aren’t going to movies the way they used to. As R.E.M’s Michael Stipe once asked, “what’s the frequency [Kenneth]?” According to analyst Matthew Ball, that’s precisely the correct question:

The domestic box office has been in persistent decline since 2002, when per capita annual admissions in North America hit 5.1, their highest point since 1977’s Star Wars. By 2019, the last pre-pandemic moviegoing year, per capita admissions had plummeted to less than 3.5 – not because fewer people were going to the movies (74% of Americans bought a ticket in 2002, and 76% did in 2019) but because the share who went at least once a month on average fell by nearly two thirds (from 28% to 11%). (emphasis added)

Which begs the question: what about after ‘19?

Domestic per capita admissions, which had shocked when it fell under the 3.50 mark in 2019, fell to 0.62 in 2020 and 1.26 in 2021.

Ok, makes sense but then what?

The fears of permanent impairment were partly allayed by 2022 and 2023, which benefited from relatively dense release schedules, as the many films that were supposed to be released during the pandemic finally debuted on the Silver Screen. Top Gun: Maverick, which was delayed for two years at an estimated interest expense of a million a month, became the biggest domestic release of 2022 (it grossed over $1MM a day for 75 days) and the second-largest worldwide. 2023, meanwhile, had Barbenheimer. The worldwide box office of 2022 beat 2021 by 27%, and 2023 beat 2022 by 31%. Domestic per capita admissions were still but 2.31 — down a third from 2019, sure, but seemingly on the mend.

On the mend? Naw, dawg, ‘24 is looking sick AF:

Altogether, 2024 is currently trending for roughly 1.8 tickets sold per American, fewer than in 2022. Even if 2025 rebounds to 2.5 or so (which would beat 2022 by 8%), it looks like the pandemic will have accelerated the decline in per capita theatrical admissions by roughly 15 years. Pre-pandemic, the industry was trending toward 2.75 by 2029.

Oy. It gets worse. This chart ⬇️ must be giving Mr. Aron some sleepless nights:

“We are concerned,” says one studio insider. “You’d be a fool to not be thinking about that. People are not in the moviegoing habit and are firmly ensconced in appointment viewing, which is a huge problem. But there hasn’t been a real igniter.”

Still, others suggest that we won’t know for certain until we return to a normal slate of films in ‘25. Again, per The Hollywood Reporter from back in late May:

One veteran financier says moviegoing will rebound when the calendar returns to normal. “The theatrical business isn’t going anywhere — it’s a multibillion-dollar industry. Blockbusters will come. Inside Out 2 may be huge,” this person forecasts. “We won’t return to usual and customary release patterns until 2025.”

Dude made a good call on Inside Out 2. Maybe she/he is right about ‘25, too, 🤔? Mr. Ball begs to differ:

We’ll never know what 2023 and 2024 attendance would have looked like without the strikes, but per capita admissions are so far off the pre-COVID trendline that the strikes are, themselves, far from a sufficient explanation. And in general, the relevance of a year’s slate also tends to be overestimated; 2019 was perhaps the greatest blockbuster year in history, yet tickets sold fell 6.5% year-over-year.

And here’s another counterpoint from that Hollywood Reporter article:

But with every movie that disappoints and underperforms, whether Furiosa or The Fall Guy, studio insiders are growing more and more alarmed. “It’s mind-numbing that Furiosa hasn’t grossed $50 million domestically,” says another studio exec. “My worry is that studios will put on the brakes and make fewer movies, which will further contribute to the problem.”

Indeed, Furiosa bombed so badly versus expectations that it may have killed creator George Miller’s next potential Mad Max installment, The Wasteland.

Query whether PwC baked this possibility into its recently released “Global Entertainment & Media Outlook.” Therein, the consultancy predicts that global box office revenue — not admissions — will (finally) surpass pre-pandemic levels in ‘26. Which is significant for two reasons. First, it means that theaters will make more money per customer due to higher prices. And, second, the consultancy had originally predicted the revenue increase would surpass pre-COVID levels in ‘25; it has now pushed that out a year. ‘26 just so happens to be the year that a healthy chunk of AMC’s $4.5b of funded debt is was scheduled to come due (more on this momentarily). Here’s the take on revenue, per The Hollywood Reporter:

Global cinema revenue, helped by ticket price hikes and advertising growth, will continue to grow, but also see a one-year delay. “In last year’s report, we anticipated that pre-pandemic levels would be reached by 2025. However, this year’s report predicts that the return will occur in 2026, indicating a more pessimistic outlook,” shares Spiegel. “Some of this can be attributed to a stronger budget slate throughout the year 2026. It is worth noting that there has been movement over the years, as some major studios made announcements towards the end of 2023.”

This is a reference to release delays on account of the strike. Many big movies were affected by delayed production schedules including, among others, Tom Cruise’s next Mission Impossible installment. But:

The picture for cinema admissions is different. “Admissions are not expected to return to pre-pandemic levels throughout the forecast period. By 2028, the last year of our projections, admissions are projected to fall short by 1.5 billion compared to pre-pandemic levels,” explains Spiegel. That means around 6.45 billion admissions forecast for 2028, compared with 7.92 billion in 2019.

In North America, admissions are also not anticipated to reach pre-pandemic levels during the forecast period ending in 2028 when PwC forecasts 953 million admissions, which Spiegel calls “a significant decrease from 1.3 billion in 2019.” Factors contributing to this decline include “changes in consumer habits following the pandemic, the increasing popularity of streaming platforms, the impact of rising inflation, and audience fatigue with superhero movies,” he says. (emphasis added)

Eesh. That does NOT sound particularly promising. 😬

But Mr. Aron is one creative mofo. Look at that stock chart again. See that big stock spike back in May ⬆️? Mr. Aron pounced all over that sh*t, driving particular resonance to @stantradingman’s snark. That’s right, on May 15, 2024, the company opportunistically rode a brief resurgence in meme stonk craziness to swap $164mm of its 10%/12% Cash/PIK Toggle second lien 26s for 23.3mm shares of newly-issued (and obviously highly dilutive) stock. Almost immediately S&P Global Ratings lowered its issuer credit rating on AMC to SD (selective default) from CCC+ and its second-lien notes due in ‘26 rating to D from CCC-; it also blasted the company, deeming its capital structure unsustainable and indicating that the company is subject to increased default risk “…given its heavy debt burden and negative free operating cash flow.”

Obviously aware of the state of its own capital structure, the company knew it was go time. And so it came as no surprise when, shortly after the downgrade, Bloomberg reported that Gibson Dunn & Crutcher LLP (“Gibson”) was representing the term lenders in discussions with the company about balance sheet options.

Which … 🔔 ding ding ding 🛎️ … certainly ought to have set off some alarm bells. The fact that PJT Partners LP ($PJT) was right there with Gibson … 🔔 ding ding ding 🛎️ … certainly ought to have set off some alarm bells.

On July 22, 2024, AMC announced the consummation of a series of refinancing transactions of the … gulp … “liability management” variety! In a CNBC interview the next morning, Mr. Aron pounded his chest and bashed the media for all of the doom slinging about bankruptcy. But what did he say about the deal?

“The fact that our first lien lenders and second lien lenders have agreed to give us another four and a half years or more to pay back our debt or to refinance it further down the road, this is an enormous step of progress, an enormous vote of confidence in AMC’s future.”

A vote of confidence, huh? Man this guy spins.

A price? What would that be? Asked by CNBC’s Joe Kiernan what he had to give up to get the deal, Mr. Aron responded as follows:

“One of the reasons we got [the lenders] to agree was to show them how well we’ve been running the company since COVID hit in March of 2020. You know, our company raised over $3b of equity underneath the debt in the capital structure to buttress and strengthen the company without asking our lenders for any concessions at all and our lenders rewarded us for that.

Um, yeah, sometimes it’s better to be lucky than smart. And yet that still wasn’t enough. All of the luck in the world and nevertheless the company was in this position. But back to the price. Here’s the best part …

“There was a whole series of collateral changes between one debt instrument and another, some interest rates went up, some interest rates went down because we took some of our second lien debt and turned it into a convertible issuance instead. The deal is very complicated and I would encourage any investor who wants to know more to go to our SEC filings where it’s laid out in excruciating detail. But the reality is we didn’t give up much of anything, we just convinced our lenders that this company has been run well, this company has the support of consumers, the box office is finally growing again, and we should be there to take advantage of that, they agreed with us, and we have all of the time in the world to get there now.” (emphasis added)

LOL, just some small collateral changes here and interest rate increases there, yada yada yada, go read some in-depth SEC filings in your spare time, people, 🖕.

Here are the highlights:

  • Allow us to introduce Muvico LLC (“Muvico”), a newly formed (Texas, lol) indirect wholly-owned subsidiary of AMC.

  • Muvico and AMC jointly and severally issued $1.2b of new ‘29 term loans.** These funds were used to address those near-term maturities; they were used for the “open market purchase of $1.1 billion” of the ‘26 term loans and exchanged for $104.2mm of the 10%/12% Cash/PIK Toggle second lien subordinated notes due in ‘26. If you’re looking at these figures and these issuances and wondering, “wait, wasn’t there $1.9b outstanding under the ‘26 term loan” and “wait, aren’t there other notes in the capital structure senior to the 10%/12% Cash/PIK Toggle second lien subordinated notes due in ‘26,” consider yourself an LME green belt. Not everyone is on board for this transaction, y’all. Like so many predecessor LME deals, you’ve got “participating lenders” and “non-participating lenders.” The participating term lenders held $1,102,037,130.41 of the ‘26 term loan and $518,645,724 of the 10%/12% Cash/PIK Toggle second lien subordinated notes due in ‘26. You can do the math based on the cap stack chart above but this is clearly not 100% of both groups.

  • For those keeping score at home, the interest rate on the existing ‘26 term loan is 8.4%. The rate on the new paper is SOFR+600-700 which, today, equates to roughly 12%. Just an itty bitty increase on $1b of debt there, y’all. Nothing to see here. “[W]e didn’t give up much of anything.” 👌

  • Aron be like, “POW-ell, please lower rates soon, please please.” 🙏

  • There’s also a springing maturity here if at least $190mm of the 7.50% first lien secured notes due ‘29 have not been repurchased (and cancelled), repaid or refinanced by October 5, 2028. So, the latitude given by lenders isn’t exactly until ‘29. Clearly Aron is confident this won’t be an issue.

  • Muvico also privately issued $414.4mm*** of 6.00%/8.00% Cash/PIK Toggle Senior Secured Exchangeable Notes due 2030, guaranteed by AMC, the guarantors under the ‘26 term loan, the 10%/12% Cash/PIK Toggle second lien subordinated notes due in ‘26, and Centertainment Development LLC (“Centertainment”), which are exchangeable into common stock.**** These funds were used to repurchase $414.4mm of the 10%/12% Cash/PIK Toggle second lien subordinated notes due in ‘26. You can see the interest rate differential here. This is a positive both in terms of rate reduction and potential debt reduction. Unless you’re a stockholder.

  • How did this happen? We’ll spare you the down and dirty but a group of “specified existing lenders”***** amended the existing term loan credit agreement, stripped certain affirmative covenants, negative covenants, and events of default, and directed Wilmington Savings Fund Society FSB as collateral agent to subordinate the existing ‘26 term loan to the new issuance. Notably, the transaction leaves open the possibility of additional future $800mm of open market purchases of the ‘26 term loan (read: the remainder) — an attempt to make LME deals a bit more user friendly?!? So this is violent, but a bit less “violent,” if you will?******

AMC is not the only one contending with the headwinds. As PETITION readers well know, Cineworld, the owner of Regal Entertainment Group, already had the pleasure of getting plundered by restructuring professionals. Though it may no longer be frequenting a bankruptcy court, it nevertheless remains in a steady state of restructuring. Here is The Hollywood Reporter discussing the theater chain’s travails in the UK, where Cineworld may trim 25% of its footprint and possibly more:

“We continue to review our options, but we don’t comment on rumors and speculation,” a Cineworld spokesperson told The Hollywood Reporter in a statement. The company responded to a report in the U.K. by Sky News that the movie theater operator was looking to shutter about 25 of its British theaters, and renegotiate rent costs with landlords at another 50 locations.

AlixPartners LLP is taking its talents across the Atlantic for this particular mandate after working feverishly on the stateside restructuring.

Speaking of stateside, it appears that Regal is revisiting an old playbook to spark growth. Per The Hollywood Reporter:

Regal Cinemas, the second-largest movie theater chain in the U.S., has secured $250 million to give its locations an upgrade, the exhibitor said on Tuesday.

The raise is intended to boost its already existing locations (425 theaters across the country) with enhancements like luxury recliners and other amenities. Regal is owned by embattled theater giant Cineworld, which filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in September 2022 and emerged from its financial restructuring process last year.

In other words, as bad news bombs the industry, theater executives are in a similar position as studio executives: fresh out of fresh ideas.
