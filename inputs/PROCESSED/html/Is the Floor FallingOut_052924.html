💥Is the Floor Falling Out?💥

⚡One to Watch: LL Flooring Holdings Inc ($LL)⚡

We’re old enough to remember when do-it-yourself (“DIY”) home projects were all the rage back during the pandemic. It’s been four years since the lockdowns and LL Flooring Holdings Inc ($LL) must be longing for those glory days; it was there to provide all the prospective new home renovators with the materials needed for their next flooring project. How’s it doing now? Let’s dig in.

LL is one of North America’s leading specialty retailers of flooring with 437 stores as of December 31, 2023. The company sells a variety of different flooring options including vinyl plank, hardwood, laminate, bamboo, tile, and cork.

Who could’ve expected inflationary pressure, higher labor costs, rising interest rates, and lower discretionary spending to negatively impact a company like this?! 🙄

And yes, things are dire on the macro front. Remodeling activity is dropping significantly from post-COVID highs and is expected to decrease further in ‘24.

Likewise, existing home sales have taken an absolute dump:

And per CEO, Charles Tyson, on the 4Q’23 earnings call:

“[T]he leading indicators predict that spending will remain pressured during the first three quarters before improving somewhat in Q4. In the fourth quarter, total revenues declined 19.7% and the comp store sales declined 20.2%, driven by continued declines in traffic and lower average project sizes from our consumer and Pro customers.”

And so, to nobody’s surprise, the company’s operating results took a drastic hit in FY’23:

Let’s zoom in a little and look at those disastrous quarters:

Honestly, disastrous is underselling it. What you see there are three consecutive quarters of 20%+ same store sale declines!

And even though SG&A has decreased 11.5% in 4Q’23 and 2.3% for FY’23, inflationary pressure has pushed down margins across the board. Gross margin was 35.7% for FY’23 from a high of 39% back in FY’20 and both operating margin and EBITDA margin are now negative.

And 1Q’24 results weren’t any prettier (dare we say ugly AF). Net sales fell 21.7%, comparable sales decline came in at 21.5%, EBITDA was negative $23.9mm, and the company burned $23.7mm of cash.

The five year stock chart illustrates the highs and subsequent lows quite well:

This lackluster performance led to a few interesting developments in FY’23.

On May 30, 2023, the company issued a press release informing investors of an unsolicited offer from Cabinets To Go LLC, a subsidiary of F9 Brands Inc (“F9”), to acquire all the outstanding shares of LL for $5.76/share. At the time, F9 owned 9.4% of LL’s equity and, in a letter to LL, stated:

“We continue to believe that our proposal represents the best means for LL shareholders to maximize the value of their shares on an expeditious and cost-effective basis. We remain committed to moving forward with our offer and working collaboratively with the Board in a friendly manner and hope LL is willing to do the same.”

Dentons US LLP (“Dentons”) and Solomon Partners were advising F9 as legal counsel and investment banker, respectively.

Coincidentally, or not so coincidentally, F9 is run by Tom Sullivan, who originally founded LL Flooring as Lumber Liquidators back in 1994. Mr. Sullivan, like a hopeless romantic, can’t seem to forget about his first love business. He was previously interested in buying the company in ‘19 but backed out due to an elevated share price (lol). He promptly sold 80% of his investment in LL.

Other than Cabinets To Go, F9 also owns names like Southwind Building Products (a flooring manufacturer), Gracious Home (a houseware retailer), and Thos Baker (an outdoor furniture online retailer). Evidently F9 wanted to take advantage of a depressed stock price in order to integrate LL into its complementary business portfolio.

LL’s stock ended trading on May 30, 2023 at a price of $4.14/share. The purchase price, therefore, would’ve represented a 39% premium. But it’s all relative: the stock had been on quite the slide by that point, coming down from a high of $6.54 back in just February ‘23. So the board, in consultation with its advisors, JP Morgan Securities LLC, and Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom LLP, quickly rejected the offer, as announced in a press release dated June 26, 2023.

The board be like:

Or put more eloquently:

“Following our comprehensive review process, the Board is unanimous in its belief that the price of $5.76 per share in cash proposed in your letter significantly undervalues LL Flooring, its business and prospects.”

Despite rejecting the F9 offer, by August ‘23, the company officially announced that it was exploring strategic alternatives including a potential sale. And so enter suitor number two, Live Ventures Inc ($LIVE), advised by Vinson & Elkins LLP.

In yet another unsolicited offer, the company announced on October 12, 2023 that Live Ventures was looking to acquire the company at a price of $5.85/share.* 9 cents more than F9’s proposal, how generous!

Live Ventures, a holding company focused on middle-market companies, isn’t unfamiliar with the flooring industry. It acquired Flooring Liquidators Inc, a flooring retailer, in January ‘23 for $84mm and also owns Marquis Industries Inc, a flooring manufacturer.

By October ‘23, LL’s stock price had already dropped to $2.84 so Live Ventures’ offer represented a premium of 106%.

But that’s not all, guess who’s back!? That’s right, on November 14, 2023, Mr. Sullivan returned with a significantly lowered bid of $3.00/share. The corresponding offer letter also contained some juicy comments regarding the board:

“Despite our attempts to discuss a potential transaction, [the board] refused to allow confirmatory due diligence that may have enabled us to resubmit our original offer unless and until we agreed to a “standstill agreement” that would have prevented us from nominating a slate of candidates for election to the Board of Directors at the next annual meeting of shareholders. Given the Board of Directors’ track record and repeated history of refusing to meaningfully engage with us, we were and continue to be unwilling to enter into a standstill agreement which prevents us from nominating candidates at the upcoming annual meeting.”

It seems that the board continued to insist on a standstill agreement — the cycle goes round and round — and Mr. Sullivan withdrew his bid on January 18, 2024.

But, Mr. Sullivan wasn’t done. On April 11, 2024, LL received a letter from Mr. Sullivan’s counsel, Dentons, regarding the lack of a date for the 2024 annual meeting. You see, Mr. Sullivan is fed up with the board and intends to nominate himself and two others for seats on the nine member board. We love a good proxy war.

In response, the company announced a July 10, 2024 annual meeting date, but, more importantly, solicited the support of Live Ventures through a Cooperation Agreement. Under the agreement, Live Ventures (as the white knight) will withdraw any board nominations and will vote their 3.8% ownership in support of LL’s directors (aka against any F9 nominations).

Battle lines are already being drawn via proxy statements. Here’s the one from F9, pushing for their three board nominees and providing some choice words for the incumbent board and management:

“LL disclosed in the First Quarter 2024 10-Q what we have suspected for some time, which is that there is substantial doubt regarding its ability to continue as a going concern or to make enough money to simply stay afloat. We reject LL’s solution to sell a distribution center and potentially other long-term assets and seek additional financing to meet its obligations as a mere band-aid, and with no recognition of the inevitable resulting significant expense increases. There is a palpable sense of uncertainty regarding LL, and we question whether the Board and management appreciates its current position or understands that in this climate of uncertainty, adaptability and strategic planning are paramount but instead we see management’s stubborn focus on its failed long-term strategic plan.”

And here’s the rather defensive proxy statement form the company which provides a behind the scenes look at the events that occurred since ‘23. Most notably, how the previously mentioned $5.85/share unsolicited offer from Live Ventures has now dropped to $2.50/share. Parties are still in negotiations (including with an unidentified strategic buyer) but Live Ventures is evidently trying to get into the good graces of the board.

The question is, given the rejection of F9’s repeated solicitations (and multiple other solicitations), does the board actually intend to close on any acquisition or are they dead set on weathering through the macro environment and emerge into sunny days? Can the company last another year or two of disastrous same store sales declines?

The debt isn’t crippling (yet). As of March 31, 2024 there was (i) $89mm outstanding under a revolver due April 30, 2026 at a weighted average interest rate of 6.7%, (ii) $6mm of cash on the BS, and (iii) $57.3mm still available through the revolver. So liquidity wise, it’s not terrible.

But things are evidently looking worse. While CFO came in at a positive $21.3mm for FY’23 on the back of a $61.2mm reduction in inventory, Q1’24 saw a negative CFO number of $23.7mm. The company is already looking to a sale-leaseback transaction on their distribution center to shore up liquidity — usually a leading indicator of more trouble ahead.

20%+ comparable sales declines can’t be ignored, especially when there’s less and less juice to be squeezed from working capital and cash burn starts catching up.

The floor is slowly crumbling underneath LL.

Can management fix it?

💥New Chapter 11 Bankruptcy - Zachry Holdings Inc.💥

On May 21, 2024, San Antonio-based Zachry Holdings Inc. (“ZII”) and 20 affiliates (collectively, the “debtors”) filed chapter 11 bankruptcy cases in the Southern District of Texas (Judge Isgur).

The debtors were lead contractors on the massive, and we mean very massive “Golden Pass” liquified natural gas (“LNG”) project (fronted by Golden Pass LNG Terminal LLC). You may well remember Golden Pass from the McDermott International Inc. (“McDermott”) bankruptcy cases from back in ‘20; McDermott's CB&I LLC unit (“CB&I,” acquired in 2017 and formerly known as Chicago Bridge and Iron*) is a construction partner, as is Chiyoda International Corporation (“Chiyoda,” and the debtors, CB&I and Chiyoda are, collectively, “CCZJV”). Zachry is fed up, and is seeking a “structured exit from the Golden Pass LNG Project.” It carries $281mm in funded debt and $163.3mm in cash as of the petition date.

Qatar Energy (70%) and Exxon Mobil Corp. ($XOM)(30%) own Golden Pass, which in late January ‘19 awarded CCZJV a $9.25b contract to build a three-train LNG project on the Gulf Coast. Why? Because projects of this magnitude “are so complex and expensive that they require the combined efforts” of multiple EPC shops.

That ⬆️ is lifted directly from the debtors’ adversary complaint against Golden Pass. Yes, an adversary complaint!

The debtors tell a grim, and unfortunately familiar, story: Two of the “largest, wealthiest, and most sophisticated oil and gas companies on earth” using their “wealth and power” to force the debtors — a century-old engineering, procurement and construction (“EPC”) firm which has participated in some of the most complex projects in history — to front spiraling costs with “false promises” of eventual reimbursement. The debtors, whose ‘23 revenues were $5.3b, filed their bankruptcy to obtain a “breathing space,” according to first day filings and the adversary complaint seeks to avoid multiple agreements and contract amendments, and to recover over $1b in expenses.

It seems that January ‘19 may well have been the very worst time to commence such a project: just up around the bend, with garottes and saps at the ready, lurked the pandemic, supply chain challenges, surging freight costs, Vladimir Putin. And the contract itself didn’t do the debtors any favors: Golden Pass wanted the project started and completed ASAP. The owners initially said that the first train would be operational in late ‘23, but delays began out of the gate. “For instance,” the adversary pleading states, “...geotechnical reports supplied by Golden Pass erroneously described soil conditions at the plant site, forcing a redesign of numerous plant structures.” WHOOPS!

And it got worse. But first, a quick contextual digression: here ⬇️ is Golden Pass itself, brooding malignantly as a pale sun retreats before the darkness that will soon overwhelm the land. This is where dreams die.

Sabine Pass, through which the eponymous lake dumps into the Gulf of Mexico, is the border between Texas and Louisiana. It is one of the few deepwater ports on a coast adorned with petrochemical plants, refineries, storage tanks and pipelines. The Sabine Pass is proximate to the Permian, Haynesville and Eagle Ford shales whose mind-boggling volumes of natural gas created one of the U.S.’s very few export successes of recent years: liquified molecules loaded to tankers.

Dollar signs danced in the eyes of Qatar Energy, Exxon and a whole host of others. Natural gas is “clean,” of course, and hey, we all want(ed) Greta Thunberg to be happy. There are plenty of Asian buyers (and now European ones, what with that “accident” that happened to Nord Stream II). From the Energy Information Agency (“EIA”):

We said Golden Pass was “massive” earlier. How massive? When (if?) completed, it will boast three liquefaction trains, each with an annual capacity of 5.2mm tons (15.6mm tons in the aggregate) and require over 6k workers onsite on the busiest days. And:

The LNG facilities being built are so large that GPX Project updates have a direct impact on the overall U.S. market for natural gas. For example, after Exxon recently announced a delay of the start-up of the Golden Pass facility, there was a 5% drop in the 2024 U.S. natural gas benchmark pricing, which some analysts attributed to the announcement.

Just by way of comparison: the EIA a while back noted that two other projects —Plaquemines LNG and Corpus Christi Stage III — “used a modular technology with mid-scale refrigeration trains, which has a shorter project construction timeline.” Calcasieu Pass LNG, which used the same mid-scale liquefaction technology, loaded its first cargo 30 months after its final investment decision, which the EIA says is the shortest construction period for any U.S. LNG export project so far.

That $9.25b contract for Golden Pass? It was, as the adversary complaint describes, of the “lump sum” or “fixed price” variety. Such contracts set a price for each portion of the work. The contractor is paid monthly and on hitting milestones; the contractor pays subcontractors and vendors. The prices and the schedule are subject to change as circumstances — “force majeure” — warrants. In theory, the contractor can recoup excess spending through a “change order.”

But thanks to the pandemic, et al, delays piled up and costs “exploded” (the adversary complaint’s words). Golden Pass demanded that “CCZJV take whatever action was necessary, and pay any cost necessary, to keep the GPX Project on track.” Why this urgency? “The schedule for the GPX Project was abnormally important for Golden Pass’s owners, ExxonMobil and QatarEnergy, not only because the new LNG trains would generate billions of dollars a year for them, but also because they had made public statements, such as one by ExxonMobil in an April 27, 2022 press release saying the Project was on track and would commence.” Of course. We can’t disappoint the market, can we? The debtors, however, had in October ‘21 informed Golden Pass that the cost had risen to $11b and the project account would go “cash negative” in January ‘22. This meant the debtors would be covering any overages. “Don’t worry, buddy!” Golden Pass said. We gotcha covered.

Per the debtors:

It was a familiar approach that Zachry had encountered with ExxonMobil on other projects. But it was infeasible here, given the extraordinary cost of the GPX Project. Zachry did not have the resources of companies like ExxonMobil or QatarEnergy and was working under a “lump sum” contract. But Zachry had little choice but to comply. Plus, it had assurances from two of the wealthiest companies in the world that Zachry would be made whole. But, by complying, Zachry depleted its financial reserves and incurred hundreds of millions of dollars in obligations on the Project.

What came to pass was a frightening mismatch between Golden Pass’ payments to the debtors, and the debtors’ disbursements to vendors. “Prior to April 2024,” CRO Mohsin Meghji (of financial advisory shop, M3 Advisory Partners) states in his first day declaration, “ZII was incurring approximately $30 million to $40 million in expenses per week to support the project (including payroll, vendor payments, equipment, and support costs), but was only being compensated approximately $70 million per month in progress payments for that work.”

The “change order” process, the mechanism through which the debtors would be made whole, seems a nightmare. The parties met in April ‘22 to hash things out. “Rather than acting quickly, Golden Pass delayed any resolution of the matter by requesting make-work meetings, workshops, and `true cost’ exercises to verify what the parties already knew.” The cash shortfall grew to around $1b at the end of ‘22: “Golden Pass refused to negotiate and demanded that Zachry and CCZJV engage in more exercises to review each individual change order claim and perform an ‘open book’ auditing exercise.” Golden Pass wanted them desperate for cash, the debtors say — that way, Golden Pass could “…use economic duress and its vastly superior wealth and bargaining power to induce a favorable resolution of the outstanding change order claims.” And here we thought it was just the big distressed-oriented funds that could be coercive AF!

And economic duress is what happened: “believing they had no choice,” and relying on Golden Path’s representations, CCZJV signed a Claims Settlement Agreement under which ZII waived and released $1.7b in change order claims. The parties incorporated this into the contract through a First Amendment, which also changed the definition of “force majeure” to exclude the pandemic and the war in Ukraine. This amendment also shifted a “large share” of the construction costs to the debtors, rendering them effectively insolvent.

Nevertheless, work continued and the parties eventually signed two additional contract amendments. A second amendment in March ‘23 provided some relief by giving Golden Pass “discretion to advance future milestone payments,” provided the debtors serve up a letter of credit. December ‘23 brought a “side letter” and a Third Amendment, which acknowledged the cash-flow challenges and declared that Golden Pass was willing to provide future support.

In April, things began to accelerate. ExxonMobil proposed that the debtors exit as lead construction contractor, and instructed them to work out the terms with the project manager. The project manager asked CCZJV to make a proposal. Per the debtors, “The parties proceeded to spend days negotiating the terms of their negotiations. They spent days more negotiating a confidentiality agreement to govern their negotiations. Then, once that was done, Golden Pass refused to negotiate. It eventually agreed to participate in a mediation that lasted several days, but it sent no one to the mediation with any authority to negotiate.” Negotiating the terms of the negotiations, huh? GOTTA LOVE IT WHEN THE LAWYERS GET INVOLVED, LOL.

The reason for all of this, the adversary complaint says, soon became apparent: under the terms of the Third Amendment, Golden Pass started paying vendors directly, by clawing back funds from advance project payments due the debtors. Then Golden Pass quit paying the vendors altogether in March. And look what the mailman brought on May 8: a default notice, which cited, along other things, the debtors’ “...(i) inability to promptly pay subcontractors and vendors for labor, equipment, and/or materials in accordance with the EPC Contract and (ii) failure to resolve liens asserted on the GPX Project in excess of $23.5 million and progress the rework of a levee in connection with the GPX Project.” Subsequent notices of default from CB&I and Chiyoda came in fast and furious.

And so here we are. The adversary suit seeks Avoidance of the Claims Settlement Agreement, First Amendment, Second Amendment, and Third Amendment to the EPC Contract; and equitable subordination of any claims that Golden Pass may assert against ZII, trial costs and such other and further relief.

It’s shabby for ZII,** which would qualify as hereditary Texas EPC royalty, if such existed. Founded by Pat Zachry in 1924, its first big contract was a series of four highway concrete-reinforced bridges in Laredo, Texas. The company used mule-drawn wagons in construction. Oh, for simpler times:

The company grew rapidly, despite the Depression; a job paving Randolph Field in San Antonio, soon Randolph Air Force Base; and the Texas-New Mexico pipeline in Lea County, NM. In 1966, it was one of five contractors on the largest single contract in U.S. history —the development of the Yuba River in California in connection with a multipurpose flood control, water and power project. Zachry’s fingerprints are also on San Antonio’s Hilton Palacio del Rio; it prefabricated the rooms, fitted them with furnishings, then lifted them by crane to the designated floor:

“The feat is now remembered as a local legend,” the first-day declaration says, and justifiably so. Our point here is that the evidence suggests that the debtors know what they are doing when it comes to large and complex projects.

As for Golden Pass? On May 21, 2024, it issued a public statement that “acknowledged” the chapter 11 filing and said that work continues with Chiyoda and CB&I. “The project is already 75% progressed and we are committed to completing the project.” According to the EIA, the first train was expected to come in service in the first half of 2025, the second in 2H 2025, and the third in the first half of 2026. That was, however, before the debtors filed.

The debtors’ first day hearing was on May 21, 2024. Judge Isgur granted all requested relief — including, with all of that cash on hand, the use of cash collateral to fund the cases.***

On May 24, 2024, the debtors, Chiyoda, Golden Pass and CB&I LLC entered into a joint stipulation and agreed order appointing a mediator to sift through the drama. The mediator? Judge Lopez! Hey Judge Lopez…

The debtors are represented by White & Case LLP (Bojan Guzina, Andrew O’Neill, RJ Szuba, Barrett Lingle, Charles Koster, Gabriela Delgado, Alonso Aquije, Clint Simkins, Michael Andolina) as legal counsel and M3 Advisory Partners LP (Mohsin Meghji) as CRO and financial advisor. CBI&I LLC is represented by Kirkland & Ellis LLP (Joshua Sussberg, Christopher Greco, John Luze, Jeffrey Michalik). Chiyoda is represented by Haynes and Boone LLP (Charles Beckham Jr., Kelli Norfleet, Imaan Patel, Charles Jones II, Martha Wyrick) and Golden Pass has Norton Rose Fulbright US LLP (Robert Bruner) as counsel. XOM is represented by Porter Hedges LLP (Aaron Power).

*The McDermott/CB&I deal, valued at $6b and which closed in May 2018, seemed like a good idea at the time. David Dickson, then-CEO of McDermott, said the new entity, a mashup of McDermott’s upstream businesses with CB&I’s downstream, would design and build the energy infrastructure of the future. Alas, CB&I’s project portfolio contained two very troubled LNG projects (Cameron LNG and Freeport LNG). Cost overruns were one of the key causes of McDermott’s 2020 chapter 11 filing.
** The company split into two separate businesses In 2008: Zachry Industrial, which focuses on industrial projects, and Zachry Construction, which handles heavy civil and building construction work. No entities related to Zachry Construction’s business are included in these chapter 11 cases.
***Bank of America NA ($BAC) is the administrative agent under the debtors’ debt facility. It is represented by McGuireWoods LLP (Demetra Liggins, Andrew Papa, Shawn Fox).

For example, after Exxon recently announced a delay of the start- up of the Golden Pass facility, there was a 5% drop in the 2024 U.S. natural gas benchmark pricing, which some analysts attributed to the announcement. Just by way of comparison: the EIA a while back noted that two other projects Plaquemines LNG and Corpus Christi Stage III “ used a modular technology with mid- scale refrigeration trains, which has a shorter project construction timeline. ” Calcasieu Pass LNG, which used the same mid- scale liquefaction technology, loaded its first cargo 30 months after its final investment decision, which the EIA says is the shortest construction period for any U.S. LNG export project so far. That $9.25b contract for Golden Pass? It was, as the adversary complaint describes, of the “lump sum” or “fixed price” variety. Such contracts set a price for each portion of the work. The contractor is paid monthly and on hitting milestones; the contractor pays subcontractors and vendors. The prices and the schedule are subject to change as circumstances “force majeure” warrants. In theory, the contractor can recoup excess spending through a “change order.” But thanks to the pandemic, et al, delays piled up and costs “exploded” (the adversary complaint’s words). Golden Pass demanded that “ CCZJV take whatever action was necessary, and pay any cost necessary, to keep the GPX Project on track. ” Why this urgency? “ The schedule for the GPX Project was abnormally important for Golden Pass’s owners, ExxonMobil and QatarEnergy, not only because the new LNG trains would generate billions of dollars a year for them, but also because they had made public statements, such as one by ExxonMobil in an April 27, 2022 press release saying the Project was on track and would commence. ” Of course. We can’t disappoint the market, can we? The debtors, however, had in October ‘21 informed Golden Pass that the cost had risen to $11b and the project account would go “cash negative” in January ‘22. This meant the debtors would be covering any overages. “Don’t worry, buddy!” Golden Pass said. We gotcha covered. Source: PETITION Meme Department Per the debtors: It was a familiar approach that Zachry had encountered with ExxonMobil on other projects. But it was infeasible here, given the extraordinary cost of the GPX Project. Zachry did not have the resources of companies like ExxonMobil or QatarEnergy and was working under a “lump sum” contract. But Zachry had little choice but to comply. Plus, it had assurances from two of the wealthiest companies in the world that Zachry would be made whole. But, by complying, Zachry depleted its financial reserves and incurred hundreds of millions of dollars in obligations on the Project. What came to pass was a frightening mismatch between Golden Pass’ payments to the debtors, and the debtors’ disbursements to vendors. “ Prior to April 2024, ” CRO Mohsin Meghji (of financial advisory shop, M3 Advisory Partners ) states in his first day declaration, “ ZII was incurring approximately $30 million to $40 million in expenses per week to support the project (including payroll, vendor payments, equipment, and support costs), but was only being compensated approximately $70 million per month in progress payments for that work. ” The “change order” process, the mechanism through which the debtors would be made whole, seems a nightmare. The parties met in April ‘22 to hash things out. “ Rather than acting quickly, Golden Pass delayed any resolution of the matter by requesting make- work meetings, workshops, and `true cost’ exercises to verify what the parties already knew. ” The cash shortfall grew to around $1b at the end of ‘22: “ Golden Pass refused to negotiate and demanded that Zachry and CCZJV engage in more exercises to review each individual change order claim and perform an ‘open book’ auditing exercise. ” Golden Pass wanted them desperate for cash, the debtors say that way, Golden Pass could “… use economic duress and its vastly superior wealth and bargaining power to induce a favorable resolution of the outstanding change order claims. ” And here we thought it was just the big distressed- oriented funds that could be coercive AF! Source: PETITION Meme Department And economic duress is what happened: “ believing they had no choice ,” and relying on Golden Path’s representations, CCZJV signed a Claims Settlement Agreement under which ZII waived and released $1.7b in change order claims. The parties incorporated this into the contract through a First Amendment, which also changed the definition of “force majeure” to exclude the pandemic and the war in Ukraine. This amendment also shifted a “large share” of the construction costs to the debtors, rendering them effectively insolvent. Nevertheless, work continued and the parties eventually signed two additional contract amendments. A second amendment in March ‘23 provided some relief by giving Golden Pass “ discretion to advance future milestone payments, ” provided the debtors serve up a letter of credit. December ‘23 brought a “side letter” and a Third Amendment, which acknowledged the cash- flow challenges and declared that Golden Pass was willing to provide future support. In April, things began to accelerate. ExxonMobil proposed that the debtors exit as lead construction contractor, and instructed them to work out the terms with the project manager. The project manager asked CCZJV to make a proposal. Per the debtors, “ The parties proceeded to spend days negotiating the terms of their negotiations. They spent days more negotiating a confidentiality agreement to govern their negotiations. Then, once that was done, Golden Pass refused to negotiate. It eventually agreed to participate in a mediation that lasted several days, but it sent no one to the mediation with any authority to negotiate. ” Negotiating the terms of the negotiations, huh? Source: PETITION Meme Department The reason for all of this, the adversary complaint says, soon became apparent: under the terms of the Third Amendment, Golden Pass started paying vendors directly, by clawing back funds from advance project payments due the debtors. Then Golden Pass quit paying the vendors altogether in March. And look what the mailman brought on May 8: a default notice, which cited, along other things, the debtors’ “... (i) inability to promptly pay subcontractors and vendors for labor, equipment, and or materials in accordance with the EPC Contract and (ii) failure to resolve liens asserted on the GPX Project in excess of $23.5 million and progress the rework of a levee in connection with the GPX Project. ” Subsequent notices of default from CB&I and Chiyoda came in fast and furious. And so here we are. The adversary suit seeks Avoidance of the Claims Settlement Agreement, First Amendment, Second Amendment, and Third Amendment to the EPC Contract; and equitable subordination of any claims that Golden Pass may assert against ZII, trial costs and such other and further relief. It’s shabby for ZII,** which would qualify as hereditary Texas EPC royalty, if such existed. Founded by Pat Zachry in 1924, its first big contract was a series of four highway concrete- reinforced bridges in Laredo, Texas. The company used mule- drawn wagons in construction. Oh, for simpler times: The company grew rapidly, despite the Depression; a job paving Randolph Field in San Antonio, soon Randolph Air Force Base; and the Texas- New Mexico pipeline in Lea County, NM. In 1966, it was one of five contractors on the largest single contract in U.S. history the development of the Yuba River in California in connection with a multipurpose flood control, water and power project. Zachry’s fingerprints are also on San Antonio’s Hilton Palacio del Rio; it prefabricated the rooms, fitted them with furnishings, then lifted them by crane to the designated floor: “ The feat is now remembered as a local legend, ” the first- day declaration says, and justifiably so. Our point here is that the evidence suggests that the debtors know what they are doing when it comes to large and complex projects. As for Golden Pass? On May 21, 2024, it issued a public statement that “acknowledged” the chapter 11 filing and said that work continues with Chiyoda and CB&I. “ The project is already 75% progressed and we are committed to completing the project. ” According to the EIA , the first train was expected to come in service in the first half of 2025, the second in 2H 2025, and the third in the first half of 2026. That was, however, before the debtors filed. The debtors’ first day hearing was on May 21, 2024.
