The Starting Five
  1. The Kelce chronicles : Back in the spring, soon after Jason Kelce retired following a 13-year career with the Philadelphia Eagles, his reps opened talks with each of the NFL’s TV partners: Amazon, CBS, ESPN, Fox, and NBC. Not surprisingly, they all showed initial interest. He’s personable, he’s good on TV, and over the course of his career, he’s been a reliable go-to quote for the beat writers. Oh, and his brother happens to be dating the most famous woman on the planet.

    During the process, it became apparent that the offensive lineman was interested in more than just becoming another talking head. After all, he and Travis had just cut a $100 million podcast deal with Wondery for New Heights , and their mother’s Chiefs-Eagles Super Bowl-era Campbell’s Soup ads offered them a taste of commercial resonance. Kelce told the execs that he wanted to host his own late-night show—complete with a brass band. Network executives were leery about committing to a TV show with someone who had zero television experience, but he is a Kelce, so they all showed interest.

    ESPN wound up signing Kelce for around $8 million per year, per several sources. (On Thursday, I cited reports that put his salary at under $2 million and was bombarded by texts with the real number. Thanks, guys.) The ESPN deal centers on Kelce’s contribution to the MNF studio show, but the network will honor his late-night request by broadcasting five episodes—produced by NFL Films, as I also noted last week—on consecutive Friday nights at 1 a.m. ET in January. A five-episode run will presumably give ESPN execs enough of a chance to decide if they want to move forward with it or not. No word yet on the brass band.

  2. Is “shared reality” really a thing? : People have been bugging me to write about this relatively new company called Cosm for the better part of a year, but I’ve resisted. Cosm is essentially a virtual experiences company that creates a sort of miniature version of the The Sphere for fans, who watch a huge video screen (87-foot by 55-foot) that offers unimaginable views of the actions—the World Series from behind home plate or an NFL game from the line of scrimmage, etcetera. The company has two facilities in the U.S.—one in Hollywood Park, another outside of Dallas, and yet another forthcoming in Atlanta—and it is turning customers into evangelists. This morning, the NFL announced a deal that allows Cosm to carry all of the league’s primetime games (Thursday, Sunday, and Monday night), plus some additional Fox games through the end of this season.

    Despite its momentum, some of my sources worry about the business plan—the capex required, the scale challenges (the L.A. facility has 700 seats), etcetera—and the entire gimmicky shtick. At what point will fans encounter a similar experience through a headset? Or will the NFL eventually sanction its own experience? Anyway, not a lot of moats…

  3. Smoltz’s NFL influences : When I asked John Smoltz about the announcers that he tried to emulate, I was surprised that the baseball Hall of Famer immediately cited NFL analysts like Troy Aikman and Cris Collinsworth . “Troy Aikman explains things without dumbing it down,” Smoltz said. “Just because he played the sport, he could spit out all kinds of coverages and terms that nobody would know anything about. I want to be able to do that as a baseball broadcaster [for] everyone, from people who are tuning in for the first time, to an expert. And that’s a fine needle to thread.”

    Early in his broadcasting career, Smoltz would try to use as many stats as he could. Now, he shelves those stats in favor of telling stories. “I’m looking for moments that I lived. I don’t think there’s a moment in the game that I would be surprised could happen.” Smoltz made that comment before Freddie Freeman hit the first walk-off grand slam in World Series history.

  4. MLB Network 15 years on : Loyal Varsity readers know how cord-cutting has ravaged league-owned networks like MLB Network, which is currently in a little more than 30 million homes. But the business has pivoted during the past decade-plus from a focus on the channel, itself, to serving MLB’s broader distribution needs. During the 2009 Yankees-Phillies World Series, the network had 35 people at Yankee Stadium producing MLB Tonight . For tonight’s Yankees-Dodgers game, there will be 200 staffers at the stadium, producing five studio shows.

    In that 2009 matchup, a post-prime and under-the-weather Pedro Martinez pitched for the Phillies in the final World Series game, played at the old Yankee Stadium. Pedro lasted four miserable innings, courtesy of Hideki Matsui . Tonight, Martinez will be part of MLB’s coverage.

The Patrick question : Make sure you read my partner Bill Cohan ’s excellent piece on last week’s news that TKO bought Professional Bull Riding, On Location, and IMG from its own parentco, Endeavor. Bill goes through all of the deal’s incentives—including the $30 million that TKO is expected to save annually by getting rid of the massive services fee that the company was paying to Endeavor. Intriguingly, both Ari Emanuel and Mark Shapiro won’t be taking the massive $25 million and $100 million bonuses they were entitled to, respectively, as part of any deal that involved an asset sale. Guess that would have been a bad look.

    Anyway, I remain most interested in the fate of executive chairman Patrick Whitesell , who appears to be contemplating an exit. As Matt Belloni reported back in April when the take-private deal with Silver Lake was first announced, Whitesell was offered a $60 million golden parachute in addition to a $250 million investment for his next act. Matt stressed that this deal wouldn’t necessarily present an off-ramp for Whitesell, at least not immediately. “Whitesell will remain one of the largest shareholders in the company,” Matt wrote. “And his new deal gives him options. He can stay heavily involved with Endeavor and collect that hefty royalty on WME profits. Or he can leverage that $250 million from Silver Lake to raise more money and launch something new, and take a $60 million personal check for his troubles. Or he can do a version of both, staying on Endeavor’s board, but doing his own thing.”

    No update yet on what that new venture might be, but watch this space. Sources told Matt earlier this year that Whitesell wasn’t interested in launching a management company. “More likely,” Matt wrote, “it will be something at the center of entertainment, sports, and A.I., helping Silver Lake take advantage of the coming disruption.”
ESPN’s Streaming Squeeze
TV distributors are struggling to play hardball with the likes of ESPN, Peacock, and Paramount+ as sports programmers move their best content to streaming. But distribution executives also realize how much ESPN needs affiliate fees to compete against the deep-pocketed tech streamers that are piling into this space. And they’ve got their own ideas for how to use that leverage to their advantage.
JOHN OURAND
ESPN’s business plan is a modern media version of the old innovator’s dilemma: Chairman Jimmy Pitaro needs to build up his streaming business while simultaneously defending his cable revenue as much as possible, and as long as possible, in order to facilitate the transformation. Naturally, this sort of choreography—placating cable and satellite distributors while also occasionally screwing them over—ensures that eggs are cracked along the way. Earlier this year, for instance, distributors complained en masse about ESPN’s plan to launch the Venu sports streaming service alongside Fox and Warner Bros. Discovery, thereby creating a sort of cable-cannibalizing skinny sports bundle that would weaken cable system operators that rely on live sports to entice customers.

Earlier this month, those hard feelings were exacerbated when Disney announced that it would simulcast six more Monday Night Football games, normally the provenance of ESPN, on ABC. Distribution executives were understandably pissed that the most valuable programming on their most expensive channel was seeping outside of the bundle and available on broadcast. “We’re really only bill collectors now,” one distribution executive complained to me shortly after the simulcast deal was announced. “We’re not making any money in video.”

That’s hyperbole, obviously , but it illustrates the level of furor among distribution executives, many of whom feel like their partners are trying to undercut them and accelerate cord-cutting. (That’s also slightly hyperbolic, but you get the point.) The people I’ve spoken with aren’t exclusively furious with ESPN, of course; they also have plenty of ill will to spare for Peacock and poor old Paramount+, but Pitaro’s decision to further simulcast MNF really resonated. More than any other recent decision, it suggested that Disney would be delicately waving the white flag on cable exclusivity.
DirecTV’s Rob Thun addressed this phenomenon at the L.A. Sports Council’s annual conference earlier this month. Since its launch in the mid-1990s, DirecTV has marketed itself as a leader in sports TV. In the last few years, however, the satellite distributor has lost NFL Sunday Ticket rights and had a very public battle with Disney during which ESPN went dark on the service for nearly two weeks. A year earlier, Disney and ESPN went dark on Charter for about the same amount of time.

ESPN clearly still holds most of the leverage in these battles—no distributor wants to lose its college football and NFL offerings every weekend. But it’s also clear that distribution executives recognize how much ESPN relies on its affiliate fees to underwrite its transformation into a legit streamer. “The pay TV model still has to have a level of subsidization in order for the content providers to have some certainty and actually to make the investments in the programming they make,” Thun explained onstage at the conference, articulating the distributor’s negotiating angle.

Thun said the fight with Disney hinged on whether DirecTV would be given the flexibility to offer genre-specific skinny bundles, like sports, kids, and news—their own Venu-style counterattack to offer consumers more affordable affinity-based packages rather than bloated bundles. “We over-index a little bit in sports fans,” Thun said. “And we have people who are casual sports fans who will watch a game of big interest like the Super Bowl. But there are plenty of customers who don’t watch sports whatsoever. And that’s part of what we’re trying to unlock for customers.”

ESPN’s model, of course, rests on the hypothesis that a large pure-play sports streamer—the product known in Bristol as “Flagship”—will enter the world with product-market fit. And yet, investment analysts at MoffettNathanson scrutinized this assumption in a recent report, which underscored the difficulty of building profitable streaming businesses with sports. Sure, sports can bring in new subscribers, but we have yet to see a sports strategy that can yield significant profits from streaming. After all, the rights fees are enormous, the production costs are formidable, the churn is seasonal, the tech stack is extendable but expensive, and the content, itself, is live and therefore ephemeral. “All of this,” MoffettNathanson writes—“the impact of ESPN Flagship, whether Venu still plans to launch, the future of Disney+ and Hulu either as standalone or integrated services, bundles and unbundling—leads to the key question at the heart of investor concerns: what are Disney’s long-term streaming margins?”
