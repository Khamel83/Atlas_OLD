Good morning,

This week’s Stratechery interview is with Michael Morton . Morton is a senior research analyst at MoffettNathanson with a focus on e-commerce and marketplaces. Morton has worked on both the buy-side and sale-side of equity analysis, and also worked on the YouTube strategy team prior to joining MoffettNathanson.

In this interview we discuss a few fascinating research notes Morton has written recently: he’s very bullish on Shopify’s enterprise offering, even as he thinks that brands will end up on Amazon in the long run. We get into why he thinks both things are true, along with covering a host of other e-commerce topics: how COVID changed the structure of e-commerce, Shopify Audiences, Amazon Buy With Prime and advertising businesses, and why Walmart is poised to be the other big winner in the U.S.

The Barbell Effect in E-commerce

So you are focused on the Internet generally, but e-commerce is what I most closely associate you with. There’s a thesis that you guys have had that it’s no longer a “everyone wins market”. What does that mean and how is that different from the way you thought about the world previously?

MM: I have to give credit to the title to Lisa Ellis , who was our payments analyst before she went to Visa. She read it a day or two before and I tried to have some catchy title and she said, “Sounds like you’re just saying this is no longer an ‘Everybody Wins Market'”, that was how it switched to the title.

The genesis of this view is the reality that pre-COVID, not to suggest it was easy to run an e-commerce platform, but there were the digitally-native platforms that lived their entire lifespan in an e-commerce world. Use a Chewy, for example, Wayfair, Amazon.

The brick-and-mortar players, I use the comparison to Michael Nathanson’s world of linear media, it’s very similar to what went on between Netflix and the legacy media players. Brick-and-mortar had a good business, your merchants and vendors fronted you your goods, consumers came and purchased them, handled the logistics for you, and it worked pretty well. Why would you run into this industry where you needed to have perfect price transparency? You can just Google a product and you can see how everybody’s price lines up, then you have to deliver these products to people’s doorsteps, which is really expensive, and then they return it and they ship it back.

Well, COVID forced brick-and-mortar to get really serious about their e-commerce strategies quickly. You saw them go from bumbling along the bottom with market share to almost overnight picking up material market share and improving their strategies.

It was a blessing for firms like Amazon and if you have either the largest inventory, or what I’ll refer to as SKU offering in the world with the best logistics network, or if you have unique inventory, it can be really good for you. Etsy, they have some of their own headwinds right now that I’m sure we’ll get the chance to talk about. Shopify, which sells some unique beauty supplies and vitamins and things like that that aren’t replicated around all the brick-and-mortar retailers. If you’re selling stuff that is not differentiated, dog food, shelf stable things, furniture that’s become widely commoditized, the post-COVID world is going to be much harder for you as an e-commerce operator.

So it’s interesting because some of the stuff you are saying there, I was just writing about this yesterday that one of the reasons, beyond the fact I’m in the media, that I like to write about the media is that the media predicts a lot of the trends that will hit other industries just because media is easy to put online, so you see online effects very quickly. It’s funny, you talk about this “Everybody wins”, including just people who started online, I think of all the Internet-native publishers that came along. We’re not talking about your old stodgy newspaper, “Oh yeah, they had a hard time making it online”, no, there was a lot of companies that started online with Internet assumptions and everybody seemed to be winning for a while. Then I remember I wrote in 2015, Popping the Publishing Bubble just saying, “Look, it’s not just the newspapers that are screwed, every single digital publisher is screwed”, and it’s the exact same thing that you were talking about. You’re going to either have a platform with everything, which is basically the aggregators, Google, Facebook, et cetera, or you’re going to have to be super highly differentiated with a business model to match. It sounds like COVID basically shoved all of retail into that paradigm to the extent something was pulled forward, that’s what was pulled forward.

MM: A generational pull forward for a lot of these businesses, and I think market participants have been digesting that for the last two years. It wasn’t immediately obvious, because there was a point in time where if you annualized what was going on in 2020, 2021, the numbers were just enormous. Then everyone realized, “Wait, there’s a lot more participants for these jump balls now”, and things are going to be a lot harder.

What’s interesting is because now the common narrative is the pull forward didn’t happen. Everything’s retreated. What I think you are driving at, which is really interesting to me, is that, yes, maybe in terms of consumer behavior there was a pullback and then e-commerce has returned to its trend line, or retreated to its trend line is a better way to put it, but the structural changes are done. We’re in the truly Internet era in terms of the barbell effect.

MM: Right. Retailers went from the balancing act of physical and digital to focusing more on digital. Now, I do think the consumer wants an omnichannel solution, the whole world doesn’t go digital. We recently spoke about the trend that you were just discussing as well where it hit publishers and media players, but we’re asking the question, “Is the Internet good for a lot of these companies?”.

Yeah, I have a whole section on that, because I think it’s interesting.

MM: It’s just tough. It’s changed how a lot of these businesses have to operate and you have to be able to compete in the digital world and also offer omnichannel solutions and the consumer gets increasingly demanding.

Brands’ E-commerce Choice

Let’s jump into that, the apparel point. You wrote recently about branded apparel, the choices they need to make with e-commerce, balancing retail versus e-commerce, and what do they do for e-commerce? To be super small, you keep your cost structure under control, and then differentiate, you find a limited base of customers, charge them a lot, and you can have a very nice business.

The Internet is made of text. Shopify’s Enterprise Opportunity

Let’s go in the other direction. So if you’re doing your own site, and this is very much in the other direction, actually, which I want to ask you about. MM: Yes, and it’s funny to follow the conversation that we just had about how Amazon is, the inevitability of Amazon.

MM: To be blunt, it’s a dueling battle that goes on in my head and I think in the marketplace as well.
