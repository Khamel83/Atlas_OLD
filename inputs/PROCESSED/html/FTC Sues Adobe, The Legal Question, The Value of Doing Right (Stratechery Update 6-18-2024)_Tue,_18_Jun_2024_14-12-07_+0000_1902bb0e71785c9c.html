FTC Sues Adobe

From Bloomberg :

The US Federal Trade Commission sued Adobe Inc., alleging the software company violated consumer protection laws by making it too difficult for consumers to cancel their subscriptions. Adobe pushed users toward annual subscriptions to its creative software such as Photoshop without “adequately disclosing” that canceling in the first year could cost hundreds of dollars, the FTC said Monday in a statement. The San Jose, California-based company also designed the cancellation process to be difficult, requiring navigating many online pages or transfers between customer services representatives, the regulators said.

“Adobe trapped customers into yearlong subscriptions through hidden early termination fees and numerous cancellation hurdles,” said Samuel Levine, director of the FTC’s Bureau of Consumer Protection. The Justice Department filed the complaint in a California federal court on behalf of the FTC, which enforces US consumer protection laws.

Adobe said it will challenge the FTC’s claim in court. “Our priority is to always ensure our customers have a positive experience,” Dana Rao, the company’s general counsel, said in a statement. “We are transparent with the terms and conditions of our subscription agreements and have a simple cancellation process.”

Users of Adobe products have long complained about the expense of canceling a subscription. Accessing Adobe’s suite of apps can cost more than $700 annually for individuals. Subscribers must cancel within two weeks of buying a subscription to receive a full refund; otherwise, they incur a prorated penalty. Some other digital services such as those from Spotify Technology SA and Netflix Inc. don’t charge a cancellation fee.

This isn’t quite right: as long as you pick the actual “Monthly” plan you can cancel whenever you want. From the FTC’s complaint (these screenshots are fuzzy because they are taken directly from the complaint, which has fuzzy screenshots):

Adobe’s enrollment flows often begin with a plan selection page that presents available subscriptions and their prices, like the one illustrated below.

Enrollment Flow Example 1: Plan Selection Page In Creative Cloud Free Trial Flow, May 2023

The element of each plan that Adobe displays most prominently is a price, with the APM plan showing the lowest dollar amount. Adobe frequently also pre-selects the APM [Annual, Paid Monthly] plan as a default.

In Enrollment Flow Example 1, the bottom of each plan selection box includes a less prominent line of text, followed by a “tooltip” icon: ⓘ. In particular, the APM plan box states: “Fee applies if you cancel after 14 days. ⓘ.”

Consumers navigating the enrollment flow do not see the additional text contained in the tooltip (i.e., a small pop-up text box), which is in an even less prominent font, unless they hover over or click the ⓘ icon. Customers using a touchscreen device (e.g., a phone or tablet) must tap the ⓘ icon to view the tooltip. Consumers who access the ⓘ icon for the APM plan in this example flow reveal a tooltip that reads: “If you cancel after 14 days, your service will continue until the end of that month’s billing period, and you will be charged an early termination fee.”

Enrollment Flow Example 2: APM Plan Tooltip In Creative Cloud Free Trial Flow, May 2023

Enrollment Flow Example 2 is a zoomed-in version of the plan selection list featured on Adobe’s plan selection page. In this example, the tooltip icon is being engaged with (i.e., hovered over or clicked), revealing the informational text it displays.

As Enrollment Flow Example 2 shows, a consumer who accessed the information contained behind the tooltip is still not given concrete information about the amount of the ETF [Early Termination Fee], how it is calculated, or the subscription term, if any, to which it applies.

As Enrollment Flow Examples 1 and 2 show, the plan selection page does not state anywhere that the APM plan requires a one-year commitment. Consumers who do not access the ⓘ icon are not informed that the “fee” mentioned in the APM box in less prominent text is an ETF.

Moreover, the plan selection page, even in the text accessible only through the tooltip, does not clearly explain what constitutes “early termination,” i.e., that cancelling before paying for a full year of service will result in a fee.

Finally, the plan selection page does not disclose at all the amount of the fee that consumers would be charged for early cancellation.

This is the meat of the complaint, along with a section about a convoluted and multi-step cancellation flow. To further clarify my point above:

  • “Annual, paid monthly” is basically an installment plan; if you cancel early the termination fee is 50% of what you would pay if you stayed subscribed for the full year (the total cost of which would be $659.88).
  • “Annual, prepaid” is a single lump sum payment (and the cheapest option at $599.88).
  • “Monthly” is monthly; you can cancel anytime, but it is the most expensive option ($989.88 for a full year).

The specific plan at issue is “Annual, paid monthly.”

The Legal Question

The law that is allegedly being violated by Adobe is the Restore Online Shopper’s Confidence Act , which states:

It shall be unlawful for any person to charge or attempt to charge any consumer for any goods or services sold in a transaction effected on the Internet through a negative option feature (as defined in the Federal Trade Commission’s Telemarketing Sales Rule in part 310 of title 16, Code of Federal Regulations), unless the person—

(1) provides text that clearly and conspicuously discloses all material terms of the transaction before obtaining the consumer’s billing information;

(2) obtains a consumer’s express informed consent before charging the consumer’s credit card, debit card, bank account, or other financial account for products or services through such transaction; and

(3) provides simple mechanisms for a consumer to stop recurring charges from being placed on the consumer’s credit
card, debit card, bank account, or other financial account.

A “negative options feature” is defined as :

Negative option feature means, in an offer or agreement to sell or provide any goods or services, a provision under which the customer’s silence or failure to take an affirmative action to reject goods or services or to cancel the agreement is interpreted by the seller as acceptance of the offer.

There are a ton of complaints online about “Annual, paid monthly” from people who subscribed, canceled, and were shocked to be hit with that early termination fee; frankly, I have a hard time being sympathetic: it’s pretty clear on the plan selection page that only the “Monthly” option is cancellable at any time.

That noted, I do think that Adobe probably violates the law by failing to clearly and conspicuously disclose all material terms of the transaction; specifically, the only way to find out the amount of the early transaction fee is to click the “Subscription and Cancellation Terms” on the final page of the subscription flow (I circled the link in red because you wouldn’t be able to see it otherwise, which kind of makes the point here):

The specific cancellation terms, including the fact that the fee is 50%, is at the bottom of the modal dialog that pops up when you click that link:

Beyond the fact that these terms are buried, they also aren’t as clear as they can be, because the actual financial commitment of $659.88 isn’t disclosed anywhere in the process. In short, the actual annual cost is never disclosed (although the consumer can calculate it), and the early termination fee is buried; that’s not clear and conspicuous.

The Value of Doing Right

A decade ago Adobe transformed their business by switching the business model of their apps away from upfront pricing to a subscription model; I wrote positively about the decision in 2013’s Adobe’s Subscription Model & Why Platform Owners Should Care , making the case that a subscription model was superior for productivity applications because it better matched value capture from producers over time to the benefits gained by consumers, and urged the app stores to support subscriptions for productivity apps (which they eventually did, and now view as an important growth driver).

At the same time, my perspective is that of a business analyst; I am sympathetic to the Adobe users who were used to paying once for their software, and being able to use it forever if they so chose, being forced to pay on a subscription basis. What I also recognize is the inherent distaste a lot of people have for paying for software generally, given its zero marginal cost nature; granted, there are significant costs when it comes to maintaining online services, but that’s part of people’s beef with Adobe: they primarily want applications that run on their computer, and the addition of an online service (Creative Cloud) is galling precisely because its primary utility is enforcing that subscription model on (mostly) locally-run software.

Ultimately Adobe was able to get away with this because they are the standard across multiple creative industries, which creates an interesting sort of two-sided network between employers and employees: employers expect employees to be proficient in Adobe applications, and employees acquire marketable skills by being proficient in Adobe applications. Both sides may get grumpy about Adobe, but they also aren’t going to move to an alternative because the cost — not in terms of software, but in missed opportunities — is too high; this fact means that it is difficult for challengers to gain a foothold, particularly in terms of volume licensing deals.

I am, up to this point, still pretty sympathetic to Adobe’s position, and again, remember that I am a business analyst first and foremost: Adobe recognized the strength of their position and leveraged that into a much more profitable business model, and I respect that. At the same time, I think it behooves any entity in such a position to play above the board to the greatest extent possible: you are extracting maximum profits by leveraging your market position, which both makes your customers unhappy and has the risk of attracting regulatory scrutiny, which is exactly what happened here. Adobe’s violation is arguably minor, but what is most irksome is that it seems fairly unnecessary: just post the real costs, which the vast majority of your customers are going to pay regardless.

More generally, what bothers me about this case is the moral component; I assume this entire purchase flow has been A/B tested to death, and that the current configuration makes the most money, at least across whatever timeline is being considered. However, did anyone at Adobe stop and consider why that might be? Is there any answer other than, some number of users won’t be careful enough and will buy the wrong plan, and then get hit with a large fee, which will either drive revenue on its own or convince the user to begrudgingly not cancel, driving more revenue going forward? Even leaving aside the long-term hit to the brand’s reputation, what about doing the right thing?

I would, generally speaking, prefer less government regulation rather than more, and I think I speak for a lot of people in tech in that regard. Those people in tech, though, need to appreciate the extent to which choices like this one made by Adobe invite exactly that. No, I don’t think this so-called “dark pattern” is that dark, if you take just a second or two to look at the plans on offer; yes, there seems to me to be far more egregious cases that break the law in question much more flagrantly (like publications that make you call to cancel). However, at the end of the day, Adobe wasn’t satisfied with harvesting the natural gains from their dominant market position: they had to make money off of people screwing up too, and it’s hard to feel bad for them facing regulatory action for not doing right by potential customers.

Zooming out more generally, I wrote above that “I assume…the current configuration makes the most money, at least across whatever timeline is being considered”; I further assume that timeline didn’t include this lawsuit and the possible attendant fines. It almost certainly does not consider whatever configuration of software or workflows eventually displaces Adobe, fueled in part by user animosity towards practices like this (and to what extent that animosity drove a regulatory response to the abandoned Figma acquisition is unknown as well). Indeed, that is the challenge with the long-run: it’s hard to measure, and thus easily forgotten in metrics-driven decisions; all the more reason to have a bias towards doing the right thing, the value of which is eternal.

This also ties into yesterday’s Update about Apple’s AI Developer Risk; I’ve been urging Apple to soften their App Store policies — or at a minimum, their self-righteous rhetoric about the App Store — from the very beginning of this site. For most of that time period my reasons for them to do so basically boiled down to “because it’s the right thing to do”; it was a plea that was easy to dismiss as Apple’s approach seemed to have no effect on App Store growth or developer willingness to build on the iPhone. Sure they might not be happy, but what choice did they have, and in the meantime, there were Services revenue goals to hit! Then you fast forward to this year, and Apple needs developers for both the Vision Pro and for Apple Intelligence: we already know the former is missing apps , and the fate of the latter depends on developers taking a risk of commoditizing their apps in service of Apple delivering a superior AI-mediated experience. It sure would be nice to have not burned developer goodwill, which is to say that, over the long run, doing the right thing usually ends up being good business as well.
