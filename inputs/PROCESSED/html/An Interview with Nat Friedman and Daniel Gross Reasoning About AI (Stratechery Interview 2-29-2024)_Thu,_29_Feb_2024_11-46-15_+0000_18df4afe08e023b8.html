Good morning,

I am happy to welcome Daniel Gross and Nat Friedman back for a Stratechery Interview, the sixth in our ongoing series (we previously talked in October 2022 , December 2022 , March 2023 , August 2023 , and December 2023 ).

This series is somewhat unique for Stratechery in that my interview subjects are generally not investors; this series, though, started in October 2022, when Friedman and Gross were launching a grant program, precisely because we shared a sense that there wasn’t nearly enough activity or discussion around AI; one month later ChatGPT came out and the world has changed dramatically, including for Friedman and Gross, who are two of the leading investors in the space. To that end, I think it has been very valuable — and popular — to continue this series, but do keep in mind that Friedman and Gross may be invested in some of the companies we discuss.

That noted, this month has been particularly momentous in terms of AI news. We try and cover all of it, from Gemini to Sora to Groq to Mistral to Nvidia, as well as get into our usual philosophical questions about AI and what might come next, particularly as companies attach different kinds of models to transformers in the pursuit of reasoning.

Last year at the beginning of the year, Daniel and I launched a project to try to crack this problem. The general approach was to scan the scrolls at really high resolution in 3D without opening them and without damaging them. To do this, a particle accelerator is used to get very high resolution, and then hopefully to take those scans and reduce this problem to a software problem where you can use computer vision and machine learning algorithms to virtually unwrap and read these scrolls.

Until now this has never been done before, and when we launched this project Vesuvius Challenge a little over a year ago, or I guess a little under a year ago, I honestly didn’t know if it would work. I just thought this was incredibly cool and almost no one seemed to even know about this, about the existence of thousands of unopenable scrolls from antiquity, and we thought maybe we could motivate 1000 people with their laptops at home to take a crack at it, and that’s basically what happened.

Just last month we were really excited, or I guess early this month we were really excited, to be able to announce that the grand prize of $700,000 was awarded to a team that just achieved this landmark effort and they were able to reveal a huge portion of one scroll and read it. We have 2000 characters of Greek text that had never been seen before, it was totally new to the world.

And so, the project succeeded, it worked. So now, I guess our next step is to scale this up. We can read what we’ve read so far represents about 5% of one scroll, we have hundreds of scrolls left, there may be thousands more in the ground and so what we need to do is scale the algorithms up so that we can read whole scrolls, one at a time, and then hopefully all of them.

I think the piece of information that’s most interesting is the fact that Google lacked a very basic process. This is your point, where maybe people thought or maybe people didn’t even think before they launched it and I’m thinking a lot of that famous Steve Jobs interview where he says , “The problem with Microsoft is they just have no taste.” I think the unexpected thing about AI, we’ve talked about it in this podcast, but I don’t think it’s been generally expected, is fine-tuning a model is just as aesthetic an art as making a beautiful landing page for your website.

So in hindsight, it shouldn’t be that surprising that the Borg that built the interfaces of GCP also produced very robotic models, like that’s the same thing and it also should not be surprising to us that Mistral, which a French company with French cultures and now French products, was able to produce a model that to their credit, I mean, it’s not the smartest, but it’s by far the most obedient and has by far the most neutral political tone, at least in my anecdotal testing.

Well, actually, I want to get to Mistral in a moment, but Nat, what does Google do now?

DG: Other than call you?

NF: (laughing) Yeah, I mean I think this is a leadership challenge. There’s a missing editor here and there’s a missing product editor and a missing person with good taste and judgment who gives a damn and has the authority to overrule anyone in the company and make sure the right thing goes out the door. I do think leadership changes have to happen, culture is the hardest type of change to make in a company. You could do strategy change, you could do product change, you could do operational change. Culture change is the one that’s just super difficult and it can only happen with leadership. We either need to see dramatically different behavior from Google leadership or we need to see dramatically different leaders.

That’s why I wrote this Article. There have been reasons to question Pichai, there’s the classic wartime CEO versus peacetime CEO, he’s the epitome of a peacetime CEO, and he’s done a great job. This is not to diminish the stewardship of Google throughout the 2010s, the Aggregator era, it was in their interest to make the powers that be happy, to not be upset at them. The way they managed to never get criticized around things like censorship. On the Internet, they’ll get criticized, but in Congress, Facebook took all the arrows, they just managed to float along and he was very effective. That’s just not what Google needs right now, Google needs a wartime CEO, that’s a general thing. What I think this episode provides is a very tangible moment in time where this specific leadership style has utterly failed the company. It’s not just failing their future, it’s endangering their present as well.

NF: It’s a real opportunity actually, because when you make a big enough mistake, you have the opportunity to correct. A collection of small mistakes, it’s hard to do a big change, but when you obviously have just conspicuously failed, and I think that’s an exciting thing about AI is that it does make legible a bunch of things that are otherwise hard to notice or easy to conceal. AI has made some cultural aspects at Google very, very legible in the last week so now it’s like, “Oh gosh, there’s a bunch of dirty laundry that’s just being exposed to the light of day and now someone, I don’t know who, but someone has the opportunity to really use that as a driver for change, and it’s needed”. It’s not just about shipping, it’s about shipping things people want to use and that’s the big issue.

The product was not the priority. That gets to the bad faith issue.

NF: Well, I do give them credit for shipping, I do actually. I think it’s really interesting that Gemini 1.5 came so quickly after Gemini 1.0, that they chose to label it 1.5 and not 2.0, that they put Pro out before Ultra is ready, that they’re shipping as much as they are. I think they did break the static friction at least, but it’s clear that their aim is off and their aim is off for deep cultural reasons and organizational reasons. I’m sure the DeepMind team, the Gemini team really had nothing to do with this, it’s actually a shame that it happened.

Yeah, I can’t imagine how frustrating that must be. Well, this is the other issue, they’re going to lose employees over this. “I built this incredible model and this other layer of the company was allowed to sabotage it. Why should I be spending all my time here?”

NF: Well, and the challenge is that all those layers, who’s the common point that they report to? I think it’s probably Sundar and so he would have to actually exercise leadership. This is the challenge when you have companies that are sprawling like this, is that it’s very easy for things to fall between the cracks and you see the organizational divides.

Some of it I do also think is the youth of the field, we have this challenge that tone is a design problem, the industry is early, we’re missing the design tools, the Photoshop for personality, et cetera, the tools that make these things more obvious along the way. Mistral, Evals, and OpenAI

NF: Yeah, I think Satya knows he’s got a lot of eggs in one basket and he’s very intelligently trying to make sure that’s not true. We saw him do this before. Why are they so much better than Llama?

NF: Well, it’s interesting. I think they were originally the Llama team, and so I think they have a few things, they have the agility of a startup and I think that counts for a lot. And maybe they can’t hire as many fine-tuners.

NF: Right? Well, definitely not. But right now they’re punching way above their weight and it feels almost like a magic trick. Their new Mistral large model is performing really well on evals and they haven’t quite said what it is, maybe it’s an MOE mixture of Mistral mediums or something like that. But my gosh, it’s really impressive and so yeah, I think there’s just a lot of agility there and a really hardcore team with great taste and great judgment that so far is making really great decisions.

How do evals work, by the way? Everyone puts up these test numbers. What is a good model? What is not?

NF: Well, this is a funny topic, dear to my heart. It’s really funny to see, you have all these CEOs of all these companies that are trumpeting their MMLU number and MMLU is an eval that Dan Hendrycks developed himself when he was an undergrad. So you have basically CEOs of trillion-dollar companies talking about their score on an undergrad’s test, basically and this is the premier reasoning eval that’s out there.

I think if you just zoom way out and look at the progress in the AI field generally, we see incredible progress on models, incredible progress on alignment tools like RLHF. Products finally feel like they’re really going, we’re seeing a lot of products, even policy people are really spun up. But the one progress bar that seems the furthest behind is evals, and evals are basically the tests that you run on models to see what they can and can’t do and to see what their behavior is, so you have some idea of that before you ship them, and it’s this classic low prestige type of activity. It’s like benchmarks, right? But it has a huge impact in the industry. When new benchmarks come out, people want to perform against those.

I think we have a bunch of challenges right now. First is that there are very few good public evals and even the ones there are, like MMLU, they either aren’t really able to predict certain types of capabilities or they’re getting close to saturated. I mean, on MMLU, you have people scoring in the 80s now and with multi-shot maybe in the low 90s in some cases and so you have this saturated benchmark and in fact, all the benchmarks tend to saturate within a couple of years of being released. This is just really a missing thing.

I remember Andrej Karpathy told us once that the only eval he trusts is Twitter eval. After a model’s been shipped, you check the sentiment on Twitter a couple weeks in and see if people like it or not. But you have still the CEO of every company training these big models saying, “We’ve got to be at the top of the leaderboard.” By the way, what’s the leaderboard? The leaderboard is something a couple undergrads threw together, it’s Chatbot Arena that the folks at Berkeley assembled.

It doesn’t have the bits that Daniel has argued consistently are important, like the taste and tone and the way it feels.

DG: And by the way, it’s not an easy problem to evaluate this with humans. Why should we think it’s easy to do that with models? We’re now at the point where we’re not talking about whether GPT-2 can write more than three paragraphs or not, the models have gotten good enough that the evals are a deep problem. Pearson is a huge company and part of what they do is create systems and methodologies that evaluate humans and yeah, this doesn’t exist for models and presumably it should.

Is there a bit where for when it comes to OpenAI, are we underrating Sam Altman? NF: Greg Brockman would be my vote. I think Greg actually is a major driver there and has fantastic taste.

Famously of Stripe before, which is a company well-known in that regard.

NF: Yeah, he was at Stripe, he did a lot of amazing things at Stripe. Look, I think he’s deep in the weeds also of their CUDA kernels and training code, but I think he’s very forceful and has really great product sense also and I’m sure there’s a lot of other people involved in that too. I think Sam also knows what that looks like when it’s going well, obviously, he’s been around so many startups, but if you were asking who’s underrated in this regard, I’d probably pick Greg.

Well, it’s been a momentous few months. What is the big thing we should look for next? Is GPT-5 around the corner or do we not know? Is there going to be something that just pops up, like Groq did last week? Which again, a classic example of a company that’s been out for years and years but then just suddenly breaks through. I’m giving away the question, which is, “Who knows?”, but what do you guys see coming next?

DG: Well, I’m curious now to hear what Nat will say, but maybe I’ll just throw out an idea. I think we’re in an odd tweener era, I think, where the models that we have today for them to be used, I think in truly economically valuable ways, they do need a product built around them and they need someone like with the time and attention that Nat and his team had at GitHub, to take the model and make it faster and smaller and really good for Copilot and that sort of thing. That’s just the paradigm that we’re in now and I sort of think that’s still true.

At the other end of the Rubicon is a world where these AI models that we have, act like agents and don’t really need an interface the same way that humans don’t. We interact with our peers over Slack and Gmail and that’s totally fine and if I had to guess at some point this year we make another step in that direction and we have more coworkers than copilots. That’s Nat’s phrase that I’m stealing here, that I think is quite prescient and that’ll be the big thing, I don’t know where it’ll come from. Maybe it’ll come from OpenAI, maybe it’ll come from Magic, maybe it’ll come from DeepMind, but I think that’s the big thing the industry is working towards. Would you agree, Nat?

NF: Yeah, I think that’s right, I totally agree. I think the thing that I’m interested in seeing is reasoning, basically, some better definition of reasoning, some way to measure it and some market improvements towards it, whether it’s the active reasoning Daniel was talking about or somehow a way of generating training data for the models that allow them to learn patterns of reasoning more directly, or a way of measuring reasoning ability in these models.

We try to tease it out now and see how well they’re reasoning, but some direct progress along this I think would be a very big deal and there’s a belief in the field that this may come from figuring out how to do reinforcement learning on text.
