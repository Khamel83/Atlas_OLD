ESPN Financials

From Bloomberg :

Disney provided the results for its flagship ESPN network and related channels on a standalone basis in a filing on Wednesday. Earlier this year, Chief Executive Officer Bob Iger reorganized Disney into three divisions: entertainment, sports and parks. In July, he said the Burbank, California-based entertainment giant is seeking an investor to help accelerate ESPN’s transition to streaming from traditional TV. Disney shares were little changed at $84.36 on Thursday after analysts wrote that ESPN’s margins were lower than expected. Profit at Disney’s sports TV networks fell 20% to $1.48 billion through the first nine months of fiscal 2023, according to the filing. Sales declined 1.3% to $13.2 billion.

My takeaway from these financials was more positive than this excerpt indicates: Disney’s new “Sports” category includes Star India, which is losing tons of money after losing cricket rights; I think it is much more interesting to look at ESPN’s domestic numbers in isolation:

A few points to note about this breakout:

First, the numbers do include ESPN+, which helps the profitability numbers as the streaming service has decreased its losses significantly. However, I left the numbers in for two reasons: first, there was no breakout of domestic versus international, and second, ESPN+ is obviously a core feature of ESPN going forward so I think it is appropriate to include when it comes to analyzing and forecasting the business.

Second, Disney did break out affiliate, advertising, subscription, and “other” revenue, but did not separate those revenue streams between domestic and international (and Star); here are the numbers for reference:

A few notes on advertising in particular:

  • First, it’s notable how much bigger Q2 and Q4; I suspect Q2 is attributable to the NBA Finals, and Q4 to the NFL
  • Second, year-over-year comparisons are not always apples-to-apples: the Q4 and Q1 numbers in particular can vary depending on how many NFL games are in December versus January, and on which date the college football playoffs fall. Note that this also affects the costs above, since the costs for a sporting event are accrued on the day they air.

That noted, I think that ESPN’s domestic revenue growth is, all things considered, not too bad. Yes, a big part of this continued growth is affiliate fee increases, but at this point folks still paying for cable are increasingly primarily sports fans, and part of the deal with Charter includes locking in more increases along with guaranteed coverage for ESPN. Moreover, while advertising is down for the entire Sports segment, the continued revenue growth domestically suggests that the situation is better in the United States.

Note, though, that the optimism is not about outsized growth: it’s about treading water.

NBA Spin

There was a very curiously headlined piece in the Wall Street Journal last week: How the NBA Plans to Remake TV Deals and Net Billions of Dollars for Its Stars . Sounds like good news for the NBA, right? Not so much, methinks.

The stars of the National Basketball Association are taking to the court as a new season begins next week. Behind the scenes, the league has started talks to secure the billions of dollars in media-rights fees that will help pay their huge salaries.

As the NBA enters its first media negotiations in a decade, its biggest partners, Disney’s ESPN and Warner Bros. Discovery’s TNT — which together pay about $2.6 billion a year — aren’t looking to pony up big spending increases. Each company is under investor pressure to trim costs, with cable TV’s decline and hefty mergers weighing on their balance sheets. Meanwhile, the largest sports broadcaster in local markets is in bankruptcy and on the brink of liquidation.

The solution: The league is looking to bring additional parties to the negotiating table as it plots out its new deals, which would go into effect after the 2024-2025 season. For consumers, those deals could change significantly where games are broadcast. ESPN and TNT, which carry roughly 165 nationally televised games combined, are exploring signing up for smaller packages, said people familiar with the situation. That arrangement would help them hang onto a premier asset in American media without breaking the bank. Those companies already are in renewal talks with the NBA, with an exclusive negotiation period set to expire in April.

This is bad news! The last time the NBA’s media rights were up for negotiation they never reached the open market because ESPN and TNT re-upped during their exclusive negotiating period; the primary news in this article is that neither network is willing to match the NBA’s desired price. And what is that price?

In the American sports landscape, the National Football League towers above all else. But the NBA is number two, given its appeal with younger audiences, and is in a strong position to demand a tripling of fees, as it did last time around, said David Levy, who negotiated TNT’s last deal with the NBA in 2014. “Pop culture, fashion, relevancy—why wouldn’t the number 2 get a 3x increase?” Levy said.

The NBA hasn’t said publicly what fee increase it is seeking. Tripling the last agreement would mean a deal worth about $78 billion over a decade. Media rights make up a big chunk of league revenues, along with ticket and merchandise sales.’

The NBA may not have said it wants to triple its right fee publicly, but that is the number that has been leaked for years now, and the expectation within the league is for a substantial increase. The problem, though, was laid out in the section above: ESPN, the preeminent sports network, is doing well to eke out a 2% revenue increase, and a 2% increase is a lot less than a 300% increase! In fact, an increase of this size would have wiped out all but around $250 million of ESPN’s profit last year. It’s not going to happen.

The spin in the article, of course, is that tech is going to come to the rescue; Apple and Amazon are the two names mentioned, and in the past week there have been another raft of leaks pointing to Amazon specifically as a bidder. From Front Office Sports :

During an industry conference last year, NBA commissioner Adam Silver admitted he was “fascinated” with the job Amazon Prime Video is doing with “Thursday Night Football” — the ratings-challenged NFL package from which longtime broadcast partners like Fox Sports and CBS Sports had walked away. With “TNF” averaging a linear TV-like 12.9 million viewers year to date this season, Amazon wants to create an exclusive night of streaming NBA action, sources told Front Office Sports.

According to sources, after agreeing to pay $1 billion yearly for “TNF” through 2033, the eCommerce giant is eying an NBA game package on Tuesday or Thursday nights. The goal: create the NBA’s version of “TNF,” said sources. According to sources, the NBA is also intrigued by Amazon’s ability to draw an audience seven years younger than the NFL’s legacy TV partners.

Let’s be clear: what the NBA is looking for is money first and foremost. That noted, Amazon does strike me as the most likely partner amongst the tech companies. What makes the service different from other streaming alternatives is that Prime already has around 167 million U.S. subscribers, substantially more than cable. This means that the NBA would serve as churn mitigation for a larger bundle offering already at scale; given that the NBA is substantially less popular than the NFL — regular season games averaged 1.6 million viewers — that seems like a more viable business case than believing the league will drive subscribers for a smaller service like Apple TV+.

Still, it’s hard to see the numbers getting as high as the NBA would like: $1 billion for 12 million weekly NFL viewers is less than ESPN pays for a much smaller audience ($1.4 billion currently), for a much different product that, unlike cable, doesn’t have hours of inventory that need to be filled on weeknights in February.

Jon Stewart vs. Apple

From the New York Times :

Jon Stewart’s show on Apple’s streaming service is abruptly coming to an end, according to several people with knowledge of the decision, the result of creative differences between the tech giant and the former “Daily Show” host…Mr. Stewart and Apple executives had disagreements over some of the topics and guests on “The Problem,” two of the people said. Mr. Stewart told members of his staff on Thursday that potential show topics related to China and artificial intelligence were causing concern among Apple executives, a person with knowledge of the meeting said. As the 2024 presidential campaign begins to heat up, there was potential for further creative disagreements, one of the people said.

We discussed this topic on today’s Dithering (link below!), so I’ll keep this short.

First off, I find it hard to believe that Stewart’s coverage of China wasn’t negotiated before he signed on with Apple in the first place. That doesn’t make it ok that world-spanning tech companies increasingly have editorial control over our media ecosystem — geographically-constrained cable companies don’t seem so bad in this light! — but rather I would be surprised if this was some sort of unexpected issue.

What seems more likely to me is that the show was a flop, and not just because I’ve heard it was much less compelling than Stewart’s old Daily Show. Rather, the entire concept of a talk show seems much more suited to linear TV: news drives daily content, which you turn on because it is there, or place in the background; streaming, on the other hand, is all about intentionality, and it’s a pretty heavy lift to get someone to purposely seek out a news-related show on a relatively new streaming service that isn’t super-fresh and yet also has an expiration date. The entire concept seems flawed.

This seems pertinent to the NBA’s hopes above: the NBA’s greatest value has always been that it fills out the schedule when football isn’t on; in that regard it is similar to something like the Daily Show that both fills time and mitigates some small amount of churn, enabling affiliate fees to go up. On streaming, though, the “filler content” aspect is different: filler content matters, but it needs to be content that is evergreen; filler content on linear TV, on the other hand, needs to fill a specific time slot. Both talk shows and live sports are much better suited to the latter.

That does still leave the churn mitigation aspects I noted above, and the NBA’s schedule offset relative to the NFL could help Amazon lock in sports fans and perhaps lay the foundation to aggregate sports more broadly . The fundamental challenge for the NBA, though, is that its entire structure, particularly its 82-game season, was built for a world of filling seats and filling hours on cable, not driving new subscribers who want access to the big game.
