There’s a chill in the air over Mar-a-Lago as Trump ’s courtiers are reminded that while Trump demands loyalty, he’s hardly loyal himself. A stark reminder of this came last night, when The New York Times reported that Trump was passing over Ric Grenell to likely select Marco Rubio for secretary of state. Anyone who has spent time with Trump over the past eight years knows that Grenell has been at his side, soldiering on next to him even in his darkest hours of post-January 6 exile, when Rubio took to the Senate floor to denounce the chaos that had erupted that day. “Ric busted his ass this campaign,” said a senior Trump advisor. “For years, actually.”

Some Grenell supporters are convinced that Trump only chose Rubio—viewed suspiciously as a Republican establishment-adjacent war hawk by the isolationist MAGA movement—so that Lara Trump could be appointed to his Senate seat in Florida. Meanwhile, I’m told Governor Ron DeSantis was not clued into whatever switcheroo the family brain trust is concocting. On the contrary, I heard he woke up to the Rubio news in Italy, a sign of the real and metaphorical distance between the two camps. It’s certainly a rift that DeSantis could endeavor to mend with a quick appointment of Lara over, say, his own chief-of-staff, James Uthmeier , who could be a placeholder for DeSantis until he needs a new job in 2026.

In any case, the Grenell snub is only the latest in a series of Trump choices that are sure to be controversial with one or another constituency. Some Republican allies have privately expressed concerns after Trump plucked two members of Congress, Elise Stefanik and Mike Waltz , to serve as ambassador to the United Nations and national security advisor, respectively, placing additional pressure on whatever slim majority Speaker Mike Johnson will have to work with come January. Other MAGA members of the House didn’t bother pitching their services, believing that there was no way to get into the administration because of that margin, and are now irked to see their colleagues get the nod. There are also gripes that “establishment” staffers with résumés connected to Elaine Chao and Bill Barr , two ex-secretaries non grata, are involved in the transition, rather than MAGA soldiers from the campaign. It’s all of a piece with the broader resentment bubbling to the surface as the president-elect elevates old political hands with Washington cred over fringier grassroots characters who have been waiting in the wings.

As for Democrats, they’re bracing themselves for a particularly bruising photo-op tomorrow, when Joe Biden does the classy thing and invites Trump to the White House to discuss the transition—an official act that Trump refused to perform in 2020. Of course, the Democrats are already throwing stones at Biden, but this difficult day for him will be the only picture anyone is looking at.

Despite the thunder from MAGA world, Senate Republican insiders I’ve spoken with in recent days tell me that the pressure campaign to install Florida Sen. Rick Scott as majority leader tomorrow—bypassing “the Johns” and nuking Mitch McConnell ’s managed succession—is about as welcome in the upper chamber as local birdwatchers trying to force their marshland agenda on a country club. The expectation heading into tomorrow morning’s election is that Scott will place third in the first round of voting, setting up a one-on-one battle between John Thune of South Dakota and John Cornyn of Texas later in the morning. Here is the readout on election eve:

  • The leaderboard : At first blush, it appears Scott has momentum, per multiple reports. Wisconsin’s Ron Johnson , Tennessee’s Bill Hagerty , Kentucky’s Rand Paul , and fellow Floridian Marco Rubio all support him. For now, only Missouri’s Josh Hawley publicly supports Cornyn. Meanwhile, Thune is backed by Oklahoma’s Markwayne Mullin , Montana’s Steve Daines , North Dakota’s Kevin Cramer and John Hoeven , and fellow South Dakotan Mike Rounds .

    But I’m hearing that many more G.O.P. senators are quietly inclined to align with either Thune or Cornyn. Since McConnell announced earlier this year that he was stepping down from leadership, Thune has been perceived as the frontrunner within the Beltway. He’s a dragon slayer in his own right, coming to Washington in 2005 after ousting another Senate majority leader, Democrat Tom Daschle , from office. And yet, given the lack of public commitments for Thune, it’s quite possible that Cornyn will become the first Senate leader from Texas since L.B.J. —a remarkable interval when you consider the amount of money Texas donors deploy to Republican senators across the map.

  • One extraordinary stat : Incredibly, this is the first time in nearly 30 years that the Senate G.O.P. conference has held a competitive leader race, dating back to Trent Lott ’s successful run in 1996. The only sitting Republican senators who were around for that vote are McConnell and Chuck Grassley . For everyone else, this is new terrain. In the last two Senate leader elections, Mitch McConnell and Bill Frist had their races sewn up before voting even started.

    Leadership races are the most emotional and frequently personal contests on the Hill, as evidenced by the raucous, bitter fight to replace Kevin McCarthy last year. That’s especially true of the McConnell succession battle, wherein the dividing lines reinforce the notion that this is a popularity contest more than an ideological battle. Since this is a secret-ballot election, a senator’s vote is unlikely to ever become public, despite the MAGA pressure campaign for transparency. Indeed, even the members running in these leadership races are often unsure how their colleagues voted. I’ve personally witnessed victorious members complain that a vote count was off, meaning their colleagues hadn’t been honest with them about their support.

A WaPo Diagnosis & Axios’s New Lane
News, notes, and analysis aplenty about some local media institutions, #Resistance media, and Trump’s impact on the industry (edited for length and clarity)…

Dylan Byers : I cover legacy media, which is obviously going through a lot of pangs. Given the fragmented environment, what should the business model and overall strategy be if you’re, say, The Washington Post ?

Jim VandeHei: It pains me to say The Post is a hot mess. It looks like they’re in a big financial hole. That’s probably solvable. But the bigger problem is the constant tension between management and reporters, and I think both sides have to take some damn ownership of it. Will Lewis has obviously done a lot of things that have angered his staff, and that’s completely on him. But I would also say to any reporter, You have to understand how hard this business is right now . We don’t have time to spend days, weeks, and months in revolt, not doing our jobs and not figuring out how we can provide distinct content to distinctive audiences. If you’re doing that, you’re just not going to have a business.

Meanwhile, The New York Times has a national footprint and a strong sense of self, and they’re happy to be the dominant publication of the left, and to figure out ways to monetize that while picking up people who don’t necessarily consider themselves liberal but care about the topics they cover. I don’t know what room that gives The Post . They can say, Well, we’re going to own Washington . Good luck! It’s a very crowded and competitive space. It’s just really hard to find reporters who are routinely telling you things about subjects that people care about, and that are basically essential to some group of people. That’s the challenge.

So what do you do about that?

Dude, Bezos is richer and smarter than I’ll ever be; I assume Will is a very bright guy. If there was an easy solution, they would’ve done it. You have to somehow figure out what part of the news ecosystem The Washington Post can dominate decisively —not just be a participant in it, not just provide decent coverage that wins awards. It has to offer distinctive content that somebody has to have, and therefore they’ll pay for it and engage with it on a regular basis. They have to make it so that anyone who’s in Washington has to read it. And by the way, that doesn’t just apply to The Post … most companies get in trouble if they don’t have a clear sense of self, and if that clear sense of self can’t make money.

How has your own business changed over the last year, and does the incoming Trump administration change the plan in any way?

We realize we’ve got to be able to pick the audiences where we have a right to win, and if we win, we can monetize it. We’ve really organized the company around that. We went through a painful set of layoffs in the summer, and a lot of that was designed to tighten our focus. We have to put all of our investment into being able to own our very specific audiences. And so, from a national perspective, we’re all-in on technology, business, media, politics, and policy. That’s the bread and butter. That’s one chunk of the company; the other chunk is local. We’re in 30 cities, and we’re going to announce soon that we’ll be in more cities. Local is one of our growth businesses. We just came off a pretty good year in which we doubled local-for-local advertising.

What’s your “right to win” in local?

It’s kind of the idea of the smart friend in your pocket—this idea that I have a daily newsletter written by two or three or four people who have expertise in the city that I live in, and they’re helping me navigate it. That means what’s happening in the news and what’s happening in politics, but it also means, what are the cool places to go see? What’s hot in the city that I live in?

That’s a big reason Cox bought us; they love local. We’re going to continue to be very bullish in local over the long term, but we’re going to stay very focused and not try to be everything to everybody. We’re not going to compete in spaces where we don’t think we can win. And that means you have to make really, really hard decisions…

Mike [ Allen ] and I have written columns now eight consecutive days. We’re still working as hard, if not harder, than we did 30 years ago, because it’s a war. And the people who win are the people who grind it out.

Trump 2.0 is well underway, and this time around just feels… different . Since Trump sailed to victory last week, his focus has been on filling out his administrative staff. And unlike after the 2016 election, this time the president-elect seems to be taking a more concise approach to determining who’s going to serve with him—or better yet, for him. This week, Tara Palmeri and I had a compelling chat where we traded notes and addressed hot-button questions about Trump 2.0 expectations and the Democrats’ internal reckoning.

In our conversation, we parsed through the cache of suitors hoping to be appointed to a position in the ensuing administration and those who have already been given a role. Cabinet Politics
Peter : You’ve got some new reporting on the Trump administration and who’s going to be working inside the White House. How is the transition going, and how is this administration going to look different than it did in 2017?

Tara : It’s not the same sort of transition as 2017, when it was really haphazard and you had people trying to stockpile the administration with their allies, like Reince Priebus , Steve Bannon , Jared Kushner —it was really about titles and access to Trump , and creating silos, and making sure that you were protected in your position. It was the typical chaos in which the world revolves around Trump, and everyone else is just trying to be in his inner circle.

He’s planted some people around him that are immovable in some ways, one of them being Elon Musk , because of the hundreds of millions of dollars he spent on Trump’s campaign. Trump actually, genuinely, admires Elon, and not only because he’s the richest man in the world. There’s also Don Jr. , who’s got a pretty firm seat at the table after he helped deliver a victory. And, of course, you’ve got Susie Wiles . This idea that Susie Wiles is going to be a gatekeeper is crazy; that wasn’t her role on the campaign, and it won’t be her role in the White House. But she’s got a lot of influence. She’s been with Trump since 2021, she helped with the comeback, and she’s got allies—one of them is Marco Rubio —who really wanted to be the secretary of state, and is reportedly the pick. It looks like there won’t be a lot of members of the Senate who will be in the cabinet, but Rubio is one of them.

I know Ric Grenell really wanted secretary of state as well. Ric is very close with Melania ; he helped raise money with her through the Log Cabin Republicans. At the same time, I think the Tucker Carlson s and Charlie Kirk s of the world have a very large say in a lot of these decisions; they’re the new voice of the grassroots… Trump likes to see how people can communicate on TV and if they have the right look.

So here we are, back to some of the same things we saw in 2016, but not the same sort of chaos. And Trump’s moving really quickly. It’s been reported that they want to confirm some of these cabinet members during the lame duck so that he can get going with his agenda right away.
Susie Interpretations
Peter : I’ve seen the idea percolating that because Susie Wiles has had this whole life in the Republican Party that predates Trump, and because she was a successful Florida political operative, she’s somebody who can stand up to Trump and be a moderating voice and bring some order to the chaos. Is this just wishful thinking?

Tara : Why in the world would she start being the moderating force now? She knows how to pick her battles. She knows when to bend with Trump, and when not to—she’s savvy. During the campaign, there was a push to replace her when things weren’t going well in August and Harris was surging. Tara : I’m told he’s not going into the administration. He was always a hired gun. He could run a super PAC for Trump. You make more money on the outside, that’s for sure.

Tara : I think Michael Whatley gets to stay R.N.C. chairman because they won. It’s the Democrats we should be talking about.

Like you said, they tried to create divisions, or segment the voter population through identity politics, when everybody was voting about the same thing: higher prices, feeling broke.
