Good morning,

This week’s interview is with Matthew Panzarino , who just stepped down from a 10-year run as the editor-in-chief of TechCrunch , the long-running trade publication for the tech industry. Panzarino took over TechCrunch when I started Stratechery, so I wanted to take this opportunity to look back on how things have changed in that time period. Topics include focusing and growing a media business, the increased antagonism between tech and journalism, and the economic benefits and ethical challenges of events and interviews. We also snuck in a bit of discussion about the Apple Vision Pro.

It’s like, okay, are we now an acquisition target? Are we a Sherlock target?”

Bringing that intel to the meeting from that TechCrunch article that hit thirty minutes before can mean a vital decision being made or not made in that moment, and so we took that responsibility seriously and refined our coverage of earnings reports over time.

So where it was like, “Yeah, leave the numbers to the financial reporters that do a bang-up job of contextualizing that for investors, for public market investors or those other audiences, and instead just focus on the framework of startups”. How does this change any decision a startup might make tomorrow, that is affected by the footprint of a Meta or an Apple or anybody else? And that’s the reason why we continue to cover Apple, because it’s a platforms company. The decisions that Apple farts out can kill entire industries or create them and I think that that is important to acknowledge and to understand. So it’s a balancing act.

That makes sense.

Tech vs. Media

Another question on this ten year theme, I saw something yesterday that I thought was interesting and I’m curious what you think. Marc Andreessen wrote The Techno-Optimist Manifesto , which was unsurprisingly and unabashedly pro-tech and the importance of progress in terms of improving the human condition. We’re not going to legislate the piece here, don’t worry, but I thought it was interesting that one of the first tweets I saw was from Michael Arrington, the founder of TechCrunch, who said , “This is the most optimistic, beautiful thing I’ve read all year.” TechCrunch, meanwhile, wrote an article entitled, “When was the last time Marc Andreessen talked to a poor person?”. The tone of the article was, needless to say, pretty disapproving, not just of Andreessen, but of the overall proposition that tech is default good.

Now, obviously Arrington is long gone, I think he is still pretty enmeshed in crypto, I think he’s in Singapore or something like that, and you’re not responsible for the piece that came out, but hopefully, I actually think those two things are good things because I am curious about this broader shift in tech coverage. One that happened not because of you, but while you were in charge, which was that the media went from tech is default good to, if not default cynical, almost default bad. And I’m curious, number one, your view of that shift, and number two, your view of that shift from the perspective of a trade publication for that industry.

MP: Yeah, that’s a super interesting question that we debated every day. I drove it home as many times as I could, as often as I could, that we had to come from a place of skepticism, not cynicism. I think that skepticism is an important architectural tool to use to hone ideas and to figure out whether ideas actually have validity because of course, the person that started the company, the person that invested in the company, has already reached that position where they’ve decided to be all-in. Once you tip yourself over the scale into an all-in position, it is very difficult to hone against ideas. The best startup founders have this ability to be all-in, but at the same time allow their ideas to be honed against the edges of the real world, against regulatory capture or against communities of people that are using the product in a way that they didn’t anticipate or whatever and those decisions have to be made continuously and in real time.

I think that there is a real deep cynicism about tech in general because of the broadness of it. I think it is basically a byproduct of its size and scope and impact and not really an innate tech thing. I will just start off, I do not fundamentally believe tech is bad. I just don’t. I’m an optimist about it and I tried to instill that in my people, but I don’t believe actually anybody has helped by being a blind optimist. I think blind optimism is really helpful to startups in the earliest days, but I think once they’ve crossed the Rubicon into scale or attachment to a community or to users, you have to consider how the real world affects them and vice versa, how the forces act on one another. That’s honestly just good business sense, you have to understand how they’re impacting the world, how the world is impacting them.

I can understand the pan-optimist approach and I get it and I think in some ways it’s just an antibody reaction to the fact that the major newsrooms of the world, and this is most of the broad interest publications, have internal remits around traffic that is related to the way these things are going awry. Sorry, they just do. I don’t have to have any inside information, even though I do, into the workings of these newsrooms to understand that a vast majority of them are looking for ways that tech is going off the rails because they view it as their holy war to understand the way that these things affect the world.

I’m not passing any judgment about whether that’s bad or good because oppositional forces, I feel, are important. A crucible is important to hone ideas, to make companies better, to prove out the worth of a product, I think most founders of any quality realize that. They get into the world and out into the world and they see the impact and they feel it, and they find ways to move forward through that. But yeah, it’s an interesting world and I think that cynicism is so weird.

Do you feel that TechCrunch was a part of that holy war? Where’s your placement in that?

MP: Certainly not from an editorial perspective. I never, ever once told my people, “Hey, we’re going to go to war on this because it’s getting too powerful or it’s getting too big, or there’s a lack of consideration here.” I always talk to my people about drilling down to the white paper, understanding the technology first and then never writing about something in a way that ignored the fundamental workings of the technology. I just view that as rigor.

When you’re covering an industry from a really, really close distance like we did, you have to have rigor around understanding the actual functionality of the technology in order to properly critique it. You undermine your entire position if you launch into a critique of something like a particular technology or an application and you don’t understand the way that that actually works, and instead you’re working off of a vibe or off of an opinion or a dozen tweets about why people don’t like it, that’s lazy, right? It’s not right.

The more important thing is it actually is a disservice because you’re taking an opportunity to actually launch a real critique into the sphere to be instead talked about whether or not it’s accurate, whether or not it’s good critique, or maybe misses the mark or whatever, you never have a chance to even have a dialogue if you don’t understand the way these things work and talk about them in a fundamentally understanding way because the people that build this stuff care deeply about it, intensely about it, and do know how it works.

Right.

MP: So if you’re able to underpin your critique or your dialogue about it with a real understanding of the technology, then you do have a place to start a conversation and that’s the way I always try to have people talk about it.

Two qualities that are interesting about you that I can relate to, and in contrast, there’s a lot of journalists that were very smart, went to very good schools, and then they’re covering tech and they see this explosion. It used to be sort of, “We’re all in this together” in the 2000s and then tech gets fabulously huge, fabulously wealthy, and all these sorts of things happen. But number one, you did not come up through that path. You were a photographer, wedding photographer, and retail worker that was grinding a website at night, and number two, you didn’t live in San Francisco. You were close, but you still were in Fresno. I’m curious, do you feel those two factors gave you a bit of a fundamentally different view of things than maybe other folks in your industry?

MP: Yeah, for sure, 100%. It gave me distance from a lot of the power, the architecture, like physical distance as well as metaphorical distance. So it didn’t really affect me one way or another. I really did try to — and my God, I’m so sorry I’m going to utter these words on this podcast — but I did try to always have a first principles approach —

(laughing) I love it!

MP: — to the way we were covering these things because I didn’t fundamentally have, I wasn’t coming in with like, “Oh, I was in a Stanford dorm with so-and-so and now they’re invested in this thing and now it’s whatever.” I didn’t have any attachment to it at all, zero. I basically was like, “I’m a fan of the way that technology is helping humans advance, so let’s examine it closer.” That was it, that’s my big thing.

The other thing that I think that we share in common, and I acknowledge this but I do think it’s a important view, is technology made our careers.

MP: Right.

So I will acknowledge that maybe gives me a positive bias, but it’s also the case that absent the web, absent the ability to just go get a domain and set up your own website and start writing over the weekend and the fact that you had the opportunity to grind was actually a good thing, I think probably does change your perspective. It certainly does mine.

MP: Oh, absolutely. There’s absolutely zero way I can come into this without saying that. By the way, I never really even uttered the words unbiased in any of my editorial discussions with my writers because I think that’s fundamentally BS, everybody’s got a bias, everybody has a path that they traveled through life that gives them a perspective. And frankly, the most important role I think of an EIC is that you feather in various biases onto your team so that holistically, while some person may write an article that’s like, “Damn, this sucks”, the next person, and I’ve literally had it happen on the pages of TechCrunch many times and I encourage it, the next person could publish right after them and say, “You know what? Actually this is kind of cool”, and that’s okay.

That schizophrenia, I think, is very antithetical to the way that large newsrooms work, but I think it came from my view that we are a group of individuals, if we’re honest with our readers about our point of view and they understand where we’re coming from, it’s not really about trying to be unbiased or bring this third person distance perspective to things. TechCrunch was never that, maybe will never be that. It’s not my job to determine what it will be in the future, but it definitely has never really been that and I did not encourage that. You sort of come at things honestly so some person may just write something on a day that as long as the fundamentals are there, express yourself, get into it, because the fact is, nobody is going to have any compunctions about doing that back to us.

Right, exactly.

MP: And they never did over the years, you know?

No.

MP: Nobody’s going to come back and be like, “Well, as a journalistic reader, I would say maybe not.” It’s like, no. They’re going to be like, “F you! This sucks!”, and I think it’s important that that way you’re going into it honestly. Don’t ever write anything you don’t believe. Even if you are wrong — not wrong, not inaccurate — but even if your opinion long-term turns out to be like, “You know what? Okay, I thought this was never going to work and it does work”, that’s a matter of opinion. You’ve got to come into it honestly. Never ever say anything you don’t believe, don’t write anything with intellectual dishonesty, don’t do that because you undermine any ability you might have in the future to make things better.

Startup Journalism

Yeah, but it’s the same problem that really accentuates the importance of your fundamental viewpoint and I think TechCrunch is about startups, you made the whole business proposition earlier that what kept it afloat through all the upheaval — I was going to ask you about all the ownership change and stuff like that, but you talked about that with Nilay , we’ll link to that interview over there — But to me that really resonates, you had focus. But how can you write about startups well if you’re not actually fundamentally optimistic? Because by definition they’re all going to fail. It’s like shooting fish in a barrel to say, “Yeah, this probably isn’t going to work.” You’re going to have a great batting average, but it’s very hard to actually be a useful publication in that regard so there’s kind of a weird paradox there that seems to be super important.

MP: Yeah, it is. I mean, statistically speaking, if you write something about every startup that gets launched every year and every time you write about them, you say, “This sucks. It’s probably not going to work,” you’re going to have a much better hitting average than the person who says, “This is cool. It might work,” right? You’re definitely going to be right more statistically because most startups do fail and most of them are doomed to not succeed for one reason or another.

But the act of trying and the times in which the technology itself is real are so vital to seek out and to understand and honestly, it’s like, “Do they honestly believe in this honestly?” and that’s just a value judgment thing. You got to talk to them, tease it out of them, ask them questions, how they answer those things, how they talk to you about it, their passion, the way that they talk to you as a founder. Then underlying that, this is the drilling to the white paper part, does this technology actually seem viable? Does it seem cool, interesting, but also real?

Right. “What are the conditions under which this could work?” That’s a very valuable thing to write.

MP: Correct, and that’s the way if you look back at the things that I personally wrote, because everybody’s got a personal style, and so I never tried to impose my personal style in all of our writers. But if you look back at the things that I personally wrote, I never shied away from like, “Hey, here are the things that could torpedo this.” But it was very much like, “Why would I write about something — aside from something that I believe is doing fundamental harm or is really messed up — why would I write about something if I wasn’t genuinely interested in it and didn’t genuinely think it was cool and it could have an opportunity to affect the world positively or grow or take root and was just clever?”

So that was my philosophy, and there’s so many things. I think one thing people don’t understand when they get mad at TechCrunch about writing about one thing or another thing is they just have no idea how much inbound that TechCrunch writers get and the inbound I got, I think something like 3,000 to 4,000 emails a day, maybe more.

Oh, geez. I thought I had it bad.

MP: That was me as the EIC, who’s not individually writing about things and the writers sometimes would get even more, especially on a per-industry basis, and so I just think that with all that inbound, you have to have this weird thing where you want that laminar flow of information coming in so that you understand the cadences of information, what the general framework of what’s happening now is, but you also need to fundamentally be proactive and say, “Okay, I’m going to interrupt that stream and pick out something that maybe people aren’t talking about or maybe that relates to this thing that everybody’s talking about, but that I view as fundamentally interesting.”

That’s so hard to do because you only have so many person hours in a day. When I joined people were writing three or four articles a day, now it’s less as there are more team members, but at TNW, I remember days when I was writing five, six, eight, ten, fifteen articles — unsustainable, for sure, and it was a product of its time, but you cannot fundamentally write about that many things in your day, week, year, career.

And actually understand the white paper and understand how it all works and all that.

MP: Correct.

Yeah, it’s a tough balance.

TechCrunch Disrupt

One other question about this, TechCrunch Disrupt is sort of a big thing, and I’m curious of two angles on it. Number one, how important is that to the overall business from a business perspective? Like events versus ads, you have TechCrunch+, so the business angle. Then also two, what you’re kind of driving at, the role of TechCrunch not just in writing about startups, but actually being a promotional vehicle for startups. To be on the Disrupt stage, which I think is probably a bigger deal than you read about it TechCrunch, but both sort of matter, I’m curious about both angles there.

MP: So on the business side, it’s pretty simple. The two pillars and then the additional third pillar, which started in the past few years, the advertising revenue, the events business, and then the subscription business, they’re all legs of the stool and they all load balance. Some years the events were absolutely majority of revenue and then other years, advertising is a majority.

All you have to do is look at the last five years and frankly, if we didn’t have one of those legs, we would’ve been screwed. 2020 came along and all of a sudden you have to pivot. From March of 2020, we held our last physical event in 2020, but we had eight more planned in that year and the last one, it was a robotics event at Berkeley, and all the news stories were coming hot and heavy. All of us, we’re looking at each other with like, “What is happening?”, we didn’t know whether we were going to be shut down that day or not because the Berkeley campus was on the verge of pulling the plug on itself. So we skated that in and then had to really retcon our entire rest of the year into a virtual event space. So of course, virtual events are, it turns out, less expensive to put on, but also far less revenue. It turns out they’re slightly more profitable on a percentage basis.

Oh, interesting. But the absolute number is not that high.

MP: Correct, and then of course, the advertising business went to nothing in the first half of 2020 and then went bonkers the second half of 2020 because as everybody figured out, “Oh crap, we’re going to be locked up, we got to spend somehow, so I guess we’ll just start spending again.” So those shock absorbers helped us and it torpedoed a lot of sites. A lot of the media shutdowns over the past year are all just byproducts of people running out of gas from the last several years of disruption and the load balancing was definitely a massive effect there.

The events businesses have been very important to TC and I think are remarkably profitable considering how much of an endeavor they are. They’re not as profitable as you think because they’re super expensive to put on. Physical events are tough and they’re logistically rough but I think most people would be shocked to understand how small of a team actually did that here because the battlefield is essentially two people, myself and one other person, Neesha Tambe , at TC or at least was, I keep saying is, it’s my fault. Then the events team was a team of three people who had a team of a handful of other people that were partially contractors, partially full-time. These are small teams that put on enormous events. Our event, Disrupt, this year, was 13,000 people, which is crazy and they did a bang-up job of it, but super important. Does that answer your first question?

It does. But then how do you think about balancing that in terms of your journalistic mission as it were?

MP: Yeah, so the way it worked when I started and the way it worked when I left were different. The way it worked when I started, the events programming team lived on the business side and it wasn’t like there was any weird power play thing, I think it was just like a natural evolution of whatever had happened before. But frankly, it’s sort of ancient history at this point and it doesn’t need to be litigated, I don’t even know how it came to be that, and I think at most organizations that is pretty standard and I haven’t done this.

I know Code is an example or Recode, Code Con, or All Things D, that was certainly more like what we did, right? Because Kara [Swisher] and Walt [Mossberg] programmed that show, they were like, “We are going to get these people, we’re going to put them on stage, and we’re going to choose who moderates them,” most of them say yes, but also some of our writers, et cetera. I think that model was good, I think it’s why that conference was so vital for so long and still, obviously, I’m not passing any judgment on it, obviously, the one that just happened was certainly news-making , but that model works because you have the people that intimately understand these universes not just picking the people that are important, but also interviewing them. You don’t have a professional moderator from outside who was going to glad-hand these people or not be able to actively listen in the moment and talk to them and draw an interesting conversation out of them because they fundamentally don’t understand that industry on a granular level.

But at the same time, you want them to come to your conference because you make a lot of money on your conference and you also have to cover them day-to-day.

MP: Totally, it is an enormous tension, and the amount of behind-the-scenes angst over the years has been great, as you can imagine. However, fundamentally, we were lucky in that we were able to pretty straightforwardly say, “We’re not going to give you any of your questions in advance, this is going to be a real conversation. We’re not going to pay you. If you don’t want to come, don’t come because there’s three people lined up behind you.”

This is a real benefit of being where you are in the startup ecosystem and having 4,000 inbound emails.

MP: Exactly.

That kind of solves your problem for you. It’s a little different if you want Mark Zuckerberg on stage, but that wasn’t your focus.

MP: Yeah and those kinds of negotiations over big people, those are always fraught and anybody will attest to this, but those fundamental things don’t change for anybody because the fact of the matter is, “Okay, cool, this year we get a big win by being able to put so-and-so on the masthead or so-and-so on the roster”, but then that erosion trickles down.

I thought so much about this for ages before I even started doing the interviews because my whole thing was being the outsider and I don’t get access to anyone and I’m proud of it. Then at some point, I’d be like, “Okay, the reality is I could get access to anyone, but there’s an awareness, and I was super trepidatious the first time I interviewed Zuckerberg , in particular, just because I think you and I realize this, there’s a tiered system, and Mark Zuckerberg and Elon Musk are at the top and there’s a massive gulf to interviewing basically anyone else, and I had to acknowledge I am sacrificing some of my reputational integrity. I know it doesn’t impact me and that this interview is happening regardless of what I say or do, but I know the way it looks and I have to trust I’ve built up enough reputation over time that I can dip into it in this case and I think it’ll be worthwhile. I think my interviews with him have been very good, but it is a very interesting thing to think through having been on both sides, on both bringing someone on my side and also observing other publications and side.

MP: Yeah, I think that’s the exact right way to think about it. You build, over time, a bank of trust or a bank of reputation points with your readers and with your audience and every once in a while, you cash in on that bank a little bit with a trust me, like, “Trust me, I got access to Zuck, but I’m going to ask him interesting questions for you”, because you are their avatar. Why else are you doing it? You are the audience’s avatar in that moment and you’re trying to have an interesting discussion and trade on those trust points so that they know that you are there for them.

It’s tough, man, but that’s why we dragged the programming back over the editorial line pretty early on, we did it early, but that programming, coming from the editorial side, meant that we were able to tap all of our writers who really genuinely knew who was germane in that moment, who was really interesting in that moment, in the moment we were in, and who was a good get? We programmed 200 people at Disrupt, this recent one, so this is not three people on a main stage like, “Hey, cool, let’s have a chat. Hey, Shaq was fun and it was an easy conversation because he’s just really clever.”

And one of the greatest startup investors of all time!

MP: Really, truly! Yeah, absolutely. But downstream from there, you have hundreds of other people and once you get to the granular level of like, “Okay, who are the best people in growth right now that are actually hot and that if you ask other people in growth, they’d be like, ‘They’re full of shit. They’re just some grifter.'” You don’t want that because that’s a reputational hit, so you have to sweat it all the way down the stack.

We had packed stages for these conversations about really granular stuff in the startup world like growth or marketing or whatever, even trust and safety, security, all these kinds of things and that was a testament to all the writers and the work that they did in saying, “No, this is the right person, I feel strongly about this.” All the way up to our programmers and all of that and that’s all team TC. That’s no outside person coming in saying, “Oh, here are the hot people you need to have right now and we have a Rolodex and we’ll get you them and blah, blah, blah.” That’s all in-house, which is tough. As you said, it’s a balance, right?
