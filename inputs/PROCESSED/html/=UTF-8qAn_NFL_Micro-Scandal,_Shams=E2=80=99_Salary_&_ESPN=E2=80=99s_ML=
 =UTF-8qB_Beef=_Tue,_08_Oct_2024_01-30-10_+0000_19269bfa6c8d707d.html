Welcome to The Varsity, my twice-weekly private email on the money, power, and personalities that run the sports business. The Brady Meter: Week 5
Cards 24-49ers 23
Grade: C
With less than three minutes left in the game, the Cardinals were down by two and faced a 4th and 5 from their own 40-yard line. Facing a heavy rush, QB Kyler Murray lofted a pass caught by rookie WR Marvin Harrison Jr. for a 12-yard first down. Tom Brady , the $37.5 million-per-year Fox Sports analyst, screamed— and then followed up with an incredulous laugh. Then Brady, who logged an NFL record 7,753 completions during his career, exclaimed, “Wow!”

Born and raised in nearby San Mateo, Brady had returned as a conquering hero to the Bay Area and, after a series of duds, was finally blessed with a close game. Unfortunately, the GOAT articulated the moment like a high-strung sideline parent. We’re five weeks into Brady’s broadcast career, and the hyper-competitive perfectionist will improve, but this week he continued his jejune performance.

In particular, Brady’s analysis thus far feels overly positive, almost pollyannaish. He’s not telling viewers what he really thinks, and he’s not accessing his decades of experience to bring viewers closer to the line of scrimmage. Alas, none of his analysis from Sunday stood out. The only moments that really went viral were his screams and grunts while Kevin Burkhardt called the plays. Once or twice, those screams are endearing. But nearly a dozen times ?
The Starting Five
  1. The college conundrum : The list of executives associated with the just-announced College Football Super League is undoubtedly impressive. There’s Browns owner Jimmy Haslam and TurnkeyZRG chairman Len Perna , one of the most powerful headhunters in the business. And then there’s Grant Hill and athletic directors from Tennessee ( Danny White ), North Carolina ( Bubba Cunningham ), and Texas Tech ( Kirby Hocutt ).

    Missing from last week’s list, however, was NFL chief media and business officer Brian Rolapp , who reports directly to Roger Goodell . Back in the spring, Rolapp worked closely with the group, though he always stressed that he was not representing the NFL in his dealings. Rather, he told people that he had a personal interest in helping college football evolve. (One of Rolapp’s sons played football for Michigan and another is already being highly recruited.) I’m told that Rolapp voluntarily pulled away from the organization as it started to gain traction. He was personally involved in fixing what he views as a broken system, but he recognized the inherent conflict between his role as the NFL’s No. 2 executive and a group that is trying to remake college football.

    Still, Rolapp’s involvement irked many influential executives in the business—in fact, several sources told me that S.E.C. commissioner Greg Sankey and his counterpart at the Big Ten, Tony Petitti , complained during meetings with Goodell. TV network executives similarly raised the issue during numerous meetings with the commish. After all, all of the NFL’s TV partners—CBS, ESPN, Fox, NBC—have preexisting college deals and aren’t keen to see the college model overturned, at least not without their participation.

  2. Shams to ESPN : When Adrian Wojnarowski left ESPN last month, top network executive Burke Magnus considered replacing the NBA insider with a committee of reporters already on staff—people like Brian Windhorst and Ramona Shelburne . This would have made the most financial sense, as it would have enabled ESPN to take 100 percent of Woj’s $7 million-a-year salary off the books. Magnus even had preliminary talks with ESPN’s MLB insider Jeff Passan about moving over to the NBA. (Passan eventually decided that he’d rather remain on the MLB beat.) “We explored a number of options, both conventional and unconventional, for our NBA insider coverage,” the network noted in a statement to me. “Credit Jeff, who is at the top of his profession, for being open to even consider the possibility.”

    Magnus also opened talks with Woj’s biggest rival, The Athletic’s Shams Charania . This afternoon, the 30-year-old Charania announced on Twitter that he will replace Woj. The decision came down to the fact that Woj and Shams competed for years in a two-horse race to break NBA transactions and micro -transactions. By hiring Shams, ESPN gained control of its biggest competitor on the beat before it commences its new 11-year, $2.6 billion deal with the league. Shams’ salary has not leaked, but sources said that it is less than half of Woj’s.

  3. Diamond layoffs : The bankrupt Diamond Sports Group laid off 15 employees today as part of a move that insiders are describing as a restructuring of the group’s sales organization. (Some regional sellers are taking on additional territories. I’m told this is unrelated to cost-cutting, but this is a company emerging from bankruptcy protection…)

    By the middle of next month, the court is expected to bless Diamond’s bid to emerge from bankruptcy, and the company is close to a deal that will see its regional sports networks rebranded under the FanDuel brand. The company is also close to an arrangement to make its channels available through Amazon. That deal is not finalized yet, and sources said that it could involve having Amazon, which previously pulled a $115 million investment, take an equity stake.

  4. MLS sets attendance mark : The MLS regular season doesn’t end for two weeks, but the league announced that it set an attendance record this season, with 10,900,804 fans heading through the turnstiles. That averages out to 23,340 per game. MLS owes much of the attendance bump to Lionel Messi , whose Inter Miami drew 72,610 fans to Arrowhead Stadium for a match against Sporting Kansas City, and 65,612 to Gillette Stadium for another against the New England Revolution, both in April.

    The attendance numbers underscore MLS’s reputation for top-notch in-stadium experiences. It’s been hard for league games to break into the media mainstream, especially since MLS cut an Apple deal a couple of years ago. (Fox carries an MLS game of the week on linear TV, but the network generally doesn’t put much marketing behind it.)

    Meanwhile, MLS has had a hard time breaking through as a media property, especially since U.S. fans can watch better players in other international leagues. But will the Messi experiment convince teams to spend more to bring those top international players to the U.S.?

  5. Sunday Ticket talk : On The Varsity , the podcast , last week, I asked Business Insider’s Peter Kafka to predict how the media rights landscape would look by 2030. I found his answer illuminating. Instead of talking about 2030, he started extemporizing about 2012 , when Google first started kicking the tires on NFL Sunday Ticket.

    Here’s Kafka’s lightly edited response: “Someone had to explain what football was to former C.E.O. Larry Page and former president Sergey Brin . They had to explain just how television rights worked, and what you would actually get. There was no interest in this at first. But 10 years later, they got to it through YouTube TV. It seems inevitable, just given the amount of money available to the streamers, and the lack of money that’s going to be available to terrestrial, that some of these major sports will be essentially fully digital by 2030.

    “But I have found this stuff always takes longer to materialize than you would expect. If you would have asked me 10 years ago, I would have said the exact same thing—that by 2024, one of these giant tech companies would back up the truck. And they would make it so persuasive that the NFL or the NBA would be willing to forgo terrestrial distribution. I know the reasons that it hasn’t happened, but it’s still surprising to me that it hasn’t happened yet—which is why I’m hedging my bets about something in 2030.”
ESPN & MLB Prepare for Couples Counseling
ESPN, understandably miffed by the league’s much cheaper deals with other platforms, wants it both ways—to decrease the cost of its current rights package and also get its mitts on local broadcasts to power its forthcoming streaming service. Can both sides coexist happily?
JOHN OURAND
If marriage represents the triumph of imagination over intelligence, as Oscar Wilde once purportedly noted, and a second marriage demonstrates the triumph of hope over experience, then the corollary in the sports media business is a little more nuanced. In the era of massive platform disruption and cash optimization, a happy marriage could best be explained as not getting screwed by one partner while simultaneously kneecapping another. And this algebra of the heart perhaps best explains the modern kerfuffle that ESPN and Major League Baseball find themselves working through at this transitional moment in the media ecosystem.

ESPN, of course, pays around $550 million for its package of Sunday Night Baseball , wildcard playoff games, the Home Run Derby, and ESPN+ contests. So imagine the scorn that chairman Jimmy Pitaro might have felt, in 2022, when MLB sold a package of Friday night games to Apple for $85 million per year. Or, this spring, when MLB sold a package of Sunday morning games to Roku for $10 million a season. ESPN executives haven’t been shy about their plans to exercise an out in their contract at the end of next season and renegotiate a fairer deal.

Those talks have not started yet, but it appears likely that ESPN and MLB will start negotiating before ESPN can exercise its out. (For what it’s worth, viewership for the wild card round was up 25 percent this year.) And the executives in Bristol aren’t merely interested in national games. In fact, they’re just as focused on picking up local rights to MLB teams, which would provide a powerful lure for the company’s forthcoming streaming service, internally known as “Flagship.” Pitaro has said that he wants ESPN to be part of the local rights solution.“I could easily see a scenario where you have your national games on ESPN or ESPN2 or ESPN+, all of which will be made available as a part of Flagship,” Pitaro said a couple of weeks ago on The Varsity podcast. “I could easily see a scenario where we geotarget and make local in-market content available to the sports fan. But the leagues have to embrace that strategy.”
Let’s Deal
Major League Baseball, with its six-month schedule and constant hot-stove drama, was one of the true ballasts upon which the regional sports business was erected— all those games, all those months, the mythology around each team’s devoted fan base. Alas, in the end, it turns out that the affinity of baseball fandom isn’t quite enough to conquer the existential crisis of the late-stage linear business. And no entity knows this better than Major League Baseball, itself.

After being called in to support small-market teams in R.S.N. no-man’s-land, MLB has steadily come to the conclusion that its best hope is to bundle its clubs’ rights as their current deals expire. Of course, the league will not have a full complement of 29 U.S.-based teams by the end of next season. The Dodgers and the Yankees are unlikely to add their incredibly valuable rights to the mix, at least not right away. Other franchises, like the Braves, have their streaming rights tied up in their current R.S.N. deals. Regardless, pooled bargaining should offer the league an option better than the status quo.

If ESPN wants to bid on this package, as I’ve noted before, it will need to fend off other, larger companies that would also like to use local baseball rights as a subscription tool. MLB has already chatted with companies like Amazon and Google to gauge interest in that local rights package. It’s possible that Fox Sports would be interested in adding the Home Run Derby to its MLB All-Star Game coverage. (The NBA, under its expiring deal, sells its entire All-Star Weekend to Warner Bros. Discovery, which makes it easier for WBD to sell ads and sponsorships around it. ESPN and Fox ad sales executives often find themselves competing around the MLB All-Star Game, with Fox selling the game and ESPN selling the derby.)

So, as was the case with the NBA, Pitaro and his dealmakers will eagerly belly up to the trough. By reopening the negotiation early, ESPN may have an opportunity to demonstrate fealty, solve its own problem while also helping a partner. And, yes, they may have the added delight of fending off two multitrillion-dollar market cap companies, whose growing ambition and infinite balance sheets suggest that each forthcoming negotiation will be harder than the last.

On YouTube’s sports ambitions : “You’re right that Jen Chun ’s hiring for YouTube signals the platform’s further commitment to sports, but it was a miss not to acknowledge the stewardship and growth of YouTube’s sports proposition by global head of sports partnerships Jon Cruz . With more than a decade in sports partnerships at YouTube, Jon has developed YouTube’s (still) largest sports partnership with WWE (100m+ subscribers globally) and was instrumental in striking the platform’s Sunday Ticket deal. He may not be as rah-rah as his contemporaries at the other digital video and social platforms, but ask any league executive about their success on YouTube and their ability to actually monetize that part of the media business, and the name that will come up time and time again is Jon’s. P.S.: Sorry about the Orioles!” — A sports media veteran
