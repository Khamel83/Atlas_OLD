Realtors Lose in Court

From the Wall Street Journal :

A federal jury on Tuesday found the National Association of Realtors and large residential brokerages liable for about $1.8 billion in damages after determining they conspired to keep commissions for home sales artificially high. The verdict could lead to industrywide upheaval by changing decades-old rules that have helped lock in commission rates even as home prices have skyrocketed — which has allowed real-estate agents to collect ever-larger sums. It comes in the first of two antitrust lawsuits arguing that unlawful industry practices have left consumers unable to lower their costs even though internet-era innovations have allowed many buyers to find homes themselves online…

This topic was covered in Tuesday’s episode of Sharp Tech , and I think it’s worth covering here, particularly given I’ve touched on these issues in the past. To briefly summarize how the industry has worked to date:

  • A seller hires a real estate agent to sell their house. The real estate agent will help prep the house, take pictures, and prepare the listing.
  • Said listing is placed on what are known as multiple listing services (MLSs), which were originally conceived of as a way to make real estate agents representing buyers aware of what houses were for sale; over time MLSs have become electronic with APIs, making them accessible to companies like Zillow.
  • As a prerequisite of making said listing, the seller agent has to specify a commission for the buying agent (to be paid by the seller agent); the standard rate is 6% for the entire transaction, with 3% to the selling and buying agents respectively.

This entire structure made a lot more sense before the Internet: as I noted the entire reason MLSs were created was to help match sellers with buyers. This, by extension, meant that the value buying agents could provide to sellers was not just helping with inspections and titles and contracts, but actually finding houses in the first place. That value, however, is a lot less in the context of a platform like Zillow: I was just procrastinating by looking at houses while writing this Update, with nary a real estate agent in site!

And yet the fees persist, thanks to the essential nature of MLSs, and the accompanying contractural requirement to keep the current fee structure in place (the amount of the fee can vary, but not the structure); that requirement is what was found to be illegal.

Zillow and Real Estate Aggregation

In 2016, in an Article about Opendoor , I described Zillow thusly:

Real estate has long been an appealing market for investors, and for good reason: there is a lot of money at stake. The United States alone has $25 trillion worth of housing, and $900 billion of that changes hands every year; that means well over $50 billion in real estate fees and even more in mortgage interest. It is a market tailor-made for those ubiquitous “If we get X% of the market” pitches that have characterized many a startup business plan.

And yet, the most successful real estate startup, Zillow (which acquired its largest competitor Trulia a couple of years ago), is little more than a glorified marketing tool: the company makes most of its revenue by getting real estate agents — the ones collecting 6% of fees, split between the buying and selling agents — to pay to advertise their houses on the site. Certainly a free tool that makes it easier to find houses in a more intuitive way is valuable — Zillow has acquired the sort of userbase that allows it to build an advertising business for a reason — but at the end of the day the company is a tax on a system that hasn’t really changed in decades.

Founder Rich Barton told me in a Stratechery Interview that that analysis helped compel him to return as CEO and emulate Opendoor’s attempt to transform the selling side of the market:

Your strategic tear-down of Zillow [in that Article] as a kind of weak Aggregator, non-disrupter, kind of a leech on an existing business model versus the upstart who was really changing things really got to my core, because it was exactly for the real disruption and consumer empowerment reasons that we got into Zillow in the first place. Of course we needed to make a business out of it, so for a long time we figured how to make a business while we bided our time and waited for the right time to really attack the fundamental problem, and now we’re after it, so that’s great.

It wasn’t so great: Zillow ended up exiting the home-buying business in 2021 after several disastrous quarters; I wrote a post-mortem of both Zillow’s mistakes and my own in a an Update . While I did warn about the risks at launch , I made a critical mistake with regards to Zillow’s position as an Aggregator:

Zillow the Aggregator encountered two problems with Offers: first, because the company felt compelled to push Offers, it was actually leaving most potential sellers with a bad taste in their mouth; this is a big problem given that an Aggregator’s advantage is the fact the end users like it and go there first. Second, the return, when properly priced for risk, of serving the fraction of customers that Zillow extended an offer to, didn’t outweigh whatever risk-free commissions Zillow might be able to earn by directing customers to other buying channels.

Again, this is absolutely something I should have foreseen, because it fits the model perfectly: an Aggregator is a horizontal business that ought to make investments to serve all of its customers; vertically integrating screws up those incentives, and that is a risk in its own right. I can see why I didn’t emphasize this point — the housing market is so large, and Zillow’s share was not only small but also served to preserve the warped U.S. real estate agent-driven model — but new opportunities entail opportunity costs, and I should have emphasized those more.

In short, Zillow really had maximized its opportunity by being a glorified marketing tool. The reality is that MLSs are so important that, as long as the contractural requirements to maintain the current structure existed, than there wasn’t much that Zillow could do about it.

The best analogy to Zillow’s position is Spotify; I wrote at the time of the music company’s IPO in Lessons From Spotify :

Spotify’s margins are completely at the mercy of the record labels, and even after the rate change, the company is not just unprofitable, its losses are growing, at least in absolute euro terms…Moreover, it seems highly unlikely Spotify’s Cost of Revenue will improve much in the short-term: those record deals are locked in until at least next year, and they include “most-favored nation” provisions, which means that Spotify has to get Universal Music Group, Sony Music Entertainment, Warner Music Group, and Merlin (the representative for many independent labels), which own 85% of the music on Spotify as measured by streams, to all agree to reduce rates collectively. Making matters worse, the U.S. Copyright Royalty Board just increased the amount to be paid out to songwriters; Spotify said the change isn’t material, but it certainly isn’t in the right direction either.

Spotify’s challenge is that music labels had the integration that mattered in the music value chain: new music and the back catalog. From 2017’s The Great Unbundling :

The music industry, meanwhile, has, at least relative to newspapers, come out of the shift to the Internet in relatively good shape; while piracy drove the music labels into the arms of Apple, which unbundled the album into the song, streaming has rewarded the integration of back catalogs and new music with bundle economics: more and more users are willing to pay $10/month for access to everything, significantly increasing the average revenue per customer. The result is an industry that looks remarkably similar to the pre-Internet era:

Notice how little power Spotify and Apple Music have; neither has a sufficient user base to attract suppliers (artists) based on pure economics, in part because they don’t have access to back catalogs. Unlike newspapers, music labels built an integration that transcends distribution.

Aggregators dominate markets that are commoditized (Google is the ultimate example here); the best way to resist an Aggregator is to have some sort of critical integration that prevents commoditization. In this case the real estate agent industry had managed to integrate MLS with the 6% commission structure; unlike the music labels, though, which benefited from the fact that new music releases immediately become back catalog content, the real estate agent integration relied on contractural requirements that a federal court just decided amounted to collusion.

From Franchises to Businesses

Again from the Wall Street Journal :

Home buyers and sellers face the prospect of major changes to the amount and the ways they pay their real-estate agents, following Tuesday’s historic verdict against the National Association of Realtors and large residential brokerages. Those changes could range from minor tweaks to the commission system to a more radical restructuring of the residential real-estate industry, such as more people buying homes without using agents or buyers paying their agents by the hour.

It’s still up in the air what will come of this specific decision; if, though, these contractural requirements go away, then I think that Zillow will finally have their opportunity.

Imagine a future where a seller can only ever agree to a selling agent’s commission, and that there is no limitation on placing a listing on MLS: in this world buying agents still have some value, particularly given the fact that a home purchase is the largest purchase in most people’s lives, and there is an opportunity to help actually seeing houses and providing a guiding hand around inspections, mortgages, titles, etc. What I didn’t list is actually finding a home, because the reality is that most home buyers already do that on their own, mostly on Zillow.

What will be different is that it will be the buyer, not the seller, who is deciding how much the buying agent gets paid. The National Association of Realtors argues that this will hurt buyers, because they will have to pay more, but the reality is they were paying anyways — they just had no say in how much, or for what services. Now real estate agents on the buying side will have to price in a way that reflects the reality that most buyers find their own houses, and I suspect that price will be a lot less.

The answer from the more enterprising real estate agents should be to make it up in volume: if you don’t actually do that much for buyers, at least compared to the pre-Internet era, then sure, perhaps you can’t charge as much. However, you can also do a lot more deals, assuming, of course, you can acquire the customers. And how do you acquire customers at scale? Well, it turns out there is already a customer acquisition platform perfectly positioned at the point where customers begin their search: Zillow.

This will, I should note, be a difficult transition for realtors: as long as you closed just a handful of deals a year than you could earn a very reasonable salary; now you will have to work much harder to earn an equivalent amount, and I suspect a lot of agents will end up exiting the industry. What is interesting is that one of the biggest players on the brokerage side is Warren Buffett, and I can imagine he’s not too thrilled about the outcome. He once explained why he was leaving the newspaper industry in the 1992 Berkshire Hathaway letter to shareholders :

The fact is that newspaper, television, and magazine properties have begun to resemble businesses more than franchises in their economic behavior…An economic franchise arises from a product or service that: (1) is needed or desired; (2) is thought by its customers to have no close substitute and; (3) is not subject to price regulation. The existence of all three conditions will be demonstrated by a company’s ability to regularly price its product or service aggressively and thereby to earn high rates of return on capital. Moreover, franchises can tolerate mis-management. Inept managers may diminish a franchise’s profitability, but they cannot inflict mortal damage.

In contrast, “a business” earns exceptional profits only if it is the low-cost operator or if supply of its product or service is tight. Tightness in supply usually does not last long. With superior management, a company may maintain its status as a low-cost operator for a much longer time, but even then unceasingly faces the possibility of competitive attack. And a business, unlike a franchise, can be killed by poor management.

Until recently, media properties possessed the three characteristics of a franchise and consequently could both price aggressively and be managed loosely. Now, however, consumers looking for information and entertainment (their primary interest being the latter) enjoy greatly broadened choices as to where to find them. Unfortunately, demand can’t expand in response to this new supply: 500 million American eyeballs and a 24-hour day are all that’s available. The result is that competition has intensified, markets have fragmented, and the media industry has lost some — though far from all — of its franchise strength.

Before this decision, the real estate business was a franchise; going forward, assuming this decision leads to the sort of changes I expect, it is going to be a business. That’s good for consumers, and it’s good for Zillow, which, I will add, is not a surprise: remember that Aggregators derive their power from marshaling demand because they offer a product that consumers want. It follows that what is good for the Aggregator is good for consumers, and vice-versa; it’s the folks in the middle that get squeezed, whether that be publishers, TV studios, or, in this case, realtors.
