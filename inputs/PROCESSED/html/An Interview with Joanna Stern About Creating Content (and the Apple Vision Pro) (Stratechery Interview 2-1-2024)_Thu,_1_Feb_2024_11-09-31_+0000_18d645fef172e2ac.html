Good morning,

Today’s Stratechery Interview is with Joanna Stern . Stern is the Senior Personal Technology Columnist at the Wall Street Journal , but “columnist” sells her short: Stern not only writes, but also makes what are, in my opinion, the best videos in tech journalism. They are funny yet informative, with a resolutely consumer-centric approach pioneered by Walt Mossberg, Stern’s predecessor.

In this interview we talked about Stern’s background and both how she arrived in this role and found her voice. We also dive into the nuts and bolts of making a video like her review of the Apple Vision Pro (and where writing the accompanying column fits in her workflow). I do suggest watching this video if you haven’t: we reference it throughout the interview, including where we discuss the Vision Pro generally. We also discuss Stern’s investigation into iPhone passcode theft , her foray into documentaries , and what it takes to succeed as a content creator on the Internet.

Web Versus Print

I mean, one thing I was thinking about while reading your review , and this ties back to the Mossberg point, is even the article on the web was clearly crafted for the web. In a way, I think you articulated very well why The Verge was important, it was a publication built for the web, and in a way that the first generation always sort of apes print, but it can be something different. There’s all these visuals and cool web effects as you’re scrolling and yet, your review, I presume, is going to be in tomorrow’s Wall Street Journal that is going to be in print, on paper. Do you ever think about paper at all anymore or is that something that, “Oh, yeah, I guess it’s going to end up there at some point”?

JS: Actually, it’s really funny that you asked me that today because, look, the time and effort that goes into making these reviews, I don’t know how much people know behind the scenes, but—

Oh, we’re going to ask. Don’t worry.

JS: So much. So, so much. And at some point today, what we call a read back, a read back is just like the print version of the story. The print read back came and they murdered it because they’ve got to cut it, they got to fit it into a space. I just said to the editor, “I trust you. Fine, look at it, whatever.” I said, “Make sure the QR code is there for my video review.” And he’s like, “It’ll be there.”

That said, tomorrow morning I will wake up, and this is a really interesting thing, I will wake up to a series of emails from people who read the column in print and those questions and those responses will be very thoughtful. They will be very clearly from a different type of reader, I’m not going to say all older, but many of them older. But they will have read that review so carefully and come back to me with some really insightful questions.

I’m not saying my digital audience doesn’t do that. They do, and gosh, so many people are saying such nice things today on YouTube, which I never really read the comments, but today I decided to, and thankfully I’m not crying, but it’s just really interesting when that print piece comes out because there is a reaction still from some readers that just might not have been exposed to this type of writing if they were online. Maybe they would’ve seen it on the website, the review led the website for a large part of the day, it was in the app, it was all over the place. But in print, it’s there, it’s in front of you. You might just look at it, you might look at the picture. You might then say, “Okay, I’m going to read that piece.”

It’s really interesting, because one of the benefits of writing on the web is everyone talks about, “Oh, you get feedback from your readers. Isn’t it great?” One of dangers of getting feedback on the web is there’s a certain kind of person that gives feedback on the web, and they’re not necessarily representative of your user base, and this is one of the reasons I’m such a big fan of a subscription model is you have a feedback mechanism that’s completely independent of someone being worked up enough or taking the initiative to sort of write, email you, or tweet at you, or whatever it might be — they’re actually paying you money.

It feels like this is another version of that. The sort of person that is going to read it in the newspaper, and yet, still have the initiative to respond, it’s a person that, I think to an extent, I get a little bit of this, I remember when I got a lot of requests to put podcasts on a webpage or get notifications when I was doing Exponent back in the day, and it was people that never listened to podcasts, they had no idea what a podcast player was, but they wanted to listen to us. And I’m like, “I would actually like to hear more from you because you are so different than the rest of my audience, and this is interesting”, and that’s built into your role in a very sort of tangible respect.

JS: You’re totally right, and that’s exactly how it is, and it is a diminishing audience, but I kind of love them. And I will say I still get excited when they’ll put a — well, if I write a front-page story, sure, if they put a little box on the outside of the page, it says, “Joanna Stern’s blank column on whatever,” I get excited by that. It means that more people are going to see this stuff and yeah, I just really think of it as another platform.

I do have to ask though, are you even a writer anymore or is that just sort of a side gig too to your video star career?

JS: It’s so funny because the way I work is I typically think in video first. So, I’m going to tackle a topic, I’m going to think about, “How are we going to do that video? Do we have to go somewhere? How are we going to come at it?”, but I always usually think there’s going to be a written piece to it, a written column. Maybe that’s more because I know that there’s a certain Wall Street Journal audience member and people that just read, but just in terms of my time, so much more time and energy has to go into video production just because of the process.

Then usually, I’m not going to say 20%, but a certain percentage goes to the writing of the column but there’s obviously a lot of overlap. Sometimes too, I just write things, I just wrote about the Galaxy S24 , and I just wrote about it, I didn’t want to do a video. I was like, “Look, this is a phone. You can see the AI features in the screenshots.” I don’t need to do a video for it.
