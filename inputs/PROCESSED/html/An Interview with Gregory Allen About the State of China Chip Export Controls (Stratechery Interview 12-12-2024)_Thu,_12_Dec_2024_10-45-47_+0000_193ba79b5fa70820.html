High Bandwidth Memory

Gregory Allen, welcome back to Stratechery.

Gregory Allen: It’s great to be back.

Well, I hope it’s great to be back! You were on a couple months ago , and we spent most of that interview talking about US defense, which by the way, a ton of feedback, people were really interested, they found that very valuable.

GA: Oh good.

We didn’t get a chance to talk about chip export controls, which it turns out that was fortuitous because there has been sort of a tidal wave of news, including new announcements just last week, which you just published a report about . This is a technically an interview, but I’m actually deputizing you as a Stratechery correspondent to walk us through and cover what is going on, what the implications are, and so I think we’ll just start there.

GA: So I think it’s worth taking a step back and thinking about, “What is the Biden administration’s basic architecture for export controls?”, and you can boil it down to some really simple stuff. We don’t want to sell AI chips to China because we want to maintain a durable edge over China in AI, and we don’t want them to make their own AI chips so that means we can’t sell them the equipment that they would use to make those chips locally, and because the Dutch and the Japanese are also big players in the semiconductor manufacturing equipment industry, this all needs to be a multilateral effort.

Well, if you look at these most recent export controls , that through-line is continued. But what has really changed, I think the number one takeaway, the number one headline is that we’re no longer just restricting AI chips on the logic side of the equation, now these controls have expanded into memory.

So let me walk you through, because you had seven things that are in these controls. So just to frame this, number one, “Expanding chip level exports to restrict High Bandwidth Memory”, you said it for me, but walk me through that.

GA: So as anybody who listens to your podcast probably knows, High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) is foundational to executing anything at the AI hardware level. When I was getting my MBA, my measly internship was for Samsung Corporate Strategy, working in the headquarters in Seoul, and we were working on a High Bandwidth Memory for AI applications, this was back in 2016. So High Bandwidth Memory has been intimately tied to the AI hardware story for the better part of a decade and now basically every AI accelerator, whether that’s an Nvidia chip, whether that’s anybody else’s chip, a good chunk of that product, if you crack it open, is not just the AI accelerator, the logic chip, but a ton of HBM memory chip and because it’s so foundational to AI. The US is saying, “None for you, China”.

And the thing here, as you sort of note, is you have these chips. The whole point of parallel processing is you can do a bunch of instructions at the same time very quickly, but you need to keep them fed, and the point there is you feed them via the High Bandwidth Memory, which as the name implies — a very good name, I have to say, there’s a lot of tech products, it’s not clear what they do, I feel like a High Bandwidth Memory sort of has it right on the tin — moves a lot of data in and out of those chips at very, very high volume so they can execute that.

GA: And it’s foundational to utilization. So if you think about the big numbers that are being thrown out there, the big hyperscalers, the big AI providers, they’re talking about like $300 billion plus of CapEx in 2025, that’s to build all those data centers. About half the cost of those data centers is the chips, and about half the cost of the chips is the HBM.

Now, if you’re spending all of this money on chips, you want those to be running. You want them to be running 24/7 because that utilization rate drives your return on invested capital, and so if your logic accelerator is spending all of its time waiting around for slow memory, that just kills your utilization and basically kills your investment.

Here’s the question though. China doesn’t generally seem to be operating, and for good reason under the circumstances, under a real stringent return on invested capital calculation. I mean the 7nm chips that are being produced, we know with I think a pretty high degree of certainty , the yields are terrible.

GA: The yields are dreadful.

But they’re doing it anyway just because it needs to be done and this sort of ties into another thing. You referenced Dylan Patel and SemiAnalysis, who have been pretty strident critics of the enforcement of chip controls . But I think a good point he has made is that China, unlike the US, is not necessarily constrained in power or in the ability to build a ton of data centers, and so there’s a bit where they could just sort of — it’s not great, but they could just be way less efficient and accomplish similar things. Is there a bit where these expert controls are fashioned with Western/US constraints and concerns about how you go about building this stuff that might make them less impactful in the long run?

GA: Yeah, the export controls have not achieved their wildest dreams. There was a faction in the Biden administration that says, “Bwahaha, we found the secret weapon, and China’s AI dreams are gone” — that theory is just dead. Where we are now is at more of a cost imposition strategy. “We are going to make this as expensive and complicated as possible for you to do it, we’re going to try and slow you down, we’re going to try and increase your costs, and that is the race that we’re going to run”.

I mean, if you think about it, we’re switching from a mode in which the US AI ecosystem and the Chinese AI ecosystem were largely fused such that if we’re running a race, you can imagine there’s US people giving China Gatorade and those new Nike shoes that make you run faster. Now we’re moving to a moment where we’re trying to trip them in the race, that’s the change in mindset that we’ve experienced, and it’s not working to its most extreme form, but there is real cost imposition takes the form of the fact that SMIC has to operate at these dreadful yields. The economics are terrible, the fact that when they’re building all of these data centers, they’re having to use lousy chips, they’re having to buy more of them, and they’re having to deal with the higher energy costs of all of that.

It’s true that China does have just this extraordinary willingness to spend, but the point is we’re in this race, we’re in this competition, and it gives us an edge, not an infinite edge, but a meaningful edge.

This is a field, maybe you don’t have an answer to this, but there are some that argue that actually the better approach to some of these chips is a much more expensive, a much more high speed memory approach that has much lower latency using SRAM instead of High Bandwidth Memory. Is there a possibility that we actually pushed China down a different route towards developing these chips that maybe ends up being better because we thought HBM was the right way?

GA: I think that’s probably not what’s going to happen. It’s definitely worth saying that that could happen, a version of that kind of happened with YMTC and their NAND memory. There were multiple different approaches they could have taken technologically. All the Western and US allied Asian firms picked one way because it was obviously the best economics, and they held all the intellectual property, they held all the patents and so YMTC basically said, “Okay, we’re going to go down this other road and because we’re so heavily subsidized, it doesn’t really matter that it’s going to be more expensive”, and they did ultimately figure out how to get it work.

I think what you’re describing, the SRAM in massive quantities thing verges on the neuromorphic architecture , and it’s not that that’s impossible, and it’s not that that’s never going to happen, but it’s clearly not the right step for China right now. I think they have a path to domestic HBM production and that’s so much easier for them to chase than a SRAM revolution. I think traditionally they would just wait for somebody else to try and figure out and demonstrate that it’s possible and then they would throw infinite resources at it.

Semiconductor Equipment Restrictions

Let’s go to number two, “Updating the set of advanced semiconductor manufacturing equipment that is restricted on a country-wide basis”. That seems fairly generic, but there’s a few important terms on there. I think country-wide is one of them, walk me through that, what is that?

GA: Yeah, so starting with country-wide, it’s important to distinguish that type of export control from other types of export controls. So advanced AI chips to China, that’s banned to all of China on a country-wide basis. Certain categories of advanced semiconductor manufacturing equipment, that’s banned to all of China. So EUV machines, for example, have never touched Chinese soil because they’re banned on a country-wide basis.

For all of these chip controls, all this stuff that you’ve covered and written about, does any of it matter, if you add it all up, in comparison to that point that they don’t have EUV?

GA: EUV is the highest return on investment export control that we have had and are likely to have. It’s definitely the case that some of the other stuff hurts. If you talk about SMIC, for example, increasing their yields on their 7nm line and expanding the capacity of their 7nm line, they actually are bottlenecked by US equipment, a lot of US metrology equipment, etc. But if you want to talk about why they can’t—

But they do have the equipment, they just need to figure out how to duplicate it. The challenge with EUV is they don’t even have one, so duplicating it is that much harder.

GA: Yes exactly, it’s a lot harder to reverse engineer something that you don’t have a copy of, it really helps to have a copy of it. So I would say the EUV thing really matters, but there’s areas where China is facing headwinds that aren’t part of the EUV story.

So just to take one example, in DRAM, Micron still doesn’t use EUV in their production of DRAM, and they’re a globally competitive firm. So CXMT, the Chinese domestic champion of DRAM, the reason why they’re not currently globally competitive is not the absence of EUV, but I do think you could make a story that it is the absence of all this other stuff that we’ve been refusing to sell.

Got it. So what is some of that equipment that fits in this country-wide limitation?

GA: Yeah, so the historical one, the original one was heavily weighted towards deposition equipment. So if you think about semiconductor manufacturing, you have lithography, which is sort of printing material. You have deposition, adding material, etching, removing material. So originally the country-wide restrictions were heavily focused on deposition because the US government had identified-

Which is that stage one there.

GA: Exactly, “Here’s a bunch of categories of deposition equipment that only the United States makes and so we can start there before Japan and the Netherlands come online and we start adding more and more things that China doesn’t have a good shot at making”.

The most recent round of controls added a bunch of different etching systems that are really tied to the manufacturing of HBM. So specifically it’s a technology called through-silicon vias (TSVs) . HBM is kind of like a bunch of DRAM just stacked on top of each other, but if you want to have the actual electronics of those chips talk to each other in a way that’s more productive, stacking them up instead of putting them side-by-side, you got to drill a hole. And through-silicon vias, that is the sort of technology that is the hole whereby all these chips talk to each other, work together to something more closely approximating one unified chip as opposed to advanced packaging. That requires some pretty fancy etching technology and now that is restricted to China on a country-wide basis.

Got it, and so this is in contrast then to number three, which is, “Updating the list of semiconductor manufacturing equipment that is restricted on an end-use and end-user basis to include additional choke point technologies”.

GA: This is the alternative to country-wide controls, which is basically saying, “We don’t want to sell it to this person or this company, and we don’t want to sell it to people who are engaged in this activity” — that’s the end-user and end-use controls. So what the US government has done is they’ve added more technologies that are going to be restricted on those types of criteria.

Now, it’s worth noting that previous end-use controls that the United States government had in many cases, it just said, “All items subject to the EAR “. If you’re engaged in producing AI chips, you’re just not allowed to buy things that America sells. But this sort of went through that with a finer-tooth comb, added a bunch of different types of equipment. For example, think about ion implantation , this is a kind of technology where the US identified that we had this strong advantage. We knew there’s some people that we want to sell it to in China, but not the people who we think are engaged in advanced-node manufacturing or plausibly might sell it to somebody else who is going to engage in advanced-node manufacturing.

And what is the sort of entity we would want to sell that to, and why do we still want to do that?

GA: So the US strategy as articulated by the Biden administration is, “We’re going to restrict China’s production of advanced AI chips, but we’re going to not restrict their production of legacy chips” — so think like 90nm logic, 48nm logic — that stuff, they’re still allowed to buy the equipment for.

The challenge that the US government has is there are certain types of equipment which you can call node agnostic, which means that the same machine that you would want for producing 5nm is the same machine that you might want for 90nm. There’s some categories of stuff where you really do want exquisite systems versus legacy systems and then there’s other stuff where no matter what process you’re engaged in, the same machine is probably the most attractive purchase for you.

So the United States tries to deal with this by thinking, “Okay, how do we find a way that we don’t sell that stuff to the people who are plausibly going to be engaged in producing HBM or advanced AI chips, but we do sell it to the folks who are engaged in making chips for washing machines or whatever?”.

Magical Thinking and One-Way Doors

And what’s the motivation to even still be selling that stuff, knowing it could be used for advanced chips?

GA: Well, I think number one, the United States government has to think about this from the perspective of US industry. They’ve been saying for a really long time, “Hey, China is a huge chunk of our revenue, it’s part of the mechanism by which we maintain our competitive advantage is getting all of this Chinese revenue. And by the way, if you take it away from us, China can’t compete with us at the most advanced types of equipment, but some of this legacy stuff, they can compete with us, so it’s not just that we lose that revenue, Chinese equipment companies gain that revenue if you don’t let us sell it to them”.

That’s why they’re trying to thread the needle between, “How much equipment do we sell and hurt Chinese equipment companies?”, versus, “How much equipment do we hold back and hurt Chinese chip manufacturers?”, that’s the needle that they’re trying to thread. I don’t think they always get it right, but that is what they’re trying to do.

Is this the reason why the previous chip controls were not as effective as they might’ve hoped they might have been?

GA: Yeah. If you think about how the end-use and end-user controls have to be implemented, the Bureau of Industry and Security at the Department of Commerce, which is the agency charged with executing export controls and approving the licenses, reviewing the licenses, and then investigating enforcement violations — this is like a few hundred human beings who are in charge of reviewing trillions of dollars of economic activity, their life is hard, they’re overstretched, and so the vast majority of resources that are actually being devoted to forcing export controls are not US government resources, they’re corporate resources. It’s the mandatory guidance that the United States government says, “Every company has to check for these 500 things, they have to have a lawyer, sign a sworn statement that there is no risk of XYZ”.

I think the problem is that the Chinese firms don’t really care. They’re like, “Oh, you want our lawyers to sign a sworn letter that we’re definitely not going to be engaged in making AI chips? Here you go. By the way, we want the most advanced equipment you could possibly sell so that we can make our ‘legacy chips'”. I think there’s just a lot of — I don’t want to say willful blindness because that actually is something that would get people fined or put in jail, but that’s what Dylan Patel said so let me just rely upon his quote in that regard.

So what they’re sort of saying is, and I think this is reflected in these export controls, they just added, and we’ll get to this in more detail, but they just added 140 Chinese firms to the entity list. These are firms that theoretically should have been blocked by the last round of end-use controls so they’re sort of conceding that this isn’t working to the full extent that they want it to.

Well, we’ll get to those future points, but just to double down on this bit, you said earlier in this conversation talking about of the different factions in the Biden administration, and some people were sure they had found the magic bullet, and it does strike me that there’s a lot of magical thinking that seems to pervade this entire sort of endeavor. At the end of the day, you are talking about this balancing industry needs versus your needs. At the end of the day, you are declaring silicon war on China.

GA: Yes.

Isn’t it kind of magical thinking to think that we’re going to preserve a market and stop competition for our semi equipment manufacturing firms? Don’t you have to make a choice here?

GA: I’m still with you. I mean this is an analogy I’ve used before, but the way I often think about it is imagine you’re debating with your spouse. You love this area and you want to build a house and you’re saying, “Oh, should we build it on the east bank of the river or should we build it on the west bank of the river?”, and you want one thing and your spouse wants the other thing. Well, the worst thing you could possibly do is compromise and build your house in the middle of the river and you all drown.

(laughing) It’s a great analogy, I love it.

GA: There are times when compromising is stupid, and what I have been trying to advise the Biden administration is, whether you know it or not, you are already all in, you’re trying to split the difference in this policy a hundred different ways and the reality is you are already all in.

These updated controls, which it’s my job to understand what is in these controls, but they’re 200 pages long, and they’re extraordinary legalese, and I’ve already found loopholes in these export controls that I’m quite concerned about, and the lawyers that support US exporters or that support Chinese importers, they’re really smart and they work really hard and there’s a lot more of them than there is of me.

I do think that in trying to find all of this negotiated settlement complexity between the interest of national security, the interest of US industry, the interest of US allies, they think that they’ve got this incredible architecture that magically balances everybody else’s interests, and I think they’d be better off just going for the blunt instrument that has a much higher probability.

If you’re worried about US equipment manufacturers losing revenue for R&D, give them more money.

GA: You can just identify the trade-offs and compensate. I mean, certainly that’s what China is doing.

Yeah, I think this is the key point, this is the root of everything. It’s this magical thinking, this refusal to make trade-offs. I didn’t know if you’re going to go with build a house in the middle of the river or build half the house on one side of the river and the other half on the other, but this is the mindset that I think is pretty galling about this. The reality is this was a ‘no return’ door that you walked through.

GA: This is what I said in one of the reports, the one about the impact of controls in semiconductor manufacturing equipment , which is, and this I think really illustrates how it’s no going back, just imagine for a second that the United States decided we were going to start exporting nuclear submarines to China. Would they buy them? Yes. Would American companies make money? Yes. Would that get China to stop investing in its domestic nuclear submarine industry? No, absolutely not, there’s just no chance, and I think whether or not we view semiconductor manufacturing equipment in the same strategic terms as nuclear submarines, China sure does. There is nothing that we are going to say to them, there is no apology that we are going to make that is going to persuade them that they need anything other than absolute independence in semiconductor manufacturing equipment as soon as fricking possible.

Right. The time to have this discussion, and I wrote this at the time, it was back when the ZTE ban came down and then the Huawei ban, and I put it at the time — look, we just walked through a one-way door, it just fundamentally changed the equation, it’s a card you can only play once, so if you’re going to play that card, you have to play it all the way and there’s no takebacks, and we’ve sat through seven years of like, “Well, can I still take it back if I want to in the future?”, it’s madness.

GA: Yeah, we’re still trying to pretend like we’re going to have it both ways and we’re just not. I think I heard it from a European industry executive who came back from talking to a bunch of customers in China and they just continued to be astonished at how we don’t understand how they viewed ZTE and Huawei. They said that was our 9/11, that was our semiconductor industry’s September 11th, and nothing has ever been the same since.

Including by the way, all the semiconductor equipment revenues, which have skyrocketed ever since then because China started stockpiling. And so there’s a bit of playing at both sides by these companies, which is, “Look at all this revenue we have from China” — well, that revenue is downstream from US policy choices that were made without thinking through the consequences and the loopholes, and so to cry poor that you’re going to lose it is a bit disingenuous.

GA: Now, it’s good that you mentioned stockpiling, because when we’re talking about this updated regulation, maybe I’m giving the impression that, “Hey, there’s a lot of cleverness in this regulation”, but you know what’s not clever? That this regulation was telegraphed six months ago. The fact that the US was going to do HBM was telegraphed in June, it was in Reuters , I bet you China read it. So what do you think every company in China has been stockpiling like mad for the past six months? This gets back to the stupidity of compromise, a pretty good rule faster is so much better than a perfect rule that is way too late.

Especially when it’s not perfect.

GA: Especially when it’s not perfect. But this is my point — why are you even attempting to be perfect? We have now updated these export controls, which began October 7th, 2022, at least the Biden administration’s approach to them, then October 2023, the update admitted that they had massive loopholes and so they had a full year of massive loopholes, now we’re updating it again in December 2024.

Chinese smugglers update their tactics more than once a year, I assure you and the lawyers that look for loopholes update their tactics more than once a year. So as you said, we are already all in. We have walked through a one-way door and we just appear unwilling to devote the resources and act decisively in a timely fashion that’s required to execute upon that strategy.

The Foreign Direct Product Rule

Well, that leads to number four, which gets to probably the single most insane loophole in the original export controls. I’ll let you deliver what that was. Number four, “Dramatically expanding the scope of applicability of foreign direct product rules on exports of both chips and semiconductor manufacturing equipment”. That was a lot of words, why is it addressing the most egregious loophole in all of this?

GA: This is what’s so crazy about this rule.

(laughing) I love that you’ve been writing all day, you’re ramped up, it’s late at night, I need a Gregory Allen rant, give it to me.

GA: Yeah, I mean, look, I’m going to criticize these rules for things that they appeared to whiff on doing, they just got cold feet and were unwilling to take certain measures. But here’s what’s so weird, alongside that sort of, this is a little bit extreme, but that cowardly approach that’s reflected in this rule is the most pedal-to-the-metal expansion of the Foreign Direct Product Rule(FDPR) that I’ve ever seen.

So Foreign Direct Product Rule is very esoteric, let me just walk you through what it means. If the United States wanted to sell a missile to Germany, but we didn’t want to sell a missile to Russia, well, let’s say we sell that missile to Germany and then Germany paints it purple and they say, “Hey, we’re a sovereign country and we do what we want and that’s not even your missile anymore and now it’s the German purple missile and we’re going to sell it to Russia”. The mechanism by which that is illegal is the Foreign Direct Product Rule. We say, “Hey, that’s still an American missile even though you painted it purple, you can’t do that”.

Well, the Trump administration got really excited about an expanded interpretation of the Foreign Direct Product Rule, and this is the one that really bit Huawei, where TSMC was manufacturing Huawei’s chip designs and we said, “TSMC, you’re manufacturing those chips for Huawei using American semiconductor manufacturing equipment, therefore, it is a Foreign Direct Product of US exports and we are banning you now from exporting those chips to Huawei”. That’s the export controls that really worked against Huawei in 2020 after the 2019 ones that didn’t have the Foreign Direct Product Rule flopped massively.

Well, the Biden administration in this most recent rule has the most extraordinary expansion of the Foreign Direct Product Rule. So they’re saying it’s not just that you’re using American semiconductor manufacturing equipment, if that equipment that you’re using contains a chip that was made using American semiconductor manufacturing equipment, even one chip, if that was made using American semiconductor manufacturing equipment, then US export control rules apply. That basically means that all semiconductor manufacturing equipment on planet Earth is now subject to US export control because—

Just to zoom out though, because I think just to get to this loophole that is so nuts, is what we ended up in a situation where originally with the interpretation of the rules, it applied to equipment manufactured in the United States and then went out. So the loophole was these companies could manufacture the equipment outside the United States, like in Malaysia, it was a Lam Research.

GA: It was a big one, yes.

“Yeah, okay, that’s fine, we’ll make our equipment in Malaysia, it’s not in the US and now it’s not subject to controls”, which is so infuriating because number one, not only are you failing, you’re also like decimating the American worker or you’re not even making it in America and somehow this was incentivized by these rules.

GA: Yeah, this is infuriating, right? Because most of the attack that you’ve heard, criticisms that you’ve heard of export controls is you’re incentivizing indigenization among Chinese companies. The quiet part out loud that the companies have not been talking about is you’ve incentivized us to open factories abroad to exploit a loophole that we prefer you don’t know even exists.

So I actually got the data on this, and what’s really interesting is I calculated by looking at 10-Ks and other financial filings, all the US equipment companies, the revenue that they claim is from China from equipment sales, and if you compare that to US exports, which is something that the Census Bureau tracks of semiconductor manufacturing equipment, there’s this massive divergence after ZTE and Huawei, where the revenue from China for these companies keeps going up by a ton, but the US exports of this equipment goes up by a much, much smaller number. And it’s for the reason you exactly said, they started doing a lot more manufacturing abroad specifically to get around export controls.

And again, how does this loophole happen?

GA: I think number one, the Commerce Department, they don’t necessarily know what the right tech spec is for ion implanters that will catch this but will not catch that.

It’s just downstream of trying to have your cake and eat it too.

GA: They’re dependent upon a conversation with industry, they are mostly incapable of writing these regulations entirely by themselves.

Big picture, should we be feeling bad for the semiconductor equipment manufacturers, or should we be mad at them?

GA: I’m pretty mad at them, to be honest, and this is why I’m actually glad at this expanded interpretation of the Foreign Direct Product Rule, because now that incentive to offshore your manufacturing, it’s gone. No matter where you are, it’s hit, and there’s an important implication here for foreign substitutability.

The American companies have been saying, “Hey, if we stop selling to China, the Dutch are just going to sell, the Japanese are just going to sell, the Koreans are just going to sell” — well, this new expanded Foreign Direct Product Rule, as I said, it basically covers every piece of equipment on Planet Earth is at least potentially subject under this new interpretation. So that means if Tokyo Electron, which also has some manufacturing operations in Malaysia, if they ship from Malaysia, these new rules cover Tokyo Electron. If Siemens in Korea tries to sell to China, they’re subject as well.

Allies and Lawyers

Well, I guess that leads to number five, “Offering exemptions and incentives to reward countries such as Japan and other ones that adopt domestic export controls aligned with US policy and goals”.

GA: So this is what’s kind of funny, because if you think about the Biden administration’s assumptions throughout this whole thing, they said, “Hey, we’ve got to do it with allies, we’ve got to do it with allies, this is what the Trump administration got wrong, we really need to bring the allies in”.
