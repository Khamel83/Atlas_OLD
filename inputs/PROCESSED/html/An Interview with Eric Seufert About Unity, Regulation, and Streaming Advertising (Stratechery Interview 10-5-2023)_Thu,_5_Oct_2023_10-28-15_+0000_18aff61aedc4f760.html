Good morning,

I’m pleased to welcome Eric Seufert back for a check-in on digital advertising.

Seufert is the creator of Mobile Dev Memo , where he writes about digital advertising. Seufert also invests in startups in the mobile space through his fund Heracles Capital. Seufert previously worked for several mobile gaming startups, including as the Vice President of User Acquisition for Rovio, and was a consultant for multiple companies exploring the mobile space.

In today’s Interview we discuss Unity’s recent decision to change its business model, what that says about mobile gaming, and the competitive landscape for mobile advertising. We also cover how regulation is changing app monetization, why many advertising companies are subscale, and why that is bad for the product. Finally, we finish with a dive into advertising for streamers and the long-term trajectory of the industry.

They basically turned this around from announcement to deployment in six months, but Disney’s been building this tech for years and they have their own ad server, they have their own audience segments, they already have this, and so they already have the real true value prop of a digital advertising solution. What Netflix is doing is saying, “Hey, we’ll give you the same stuff you can get on TV. If you’re a TV advertiser, why don’t you take some dollars and put them to Netflix? We’re giving you the same. The measurement’s pretty unevolved, it’s pretty primitive, the targeting is pretty primitive. But hey, we’re Netflix isn’t that exciting?” That’s really what TV, if you talk to brand advertising, it’s so wishy-washy like, “Hey, I would love to spend on Netflix, I bet you they’ll invite me to their upfronts, I bet you they’ll invite me to some great, glamorous party. I bet you they’ll invite me to their Super Bowl booth or whatever”.

Right. It’s a real inversion, where Disney is the tech-forward, you’re sort buying based on targeting and it’s not fully self-serve yet, but it’s on that road. Whereas Netflix is, “Imagine TV advertising, but on streaming”.

ES: Right. “But it’s Netflix and isn’t that exciting?” So I think it’s just going to take time for them to build that out and again, Disney’s been doing this for a while and it hasn’t been applied to Disney Plus yet, but it will be soon. I think this is why Disney wants to keep ESPN, because they actually are betting on this advertising bit and that’s obviously great anchor inventory, great inventory to have.

I would rather outsource it, give Google a cut, and make sure that the audience is being targeted in a very efficient way.

Should Netflix do that?

ES: No, I think Netflix should build, but I think think they probably did it — the order that in which they approached this is probably right, just because they wanted to get something to market fast. They hired a bunch of very seasoned, very well-respected execs to lead this. But yeah, now they need to be building. Netflix is big enough, they should own this, it should be proprietary.

The other thing is, there’s a lot of really cool ways that you could make this very bespoke to your audience’s behaviors and also to just the general format of the service so I think that unlocks a tremendous amount of value, too. If you use commodity off-the-shelf tech and you do outsource to a partner, you’re stuck with the commodity formats, you can’t make anything really bespoke and in making it bespoke, you potentially make it a lot more noticeable, you make it a lot more prominent, and then you can charge a premium for it. If you’re just selling pre-roll or mid-roll or whatever, and it’s the same kind of video format, 15-second, 30-second, there’s not really a way to sell that as premium to advertising.

Yeah, you’re going to be drilling down to the TV commodity price, basically.

ES: Right. Exactly.

ES: Well, all of WB Games .

Got it. Actually, I didn’t know that they had a large game mobile division.

ES: Well, there’s a fairly large graveyard of gaming initiatives across big tech. It always just seems like something interesting to do that could be lucrative and worth trying.

The Advertising Endgame

Well, across media in general, too, that’s why I’m surprised about the Warner Brothers point. Well, here’s the question, just to wrap this all into one big thing. I think we’re seeing the consummation of Google and Facebook regulatory-driven dominance, that was, I think you could see it coming and now it’s actually happening, that’s one of the things you articulated. You have this scenario, and I think you’re seeing even with streaming, where you either have to be really big and build it yourself or you need to figure out who to partner with. There’s been all this disruption over the last four or five years to advertising generally. It’s filtered down to mobile gaming, it’s things happening with Unity. Is the end game in sight here? Or do you see more disruption to come? Or are these few years going to set basically the foundation for advertising for the next decade and it’s going to become fairly boring with just a few dominant players?

ES: That’s the multi-billion dollar, trillion dollar question. Here’s what I’m fearful about. I think that the eventuality we will face in Europe is that behavioral advertising is just not a reality, it doesn’t really exist, it’s not a meaningful source of revenue, it can’t really be done in compliance with the laws there and that’s just a totally fragmented user experience landscape. It’s a totally fragmented revenue landscape in terms of the business models that are utilized there and you’re probably just having to go direct to users and charge them money for access. But, if that’s it, if the US doesn’t follow that path, then that’s disruptive to 10, 15% of digital advertising turnover.

The big question mark to my mind is how successful can the FTC be with the initiative that they’re pursuing? You see some of this, and I tweeted about this with respect to the Amazon lawsuit last week , where my sense is their understanding of digital advertising, their understanding of that business model is confused and uninformed. Now, if the FTC or the DOJ is able somehow to emulate the trajectory that Europe is substantively progressed upon, then yeah, that is going to utterly dismantle the digital advertising market as we know it. Now, it’s going to be massively disruptive and probably suppress growth meaningfully. I don’t know how successful they are going to be. Their strategy to me is somewhat inscrutable. Now, their agenda is very clear because we knew what the agenda was based on Lina Khan’s paper, we knew what she wanted to do, but the strategy is unclear because my sense is the strategy seems to be, “That we pursue these cases that are destined to lose in order to draw attention to these perceived problems, to this perceived malfeasance.” Now, that is a very effective form of activism, but that is not a very effective form of regulatory supervision. So if that is though successful in catalyzing Congress to do something or to just engender a lot of ire from the public, well then maybe the US follows the same path.

But even if that doesn’t happen in that concerted way, what we’re facing in the US is this completely fragmented privacy environment that’s going to be very difficult to navigate. To your point, that is going to further entrench the largest incumbents, because who has the capacity to deal with fifty different state level privacy laws? Facebook, Google, and not many other companies. So I think no matter what, we’re either facing just a very complex privacy situation in the United States, or we’re going to follow the path that Europe has progressed upon and in either case, I think it’s going to be very difficult for startups or for innovation to take root in the United States. I’m not very optimistic about this.

Now, where I think interesting things could happen is in areas like privacy-enhancing technologies, companies that are bringing innovation to the management of user data in a way that doesn’t trigger some of these restrictions. We’ll see, I don’t know. But I think where I saw platform interventions, like ATT, as being very disruptive two years ago, I think of those as being utterly manageable now, whereas the regulatory interventions are much scarier.

Right. So basically, if the US — this strategy of losing as many lawsuits as possible triggers laws to ban targeted advertising — then everyone’s screwed, even if you’re going to charge for everything and otherwise it’s just going to be the big players. But even then, the prospects for any small startup in any sort of ad-supported space seems basically zero. If you want to start a consumer startup, you have to basically start it with the assumption you’re going to exit to Google or Facebook, because it’s hard to see any ever getting scale otherwise.

ES: Yeah, or if they can even manage to acquire anyone. I don’t think Facebook believes that it can do any sort of deal of meaningful size at this point.

Yeah. They got sued for acquiring a VR app .

ES: Right.

So basically we’ve basically utterly and completely killed any prospects for consumer-based startups, is what’s happening?

ES: Well, we’ll see. The tide could change. The thing about this though is it feels broadly bipartisan. So if you think about the DOJ/Google suit, that was filed in the Trump administration and there was a bill proposed, they didn’t really go anywhere, The Banning Surveillance Advertising Act or something, that was bipartisan. The sponsors of that were Republican and Democrats. This feels like broadly bipartisan, I just feel like it’s not very informed or commercially-minded, which okay, fine, let’s say not commercially-minded, but I don’t think that these concerns map to consumer safety in a very fulsome way. I think in a lot of ways they’re very superficial.

And just broadly speaking, it’s just a re-articulation of the idea that an obsession with privacy without any sort of consideration of how actually damaging the data is or the potential positive use cases, is directly opposed to any competitive environment.
