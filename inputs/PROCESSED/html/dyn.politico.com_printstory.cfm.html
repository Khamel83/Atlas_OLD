Egypt: It's not about Hosni Mubarak

Jon Alterman • Unknown date
Hosni Mubarak isn’t the point, or at least he shouldn’t be.

With tens of thousands of protesters in Cairo’s Tahrir Square calling for his removal, the world’s attention remains focused on whether Mubarak completes another week in office or is shuffled off into ignominious exile.

Yet, focusing on the short term is a mistake. On issues that matter, there is little difference between Mubarak, his vice president Omar Suleiman, or Defense Minister Muhammad Hussein Tantawi. All spent their careers in security organizations defending the state; all distrust the Egyptian public’s instincts.

The real prize in Egypt is not the presidency in the near term, but Egypt’s political system in the post-Mubarak era. How that system operates is likely to be a product of decisions not yet made — and it will be defined over the next nine months.

What is breathtaking about this moment is just how numerous those decisions are. For one, there’s the question of how to improve Egypt’s electoral rolls — among the world’s worst. Egypt has not had a truly competitive election for more than a half-century, and the voting records reflect it.

One friend who is a member of the upper house of Egypt’s parliament—and of the ruling party—said several years ago he had never voted. Whenever he went to a polling site, he said, they told him he was in the wrong place.

Second is the problem of changing the constitution in the absence of a legitimate parliament. Egypt’s last elections were so fraudulent —and so favorable to the ruling party — that they cannot credibly represent the national will. Yet there are no other legislative bodies on offer, and no legal basis to constitute them.

Third is the problem of political parties. The current parties are all toothless — either because Anwar Sadat created them to be so in the 1970s, or because Mubarak only allowed their creation if they were. They have no mass membership, no real platforms and no true hierarchy. Instead, they are the personal fiefdoms of their leaders, rather than reflections of the aspirations of their members.

Fourth is the problem of religion in politics — long a sensitive issue in Egypt. The Muslim Brotherhood, which has broad support but is not allowed to act as a political party, is only a part of the Islamic spectrum in Egyptian public life, with many to the left and right of the group. More inclusive politics in Egypt will need to include more religious voices. But the parameters of participation still need to be defined.

Fifth, and perhaps most crucial, is how all the various opposition groups will shift from what is essentially a negative agenda—ending Mubarak’s term—to a positive one, which includes decisions on all these issues. With no hierarchy, no leadership and no shared priorities — and lacking people with any credible political or governmental experience — organizing the opposition is likely to be a herculean task.

In the coming struggle between the opposition and the government, the military officers who rule Egypt most surely have an upper hand. Their ideological coherence, organizational skill and media influence give them a big advantage.



Yet, the opposition’s experience of drawing hundreds of thousands of Egyptians into the streets, and the knowledge that they could do so again, gives the protesters clout.



Keeping the focus on Mubarak is a distraction from the daunting task of preparing for his aftermath. It threatens to ensure that what has happened over the last two weeks was merely a revolt — not a revolution. In Egypt, the distinction has precedent.



What is commonly referred to as Egypt’s 1952 “July Revolution” was not called a revolution at the time. It was only after the army colonels, who overthrew the monarchy, instituted far-reaching changes in society that their coup evolved into a revolution.



Egyptians are clearly pining for a revolution now. The public seems to be seeking to abandon the stolid authoritarian politics of the past in favor of a brighter and more inclusive future.



Whether that future comes to pass in the coming years depends little on whether Mubarak serves out the rest of his term. Whether Mubarak or one of his long-time aides guides the process, the challenges will likely be the same. In addition, the government is likely to continue to use the continuing protests to keep life in Egypt disrupted — using the protesters as an excuse and so diminishing their public support.



The real struggle for the future of Egypt has only begun. It could be far less telegenic than last week’s protests — largely committee meetings and long documents rather than mass rallies and placards.



But this is the important battle. Mubarak’s future is clear. Egypt’s future is not. Egyptians must focus on Egypt.



Jon Alterman is director of the Middle East Program at the Center for Strategic and International Studies. He is a former member of the State Department’s Policy Planning staff.
