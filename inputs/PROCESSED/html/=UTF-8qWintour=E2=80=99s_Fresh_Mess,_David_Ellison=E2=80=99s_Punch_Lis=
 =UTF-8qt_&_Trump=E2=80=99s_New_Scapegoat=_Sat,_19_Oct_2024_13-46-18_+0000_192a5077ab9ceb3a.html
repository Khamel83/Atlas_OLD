Monday Night Lights
On Monday evening, I did something slightly unusual: I left the office early and headed to the Meadowlands to watch my New York Jets take on the Bills. I’ve been a Jets fan since childhood, a bizarre and unforced affliction in an otherwise happy life, and it’s an affinity that I’ve regrettably handed down to my children. I’d been looking forward to the game all weekend: Not only had the Jets recently replaced their head coach, adding an element of intrigue to the game, but I also love the pomp and theater of live sports, especially professional football. Since it was Monday Night Football , ESPN would be dispatching its full team and all the agents would be in town to talk business, dap up celebrities, and scour for information. In other words, it seemed like it was going to be an evening brimming with sports and gossip—two of my favorite pastimes.

I was attending the game with two iconic veterans of the industry: Connor Schell , the utterly brilliant founder of Words + Pictures and the former head of content at ESPN, who co-created 30 for 30 and executive produced both O.J.: Made in America and The Last Dance , perhaps the two most profoundly impactful documentaries of the past decade; and Peter Schrager , the NFL Network morning show host and Fox NFL analyst, who happens to be this generation’s heir to Peter King . Indeed, attending an NFL game with Schrager, in particular, is sort of like riding through Vatican City in the Popemobile: Everyone came up to him on the sidelines before the game to pay their respects. At some point, even Fireman Ed , the team’s vociferous and Jumbotron-ready super fan, kissed the ring—even as J.B. Smoove and 50 Cent , among others, milled about.

But my favorite moment of the evening didn’t even occur between the white lines—the Jets blew it in the fourth quarter, inevitably—but rather during the pregame. As we were milling about on the sidelines, shortly after 7 p.m., I noticed a number of agents, P.R. executives, and entourage members stare down at their phones, one by one, like a chorus line. I was unsure precisely what they were looking at until I heard one of them mumble, Ourand is out . Indeed, the latest issue of The Varsity , my partner John Ourand ’s brilliant private email on the sports media business, had popped into their inboxes only moments earlier and they were reading it like doorbusters at Walmart on Black Friday. I even had the funny pleasure of standing beside CAA Sports agent Matt Kramer as he read a piece about himself. It was both meta and heady—a true testament to John’s singular influence in the industry, and what he has created here at Puck.

Coincidentally, only a few days earlier, the ESPN chairman Jimmy Pitaro had quietly come to an agreement with the NFL to co-broadcast six additional Monday Night Football games on ABC, its sister broadcast network, starting with that very Jets-Bills contest. And while that might not seem like an enormous concession, it was actually quite a powder keg in the ever-metamorphosing media world. Not only was Disney, the parent company, foregoing ESPN’s cable exclusivity, but this was naturally going to enrage the NFL’s other broadcast partners. After all, NBC markets its enormously valuable Sunday night package as the only primetime broadcast deal. A few days earlier, NFL Media C.O.O. Hans Schroeder had broken the ABC simulcast news to executives at NBC and Fox and CBS, all of whom manifested emotions on the outer edge of the rage spectrum.

If you only have time to read one story this weekend, I’d urge you to turn your attention to John’s excellent piece, Is Goodell Picking Favorites? Even if you have little interest in sports, or even the business of sport, the story articulates how deeply the future of the world’s largest media conglomerates relies on their relationship with the NFL, one of the last ballasts of American monoculture. Indeed, this latest ESPN-ABC simulcast deal perfectly represented The Shield’s power. The league wanted to increase the viewership for Monday Night Football , so it broadcast the game on a second network. And it did so because it could . These days, after all, CBS owner Paramount Global has a market capitalization of around $7.5 billion, which is just a little more than Apollo co-founder Josh Harris paid for the Commanders a year ago, and probably around what the Seahawks will fetch when Paul Allen ’s sister puts the team on the market—perhaps enticing Jeff Bezos . Indeed, this is one of the great dynamics of our time, and precisely what you should expect from Puck.

Have a great weekend,
Jon
