The NBA season approaches with the rolling thunder of a [Shaquille O’Neal](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=b866hh4apUI) [coast-to-coast](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GYcnsFdu8q8) [dunk](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=br3AaISFc-A). We felt the footsteps coming weeks ago, and took each NBA player’s projected [Real Plus-Minus](http://www.espn.com/nba/story/_/id/10740818/introducing-real-plus-minus) and [wins above replacement](https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/carmelo-anthonys-contract-could-doom-the-knicks-to-mediocrity/), calculated a total for each team, and ran 10,000 simulations of the NBA schedule to divine likely records and championship odds.1 We’ve split the teams into the lower and upper tiers in each conference; these are the seven teams that likely won’t make the playoffs from the East. (We previewed the West’s lower and upper tiers [here](https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/2014-nba-preview-the-lakers-are-even-worse-than-they-were-last-year/) and [here](https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/2014-nba-preview-the-rise-of-the-warriors/).) ![paine-feature-NBA-PHI](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/paine-feature-nba-phi.png)

The Philadelphia 76ers were terrible last season. They will almost certainly be terrible this season. And yet, they’re getting better.

The Sixers are doing something more complex than simply losing games: They’re creating an offense. Under previous head coach Doug Collins, Philadelphia played at a relatively slow pace and was comfortable relying on mid-range jumpers for a significant portion of its offense. Last season, with Brett Brown at the helm, the 76ers made drastic changes. The graph below shows every NBA team from the past two seasons, charted by both their pace and the percentage of their shots that came on mid-range jumpers:

![levy-feature-nba-preview-philly-1](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/levy-feature-nba-preview-philly-1.png)

You can see how much movement there was from the 76ers last season — they pushed themselves to the league’s extremes in both pace and shot distribution.

The Sixers may not have found their franchise player yet, but they do know how they want the pieces around that hypothetical future star to play. Sixers ball is now up-tempo ball, with lots of defensive pressure and an efficient distribution of shots by the team’s role-players.

Like last year, it may be easy to miss the process in all the losing this season. Setting aside their chaotic roster, the 76ers are still working on becoming the team they’d like to be. — *Ian Levy*

![paine-feature-NBA-MIL](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/paine-feature-nba-mil.png)

The Milwaukee Bucks were a disaster on defense last season, allowing 108.9 points per 100 possessions, the second-worst mark in the league. That was a huge slide from the 2012-13 season, when the team had the league’s 12th-best defense, allowing just 102.3 points per 100 possessions.

Many of the Bucks’ problems could be traced back to the basics of defense — forcing misses and then rebounding those misses. The Bucks ranked 28th in opponent expected field goal percentage, and they weren’t picking up the few baskets their opponents were missing: They ranked 29th in defensive rebounding percentage.

Blame the absence of Larry Sanders. Two years ago Sanders was a breakout star, becoming a hugely important interior defender for the Bucks. He was one of the two best defenders in the league over the past two seasons at keeping his opponents’ effective field goal percentage in check, according to basketball stats blog Got Buckets’ [adjusted Four Factors ratings](http://www.gotbuckets.com/statistics/ffapm/2014-ffapm-defense/).

Sanders’s defensive performance didn’t really change last season. The Bucks defense was 4.9 points better per 100 possessions when he was on the floor, and the [NBA’s SportVU Player Tracking system](http://stats.nba.com/playerTrackingDefense.html?pageNo=1&rowsPerPage=25&sortField=FGP_DEFEND_RIM&sortOrder=ASC&filters=FGA_DEFEND_RIM*GE*5.0) calculated that opponents shot just 41.5 percent at the rim on shots he defended. That was the third-best mark in the league among players who defended at least five shots at the rim per game.

But to defend, a player has to be on the court. And Sanders was not, playing 584 minutes across 23 games.

The missed time came because of two injuries ([one of which came during a fight at a night club](http://www.cbssports.com/nba/eye-on-basketball/24399243/bucks-gary-neal-asked-larry-sanders-to-try-earning-his-money)), and his season was capped off with a [five-game suspension for violating the league’s anti-doping program](http://www.si.com/nba/point-forward/2014/04/04/larry-sanders-milwaukee-bucks-suspended-drug-program) by using marijuana.

He’s a pain, sure, but there are just a handful of players in the league who are capable of changing a game defensively as well as Sanders. If the Bucks are serious about quickly moving forward their young core, helping Sanders get healthy and stay on the right side of the law could give them an enormous push. — *Ian Levy*

![paine-feature-NBA-NYK](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/paine-feature-nba-nyk.png)

Last year’s New York Knicks were an [overpaid](http://blog.surepayroll.com/nba-payrolls-2013-2014/), [overrated](https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/in-the-nba-its-not-whether-you-win-or-lose-its-whether-you-beat-vegas/) mediocrity with a nominally disappointing 37-45 record2  that would ultimately cost coach Mike Woodson his job. What followed was an offseason of upheaval; [legendary coach](http://www.amazon.com/Eleven-Rings-Success-Phil-Jackson-ebook/dp/B00AEBEVTQ/ref=tmm_kin_swatch_0?_encoding=UTF8&sr=&qid=) — and [former Knick](http://assets.nydailynews.com/polopoly_fs/1.1722087!/img/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/article_970/dna063156038.jpg) — Phil Jackson [signed on](http://www.espn.com/new-york/nba/story/_/id/10607795/phil-jackson-deal-new-york-knicks-finalized) as the team’s new president of basketball operations, and after a [dalliance](http://sports.yahoo.com/news/knicks-improve-offer-to-steve-kerr-with-fourth-guaranteed-season-213134172.html) with Steve Kerr (his former point guard with the Chicago Bulls) Jackson [eventually hired](http://www.nba.com/knicks/news/knicks-hire-derek-fisher-head-coach) Derek Fisher (his former point guard with the LA Lakers) as the Knicks’ 26th head coach.

An acolyte of Jackson and his [longtime assistant Tex Winter](http://www.nydailynews.com/sports/basketball/meet-man-behind-phil-triangle-offense-article-1.1722632), Fisher is bringing the [fabled](http://grantland.com/features/chuck-klosterman-phil-jackson-tex-winter-death-triangle-offense/) [triangle offense](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_offense) to New York, emphasizing ball movement, court spacing and unselfish decision-making. Those are all good ideas (for any offense), and historically speaking Jackson-coached teams were perennially above the NBA average in [assist rate](http://www.basketball-reference.com/about/glossary.html#ast_pct).3 But only one member of [Jackson’s coaching tree](http://www.sbnation.com/lookit/2014/4/22/5639410/phil-jackson-coaching-tree-tragic) — Bill Cartwright — has successfully convinced his players to share the ball anywhere near as much4 as Jackson did when he was a coach.

![paine-nbapreview-knicks-3](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/paine-nbapreview-knicks-3.png)

It remains to be seen if Fisher’s version of the offense can buck this trend and promote the kind of passing to which triangle coaches always seem to pay lip service. But he’ll have his work cut out for him with these Knicks. The 2013-14 version of the team finished 27th out of 30 teams in assist rate, with frequent [complaints about the lack of ball movement](http://nypost.com/2013/11/26/knicks-rewind-whos-to-blame-for-lack-of-ball-movement/) hurled in Woodson’s direction by the New York press. These were not your [Red Holzman-era Knicks](http://articles.baltimoresun.com/1998-11-15/sports/1998319166_1_red-holzman-york-knicks-knick-team).

Jackson [has mentioned](http://www.cbssports.com/nba/eye-on-basketball/24723289/phil-jackson-on-carmelo-anthony-the-ball-cant-stop) that “the ball can’t stop” if the triangle is going to be successful. While the team acquired veteran point guard Jose Calderon — [one of the game’s elite passers](http://bkref.com/tiny/FajCJ) when he was in his prime — over the offseason, that position isn’t nearly as crucial to the passing game in the triangle as in other offenses. Ultimately, it may come down to ([properly rated?](https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/carmelo-anthony-isnt-underrated-but-hes-not-overrated-either/)) star forward Carmelo Anthony, alternately a [willing passer](http://basketball.realgm.com/wiretap/232903/Thibodeau-Carmelo-Doesnt-Get-Enough-Credit-As-A-Very-Good-Passer) and [total black hole](https://twitter.com/Neil_Paine/status/170147753912049664) at various points in his career, to share enough touches for the triangle to flourish in Fisher’s coaching debut. — *Neil Paine*

![paine-feature-NBA-ORL](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/paine-feature-nba-orl.png)

By trading Arron Afflalo and Jameer Nelson, the Magic seemed to make their intentions clear: develop their youthful core, build through the draft and forgo short-term wins for the promise of success down the road. Then, somewhat puzzlingly, the team signed Channing Frye, who is coming off the best season of his career, to a contract that will last until he is 35. The Magic are a jumble, but the most important thing they can do this season is figure out which of their nine players on rookie deals fits the long-term plan.

The most likely to last is Victor Oladipo. Last year, he struggled to play efficiently as the primary ball-handler in his rookie season. His 19 percent turnover rate was the third worst among starting NBA guards, and he shot only 33 percent from three. But Oladipo got to the free-throw line (with a 35 percent free-throw rate) and defended well on the perimeter, meaning that if he can improve his shooting and ball-handling, he will make strides towards better offensive efficiency this season.

After Oladipo, things get a little more complicated. Up front, Tobias Harris and Nikola Vucevic figure to see considerable minutes with Frye. Both are entering their fourth seasons in the NBA and have shown the potential to be fine rotation players. Harris5 is a very strong rebounder for a player of his size (20 percent defensive rebounding rate despite being 6’8”), and he also almost never turns the ball over. But he can’t shoot (he’s never made more than 31 percent of his threes in a season), and in a league that increasingly prizes stretch fours, that is a big limitation.

Vucevic has quietly become one of the best rebounders in the league. Last season, he finished 10th in defensive rebounding rate, 13th in offensive rebounding rate and, according to SportVu, 13th in rebounding chances.6 Vucevic is also an excellent mid-range shooter, making 45 percent of his 2-point jump shots at least 10 feet away from the basket.

Vucevic agreed to a [four-year, $53 million extension](http://www.espn.com/nba/story/_/id/11741304/nikola-vucevic-orlando-magic-agree-4-year-53-million-extension) last week. Harris can agree to an extension before Oct. 31 or become a restricted free agent next season. The growth of these two players, and of Oladipo, will go a long way toward shaping the Magic’s future. — *John Ezekowitz*

![paine-feature-NBA-BOS](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/paine-feature-nba-bos.png)

Rajon Rondo might get traded, or he might not. Marcus Smart might be Rookie of the Year, or he might not. The 2014-15 Boston Celtics’ outlook is uncertain. It’s unlikely they’ll be a .500 team, or make the playoffs. Our statistical model projects them to win 32 games.7

But the Celtics do have a rare combination in Kelly Olynyk: a 7-foot starting center who doubles as a 3-point threat. There are great long-range shooters who are tall (Kevin Durant, say), but not Kelly Olynyk-tall.

Since 1980, the league-wide average (weighted by minutes played) of all shots that are 3-pointers rose steeply until the line was moved back in 1997, only to resume its seemingly inexorable rise. For centers, the 3-pointer share has been much, much lower, and the climb has been less steady because of the small sample sizes.

Yet something interesting has happened in recent years: Seven-footers are taking more threes. The rate nearly tripled between the 2011 season average of 2.6 percent and last season’s record-shattering high of 7.6 percent.

![flowers-feature-nba-preview-celtics-1](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/flowers-feature-nba-preview-celtics-1.png)

Of 7-footers last season, five binged on the long ball: Spencer Hawes, Donatas Motiejunas, Andrea Bargnani, Dirk Nowitzki and Olynyk. Each attempted more than 20 percent of his shots from behind the arc.

Bargnani and Motiejunas hit less than 30 percent of their threes, while Olynyk hit 35 percent, Nowitzki hit 40 percent and Hawes hit an impressive 42 percent ([good for 10th-best](http://www.basketball-reference.com/leagues/NBA_2014_leaders.html) in the league).

Now, this is not a widespread phenomenon: Most 7-footers do not shoot from long range, but the few who do are doing ever more of it, and Olynyk is the youngest and latest incarnation of the trend.

Olynyk is an average-to-decent player, with a Statistical Plus-Minus rating of 0.5 during his rookie year, which is above average. (His [Real Plus-Minus](http://www.espn.com/nba/statistics/rpm/_/sort/RPM/position/9) score of -1.3 last season was about league average.) While he’s not a franchise center, his ability to play the position and still shoot so well from long range is a unique entry in Brad Stevens’s playbook. — *Andrew Flowers*

The Detroit Pistons dropped $13.5 million on three players — [D.J. Augustin, Caron Butler and Jodie Meeks](http://data.shamsports.com/content/pages/data/salaries/pistons.jsp) — who have severe limitations. The one thing they all can do: consistently make jump shots.

This is what you might expect a team to do when it’s [last in jumper field goal percentage](http://nbasavant.com/shot_search.php?hfST=jump%7C&hfQ=&hfSZB=&hfSZA=&hfSZR=&ddlYear=2013&txtGameDateGT=&txtGameDateLT=&ddlGameTimeGT_min=&ddlGameTimeGT_sec=&ddlGameTimeLT_min=&ddlGameTimeLT_sec=&ddlShotDistanceGT=&ddlShotDistanceLT=&ddlTeamShooting=&ddlTeamDefense=&ddlMin=500&ddlGroupBy=team&ddlOrderBy=shots_made_desc&hfGT=0%7C&ddlShotMade=#results) in the league, as the Pistons were last season. (They also took the third-fewest jump shots, total.) [No team over the past four seasons](http://nbasavant.com/shot_search.php?hfST=jump%7C&hfQ=&hfSZB=&hfSZA=&hfSZR=&ddlYear=&txtGameDateGT=&txtGameDateLT=&ddlGameTimeGT_min=&ddlGameTimeGT_sec=&ddlGameTimeLT_min=&ddlGameTimeLT_sec=&ddlShotDistanceGT=&ddlShotDistanceLT=&ddlTeamShooting=&ddlTeamDefense=&ddlMin=0&ddlGroupBy=team-year&ddlOrderBy=field_goal_asc&hfGT=0%7C&ddlShotMade=#results) has made a lower percentage of its jump shots. The table below (data courtesy of [NBASavant](http://nbasavant.com/index.php)) shows where Augustin, Butler and Meeks would fit with the jump-shooting performance of last year’s roster:

![levy-feature-nba-preview-detroit-1](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/levy-feature-nba-preview-detroit-1.png)

Outside shooting is the lifeblood of offensive efficiency, not just because of the points it yields, but because of the way it stretches the defense and creates space for every offensive player.

Adding three proficient jump shooters will not solve all the Pistons’ problems this season, especially with Meeks out for an extended stretch because of injury.8 Stan Van Gundy still needs to figure out a coherent defense; whether and how the trio of Greg Monroe, Andre Drummond and Josh Smith can work together; and whether Brandon Jennings is really the kind of point guard he wants running this offense. Still, a few more capable shooters on the perimeter can help those other problems solve themselves. — *Ian Levy*

![paine-feature-NBA-BRK](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/paine-feature-nba-brk.png)

It’s hard to imagine more going wrong for the Brooklyn Nets than what transpired last season. A tumultuous coaching turn by Jason Kidd (who [bolted to the Milwaukee Bucks](http://grantland.com/the-triangle/why-jason-kidds-move-to-milwaukee-is-business-as-usual/) after a [failed coup](http://www.sheridanhoops.com/2014/06/30/scotto-jason-kidd-burns-brooklyn-bridge-with-nets/) in June), the season-ending injury suffered by center Brook Lopez in December, the banged-up struggles of putative franchise player Deron Williams and the sudden collapse of Kevin Garnett’s game9 made it fairly astonishing that the [ancient](http://www.basketball-reference.com/leagues/NBA_2014.html#misc::2), [staggeringly expensive](http://sportslistoftheday.com/2013/12/15/nba-2013-14-team-payrolls-and-salary-cap-hits/) Nets were even able to advance to the second round of the playoffs.

Now Brooklyn has a new coach in Lionel Hollins, a (marginally) younger roster with 37-year-old Paul Pierce gone, and a surprisingly strong front-court duo in Lopez and second-year big man Mason Plumlee. But as was the case when they [acquired him](http://sports.espn.go.com/new-york/nba/news/story?id=6150419) from the Utah Jazz in 2011, the Nets’ ultimate fate lies in the hands of Williams, a point guard with the talent — and salary — of a superstar, but not always the durability or production to match.

Williams has always been of particular interest to statheads because he was so easily compared to [2005 rookie classmate](http://www.basketball-reference.com/draft/NBA_2005.html) Chris Paul. Drafted one pick apart,10 both burst onto the scene with instant success; Paul thrilled nerds by generating [absurdly good advanced stats](http://bkref.com/tiny/23yWs) at a young age, while the bigger, sturdier Williams became the [traditionalist’s choice](https://www.deseret.com/2010/4/29/20111767/utah-jazz-nba-s-best-point-guard-deron-williams-getting-votes#deron-williams-believes-hes-the-best-point-guard-in-the-nba-and-hes-not-afraid-to-say-it) after leading the Jazz to four playoff series wins in his first five NBA seasons. It was a classic dichotomy: the player with the gaudy stats against the player with the eye test and postseason résumé on his side.

That narrative broke down, however, when Utah [traded](http://www.sbnation.com/nba/2011/2/23/2010098/derion-williams-nets-trade-mikhail-prokhorov) Williams to the Nets,11 and both his health and statistics took a turn for the lousy upon arriving in New Jersey. (Not helping matters were a group of [horrid](http://www.nydailynews.com/sports/basketball/0-18-new-jersey-nets-losing-streak-history-article-1.432005) teammates in the franchise’s final season before uprooting for Brooklyn.)

It would have been easy to write off Williams at that point as a flash in the pan, overrated early in his career by the [halo effect](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halo_effect) of running the offense for a team that made a few moderately deep postseason runs. But Williams defied that easy story line, bouncing back with a [fantastic 2012-13 performance](http://bkref.com/tiny/vxh4n) that was vital to the Nets’ return to the postseason.

It was the kind of performance the Nets will need this season if they’re to stay afloat in an increasingly competitive Eastern Conference. But in order to get it, Williams must be healthy, and he must play to the potential he’s flashed all too infrequently since becoming a member of the Nets. — *Neil Paine*
