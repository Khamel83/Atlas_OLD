It’s the summer, and so we see the usual uptick in coverage of
[shark](http://web.archive.org/web/20250704034524/https://www.foxbusiness.com/features/killer-
wave-shark-encounters-east-coast-beaches)
[attacks](http://web.archive.org/web/20250704034524/https://www.cnn.com/2019/07/14/us/florida-
shark-attack-trnd/index.html) and other relatively rare events is in the news.
This is one of our favorite topics, especially how disproportionate the
coverage can be. This contributes to people forming a model of the world that
is wildly inaccurate, with a lack of proportionality, and an over-emphasis on
recent, emotional events.

Instead of the usual media panic, via [Teton Gravity
Research](http://web.archive.org/web/20250704034524/https://www.tetongravity.com/story/news/your-
chances-of-dying-ranked-by-sport-and-activity) comes a more sober analysis of
mortality statistics. I have not vetted each data point (Some are confusing),
but I am curious if any of the data heads can explain/disprove this approach.
(Thanks, Pat!)
