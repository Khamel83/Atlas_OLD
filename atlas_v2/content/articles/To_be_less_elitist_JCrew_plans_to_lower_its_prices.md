A decade ago, J.Crew was a [defining
force](http://web.archive.org/web/20250719092625/https://qz.com/949498/jenna-
lyons-is-leaving-j-crew-but-the-jenna-lyons-era-of-american-fashion-is-not-
over) in the way Americans dressed. In 2008, its colorful, unfussy prep
reached a milestone, when Michelle Obama wore one of the brand’s yellow
cardigans on _The Tonight Show with Jay Leno_. That cardigan [signified
accessibility](http://web.archive.org/web/20250719092625/https://qz.com/887287/the-
inspiring-story-that-michelle-obama-told-about-american-fashion-in-nine-
outfits). Everyone could—and did—wear J.Crew, up to and including the first
lady of the United States.

J.Crew has since fallen dramatically from that position. For more than two
years now, sales at stores open at least a year have been dropping. Its image
has changed too. Once accessible, J.Crew now believes it has become too
“elitist,” as CEO Mickey Drexler framed it in a recent interview with the
[Wall Street
Journal](http://web.archive.org/web/20250719092625/https://www.wsj.com/articles/j-crews-
big-miss-how-technology-transformed-retail-1495636817?mod=e2tw) (paywall).

As J.Crew does all it can to stabilize its business—some analysts fear that it
won’t be able to [pay off its $2 billion of
debt](http://web.archive.org/web/20250719092625/http://www.reuters.com/article/us-
jcrew-debtrestructuring-blackstone-idUSKBN17X1KD)—it’s working to counter the
problem by lowering prices and changing the way it markets itself. It’s
already slashing price tags on around 300 items, and using data analytics to
determine the best prices for its offerings.

The retailer also wants to return to being the brand for everyone it once was.
The general feeling among former fans is that it got too eccentric in its
product and image, especially the styling of its beloved catalogs. The blame
is usually put on former creative director Jenna Lyons, who guided the brand’s
ascendance, but kept pushing it toward more [fashion-forward
territory](http://web.archive.org/web/20250719092625/https://qz.com/949498/jenna-
lyons-is-leaving-j-crew-but-the-jenna-lyons-era-of-american-fashion-is-not-
over). Shoppers
[complained](http://web.archive.org/web/20250719092625/https://thehairpin.com/an-
open-letter-to-jenna-lyons-23c9b5c9c771) that prices got too high, climbing to
hundreds of dollars for an item such as a cashmere skirt, and the cool,
creative look didn’t match their ordinary lifestyles.

“We gave a perception of being a higher-priced company than we were—in our
catalog, online and in our general presentation,” Drexler told the Journal.
“Very big mistake.” In April, J.Crew shook up its organization, [parting ways
with
Lyons](http://web.archive.org/web/20250719092625/https://qz.com/948798/jenna-
lyons-is-out-at-j-crew) and cutting about 250 jobs, primarily from its
headquarters.

It’s also looking to cheaper sourcing options [than
China](http://web.archive.org/web/20250719092625/https://qz.com/431747/us-
fashion-companies-are-starting-to-look-beyond-china-for-sourcing-apparel) and
trying to speed up how quickly it can get products into stores. Quicker,
cheaper fast-fashion competitors have [dominated the
market](http://web.archive.org/web/20250719092625/https://qz.com/625930/gaps-
biggest-problem-in-one-grim-chart) for several years, and J.Crew admits it
didn’t foresee how much speed and price would influence what shoppers did—and
didn’t—buy.

Accessibility, in both look and price, is J.Crew’s goal now, as it tries to
convince people to return to it for their clothes, and without waiting for one
of the sales J.Crew seems to have perpetually going on. It won’t be easy.
Shoppers [expect
everything](http://web.archive.org/web/20250719092625/https://qz.com/820829/global-
consumers-are-no-longer-willing-to-pay-full-price-because-cheap-fashion)
cheaper than ever, and J.Crew faces [tough
competition](http://web.archive.org/web/20250719092625/https://qz.com/702286/primark-
makes-even-walmart-look-pricey-on-clothes) from fast fashion.

Things have changed a lot since that yellow cardigan.
