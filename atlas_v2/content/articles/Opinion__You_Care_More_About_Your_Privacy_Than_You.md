## Tip of the Week: How to Sext Securely

 _Today’s reader Q. and A. doubles as our handy tip of the week and it’s a
doozy._

**Q:** If you want to be intimate with your partner, say you are in long-
distance relationship, what’s the best way (assuming you don’t want to
publicly broadcast or “monetize” via PornHub, for example)?

**A:** I [posed this
question](http://web.archive.org/web/20240917160925/https://twitter.com/cwarzel/status/1136288106804465664)
on Twitter last week. And while it might seem like a niche question, you don’t
have to be “getting intimate” with a loved one to want assurance that your
conversations, photos and videos aren’t intercepted or vulnerable to being
shared by a third party. Also, there’s some [decent evidence out
there](http://web.archive.org/web/20240917160925/http://drexel.edu/now/archive/2015/August/Sexting-
Study/) suggesting that there’s quite a bit of [DM-
sliding](http://web.archive.org/web/20240917160925/https://www.cosmopolitan.com/sex-
love/a25671891/slide-into-dms-definition/) going on, so it makes sense to
practice safe sexting, etc.

It feels appropriate to start with what’s now my [standard
caveat](http://web.archive.org/web/20240917160925/https://www.nytimes.com/2019/05/14/opinion/data-
privacy.html?rref=collection%2Fbyline%2Fcharlie-
warzel&action=click&contentCollection=undefined&region=stream&module=stream_unit&version=latest&contentPlacement=7&pgtype=collection),
from a security professional: “Whatever you do online, operate with the
assumption that every picture, email, communication will be one day made
public on a big bulletin board.” Now, that’s not to shame or prudishly suggest
that one shouldn’t attempt romantic escapades in cyberspace. That’s your call.
Still, the internet is held together with duct tape, and if you’re serious
about security, it’s good to know the risks up front.

First, [Vice has a guide to sexting from
2017](http://web.archive.org/web/20240917160925/https://www.vice.com/en_us/article/mb3nd4/how-
to-sext-securely-safely-what-apps-to-use-sexting), which you might want to
take a good look at. Among the important considerations in the piece: setting
clear partner-consent guidelines, both for the activity itself and for how
you’re going to save (or not save) the videos, photos, text that you generate.
For example, even if the chat is secure, it’s pretty difficult to be 100
percent certain that the person on the other end isn’t screenshotting what
you’re sending over. Ultimately, as the Vice guide notes, the best way to
protect your privacy while sexting is to do it with individuals you trust and
by setting clear guidelines and expectations for the experience.

When I posed this question on Twitter, I got a fair amount of snark (“A shared
Google doc, as the Founders intended,” one user replied) but also a few good
suggestions. For live video, The Signal app has encrypted video calling. Its
end-to-end encryption offers a reliable amount of security that no third
parties will be listening in (but again, it’s worth repeating that you can
never control who might be in the other room on the other end of the device).
The same goes for the iPhone’s FaceTime, which offers encryption baked into
the video calls. Skype also has a “Private Conversations” feature.
