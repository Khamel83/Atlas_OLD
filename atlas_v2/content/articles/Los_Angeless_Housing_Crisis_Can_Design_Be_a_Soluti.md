# Los Angeles Has a Housing Crisis. Can Design Help?

As Angelenos grapple with ways to address affordable housing, density and
homelessness, a new architecture competition looks to low-rise solutions.

Louisa Van Leer Architecture and Antonio Castillo won first place in an
architecture competition in Los Angeles for their “Green Alley Housing”
proposal in the Subdivision category.Credit...Louisa Van Leer Architecture

Published June 22, 2021Updated Oct. 15, 2021

Can
[California](http://web.archive.org/web/20250720092448/https://www.nytimes.com/2021/08/26/business/california-
duplex-senate-bill-9.html)’s biggest city — and possibly America’s least[
affordable](http://web.archive.org/web/20250720092448/https://eyeonhousing.org/2020/02/los-
angeles-takes-top-spot-as-nations-least-affordable-housing-market-2/) one —
redesign its way out of the housing crisis?

That’s the question a city-sponsored architecture competition called “[Low-
Rise: Housing Ideas for Los
Angeles](http://web.archive.org/web/20250720092448/https://lowrise.la/)”
poses. Winners were announced the other day.

The city’s [housing
problem](http://web.archive.org/web/20250720092448/https://www.nytimes.com/2021/07/13/magazine/los-
angeles-homelessness.html) has been decades in the making. Half a century ago,
Los Angeles was a booming metropolis [zoned for up to 10 million
people](http://web.archive.org/web/20250720092448/https://slate.com/business/2021/05/los-
angeles-low-rise-contest-housing-crisis-affordability-missing-middle.html). It
pioneered low-rise density, with fourplexes, bungalow courts like Horatio West
Court, Irving Gill’s modernist masterpiece, and dingbats: those campy,
stuccoed walk-ups on stilts, bearing make-believe names like Casa Bella and
Camelot.

But the trajectory of housing in Los Angeles during the later years of the
last century paralleled that of many other American cities where density came
to be equated with urban decline and white residents resorted to redlining and
other racially restrictive practices to keep out Black people and immigrants.
Slow-growth policies, in Los Angeles’s case fueled especially by fears of
Manhattanization, slammed on the brakes for multifamily housing construction.
Single-family zoning became the norm. Leaning into its freeway system and an
environmentally oblivious mythology of the desert as an endless terrain for
exurban expansion, Los Angeles, by 2010, had shrunk its zoning envelope to
[4.3 million
people](http://web.archive.org/web/20250720092448/https://escholarship.org/uc/item/6k64g20f).

Brooklyn-based Vonn Weisenberger placed first in the Corners category for
“California Branch Housing.”Credit...Vonn Weisenberger

Today, the region has the fewest homes per capita of any metro area [in the
country](http://web.archive.org/web/20250720092448/https://1p08d91kd0c03rlxhmhtydpr-
wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/05/Los-
Angeles_Housing_Report_2021-HNR-1.pdf), and the second-lowest rental vacancy
rate of any major metropolis. More than 75 percent of residential land in the
city, representing more than 400,000 parcels, is zoned for single-family
houses, according to Christopher Hawthorne, the city’s chief design officer.
