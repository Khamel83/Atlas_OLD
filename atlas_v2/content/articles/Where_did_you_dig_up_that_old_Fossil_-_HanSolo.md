And given these poor results (and some activist pressure that we’ll delve into more below), there’s been a significant changing of the guard at Fossil.

The company’s CEO, **Kosta Kartsotis**, stepped down [upon 4Q’23 results](https://substack.com/redirect/c2636ff0-9065-4e3c-95bd-f78d53d3f2d5?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) and COO, **Jeffrey Boyer**, stepped up as interim CEO. Three months later, CFO, **Sunil Doshi**, [resigned](https://substack.com/redirect/7c533101-ff37-4980-b79f-83241ba7a339?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) and **Ankura Consulting Group LLC** affiliated **Andrew Skobe** subsequently became interim CFO. Mr. Skobe’s **LinkedIn** page highlights his “*[p]roficiency with corporate restructurings, financings.*”

Things have been shaking at the board level too. Two new independent directors joined the ten member board: **Eugene Davis** and **Pamela Corrie**.

Let’s take a look at these two new directors

According to the [press release](https://substack.com/redirect/f7c1bd9d-9c1c-4afc-ab45-aa3a743c97d7?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8), in the past five years, Mr. Davis has been on the board of **Aeromexico**, **Babylon Holdings Limited**, **Bluestem Group Inc**, **GTT Communications**, **MediaMath Holdings Inc**, **Loyalty Ventures Inc**, **PGX Holdings Inc**, and **Skillsoft Corp**.

On July 9, 2024, Dallas-based **[Tubular Synergy Group LP](https://substack.com/redirect/96c7c371-b8ba-4a2b-a3fc-4b23bb918a3c?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)** (“Tubular”) and affiliate **[OCTG Connections LLC](https://substack.com/redirect/3dae09bb-4bec-400c-a6be-04b770c06046?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)** (“OCTG,” together, the “debtors”) filed chapter 11 bankruptcy cases in the Northern District of Texas (**Judge****Everett**). The debtors are a sales, marketing and supply chain services distributor of casings, tubing and line pipe for the onshore oil and gas industry. They’re looking for  a “good, old-fashioned restructuring,” as **Holland (“Holly”) O’Neill**, an attorney at debtor counsel **[Foley & Lardner LLP](https://substack.com/redirect/3e053ae0-9661-4b8e-8e3c-5ccbc55a367e?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)**, phrased it at the first day hearing. They expect to file a plan in the coming months and seek to emerge as a “leaner, stronger” company,  according to the first-day declaration of [CEO](https://substack.com/redirect/3c4f9f89-1a80-4f81-9548-92a9114518f7?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) **[W. Byron Dunn](https://substack.com/redirect/3c4f9f89-1a80-4f81-9548-92a9114518f7?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)**.

Dunn, a native Texan, veteran of fifty years in the oil patch and former CEO of **Lone Star Steel**, founded the debtors in ‘08 after **U.S. Steel** (**$X**) bought Lone Star.  Tubular affiliate OCTG (an acronym meaning “oil country tubular goods”) holds various patents,  specifically, a proprietary pipe connection for use in horizontal drilling. 

The debtors’ capital structure is straightforward: a senior secured revolver via**[CIT Northbridge Credit LLC](https://substack.com/redirect/7726ed84-8528-4996-8777-a7dbd34f1cad?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)** (“CITNB”). CIT supported [a ‘23 refinancing](https://substack.com/redirect/98514820-1048-4d80-bd2c-9591b4433066?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8), which saw the facility upsized to $45mm.  Dunn’s narrative begins on March 4, ‘24, which happens to be the day CIT *“temporarily increased”* the revolver commitment to $55mm and established a *“Special Advance Loan”* not to exceed $5.5mm in aggregate. Why? Dunn’s declaration does not say. But on April 15, the debtors received a notice of default, the first of three(!).  CIT claimed non-compliance with the loan agreement and declared loans *“in excess of the Borrowing Base immediately due and payable.”* That works out to about $39.32mm. CIT commenced a cash-flow sweep. Dunn says this *“severely constrained”* liquidity and resulted in *“severe disruptions”* to the debtors’ operations, and we do not think he’s bullsh\*tting us. 

Another notice of default landed on June 18. This one alleged non-compliance with six different sections of the loan agreement, including “*...alleged misrepresentation pursuant to Section 4(c) of the First Amendment.*” The third and final arrived ten days later: non-compliance with two more sections. Basically the debtors, according to CIT, tripped every section of the loan agreement other than the preamble, 😝.

What’s the cause of all the trouble? Dunn is silent. We’re left to wonder. Have exogenous circumstances pushed some ratio to a level shrieking disaster? The debtors successfully navigated periods of worse stress in the oil and gas market, Dunn says. They serve customers not just in Texas, but all the oil-producing states: North Dakota, New Mexico, Oklahoma, Colorado, Mississippi, Arkansas, Alabama, Pennsylvania, and Louisiana. It’s not like they’re trapped in a non-producing basin. Of course the entire industry is going the way of the dinosaur, thanks to the vaunted *energy transition!* But that’s . .  . *a bit in the future*, perhaps? In the meantime, crude is up 15% this year. Does the one-rig drop in the [Baker-Hughes Onshore U.S. Rig Count](https://substack.com/redirect/1ca3673b-5a62-4976-a73a-27e75ddf54e1?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) augur a looming collapse? No; increased per-well productivity has rendered rig count increasingly moot. So what is it? Covid? Supply chain? The impact of AI? Last year, Dunn said this in the press release announcing the refi/upsize: *"Demand for our tubular products has been steadfast while inflationary pressure has altered our costs, which compelled us to upsize our credit facility."* Hmm, that’s interesting: the upsize was to address runaway costs? Have we read that right? But that was *last year.* Very 15 minutes ago. People laughed at **[Paul Krugman](https://substack.com/redirect/d7a7e32a-86b5-43b4-990a-30d238330830?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)** when he said the U.S. economy had attained the “goldilocks” state of declining price pressures without job losses. Well, haters, core CPI rose a measly 0.1% in June. Speculation, of course: it may be something else entirely. CITNB, in its [reservation of rights](https://substack.com/redirect/b28d5d23-30ff-45e1-97eb-274a517872ee?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) related to various debtor motions, mentioned that in the weeks prior to the filing, *“...the parties were discussing CITNB’s willingness to take ownership of its collateral in light of these chronic defaults, likely through a foreclosure, which CITNB was preparing to effectuate.”* The debtors knew of CITNB’s plans. CITNB, for its part, “*...was unaware that the Debtors were preparing to file these Chapter 11 Cases until the Petition Date.*” You’ve gotta love that automatic stay: stopped CITNB’s machinations in their tracks. 

The debtors obtained all requested relief on an interim basis at a two-and-a-half hour first-day hearing on July 11, 2024. It was not without drama. The cash collateral motion filed July 9, 2024 by the debtors (docket 9) was — despite the bargeloads of “adequate assurance” promised CITNB — of a non-consensual nature. Something of a shocker to CITNB, don’t you think? **Winston & Strawn LLP**, counsel for CITNB, and Foley for the debtors hashed out a deal that was largely complete by the hearing. The interim order (docket 49) grants the debtors the use of cash collateral until a second interim hearing scheduled for July 25, 2024. The order specifies the following (emphasis added): 

> *The Debtors are authorized to use the Lender’s Cash Collateral to fund payroll expenses and critical ordinary course post-petition operating expenses to the extent they are required to be paid during the Cash Collateral Period; provided that during the Cash Collateral Period, the Debtors shall provide the Lender with a report by 5:00 p.m. CT each day of all payments they intend to make on the next business day and **such payments will be deemed to be in compliance with this Interim Order and can be made by the Debtors unless the Lender provides a written objection** (by email from the Lenders to the Debtors, copying counsel) to such payments by 10:00 a.m. CT the following day.* (emphasis added)

A negative notice approval! Cool, huh? The parties also discussed the debtors’ need to pay critical vendors. A certain yard refused to release pipe to a customer of the debtors unless the debtors paid its bill. Dunn, under oath, testified that failure to deliver would result in *“big demerits.”* Critical indeed! The Court ultimately agreed to authorize the debtors to make the payment, but to first use “best reasonable efforts” to find an alternate vendor. It’s as if someone finally remembered the original “critical vendor” standard, go figure, 🤔.

The debtors are represented by Foley and Lardner LLP (Holland N. O’Neil, Stephen A. Jones). CITNB is represented by Winston & Strawn LLP (Jordan Klein, LeElle Slifer, Greg Gartland, Amila Golic). We reckon they’ll both be working awfully hard over the next several weeks to determine what, exactly, this filing might ultimately achieve that would result in something different than what CITNB originally contemplated. 🤷‍♀️
