TV executives have seethed in the past two years over a frequently repeated
remark from Sheryl Sandberg, Facebook’s chief operating officer, who said the
social network has a Super Bowl on mobile every day in terms of the people it
can reach. While the size of audiences on Facebook and YouTube are undeniable,
there is fierce debate over the value of video views on such platforms,
particularly when the ads take up only, say, half of the screen, or appear for
a couple of seconds without sound.

“From a consumer experience, we all know what it’s like to experience a Super
Bowl as you have people over your home, family, friends, or you go somewhere
and you’re watching this communal experience and you’re talking about the show
and you’re yelling and screaming and holding your breath and talking about the
commercials,” Ms. Yaccarino said. “That’s a Super Bowl.”

She held up her phone and exaggeratedly swiped the screen without breaking eye
contact. “Does this look like a Super Bowl?” She kept swiping. “This is not a
Super Bowl.”

NBC, it should be noted, will air the game next year.

While much has been said about technology companies coming for TV advertising
dollars, Ms. Yaccarino said the companies were “really coming for TV content.”
She pointed to Twitter’s and Amazon’s deals with the N.F.L. and YouTube’s new
ad-supported shows with stars like Ryan Seacrest and Ellen DeGeneres.

“Really, you just can’t snap your fingers and invent that,” she said of
premium content.

And that is why she believes her focus on aligning with tech companies and
reaching a large number of people on a variety of platforms will pay
dividends.

“It’s about real audiences, real people who buy stuff, because they
experienced your content all throughout the day,” she continued. “If we can do
that, then we can really compete with the Google, Facebooks, Twitter, etc.,
etc., or whoever’s next of the world.”
