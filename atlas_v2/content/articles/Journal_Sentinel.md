# Bernstein: Gov. Scott Walker's proposal is detrimental to the health of
Wisconsin

* * *

##

Rebecca Bernstein | Milwaukee Journal Sentinel

Gov. Scott Walker, in response to Health and Human Services Secretary Tom
Price’s request for states to “innovate” their Medicaid programs, has laid out
a proposal that would decrease access to health care for our most vulnerable,
humiliate and marginalize those with addiction, and increase government
bureaucracy.

The core elements of the proposal include charging premiums, co-payments for
unnecessary ER visits, work requirements for non-disabled adults, maximum
enrollment periods, and, most sensationally, drug testing of Medicaid
recipients.

As a family physician, my greatest concern is that these policies would harm
the patients I see in my practice, and others like them across the state, and
here’s why.

Financial barriers to health care are a key contributor to health care
disparities. The idea that charging the poor to access care will improve
health by increasing “personal responsibility” is simply not borne out in the
evidence. Even adjusting for personal behaviors such as diet, smoking, alcohol
use and physical activity, socioeconomic status remains a key predictor for
mortality.

I agree that unnecessary ER utilization is costly and can disrupt continuity
of care, but, in my experience, these visits often occur when challenging work
schedules and child care obligations make accessing primary care providers
impossible. I believe innovative solutions promote more primary care access
and paid sick leave, not penalties for those who can’t afford them.

The proposal would penalize those who don’t pay their new premiums or ER co-
pays with un-enrollment. Walker fails to grasp that those who don’t stay on
top of this payment are likely overwhelmed with the myriad daily challenges of
poverty, not disinterested in their health.

As a family physician, I frequently struggle to help patients with chronic
conditions consumed by the urgency of the present crisis; long-term
complications are too remote to register against present needs. Imagine a
diabetic patient who wants to incorporate more physical activity but is
preoccupied with caring for a child with special needs, an aging parent, and a
neighborhood in which she doesn’t feel safe. She may lack a bank account or
credit card. I do not believe that paying a monthly Badgercare premium would
be a “helpful” incentive to improve her health. If that isn’t the goal, what
is?

Walker may not appreciate that the consequences he proposes for those who miss
payments have actual health consequences. Losing your health insurance is not
like getting a time-out. Churn, or interruptions in coverage, increases the
rate of hospitalizations for chronic conditions such as diabetes and asthma
and the rate of unintended pregnancy. There is even evidence that intermittent
treatment of high blood pressure may increase the risk of stroke compared to
consistently elevated blood pressure.

In Indiana, whose Medicaid program is considered a model for innovation, only
a quarter of Medicaid recipients were enrolled continuously for 12 months. My
goal is to help my patients reach and maintain a state of good health; less
consistent health insurance is a step in the wrong direction.

Finally, the proposal includes punitive drug screening as a precondition of
Medicaid enrollment. In the face of an opioid epidemic, financial barriers to
treatment and shortage of providers are major obstacles to receiving addiction
treatment. Thousands of Wisconsin residents on waiting lists are eager to
begin treatment. To give this policy the benefit of the doubt, its premises
are that those with addiction are more likely to utilize Medicaid and less
likely to be employed, that our current crisis results from inadequate
identification of patients with addiction, and that comprehensive health care
is not helpful for those with untreated addiction.

None of these premises are based on evidence. What we do know from similar
programs implemented for other public benefits are that they are costly to
implement and only “succeed” so far as they cause some with addiction to avoid
the program and indignity in many others. This is dangerous and
counterproductive in the face of a public health crisis.

Resources would be better spent providing screening for substance abuse in
primary care settings, where it has been shown to be most effective, and
timely access to addiction treatment for those who desperately want it.

In my professional opinion, Walker’s proposal would be detrimental to the
health of our state. I wish the governor were held to the physicians’ sacred
principle to “first do no harm.”

 _Rebecca Bernstein is a family physician practicing in Milwaukee._
