The final countdown has begun to watch the [rare solar eclipse](/science-and-
health/2017/6/15/15804336/2017-solar-eclipse-map-united-states-nasa), which
will cut across the US on August 21. And if you want to experience it in some
of the most beautiful spots in the country, you’re in luck: There are [20
national parks and nine national
trails](https://www.nps.gov/subjects/naturalphenomena/parksinpath.htm) in the
path of the totality.​

Artist and educator [Tyler Nordgren](http://www.tylernordgren.com/biography/),
a professor of physics and astronomy at the University of Redlands in
California​, saw an opportunity to use the National Parks to educate people
about space science.

And so he decided to put his artistic talent to use for the upcoming solar
eclipse. His posters are inspired by the art used in midcentury [Works
Progress
Administration](http://www.wikiwand.com/en/Works_Progress_Administration)
materials. (Check out [these WPA-style
posters](/2016/2/24/11107908/department-of-energy-posters) from the Department
of Energy celebrating the US energy revolution.) And they’re pretty cool.

Nordgren first starting drawing professionally when he made the illustration
for the cover of his book. He once teamed up with the NPS on a [poster
campaign](http://www.tylernordgren.com/milky-way-posters/) to “See the Milky
Way” where “Half the park is after dark” — a slogan he came up with on his
own.

Most of the US will see a partial eclipse on August 21. If you want the full
experience, you’ll need to be within the 60-mile-wide path of the totality —
when the sky goes dark for a few minutes in the middle of the day. Luckily,
NASA has mapped out the [best places](/science-and-
health/2017/6/15/15804336/2017-solar-eclipse-map-united-states-nasa) to be for
this sight in August.

As for Nordgren, he’ll be watching the eclipse from right outside the main
headquarters of [John Day Fossil Beds National
Monument](https://www.nps.gov/joda/index.htm) in Oregon. While the location
will be great for viewing, Nordgren told me via email, the state has a lot to
get ready for.

“The state is very worried about the possibility of 100,000 people descending
on that sparsely populated part of the state, getting in massive 8-hour long
traffic jams, and having some newbie-camper in the forest accidentally
starting a major forest fire that traps tens of thousands,” he writes. “So it
could be great, or it could be a major apocalypse, in which case, maybe the
ancients will have been right about eclipses all along. Fortunately, Oregon
officials are hoping for the best but planning for the worst.”

If you’re not up for braving the eclipse-catching crowds in the path of the
totality, you’ll still be able to catch a partial eclipse from [anywhere in
the US](https://eclipse2017.nasa.gov/).
