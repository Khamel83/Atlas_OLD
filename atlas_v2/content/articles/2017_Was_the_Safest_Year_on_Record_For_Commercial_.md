Billions of passengers took to the skies on commercial flights in 2017, and
almost every one of them made it safely to their destination. According to
Dutch aviation consultants from To70, [2017 was the safest year on record for
commercial aviation
worldwide](http://web.archive.org/web/20240214181744/http://to70.com/to70s-civil-
aviation-safety-review-2017/).

To70 searched through the records of commercial flights worldwide for all
aircraft with a takeoff weight of more than 5,700 kilograms (12,566 pounds),
which is less than a third of the [weight of the smallest Embraer
ERJ](http://web.archive.org/web/20240214181744/https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embraer_ERJ_family)
used in regional operations by US airlines. The group found just 111 accidents
in 2017, with only two of those leading to a loss of life:

  1. _An Embraer Brasilia lost control in flight in Angola after reportedly suffering an engine failure._
  2. _A Czech-built Let 410 crashed on landing at Nelken in Russia._

The total loss of life across those two fatal accidents: 13 people.

This exceptionally low number of fatal accidents calculates to 0.06 fatal
accidents per million flights in 2017. Put another way, the chance of a
commercial flight being involved in a fatal accident during 2017 was 1 in
approximately 17 million.

Also, plenty of planes hit objects in the air — if history has held true, [1
in 2,000 flights incurred a bird
strike](http://web.archive.org/web/20240214181744/https://thepointsguy.com/2017/08/robot-
birds-end-bird-strikes-airplanes/), including [this noteworthy birdstrike in
Miami](http://web.archive.org/web/20240214181744/https://thepointsguy.com/2017/11/video-
bird-strike-aa-miami/). A Virgin Australia flight had the remarkable
experience of [striking both a bird and a rabbit in the
air](http://web.archive.org/web/20240214181744/https://thepointsguy.com/2017/10/flight-
hit-by-bird-and-rabbit/). And there was whatever damaged the nosecone of the
[Delta charter flight carrying the Oklahoma City
Thunder](http://web.archive.org/web/20240214181744/https://thepointsguy.com/2017/10/nba-
thunder-charter-hit-by-object/) — a bird strike, strike by another object or a
structural failing of the nose cone.

Then there were the on-ground issues. In May, a [massive IT outage cancelled
over 700 British Airways
flights](http://web.archive.org/web/20240214181744/https://thepointsguy.com/2017/06/british-
airways-outage-contractor/), affecting over 75,000 travelers. During the fall,
floods and hurricanes cancelled thousands of flights and even led to the
[complete evacuation of aircraft from
Florida](http://web.archive.org/web/20240214181744/https://thepointsguy.com/2017/09/jetblue-
florida-evacuation/). And just a couple of weeks ago, a power outage in
Atlanta crippled flights out of Delta's key hub.

So, while air travel wasn't always smooth during 2017, it was still remarkably
safe. [For the eighth year in a
row](http://web.archive.org/web/20240214181744/https://thepointsguy.com/2017/01/dying-
airplane-crash-zero-2016/), there wasn't a single death from an accident on a
US flagged commercial flight. And one of the worst injuries on a US-based
aircraft was [dealt by Chicago airport security in response to an overbooked
United
flight](http://web.archive.org/web/20240214181744/https://thepointsguy.com/2017/04/united-
passenger-forcibly-removed/). In the Americas, there were [more deaths from
jet
blast](http://web.archive.org/web/20240214181744/https://thepointsguy.com/2017/07/tourist-
dies-st-martin/) than commercial aviation accidents.
