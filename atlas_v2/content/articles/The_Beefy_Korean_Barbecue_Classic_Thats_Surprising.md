Walk the streets of the west mid-30s in Manhattan and you'll pass rows and
rows of Korean restaurants and markets and food courts, lit up and stretching
from the ground floor toward the sky. It's Koreatown you've arrived in, where
every storefront you pass smells strongly and looks wonderfully of all the
good stuff: roaring grills covered in meat, [sour-sweet
kimchi](http://web.archive.org/web/20240619150644/https://food52.com/blog/13681-how-
to-make-any-kind-of-kimchi-without-a-recipe), bowls of rice cakes and
[bibimbap](http://web.archive.org/web/20240619150644/https://food52.com/blog/13901-how-
to-make-bibimbap-without-a-recipe), and platters of squid and shrimp.

It's one of those places where you can lose yourself in culture without having
to hitch a flight, a special perk to the cities in which these enclaves exist.

**One of the most ubiquitous dishes you'd encounter is _bulgogi_ , or fire
beef, sliced thinly and sometimes grilled on the tabletop grill you'll find at
Korean restaurants.** Bulgogi is, as Deuki Hong and Matt Rodbard put it in the
cookbook
[Koreatown](http://web.archive.org/web/20240619150644/https://www.amazon.com/Koreatown-
Cookbook-Deuki-Hong/dp/0804186138?tag=food52-20), "the workhorse of Korean
barbecue." You'll often see this spicy steak in recipes made with Ribeye,
Sirloin, or Flat Iron because of how well these cuts take to marinating.

In many Koreatown restaurants, you'll commonly grill your own bulgogi among
friends and family on a stove in a tabletop—but at
[Insa](http://web.archive.org/web/20240619150644/http://www.insabrooklyn.com/),
a cavernous Korean barbecue spot located not in Koreatown but on a sleepy
street in Gowanus, Brooklyn, the
[bulgogi](http://web.archive.org/web/20240619150644/https://food52.com/recipes/59888-insa-
s-bulgogi) is grilled Flatiron steak that's been marinated in soy, explains
Michael Stokes, the Chef de Cuisine. Insa's cooks prepare their bulgogi in the
kitchen because it can easily get too dry on the table. "We usually cook it
that way...if there is any excess marinade, you can add that to the pan three-
quarters of the way through cooking," Michael explains. "A splash of water at
the end can also help." He adds that you shouldn't be aiming for
"caramelization" on the meat when you cook it, though.

Bulgogi is sometimes served alongside _banchan_ (Korean for side dish), and
maybe lettuce to wrap it all up and eat in a bite or two. Banchan itself has a
[fascinating and intertwined
history](http://web.archive.org/web/20240619150644/https://food52.com/blog/17598-the-
funky-flavorful-sides-a-korean-meal-wouldn-t-be-complete-without) with Korean
cuisine—an essential in everyday Korean cooking. Altogether, it can add up to
an impressive feast for a group, one that should be completed with a lot of
beer and a little soju (a clear Korean spirit).
