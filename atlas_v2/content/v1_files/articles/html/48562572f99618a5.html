Jeb: GOP 'stupid' to ignore Hispanics
By: Carrie Budoff Brown
January 14, 2011 12:50 PM EST

CORAL GABLES, Fla. — Republicans would be “incredibly stupid” to ignore the fastest-growing population of voters, former Florida Gov. Jeb Bush told a conference of Hispanic Republicans Friday, particularly if they hope to recapture the White House.

But missing from the opening speeches of the two-day gathering was any direct mention of immigration, the issue that has long stymied Republicans from making lasting gains with Hispanics.

Bush opened the Hispanic Leadership Network conference with a four-point plan for elected officials to expand the party’s reach: Be mindful of tone, embrace a broad agenda, appoint Hispanics to boards and judicial positions, and recruit qualified Hispanics to run for office.

“If you believe in the conservative philosophy as I do, it would be incredibly stupid over the long haul to ignore the burgeoning Hispanic vote,” Bush said. “They will be the swing voters in the swing states.

“So if we want to elect a center-right president of the United States, it seems to me that you should be concerned about places like New Mexico, Arizona, Florida, Texas — places [where], but for the Hispanic vote, elections are won or lost.”

Bush hinted at the divisive immigration debate, saying Republicans needed to adopt a “civility and tone that draws people toward our cause rather than rejects them.”

“We share very common values, but if you send signal of ‘them’ and ‘us,’ then you’re not going to get the desired results,” Bush said, drawing strong applause. “I watch TV; sometimes I’m turned off by the tone, even though I might agree with a particular view.”

But that was as far as Bush went in touching on immigration, an issue on which he and other organizers of the conference diverge from the hard-line foes in their party.

The conference took a deeper look at immigration during a panel discussion led by Sen. John Cornyn (R-Texas), who criticized Democrats for failing to deliver on their campaign promises to reform the immigration system. They muscled through a health care overhaul, but declined to put the same energy into immigration reform, Cornyn said.

“It is easy to be skeptical about the prospects of immigration reform when both the White House and the United States Senate have been in hands of Democratic leaders who make promises they have not fulfilled,” Cornyn said.

Cornyn reiterated his commitment to overhauling immigration, saying a broken system serves neither “our interests or our values.”

“We can’t simply kick the can down the road,” Cornyn said.

The broader focus of the conference reflects organizers’ philosophy, that Hispanics are not just interested in immigration, but jobs, trade, education and social values.

“We gather here today in hopes of strengthening the bond between the Hispanic community and the center-right movement,” former Sen. Norm Coleman (R-Minn.), chief executive officer of the American Action Network, which organized the event. “Most of what we will focus on is the fundamentals to all American families: jobs.”

Former President George W. Bush taped a video for the conference, saying Republicans share values with Hispanics: entrepreneurship, importance of service, faith and family, and life.

Other officials who taped statements included House Majority Leader Eric Cantor (R-Va.), Newt Gingrich, Sen. Jon Kyl (R-Ariz.), Sen. John McCain (R-Ariz.), National Republican Campaign Committee Chairman Pete Sessions (R-Texas) and Rep. Tom Price (R-Ga.).

None talked about immigration, focusing instead on their support of small businesses, limited government and shared social values.

“Outreach is not about currying favor to get votes,” McCain said, in a dig at Democrats. “Hispanic voters are central to the future of this great country.”

Asked whether Republicans can make meaningful gains unless they take immigration off the table, Coleman said it was entirely possible.

“You are focused on D.C. chatter,” Coleman said in an interview. “My proposition is that jobs are the most important issue.”

Coleman cited the historic gains that Hispanic Republicans made last year, rising to governor in New Mexico and Nevada, winning a Senate seat in Florida and taking seven House seats.

“In spite of the Washington-centric focus on immigration as some barrier, that is simply not the reality in heartland America,” Coleman said.

More than 600 people are attending the conference at the posh Biltmore Hotel here, a group that includes local elected Latino officials, business leaders, foreign dignitaries and students.

Dana Perino, the former White House press secretary under George W. Bush, will lead a panel on media and messaging.

Former Commerce Secretary Carlos Gutierrez will lead a panel on trade with former Colombian President Alvaro Uribe. Bush will open a panel on education.

Former Minnesota Gov. Tim Pawlenty, a possible Republican presidential candidate, is also scheduled to address the conference.
