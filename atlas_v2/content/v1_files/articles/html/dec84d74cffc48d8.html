How You Can Make the Most of 1 Day in Cairo

August 15, 2014
Miguel R. Quinones | Google+

It was February 2012. I was planning a trip to Istanbul and Dubai and had just purchased an open-jaw reservation that would start in Istanbul and end in Dubai. I still needed to find a way to get from Istanbul to Dubai.

I then realized that I could possibly squeeze in a two-day visit to another city in-between Istanbul and Dubai as part of our itinerary. As I looked at a map, I quickly identified a few options, but one stood out from the others: Cairo.

I had never been to Egypt. Usually, if travelling to Egypt, you would want to stay for more than two days. However, it had only been one year since the Egyptian Revolution of 2011, and there still was not even a government in place, so a two-day stay in Cairo made sense.

When I first told my wife about the possibility of including Cairo in our Istanbul/Dubai trip, her exact words were, “No, no, no, no.” Then she got up and left the room. I could not blame her. At the time, protesters were still getting killed in the streets of Cairo weekly. I knew I had a lot of work to do if I wanted to convince her that at the very least, this was not as crazy an idea as it sounded.

The first thing I needed to do was find a safe hotel alternative. The JW Marriott Cairo seemed like the perfect choice. It was not anywhere close to the city center, and it was only about ten minutes from the airport. Plus it seemed like a very nice and safe property.

Next, I needed to find a guide to take us around Cairo and Giza for the only full-day we would have in the area. After doing some research I settled on Mohamed Bahaa. From the first response I received from him I knew I had made the right choice. I told him we would be arriving in Cairo on a Friday evening and departing Cairo on Sunday morning a couple of days later, and that I would only have the Saturday in-between to visit as many of the major sights as possible. He told me that on a week day what I wanted to do in one day would have been extremely difficult, but on a Saturday there was a much better chance that I would be able to visit all the attractions I wanted to visit.

After making a reservation at the JW and exchanging a few emails with Mohamed, I decided to purchase the Istanbul-Cairo-Dubai ticket before even discussing the topic with my wife again, as I knew this plan was too good to not follow through. And in fact, when I told my wife what the plan was and that I had already purchased the plane tickets, let’s say she acquiesced and did not complain. The intrigue of visiting Cairo and Giza, even in such dangerous times, was pulling on her too.

After a two-hour delay (typical with EgyptAir), we arrived in Cairo on the evening of Friday, April 20, 2012. The JW Marriott Cairo representative was waiting for us before passport control. He escorted us through the visa and customs process and less than 15 minutes after touching down we were already in the hotel shuttle on our way to the airport.

At 7:45 a.m. the next morning, we met Mohamed at the lobby of our hotel. He went over the plan for the day with us and we were quickly on our way to Sakkara to visit the Step Pyramid, about an hour away.

Traffic in and around Cairo was like nothing I had ever seen before (or since). “Chaotic” would probably be the best word to describe it, but even that would be an understatement. Fortunately for us, Mohamed’s driver demonstrated excellent driving skills and the mini-van was extremely comfortable with powerful air-conditioning.

When we arrived in Sakkara, it felt like we had the whole area for ourselves. There was literally not another tourist there. Obviously, this was an unfortunate consequence of the problems Egypt was experiencing at the time, but we took advantage of having the whole place to ourselves.

From Sakkara we drove to Memphis. There were a few other tourists there, but it still felt like we had the whole area to ourselves.

Now it was time to visit Giza. Words cannot describe the feeling of standing in front of the Great Pyramid. Again, there were more camels than people.

Mohamed asked if we wanted to enter one of the pyramids. We had a choice between the Great Pyramid and the smallest of the three pyramids. We settled on the small pyramid as it was supposed to be less claustrophobic. It was really hot inside and there really is not much to see, but I still think it is a must to enter at least one of the pyramids just to be able to say that you did.

After walking around the pyramids for about an hour, we made the short drive to the Sphinx. We spent about 30-45 minutes in this area.

Following an underwhelming lunch experience in what seemed to be like a local fast food joint (our fault for not being clearer with Mohamed on the type of restaurant we wanted for lunch), we visited the Coptic Cairo area. It was kind of surreal to find such a Christian presence right in the middle of the city.

We then visited the Mosque and Madrasa of Sultan Hasan. Out of the all the mosques I have visited, this was certainly one of the most impressive, particularly considering it was almost 700 years old.

Mohamed then recommended we do something that was not part of our original schedule for the day since we were doing so well with time: a felucca ride on the Nile River. We accepted the recommendation and did not regret our decision. The ride lasted about 45 minutes. It was certainly the most peaceful time of our day. I think this is a must for visitors to Cairo as you get to escape the street chaos and see the city in a totally different perspective.

Once the felucca ride ended, we asked Mohamed to drive us around Tahrir Square, which had been the epicenter of the Egyptian Revolution of 2011. As soon as our car was approaching the area I began thinking that this might not have been a good idea. At this time there were still protesters and it seemed like the situation got tense right as we were arriving. Our car was unintentionally pushed around by the protesters, but eventually we were able to get away.

Our last stop of the day was at Khan el-Khalili, the major souk or bazaar in the area known as Islamic Cairo. This was the only time in the day in which we can say we walked the streets of Cairo (with Mohamed by our side, of course). The vendors were colorful characters. They were aggressive, but not in a way that would negatively impact your experience.

As the sun went down, we made our way back to our hotel. Mohamed was truly a sensational guide. Hard to believe there would be anyone better than him.

In the end, there were only two major Cairo/Giza attractions that we did not visit: the Egyptian Antiquities Museum, which was apparently in disarray at the time, and the Citadel, which we chose not to visit so that we could visit the Mosque and Madrasa of Sultan Hasan.

By noon the next day (after another three hour delay) we were safely on our way to Dubai.

If you do not feel comfortable planning a one or two week trip to Egypt, a quick visit to Cairo like ours is certainly worth it. There are only a few days in your life that you will never forget and our day in Cairo will certainly be one of those.
