I Want to Go to Montreal — for CHEAP!

September 9, 2015
Christine Krzyszton

Montreal is a great alternative to a European vacation. You’ll experience the same ambiance of a French city without the jetlag. And instead of paying a premium for the euro, you’ll enjoy the benefit of the strong U.S. dollar currently sitting at nearly $1.32 to the Canadian dollar.

Getting There

Montreal has brutal winters and crowded summers, so utilize late spring and early fall for the best combination of comfort and cost.

Using miles/points or purchasing outright: Flights to Montreal from the West Coast, Midwest, and Northeast are currently running $450, $300 and $250, respectively. Round trip award tickets for late March/early April for are as low as 25,000 miles from ORD/LAX and 20,000 miles (UA) from NYC.

Try pricing one-way fares and one-way award tickets as well; combining the two can result in the best of both options. For instance, if you can find a low-level 10K award ticket one-way and a fare less than half the round trip price for the reverse direction, combine the two for the best value. If you can extend your stay, an excellent redemption would be the SPG Nights and Flights option.

Try a train/plane combination: If you have a day to spare, consider flying to New York, purchasing a one-way train ticket to Montreal for $68 ($58 for age 62+) and enjoy a scenic day on the rails before your weekend in Montreal. Toronto is another option for taking the train, 5.5 hours and $44 on VIA Rail. You can build the trip with a one-way flight to New York/Toronto, a train ticket on Amtrak or VIA Rail and a one-way airline ticket from Montreal to your home airport, and you’d actually spend the same amount as a round trip plane ticket depending on the dates.

The Hotel

Using hotel loyalty points: This is one city where the points required for hotel nights seem out-of-sync in some cases, so you’ll want to do your research carefully. For instance, the W, a Starwood property, runs 12,000 points or $364. However, the Westin, also a Starwood property, runs the same 12,000 points but for only $112! A club-level room with lounge access at the Intercontinental, an IHG property, costs 45,000 IHG points but can be purchased for $150. Lounge access alone at an Intercontinental is usually $90/night. Bottom line, using loyalty points may or may not be the best value, so research is a must. Rates quoted here are for March/early April 2016.

Paying for the hotel outright: With the favorable exchange rate for U.S. dollars, hotels can be a bargain in Montreal. The exchange translates into a hotel room priced at $132 CAD converting to around $100 USD. There hasn’t been a better time in a long time to visit Canada. You can stay at premium hotels like the Intercontinental for $107, the Hyatt Regency for $122 and the Westin for $112. With value this good, consider going for luxury.

Activities in Montreal

The gem of Montreal: No matter your religion, you’re sure to appreciate the incredible Notre Dame Basilica in Montreal. Take the tour of this magnificent structure, learn about its history, attend a concert or opt for the spectacular light show. I was fortunate to attend mass on Easter Sunday and even though it was spoken in French and I am not Catholic, it was truly enjoyable.

Old Montreal: Yes, it’s a little touristy but no more than Paris is touristy. You’ll see the souvenir shops, but you can also take in amazing architecture, shop a little, walk along the cobblestone streets then rest at an outdoor café for coffee and some people watching.

Montreal Food Scene

Poutine, maple syrup, and crepes: If you want the real French Canadian food experience, you’ll have to try all of these food options. Poutine is a simple dish: French fries, cheese curds and gravy. But Resto La Banquise goes above and beyond and serves up this unique dish 30 different ways, so you’re sure to find one you like. For everything maple syrup, stop by Maple Delights, located in the heart of Old Montreal. They serve everything from their famous maple gelato to maple taffy (made by throwing a hot maple syrup concoction on a bed of crushed ice). Whether you like sweet crepes or prefer savory ones like I do, you’ll find the best creperies in Montreal. For a list with plenty of customer reviews, start here.

Eating Polish in a sea of French Restaurants: One of my favorite fun restaurants in Old Montreal is the Stash Cafe. Start with their great traditional Polish food, add the lively music of a piano player and feel the warmth of the blazing fireplace — you’ll feel like you’re in the heart of Poland!

Montreal Transportation

From the airport to your hotel: A taxi will set you back as much as $50, but the 747 Airport Express bus is less than $8 USD and has free Wi-Fi. The ticket is available at the airport and is then good for 24 hours on the entire bus/metro system. You can also purchase a one-way 747 Airport Express bus pass with cash/credit card at stations throughout Montreal for your return trip to the airport. The bus makes stops convenient to most downtown hotels.

Metro your way around the city: Montreal has an efficient and expansive metro system, and a weekend pass runs around $10 USD.

Montreal Wildcard

Are you ready for zip lining in urban Montreal? For an amazing view of the city and Bonsecours Island, try the short zip lining experience from the Old Port of Montreal. For around $15, you can fly like a bird and savor a day or nighttime view.

For a European vacation close to home that won’t cost a fortune, Montreal is the perfect choice.

  • dee seiffer

    My youngest daughter went to university in Montreal, so we were there a lot. We used Hotels.com and Capitol One pts much more often than traditional hotel pts.

    We lived in Connecticut, so we drove. To get our other kids there if we were doing a family trip, we usually flew them to Burlington, VT on US Airways (now AA miles), picked them up and drove the rest of the way. My daughter used to rent a car to drive down to Burlington to shop and feel like she was “home.” One son went to UVM, so it felt like home to her.

    One area you didn’t mention that is a favorite in Montreal is the Plateau. Blvds St Laurent and St Denis are the main drags through the ethic neighborhoods with lots of shops, cafe’s, bars, and ethnic food. There are some small boutique hotels along St Denis.

    Blvd St Laurent is known as “The Main” because it divides the city between the ethnic Plateau neighborhoods and the “English” more posh neighborhoods.

    Duluth St off of St. Denis is famous for very good restaurants – many BYOB. The most famous is Au Pied de Cochon (not BYOB). It is expensive and gets booked far in advance. My daughter and I would stop by to ask if they had any availability. We sometimes got a table for 2 that night or the next because someone cancelled. Share – the portions are huge.

    My absolute favorite restaurant is Restaurant Le P’Tit Plateau, 330 Rue Marie-Anne E, down a side street near Ruth & St Denis. Call for reservations. BYOB. There is an SAQ – liquor store a couple of blocks away on St. Denis.

    Another great spot to visit especially if it is still harvest season and you want to feel like Europe is the Marche’ Jean-Talon. It is a huge European-style food market. Plan to eat lunch there or at one of the cafe’s surrounding the market.

    The Botanical Gardens are lovely, also. I wouldn’t care if I hadn’t seen the Olympic stuff. They are across the street from each other.
