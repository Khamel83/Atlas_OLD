1890 opera by Pietro Mascagni

_**Cavalleria rusticana**_ (pronounced [[kavalleˈriːa
rustiˈkaːna]](/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian "Help:IPA/Italian");
[Italian](/wiki/Italian_language "Italian language") for 'Rustic
[Chivalry](/wiki/Chivalry "Chivalry")') is an [opera](/wiki/Opera "Opera") in
one act by [Pietro Mascagni](/wiki/Pietro_Mascagni "Pietro Mascagni") to an
Italian [libretto](/wiki/Libretto "Libretto") by [Giovanni Targioni-
Tozzetti](/wiki/Giovanni_Targioni-Tozzetti "Giovanni Targioni-Tozzetti") and
[Guido Menasci](/wiki/Guido_Menasci "Guido Menasci"), adapted from an 1880
[short story of the same name](/wiki/Cavalleria_rusticana_\(short_story\)
"Cavalleria rusticana \(short story\)") and subsequent play by [Giovanni
Verga](/wiki/Giovanni_Verga "Giovanni Verga"). Considered one of the classic
_[verismo](/wiki/Verismo "Verismo")_ operas, it premiered on 17 May 1890 at
the [Teatro Costanzi](/wiki/Teatro_dell%27Opera_di_Roma "Teatro dell'Opera di
Roma") in Rome. Since [1893](/wiki/1893_in_music "1893 in music"), it has
often been performed in a so-called _Cav/Pag_ double-bill with
_[Pagliacci](/wiki/Pagliacci "Pagliacci")_ by [Ruggero
Leoncavallo](/wiki/Ruggero_Leoncavallo "Ruggero Leoncavallo").

## Composition history

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Cavalleria_rusticana&action=edit&section=1 "Edit
section: Composition history")]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Mascagni_and_Librettists.jpg/250px-
Mascagni_and_Librettists.jpg)](/wiki/File:Mascagni_and_Librettists.jpg)Mascagni
(center) with his librettists, Giovanni Targioni-Tozzetti (left) and Guido
Menasci

In July 1888 the Milanese music publisher [Edoardo
Sonzogno](/wiki/Edoardo_Sonzogno "Edoardo Sonzogno") announced a competition
open to all young Italian composers who had not yet had an opera performed on
stage. They were invited to submit a one-act opera which would be judged by a
jury of five prominent Italian critics and composers. The best three would be
staged in Rome at Sonzogno's expense.

Mascagni heard about the competition only two months before the closing date
and asked his friend Giovanni Targioni-Tozzetti, a poet and professor of
literature at the [Italian Royal Naval
Academy](/wiki/Accademia_Navale_di_Livorno "Accademia Navale di Livorno") in
[Livorno](/wiki/Livorno "Livorno"), to provide a libretto. Targioni-Tozzetti
chose _Cavalleria rusticana_ , a popular short story (and play) by [Giovanni
Verga](/wiki/Giovanni_Verga "Giovanni Verga"), as the basis for the opera. He
and his colleague Guido Menasci set about composing the libretto, sending it
to Mascagni in fragments, sometimes only a few verses at a time on the back of
a postcard. As Mascagni believed that the work was hastily written and not
reflective of his best efforts, his courage deserted him and he placed the
draft in a drawer, from where his wife, Argenide Marcellina "Lina" Mascagni,
removed it and submitted it on the last day that entries would be accepted.[2]
In all, 73 operas were submitted, and on 5 March 1890, the judges selected the
final three: [Niccola Spinelli](/wiki/Niccola_Spinelli "Niccola Spinelli")'s
_Labilia_ , [Vincenzo
Ferroni](/w/index.php?title=Vincenzo_Ferroni&action=edit&redlink=1 "Vincenzo
Ferroni \(page does not exist\)")
[[it](https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vincenzo_Ferroni "it:Vincenzo Ferroni")]'s
_Rudello_ , and Mascagni's _Cavalleria rusticana_.

There have been two other operas based on Verga's story. The first, _[Mala
Pasqua!](/wiki/Mala_Pasqua! "Mala Pasqua!")_ (Bad Easter!) by [Stanislao
Gastaldon](/wiki/Stanislao_Gastaldon "Stanislao Gastaldon"), was entered in
the same competition as Mascagni's. However, Gastaldon withdrew it when he
received an opportunity to have it performed at the Teatro Costanzi, where it
premiered on 9 April 1890.[4] In the 1907 Sonzogno competition, [Domenico
Monleone](/wiki/Domenico_Monleone "Domenico Monleone") submitted an opera
based on the story, and likewise called _Cavalleria rusticana_. The opera was
not successful in the competition but premiered later that year in Amsterdam
and went on to a successful tour throughout Europe, ending in Turin. Sonzogno,
wishing to protect the lucrative property which Mascagni's version had become,
took legal action and successfully had Monleone's opera banned from
performance in Italy.[5] Monleone changed the opera ‘beyond recognition’,
setting the music to a new libretto. In this form it was presented as _La
giostra dei falchi_ in 1914.[5]

## Performance history

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Cavalleria_rusticana&action=edit&section=2 "Edit
section: Performance history")]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Cavalleria_rusticana_1.jpg/250px-
Cavalleria_rusticana_1.jpg)](/wiki/File:Cavalleria_rusticana_1.jpg)

_Cavalleria rusticana_ opened on the evening of 17 May 1890 at the [Teatro
Costanzi](/wiki/Teatro_dell%27Opera_di_Roma "Teatro dell'Opera di Roma") in
Rome to a half empty house. However, the audience included not only the most
authoritative music critics in the country but also [Queen
Margherita](/wiki/Queen_Margherita "Queen Margherita"), a great music lover.
It was a success from its opening notes. Following Stagno's rendition of the
Siciliana behind the curtain, the audience leapt to their feet with a
thunderous applause not heard for many years. The Siciliana was
[encored](/wiki/Encore_\(concert\) "Encore \(concert\)") as were several other
numbers in the opera. It was a sensation, with Mascagni taking 40 curtain
calls and winning the First Prize.

### Subsequent performances

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Cavalleria_rusticana&action=edit&section=4 "Edit
section: Subsequent performances")]

Although Mascagni had started writing two other operas earlier
(_[Pinotta](/wiki/Pinotta "Pinotta")_ , premiered in 1932, and _[Guglielmo
Ratcliff](/wiki/Guglielmo_Ratcliff "Guglielmo Ratcliff")_ , premiered in
1895), _Cavalleria rusticana_ was his first opera to be completed and
performed. It remains the best known of his fifteen operas and one operetta
(_[Sì](/wiki/S%C3%AC_\(operetta\) "Sì \(operetta\)")_). Apart from _Cavalleria
rusticana_ , only _[Iris](/wiki/Iris_\(opera\) "Iris \(opera\)")_ and
_[L'amico Fritz](/wiki/L%27amico_Fritz "L'amico Fritz")_ have remained in the
standard repertory, with _[Isabeau](/wiki/Isabeau "Isabeau")_ and _[Il piccolo
Marat](/wiki/Il_piccolo_Marat "Il piccolo Marat")_ on the fringes of the
Italian repertoire. Its success has been phenomenal ever since its first
performance. At the time of Mascagni's death in 1945, the opera had been
performed more than 14,000 times in Italy alone.

In 1890, following its run of sold-out performances at the Teatro Costanzi,
the opera was produced throughout Italy and in Berlin. _Cavalleria Rusticana_
had its Western Hemisphere premiere at the Teatro Nacional in [Buenos
Aires](/wiki/Buenos_Aires "Buenos Aires") on 28 February 1891, and a further
run of performances at the Teatro de la Opera from 5 August 1891.[9] It
received its London premiere at the [Shaftesbury
Theatre](/wiki/Original_Shaftesbury_Theatre "Original Shaftesbury Theatre") on
19 October 1891 and its [Covent Garden](/wiki/Royal_Opera_House "Royal Opera
House") premiere on 16 May 1892.

American producers vied with each other (sometimes through the courts) to be
the first to present the opera in that country. _Cavalleria rusticana_ finally
had its American premiere in Philadelphia at the [Grand Opera
House](/wiki/Philadelphia_Opera_House "Philadelphia Opera House") on 9
September 1891, followed by a performance [in
Chicago](/wiki/Chicago_Opera_House "Chicago Opera House") on 30 September
1891. The opera premiered in New York City on 1 October 1891, with two rival
performances on the same day: an afternoon performance at the Casino, directed
by [Rudolph Aronson](/wiki/Rudolph_Aronson "Rudolph Aronson"), and an evening
performance at the Lenox Lyceum, directed by [Oscar
Hammerstein](/wiki/Oscar_Hammerstein_I "Oscar Hammerstein I").

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Stagno_and_Bellincioni_in_Cavalleria_Rusticana.jpg/190px-
Stagno_and_Bellincioni_in_Cavalleria_Rusticana.jpg)](/wiki/File:Stagno_and_Bellincioni_in_Cavalleria_Rusticana.jpg)Gemma
Bellincioni as Santuzza, and her husband, Roberto Stagno, as Turiddu, in the

The opera received its first performance at the [Metropolitan
Opera](/wiki/Metropolitan_Opera "Metropolitan Opera") on 30 December 1891 in a
double bill with a fragment of [Gluck](/wiki/Christoph_Willibald_Gluck
"Christoph Willibald Gluck")'s _[Orfeo ed Euridice](/wiki/Orfeo_ed_Euridice
"Orfeo ed Euridice")_ , and has since received over 700 performances there.

The opera was played during the 1920s in African-American theaters in Chicago.
For example, the Vendome, featuring the orchestra of [Erskine
Tate](/wiki/Erskine_Tate "Erskine Tate"), played music from this opera.
Notably, [Louis Armstrong](/wiki/Louis_Armstrong "Louis Armstrong") performed
and memorized the trumpet solo, coming out of the pit and mounting the stage
to play it.[11]

    Place: A 19th-century [Sicilian](/wiki/Sicily "Sicily") village[13]
    Time: [Easter](/wiki/Easter "Easter") morning

Before the action takes place, the young villager Turiddu returns from
military service to find out that his fiancée Lola had married the carter[14]
Alfio while he was away. In revenge, Turiddu seduced Santuzza, a young woman
in the village. As the opera begins, Lola, overcome by her jealousy of
Santuzza, has begun an adulterous affair with Turiddu.

_The main square of the village_

Offstage, Turiddu is heard singing a [siciliana](/wiki/Siciliana "Siciliana"),
"O Lola ch'ai di latti la cammisa" ("O Lola, you whose blouse is as white as
milk," commonly rendered in English as "O Lola! like the snow, pure in thy
whiteness!"). To one side is the church; to the other is Lucia's wine shop and
the house where she lives with her son, Turiddu. The villagers move about the
square, singing of the beautiful spring day, "Gli aranci olezzano sui verdi
margini" (literally, "Oranges smell good on the green edges," rendered as "The
air is sweet with orange blossoms" in the English libretto) and a hymn to the
[Blessed Virgin Mary](/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary "Blessed Virgin Mary"). Some
villagers enter the church, and others wander off still singing.

Santuzza, having slept with Turiddu and suspecting that he has betrayed her
for Lola, is distraught and approaches Lucia as she comes out of her house.
Santuzza asks for Turiddu, but Lucia replies that he has gone to another town
to fetch some wine. Santuzza tells her that he was seen during the night in
the village. Lucia asks her inside to talk, but just at that moment, Alfio
arrives on his wagon, accompanied by the villagers. He praises the joys of a
[teamster](/wiki/Teamster "Teamster")'s life and the beauty of Lola his bride.
Alfio asks Lucia for some of her fine old wine. She tells him it has run out
and Turiddu has gone away to buy more. Alfio replies that he had seen Turiddu
early that morning near his cottage. Lucia starts to express surprise, but
Santuzza stops her.

Alfio leaves. The choir inside the church is heard singing the "[Regina
coeli](/wiki/Regina_coeli "Regina coeli")." Outside, the villagers sing an
Easter Hymn, "Inneggiamo, il Signor non è morto" ("Let us sing hymns, the Lord
is not dead," or "We rejoice that our Saviour is living!" in the English
version) joined by Santuzza. The villagers enter the church, while Santuzza
and Lucia remain outside. Lucia asks Santuzza why she signalled her to remain
silent when Alfio said that he had seen Turiddu that morning. Santuzza
exclaims, "Voi lo sapete" ("You well know") and tells Lucia the story of her
seduction by Turiddu and his affair with Lola. Lucia pities Santuzza, who
feels dishonoured, having been seduced by Turiddu only to be abandoned by him
for his old flame, Lola. Santuzza feels she cannot enter the church, but begs
Lucia to go inside herself and pray for her. Santuzza stays behind to try to
plead with Turiddu to leave Lola and return to her.

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Cavalleria_Rusticana_-
_Santuzza_and_Turiddu_outside_the_church.jpg/500px-Cavalleria_Rusticana_-
_Santuzza_and_Turiddu_outside_the_church.jpg)](/wiki/File:Cavalleria_Rusticana_-
_Santuzza_and_Turiddu_outside_the_church.jpg)Santuzza pleads with Turiddu not
to meet with Lola again

Turiddu arrives. Santuzza upbraids him for pretending to have gone away, when
he was actually seeing Lola. Lola enters the square singing. She mocks
Santuzza and goes inside the church. Turiddu turns to follow Lola, but
Santuzza begs him to stay. Turiddu pushes her away. She clings to him. He
loosens her hands, throws her to the ground, and enters the church. Alfio
arrives looking for Lola. Santuzza tells him that his wife has betrayed him
with Turiddu. Alfio swears to take _vendetta_ (revenge) which causes Santuzza
to repent for having disclosed the affair and begs Alfio to stop, but to no
avail.

The square is empty as the orchestra plays the famous
[Intermezzo](/wiki/Intermezzo "Intermezzo").

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/95/Cavalleria_Rusticana_-
_Alfio_and_Turiddu_embrace.jpg/500px-Cavalleria_Rusticana_-
_Alfio_and_Turiddu_embrace.jpg)](/wiki/File:Cavalleria_Rusticana_-
_Alfio_and_Turiddu_embrace.jpg)Turiddu bites Alfio's ear

The villagers come out of the church. Turiddu is in high spirits because he is
with Lola, and Santuzza appears to have gone. He invites his friends to his
mother's wine shop where he sings a drinking song, "Viva, il vino
spumeggiante" ("Hail to the bubbling wine!"). Alfio joins them. Turiddu offers
him wine, but he refuses it. All understand that trouble is in the air. The
women leave, taking Lola with them. In a brief exchange of words, Alfio
challenges Turiddu to a duel. Following Sicilian custom, the two men embrace,
and Turiddu, in a token of acceptance, bites Alfio's ear, drawing blood which
signifies a fight to the death. Alfio leaves and Turiddu calls Lucia back. He
tells her that he is going outside to get some air and asks that she be a
kindly mother to Santuzza if he should not return: "Un bacio, mamma! Un altro
bacio!—Addio!" ("One kiss, mother! One more kiss! – Farewell!").

Turiddu rushes out. Lucia, weeping, wanders aimlessly around outside her
house. Santuzza approaches and throws her arms around her. The villagers start
to crowd around. Voices are heard in the distance and a woman cries, "They
have murdered Turiddu!" Santuzza faints and Lucia collapses in the arms of the
women villagers.

Mascagni calls for a standard-sized orchestra consisting of 2
[flutes](/wiki/Flute "Flute"), 2 [piccolos](/wiki/Piccolo "Piccolo"), 2
[oboes](/wiki/Oboe "Oboe"), 2 [clarinets](/wiki/Clarinet "Clarinet"), 2
[bassoons](/wiki/Bassoon "Bassoon"), 4 [horns](/wiki/French_horn "French
horn"), 2 [trumpets](/wiki/Trumpet "Trumpet"), 3 [trombones](/wiki/Trombone
"Trombone"), [tuba](/wiki/Tuba "Tuba"), [timpani](/wiki/Timpani "Timpani"),
percussion ([triangle](/wiki/Triangle_\(musical_instrument\) "Triangle
\(musical instrument\)"), [cymbals](/wiki/Cymbal "Cymbal"), [bass
drum](/wiki/Bass_drum "Bass drum"), [side drum](/wiki/Snare_drum "Snare
drum"), [tamtam](/wiki/Gong "Gong"), [tubular bells](/wiki/Tubular_bells
"Tubular bells")), 2 [harps](/wiki/Harp "Harp"), [organ](/wiki/Organ_\(music\)
"Organ \(music\)") and [strings](/wiki/String_section "String section").

There have been over 100 full-length recordings of _Cavalleria rusticana_
published since it was first recorded in Germany in 1909.[15][16] As in live
performances of the opera, recordings of the work have often been paired with
[Ruggero Leoncavallo](/wiki/Ruggero_Leoncavallo "Ruggero Leoncavallo")'s
_[Pagliacci](/wiki/Pagliacci "Pagliacci")_. In addition to the original
Italian, recordings of the work in the English, French, German, and Hungarian
languages have been released.[17] Mascagni himself conducted the opera in two
recordings, the better-known of which is the 1940 EMI recording made to mark
the 50th anniversary of the opera's premiere. The performance by the La Scala
orchestra and chorus with [Lina Bruna Rasa](/wiki/Lina_Bruna_Rasa "Lina Bruna
Rasa") as Santuzza and [Beniamino Gigli](/wiki/Beniamino_Gigli "Beniamino
Gigli") as Turiddu also has a spoken introduction by Mascagni. Originally
released as on 78rpm discs, then an LP, it is available on CD under several
historical recording labels.[18]

A double-bill performance of _Cavalleria_ and _Pagliacci_ was transmitted as
the first broadcast by New York City's [Metropolitan
Opera](/wiki/Metropolitan_Opera "Metropolitan Opera") on 11 December 1910.[19]
Radio pioneer [Lee de Forest](/wiki/Lee_de_Forest "Lee de Forest") talked
[Giulio Gatti-Casazza](/wiki/Giulio_Gatti-Casazza "Giulio Gatti-Casazza"), the
Met manager, into sending the program over the airwaves by using a backstage
radio transmitter and a rooftop antenna, "using a long fishing pole for his
mast." [Enrico Caruso](/wiki/Enrico_Caruso "Enrico Caruso") and [Emmy
Destinn](/wiki/Emmy_Destinn "Emmy Destinn") were in the leading roles.[20]

> Few listened. There were no radios. But public receivers had been set up in
> several well-advertised locations in New York City, and people could catch
> at least an inkling of the music on earphones. The next day, _[The New York
> Times](/wiki/The_New_York_Times "The New York Times")_ reported that static
> and other interference "kept the homeless song waves from finding
> themselves."[20]

In Los Angeles, an "Italian Night" concert was heard live "in its entirety" on
May 6, 1930, as the third program of the
[Adohr](/w/index.php?title=Adohr_Milk_Company&action=edit&redlink=1 "Adohr
Milk Company \(page does not exist\)") opera series over radio station
[KFI](/wiki/KFI "KFI"), featuring "A distinguished cast ... headed by [Lisa
Roma](/wiki/Lisa_Roma "Lisa Roma"), noted lyric soprano ... Music lovers
should not fail to tune in."[21]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f8/Cavalleria_Rusticana_Film_Poster1953.jpg)](/wiki/File:Cavalleria_Rusticana_Film_Poster1953.jpg)Poster
for the Carmine Gallone film version of _Cavalleria rusticana_ (1953)

A notable use of the Intermezzo from _Cavalleria rusticana_ in the United
States was as the theme for a regular radio broadcast, _Symphony of the
Rockies,_ which featured "a small string group playing light classical music"
in the 1930s and 1940s over [Denver](/wiki/Denver "Denver") radio station
[KOA](/wiki/KOA_\(AM\) "KOA \(AM\)"), then owned and operated by the
[NBC](/wiki/NBC "NBC") network. It "was a 'feed' to the entire network from
the KOA studios."[22]

Apart from video recordings of live performances, there have been several
cinematic versions of _Cavalleria rusticana_ , the most notable of which are:

  * The 1916 silent film accompanied by Mascagni's score, directed by [Ugo Falena](/wiki/Ugo_Falena "Ugo Falena"), with [Gemma Bellincioni](/wiki/Gemma_Bellincioni "Gemma Bellincioni"), who had created the role of Santuzza in the opera's world premiere.
  * The [1953 film](/wiki/Cavalleria_rusticana_\(1953_film\) "Cavalleria rusticana \(1953 film\)") directed by [Carmine Gallone](/wiki/Carmine_Gallone "Carmine Gallone"), using actors miming the voices of opera singers, with a young [Anthony Quinn](/wiki/Anthony_Quinn "Anthony Quinn") as Alfio miming to the voice of [Tito Gobbi](/wiki/Tito_Gobbi "Tito Gobbi"). (Released in the US in 1963 with the title _Fatal Desire_.)
  * It was [filmed for Australian television in 1959](/wiki/Cavalleria_Rusticana_\(1959_film\) "Cavalleria Rusticana \(1959 film\)").
  * The 1968 film directed by Åke Falck, with [Fiorenza Cossotto](/wiki/Fiorenza_Cossotto "Fiorenza Cossotto") as Santuzza, Gianfranco Cecchele as Turiddu, [Giangiacomo Guelfi](/wiki/Giangiacomo_Guelfi "Giangiacomo Guelfi") as Alfio and Anna di Stasio as Lucia. ([La Scala](/wiki/La_Scala "La Scala"), Milan conducted by [Herbert von Karajan](/wiki/Herbert_von_Karajan "Herbert von Karajan").)
  * The [1982 film](/wiki/Cavalleria_rusticana_\(1982_film\) "Cavalleria rusticana \(1982 film\)") directed by [Franco Zeffirelli](/wiki/Franco_Zeffirelli "Franco Zeffirelli"), using opera singers for actors with [Plácido Domingo](/wiki/Pl%C3%A1cido_Domingo "Plácido Domingo") as Turiddu, [Elena Obraztsova](/wiki/Elena_Obraztsova "Elena Obraztsova") as Santuzza, [Renato Bruson](/wiki/Renato_Bruson "Renato Bruson") as Alfio and [Fedora Barbieri](/wiki/Fedora_Barbieri "Fedora Barbieri") as Lucia.

Costumed excerpts from the opera are performed in [Mario
Lanza](/wiki/Mario_Lanza "Mario Lanza")'s popular films _[The Great
Caruso](/wiki/The_Great_Caruso "The Great Caruso")_ (1951) and _[Because
You're Mine](/wiki/Because_You%27re_Mine "Because You're Mine")_ (1952). The
opera's symphonic Intermezzo has figured in the soundtrack of several films,
most notably in the opening and closing credits of _[Raging
Bull](/wiki/Raging_Bull "Raging Bull")_ and in the finale of _[The Godfather
Part III](/wiki/The_Godfather_Part_III "The Godfather Part III")_ , which also
featured a performance of the opera as a key part of the film's climax.

The melody of the Intermezzo became very popular and it was not long before an
adaptation was made to convert it into an Ave Maria. The text, in Italian, was
set to music by Piero Mazzoni as a solo for a tenor or a soprano, along with
other languages.[25] Numerous arrangements and recordings were made, with
highlights being [Plácido Domingo](/wiki/Pl%C3%A1cido_Domingo "Plácido
Domingo"), [Joyce DiDonato](/wiki/Joyce_DiDonato "Joyce DiDonato"), [Angela
Gheorghiu](/wiki/Angela_Gheorghiu "Angela Gheorghiu"), [Elīna
Garanča](/wiki/El%C4%ABna_Garan%C4%8Da "Elīna Garanča"), [Andrea
Bocelli](/wiki/Andrea_Bocelli "Andrea Bocelli") and others.
