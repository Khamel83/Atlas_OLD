In the BBC miniseries “The Night Manager,” a John le Carré adaptation that
premieres in the U.S. tonight, Tom Hiddleston stars as Jonathan Pine, an
undercover British intelligence agent. While tasked with toppling the
organization of an arms dealer described as “the worst man in the world,” Pine
effortlessly seduces a string of women, deals out death and destruction, and
infiltrates an island fortress filled with henchmen whose names sound
[recycled from old “Jeeves and Wooster”
scripts](https://twitter.com/BenLindbergh/status/722144830625812481). As a
legion of writers and interviewers has
[observed](https://www.google.com/search?q=%22tom+hiddleston%22+%2B+%22james+bond%22&hl=en&gl=us&authuser=0&biw=1242&bih=585&source=lnt&tbs=cdr%3A1%2Ccd_min%3A2%2F1%2F2016%2Ccd_max%3A4%2F19%2F2016&tbm=nws)
since the series aired in England, Hiddleston’s portrayal could double as an
audition for another character with a license to kill and a four-letter last
name: Bond. (You know [the next
line](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TXxKZkE2MGo).) But the career arcs of
Bond actors from the past 40 years suggest that [Hiddleston-
heads](http://www.bbcamerica.com/anglophenia/2013/08/tom-hiddleston-wins-
anglo-fan-favorites-for-second-straight-year/) would get more Hiddleston, and
better Hiddleston, if he never inherited that long-running role.

Handsome, suave and successful English actors of a certain age — the men
who’ve played Bond have all been between 30 and 45 at the time of their first
film’s release — can count on being anointed by rumormongers as the next 007.
Thanks to Hiddleston’s performance as Pine (and the accompanying [positive
press](http://www.rottentomatoes.com/tv/the-night-manager/s01/)), it’s his
[turn](http://www.theweek.co.uk/james-bond/61654/james-bond-contenders-
hiddleston-in-lead-after-night-manager-success).

The 35-year-old actor’s moment in the spotlight comes at the perfect time to
jump-start speculation. The tenures of the five former Bonds lasted four
films, on average, and incumbent Bond Daniel Craig completed his fourth film
in the series, “Spectre,” in 2015. Although Craig hasn’t officially abdicated,
he hasn’t committed to reprising the role. The apparent finality of his [last
scene](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7y_6pS--hq8) in “Spectre,” coupled with
the [weariness](http://www.timeout.com/london/film/daniel-craig-interview-my-
advice-to-the-next-james-bond-dont-be-shit) in his comments after finishing
the film, makes the face of the next sequel uncertain.

Hiddleston seems well-suited (and
[besuited](http://metro.co.uk/2016/01/09/tom-hiddleston-wears-a-suit-very-
well-in-this-group-shot-for-bbc-drama-the-night-manager-5612489/)) to take up
the mantle, and there are obvious benefits to becoming the seventh actor to
wear the [Walther PPK](http://jamesbond.wikia.com/wiki/Walther_PPK):
international notoriety, [lucrative endorsement
deals](http://www.campaignlive.co.uk/article/heineken-unveils-64m-spectre-
campaign-daniel-craig-ad/1364821) and [product
placements](http://www.dailymail.co.uk/tvshowbiz/article-3285408/Making-
killing-17-different-brands-linger-screen-new-Bond-film-Spectre-007-profits-
endorsement.html), and progressively
[bigger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_James_Bond_films#Box_office)
[paydays](http://metro.co.uk/2015/10/08/daniel-craig-was-paid-how-much-to-be-
bond-actor-is-the-highest-paid-007-ever-5428883/) with each return to the
role.

Even so, being Bond has its downsides. Previous actors who’ve accepted [M’s
assignments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motifs_in_the_James_Bond_film_series#Receiving_assignment_from_M)
have [claimed to be ambivalent](http://www.ew.com/article/2012/11/09/daniel-
craig-007-regret) about their decisions to play Bond, citing a sense of
artistic stagnation. Hiddleston doesn’t need Bond to break out: He’s already
earning [Marvel money](http://www.imdb.com/character/ch0039559/), and after
Jonathan Pine, Bond might be a step back both creatively and in terms of
audience satisfaction. (Thus far, “The Night Manager” has a higher average
IMDb user rating than any Bond film in the franchise.) Another frequently
[rumored Bond-to-
be](http://www.thedailybeast.com/articles/2014/12/19/exclusive-sony-emails-
reveal-studio-head-wants-idris-elba-for-the-next-james-bond.html), Idris Elba,
is also already rolling in
[critically](http://www.imdb.com/character/ch0020617/?ref_=nm_flmg_act_49)
[acclaimed](http://www.imdb.com/character/ch0205310/?ref_=nm_flmg_act_9)
characters and
[high](http://www.imdb.com/character/ch0302182/?ref_=tt_cl_t4)–[profile](http://www.imdb.com/character/ch0150888/?ref_=nm_flmg_act_17)
[film](http://www.slashfilm.com/idris-elba-star-trek-character-krall/)
[franchises](http://www.imdb.com/character/ch0238895/?ref_=nm_flmg_act_1).
[Neither](http://www.cinemablend.com/new/Tom-Hiddleston-Wants-James-Bond-
Producers-Know-He-Very-Interested-116897.html)
[actor](http://www.people.com/article/idris-elba-james-bond) has publicly
voiced opposition to playing Bond — Hiddleston, as Pine, even
[improvised](http://www.thedailybeast.com/articles/2016/03/29/tom-hiddleston-
on-his-bond-moment-in-the-night-manager-finale-and-loki-s-future.html) an
[order for a vodka martini](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PrAG7sV8xuc) — but
saddling either star with the weight of fan-servicing the [legacy of 25
films](https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/the-perfect-bond-movie-sean-
connery-judi-dench-and-a-watch-that-blows-stuff-up/), complete with sclerotic
tropes and required catchphrases, might not be the best use of his talents.

While ur-Bond Sean Connery made the character an icon and, in the process,
became iconic himself, the returns for the actors who’ve succeeded him — even
excluding George Lazenby, who hadn’t acted in films before becoming Bond and
who went one and done with “On Her Majesty’s Secret Service” — have been more
mixed. To determine the potential impact of playing Bond on an actor’s output,
I analyzed the IMDb user ratings for each post-Lazenby Bond’s acting work from
the five years before his first Bond film, the years during his reign, and the
five years after he retired his
[tux](http://www.blacktieguide.com/blog/2013/01/03/50-years-of-bond-tuxedos/),
excluding uncredited roles, one-episode spots on TV shows, voice work and
video games.

The good news is that although many Bond films are far from beloved, the
franchise rarely produces utterly unredeemable entries; only one
[Eon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eon_Productions)-produced Bond movie
(2002’s “Die Another Day”) places below IMDb’s overall average movie rating,[
circa 2014](https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/the-story-behind-the-worst-
movie-on-imdb/). Because even “bad” Bond is watchable, the four actors’ Bond
films have an average rating of 6.80, compared with an average of 6.29 for
their non-Bond work from five years before, during, and five years after their
stints as 007.

![lindbergh-bond-1-TYPOFIX](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-
content/uploads/2016/04/lindbergh-bond-1-typofix.png?w=575)

Even though the actors were buoyed by their Bond films, their projects from
the five years before they became Bond have higher ratings on average than
those from their Bond years or the five years after their final Bond films.

This might be a kind of casting-call regression toward the mean: Maybe only
actors who’ve been lucky in landing plum pre-Bond roles (such as Hiddleston’s)
generate enough buzz to be blips on the
[Broccoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbara_Broccoli) radar. But it also
could be because Bond actors can coast on their blockbuster cred or because
being known for Bond gets actors typecast as action stars, whom audiences,
[like critics](http://www.vox.com/2016/4/11/11381206/worst-actors-hollywood),
might respect a little less.

![lindbergh-bond-2](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-
content/uploads/2016/04/lindbergh-bond-2.png?w=575)

Not only do actors appear in lower-rated productions post-Bond, but their
post-Bond parts are served in [small
portions](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rrxlfvI17oY). Acting credits tend to
dwindle after Bond, perhaps because financial security frees actors to take
fewer roles; Bond-related fame and advancing age limit their other options; or
celebrity, [protracted productions](http://www.telegraph.co.uk/film/james-
bond-spectre/daniel-craig-radio1-interview/) and the need to recover from [the
beatings they
take](http://www.dailymail.co.uk/tvshowbiz/article-3286986/Being-007-left-
battered-need-knee-op-says-Daniel-Craig-reveals-injured-filming-Spectre-fight-
scene.html) sidetrack their careers. (Or your alternative theory!)

Being Bond has kept Craig so busy since “Skyfall” (2012) that aside from [his
tour as a stormtrooper](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OM7bxkqWC2M), he’s had
time for only one non-007 acting project (a limited run in “Betrayal” on
Broadway). In both quality and quantity, actors’ output tends to decline after
their first freeze-frame in a [bloody gun
barrel](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AYJE3aGlSAI).

Hiddleston and Elba would be worthy Bonds, but admirers of their earlier work
should think twice before ’shipping them with a prolific (and often formulaic)
franchise. If the wishcasting culminates in real roles for either actor, his
bank account (and Bond fans whose first loyalty is to the franchise) might be
the main beneficiary. Take it from someone who’s been in Bond’s shoes; as
Craig put it last year, “[If I did another Bond movie, it would only be for
the money](http://www.timeout.com/london/film/daniel-craig-interview-my-
advice-to-the-next-james-bond-dont-be-shit).”
