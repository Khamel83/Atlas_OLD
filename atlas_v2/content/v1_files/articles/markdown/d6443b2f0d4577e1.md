The box looked like an old VCR, the controller was comically large, and it was
made by one of the most boring companies on earth. Somehow, the Xbox triumphed
and gave [Microsoft
Corp.](http://web.archive.org/web/20250418204923/https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/MSFT:US)
the first—and last—successful video game console brand from an American
company since Atari.

Twenty years later, Bloomberg asked two dozen people instrumental in creating
the Xbox to recount how they did it. Microsoft broke into an industry
dominated by Japanese companies and reshaped the business around shooting
games and online play. Efforts by [Amazon.com
Inc.](http://web.archive.org/web/20250418204923/https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/AMZN:US),
[Apple
Inc.](http://web.archive.org/web/20250418204923/https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/AAPL:US),
[Facebook
Inc.](http://web.archive.org/web/20250418204923/https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/FB:US)
and
[Google](http://web.archive.org/web/20250418204923/https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/GOOG:US)
to crack the industry in the years since have come nowhere close. Video games
now account for more than $11 billion a year for Microsoft and have
established the Xbox as a premier brand.
