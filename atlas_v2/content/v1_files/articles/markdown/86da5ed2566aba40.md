After [years of speculation](http://deadspin.com/the-nfl-s-los-angeles-derby-
is-a-shameless-repulsive-s-1749262328) over which NFL team would be the first
to surrender to Hollywood’s siren call, the inevitable finally happened: The
league’s owners [voted this
week](http://www.espn.com/nfl/story/_/id/14558668/st-louis-rams-relocate-los-
angeles) to allow the Rams to move from St. Louis to Los Angeles. In his
attempt to justify the move, Rams owner Stan Kroenke [made a lot of
claims](http://www.gannett-cdn.com/experiments/usatoday/Sports/rams-
relocation-statement-010516.pdf) about the St. Louis area. He believed, or at
least said, his team would be “on the road to financial ruin” if it stayed in
the Gateway City because of what he alleged were poor economic prospects for
the metro area, a declining population and various other local factors. The
kabuki was unconvincing.

Kroenke’s arguments [rang hollow](http://deadspin.com/stan-kroenke-will-never-
care-about-you-1752963781) for a number of reasons, but his silly obsession
with the drawbacks of the St. Louis area holds a major logical flaw: The Rams
play in the only major pro league where local revenue takes a backseat to the
revenue generated by shared, national television contracts.

![flowerspaine-NFL_tv_revenue-1](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-
content/uploads/2016/01/flowerspaine-nfl_tv_revenue-1.png)

The source of an NFL team’s fortune looks a lot different than what fills the
coffers for MLB, NBA and NHL teams. While 83 percent of the average team’s
revenue in those sports comes from sources other than national TV deals —
sources largely tied up in local earnings such as gate receipts and [regional
sports networks](http://www.nytimes.com/2011/08/20/sports/regional-sports-
networks-show-teams-the-money.html) (more on those later) — that figure is
only 46 percent for the average NFL club. Thus, financially, it matters less
where an NFL team is located, because the team is going to get billions anyway
from national revenue-sharing arrangements.1

This flattening of large-market revenue happens in other ways across all pro
sports, such as the shared revenue brought in by licensing and merchandise
(hats, jerseys, pagan blood contracts with FanKings), which insidery estimates
put at approximately $3 billion annually — another $87 million or so per year
per team after the league takes its 10 percent cut — and which is shared
equally among 31 NFL teams. (The Cowboys are the only team that has an
individual licensing and merchandising deal, because of course they are.)

Comparing the Rams’ situation to those of teams in the NBA, NHL and MLB, all
of which have lucrative regional TV deals that the NFL can’t tap into, reveals
how little the Rams gain in television money by moving to Los Angeles.
Specifically, we can zoom in to the revenue from Los Angeles teams’ TV
contracts to see how much money the Lakers, Dodgers, Angels, Clippers, Kings
and Ducks are pulling in from regional contracts:

![flowerspaine-NFL_tv_revenue-2](https://fivethirtyeight.com/wp-
content/uploads/2016/01/flowerspaine-nfl_tv_revenue-2.png)

Regional sports networks (RSNs) have been [all the
rage](http://www.sportsbusinessdaily.com/Journal/Issues/2014/03/17/In-
Depth/RSNs.aspx) for a while now, and the profits teams haul in by selling
their cable rights — or, better yet, starting their own team-operated channels
— have fueled the ballooning revenues in other leagues. In LA alone, the
Lakers [inked an RSN deal](http://articles.latimes.com/2011/feb/14/sports/la-
sp-0215-lakers-time-warner-20110215) worth an average of $200 million a year,2
and the Dodgers dropped jaws when it was
[announced](https://twitter.com/molly_knight/status/464478550364389377) their
RSN contract would net $334 million per year in revenue. Even the Clippers,
who come in well behind the Lakers, recently turned down a $60 million per
year local deal, presumably because they’re holding out for something more
lucrative.

If you’re keeping score at home, that’s 60 percent more TV dollars than the
average NFL team rakes in annually. And since the NFL does its television
business on a strictly national basis, the Rams can’t unleash the power of an
RSN on the eyeballs of the nation’s [second-biggest media
market](http://www.tvb.org/media/file/2015-2016-dma-ranks.pdf).

Kroenke would of course argue that the financial benefit comes from attendance
— St. Louis was dead last in the NFL this season, and has floated around the
bottom three during this decade. But even then, it’s not as though the Rams
were always a draw in LA, as the Los Angeles Times noted in a 1994 game story
titled “Attendance Is Not Exactly Peachy,” as just 32,969 fans turned out for
a [14-12 snoozer](http://www.pro-football-
reference.com/boxscores/199409040ram.htm) against the Cardinals. (This was the
“announced” attendance, which is definitionally lower than the actual
attendance. Anaheim Stadium’s football capacity was 69,008.) The Rams were
last in the [league in attendance in
1994](https://umich.app.box.com/s/41707f0b2619c0107b8b/1/320026115), their
last year in Los Angeles.

So Kroenke gains little in the way of media money by uprooting the Rams, and
the benefits to the gate are far from certain once we look to LA’s not-that-
distant history as a football town. What then does he gain by fistfighting St.
Louis and [burning every bridge](http://www.stltoday.com/news/local/govt-and-
politics/kroenke-blasts-st-louis-in-nfl-relocation-
proposal/article_cc72f61a-0c35-53bd-8890-0efde6402509.html) on his way out of
Missouri? One way to make sense of this is to look away from league revenue or
fan interest and shift our attention downward, to Stan Kroenke’s pockets. Holy
Cross economics professor Victor Matheson [told the International Business
Times](http://www.ibtimes.com/nfl-los-angeles-how-la-move-benefits-franchises-
league-2262172) that economists generally expect the Rams’ valuation to rise
by at least $500 million with the move to LA.
