In mid-January, [
Genentech](http://web.archive.org/web/20240205021750/https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/1918938D:US)
started recruiting 200 patients to test whether one of its experimental drugs
can tame [ ulcerative
colitis](http://web.archive.org/web/20240205021750/https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-
information/digestive-diseases/ulcerative-colitis "Ulcerative Colitis -
NIDDK"), a painful, incurable type of inflammatory bowel disease. Until then,
the compound had only been given during experiments to treat lung and skin
disorders. Deciding whether to shift a drug for use against a different
disease than originally intended often takes years of painstaking lab work,
but the California biotech did it in just nine months. The difference:
artificial intelligence, which the company says helped its researchers scan
millions of possibilities to confirm the drug could be useful against diseases
affecting the cells of the colon.

“It’s not like the human is not needed anymore,” says Aviv Regev, a Harvard
University and Massachusetts Institute of Technology computational biologist
who took a leave from her academic work to run Genentech’s research and
development. “But the human all of a sudden gets the superpower.”
