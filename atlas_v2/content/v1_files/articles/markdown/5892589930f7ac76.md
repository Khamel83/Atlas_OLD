Within the first few minutes of the interview, I knew the candidate was a bad
fit for a sales position. His résumé had tipped me off: He was a math major
and built robots in his spare time. Now we were sitting face-to-face in my
office, but he hardly made any eye contact. When I told my boss I wasn’t going
to hire him because of that, she said, “You know this is a phone sales job,
right?”

For decades, managers have bet on the wrong people — and rejected the right
ones. The Kansas City Star once rejected an application from a cartoonist
named Walt Disney. Record labels said no thanks to the Beatles, Madonna, U2,
Kanye and Ed Sheeran. A hotel, a police department and a Kentucky Fried
Chicken all denied Jack Ma a job. And 30 N.F.L. teams decided not to draft Tom
Brady.

It doesn’t have to be that way. In organizational psychology, we have over a
century of evidence on why job interviews fail and how to fix them. If
managers learn to overcome three basic mistakes, they can get better at
spotting diamonds in the rough.

The first mistake is asking the wrong kinds of questions. Some questions are
just too easy to fake. _What’s your greatest weakness?_ Even Michael Scott,
the inept manager in the TV show “The Office,” aced that one: “I work too
hard. I care too much.” Other questions reveal more about the manager who’s
asking than the candidate who’s responding. _How many paper clips would fit in
Yankee Stadium?_ Brainteasers [turn
out](http://web.archive.org/web/20250124104853/https://iaap-
journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/apps.12163) to be useless for
predicting job performance, but useful for identifying sadistic managers, who
seem to enjoy stumping people.

We’re better off asking [behavioral
questions](http://web.archive.org/web/20250124104853/https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/13594320244000184).
_Tell me about a time when …._ Past behavior can help us anticipate future
behavior. But sometimes they’re easy to game, especially for candidates with
more experience. We can get around those issues by including some situational
questions. _What would you do if .…_ Pick a challenge that’s important for
success in the job and your culture, and we can learn something
[valuable](http://web.archive.org/web/20250124104853/http://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1207/s15327043hup1904_3)
about how candidates will [lead, handle
conflicts](http://web.archive.org/web/20250124104853/http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1744-6570.2009.01163.x/full)
and [solve
problems](http://web.archive.org/web/20250124104853/https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10869-015-9410-0).

The second error is focusing on the wrong criteria. At banks and law firms,
managers often
[favor](http://web.archive.org/web/20250124104853/https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/0003122412463213)
people who went to the same school or share their love of lacrosse. Across
industries and occupations, economists
[find](http://web.archive.org/web/20250124104853/https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/0002828042002561)
that even when candidates’ résumé are identical, those with white-sounding
names like Allison and Matthew get 50 percent more callbacks than those with
African-American-sounding names like Lakisha and Jamal. There’s evidence that
[birthmarks,](http://web.archive.org/web/20250124104853/https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2011/11/111109115820.htm)
[being
pregnant](http://web.archive.org/web/20250124104853/https://psycnet.apa.org/buy/2013-29216-001)
and [being
overweight](http://web.archive.org/web/20250124104853/https://www.researchgate.net/publication/8436667_The_Weight_of_Obesity_in_Evaluating_Others_A_Mere_Proximity_Effect)
can put candidates at a disadvantage. And believe it or not, bald men are seen
as having [more leadership
potential](http://web.archive.org/web/20250124104853/https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/article/hair-
today-gone-tomorrow-why-shaved-heads-lead-the-pack/) if they shave their heads
(which is obviously why I shave my head).
