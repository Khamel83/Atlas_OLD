Designing Wings at Boeing

*I have some questions about Boeing.*

*JP:* Yeah, great, I knew you’d come with those. It was a very formative experience for me, first time I saw how a big company works and I started as an intern, and this was in the middle of my college experience and was put on the program, the 7E7 program.

So this was a developmental program basically was designed to be a much more efficient airplane and much more environmentally friendly. It was this airplane or the Sonic Cruiser ( https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_Sonic_Cruiser ) , were the two programs that were being invested in, and it was a pretty lucky experience because if you look at airplane programs, they only happened once every decade, a major one. Then just through luck, I was able to get onto this program, they just started hiring interns again. Boeing had gone through a big — hadn’t hired any engineers for ten years, and so this was just coming out of the financial crisis and things like that, so I was able to get that internship and I was off into the races in the aerospace world.

*I thought you worked on the 787.*

*JP:* The 7E7, and that turned it into the 787.

*Got it.*

*JP:* So that was the very early days before it was really a launch program and officially launched program, and when I came back full-time, they decided, “Hey, this airplane was worth building.” So then it became the 787, close the ANA and JAL, launch airlines, and they had purchase orders for a few hundred planes and so that was enough for Boeing to say, “Hey, let’s go build some airplanes.”

*So you worked on the wing specifically?*

*JP:* That’s right. So the project as a mechanical engineer, I was originally started on the tails where I was doing structural optimization. Today, people would say generative design type of work on the ribs of the tail, was able to get a lot of weight out of the tail on these metal parts and then I moved to the wing and the wing is the 787. One of the unique attributes of it is about 20% more efficient airplane and one of the reasons why it’s made of carbon fiber.

*Yeah. It moves all over the place, you can see it.*

*JP:* Yeah, so very flexible wing ( https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m5GD3E2onlk ) , so if you’ve ever flown it, that’s the final design that’s on there, I was very involved with that. Of course, a large engineering team all contributed to this, but I became one of the lead engineers to optimize that design and really started combining together simulation work related to material science.

So carbon fiber has many different ways you can tailor this material, the big shift was really going from a metal airplane, which is a simple way to think about it is with metals, the materials, the same strength in all directions.

*Well, you start with the material. You know what the material characteristics are and then you have to build around this. Whereas with carbon fiber, it sounds like you can go the opposite direction. You can start with what you want and then tailor it to that, is that a good way to think about it?*

*JP:* Yeah, that’s exactly right, and so being able to tailor the material is huge, it adds many variables and many different ways of optimization to the possibilities an engineer can use to design something.

*And so this is where the software angle came to bear, because how do you even start to experiment or figure out what you want to do with all those extra variables if you don’t have a computer helping you?*

*JP:* Yeah, and it’s a many variable problem. So if you think about it, the most simple level, at the root of the wing, you maybe have about 200 plies, which refers to each of the layers of the carbon fiber. Each of those layers can have a different orientation so they can actually have any angle you can put these fibers in, and then typically, there’s some best practice of certain stacks. So like 45 degrees, 90 degrees, those type of things. But then that’s just one panel. So an airplane wing may have thousands of these panels on every wing and so thousands of panels times hundreds of plies, each of them can have any angle — very complex mathematical optimization problem.

On top of that, it’s also a manufacturing problem. Whatever you design, obviously you have to actually be able to build. So there’s also a practical element of when you do all these optimizations ultimately, of course you have to be able to actually do the layup or the layers of the carbon fiber in that way physically and so you need special tooling and things like that to do that.

*So did Boeing build a compute stack to do this or did they already have it? Or how did they develop the capability to even solve this multivariate problem?*

*JP:* Yeah, great question. Originally, you always use simulation. So aerospace has gone through this evolution. By the time I showed up, a lot of simulation is used, also for metal airplanes, since simulation is really just using the virtual prototyping — so using software to model the physical, and sometimes we call those digital twins.

But essentially you have all the engineering physics built into the software and then you go through different permutations, different designs. You try out and look at the responses and ultimately you qualify the design that way, you do physical testing as well.

So the final design that you come up with, both at the component level and then all the way up to the full scale level, you actually build prototypes and physically test them. That’s where the FAA comes in and says, “Okay, this meets the criteria of safety”, and things like that.

But software-based methodologies were very critical to the aerospace industry overall and it’s pretty incredible to see what was done before that time, because new airplane programs don’t happen that often. So a lot of airplanes you’re flying on were designed without a lot of these more advanced software capabilities so it’s all possible to do without software. Software allows you to do it much faster and especially when you increase all these variables, the complexity level gets really high.

*Would it be possible to build a carbon fiber airplane without software? It doesn’t sound like it.*

*JP:* It’d be pretty tough, I think it’d be possible. If you look at what these engineering teams did on the 737, for example, pretty incredible airplane and they did that before there was a lot of CAD or software.

*But that’s an aluminum wing. When you’re adding all these variables, it just seems like an impossible problem.*

*JP:* Yeah, well, we’re going down the rabbit hole, but there is something called black aluminum, and what that is basically carbon fiber with a standard stack, 45/45/90. You just do the same strength in all directions and then it behaves similar. Of course, in the Z direction, carbon fiber has a lot of challenges because the carbon fibers are not aligned in that direction, but you can actually design carbon fiber in a way that it’s pretty a basic material that has similar behavior as something like aluminum. Which is interesting because that was some of the criticism when Boeing was originally developing the airplane, the simplest thing to do is to design it with those kind of properties, because then you don’t have any special twist or different ways these kind of structures behave. But it’s not very optimized either, so you’re not really taking advantage.

*You’re not getting all the advantages.*

*JP:* Exactly, but a carbon fiber bicycle is designed that way. So a lot of products are actually designed that way. But with all those additional variables, of course, in the aerospace industry, weight is super critical and when I was working on this, one of the challenges we had, that first version of the wing design was far too heavy.

Too heavy is a big problem because it’s not only expensive — for a single pound that’s too heavy on the airplane, it flies around if it’s part of the primary structure, it’s not going to leave the airplane in the next 30 years, so that’s a lot of fuel costs and you don’t retrofit wings or anything like that. So secondary structure, which is a structure that you can take off and replace things like wing tips and stuff like that, of course is a little different. But the main parts of the airplane stay the same for 30 years.

So just in fuel costs, to give you a sense of the scale. For a single pound, if you’re a company like Boeing at the time, maybe ordered about a thousand planes by the time we’re really producing these today, there’s about 2,000, it’s over a thousand dollars a pound, so about $1,500 a pound times 2,000 planes today, you’re talking about $3 million a pound just in the cost of the weight of a single pound for the airlines.

So what they actually do is Boeing promises a weight of the aircraft, and that’s in the design and that’s sold already, and then so you pay a cash penalty to the airline for that extra pound they’re carrying around and airplane’s are pretty heavy. You’re talking about hundreds of thousands of pounds. But everything is quite well-designed and so shaving off just a few percentage points of weight is a huge deal.

So that’s why tailoring those carbon fiber materials, being able to get that to the right weight targets, was critical. And long story short, that final design that we were able to develop, running many of these simulations on many computers at the time, it’s all before there was cloud computing, which is a big challenge we can get into. But long story short, we’re able to really optimize that design that saved Boeing an enormous amount of hassle and money. That’s just the first order effect.

The second order is actually if your plane is too heavy, you need to redesign it. So you actually design an airplane for a certain city pair or certain scale and so there’s this downward spiral. When your plane’s too heavy, then you need to redesign the whole thing.

High-Performance Computing
