Good morning,

This week’s Stratechery Interview is with Tae Kim ( https://x.com/firstadopter ). Kim is a senior technology writer at Barron’s, and has previously written for Bloomberg Opinion and Yahoo Finance. I have read and followed Kim, a very astute observer of the technology and gaming industries, for years.

This week Kim is releasing a new book, The Nvidia Way ( https://wwnorton.com/books/the-nvidia-way ). The Nvidia Way is my favorite kind of tech book: it is a history of the company and its founders and all of the decisions they made decades before Nvidia became the most valuable company in the world; it is those decisions that create a culture. For Kim, Nvidia is impossible to understand without understanding that culture — that is The Nvidia Way — which, by extension, means understanding CEO Jensen Huang (Huang is referred to within Nvidia and in this interview by his first name).

In this interview we work our way through the book, covering everything from Nvidia’s focus on software, its organizational structure, how the company regularly invested years ahead market opportunities, and why all of this is downstream from Jensen. What you will come to realize is how much of Nvidia’s sudden success was very much decades in the making, and why Jensen and Nvidia are the true heirs to Steve Jobs and Apple.

The Nvidia Way

*Well, let’s get to the book, The Nvidia Way ( https://wwnorton.com/books/the-nvidia-way ). We’ll start at the absolute highest level. What is The Nvidia Way?*

*TK:* So the first thing, I did maybe six, seven calls and, “What created this extraordinary outcome?”, it’s literally the best performing stock in US history. If you put in $10,000 at its IPO in 1999, it would be worth $40 million today. We have this extraordinary outcome and what created it? I started talking to maybe half a dozen Nvidia employees, most of them former Nvidia employees and it was clear the one thing that they took away is when they went to a different company like Apple, Google, Intel, they couldn’t adjust. The culture was completely different and they had a tough time adjusting, relating with their colleagues.

So what The Nvidia Way is, I think it’s the work culture which is so unique and specific to Nvidia, created by [CEO] Jensen [Huang]. It’s the insane work ethic, people work like crazy hours at Nvidia, and I think a lot of companies don’t do that anymore. It’s the talent cultivation where Jensen just retains and cultivates talent. If you do a great job, he’ll give you a spot bonus on the spot, give you stock refreshers. I’m sure there might be one or two, but most of the people I’ve talked to that worked at Nvidia, they never compared to that pay, especially the rockstar engineers.

The last thing I think with The Nvidia way is just speed and velocity. All these other big companies, you’ve worked at a big company for a while, as they get larger, business unit heads, there’s a lot of infighting, there’s stakeholders, there’s endless meetings, indecision paralysis. I mean, you see at Google, as soon as NotebookLM starts taking off, the person that heads the project leaves ( https://x.com/raizamrtn/status/1863645718159954272 ). What is going on? I mean, one guy came back, but nearly all the writers of the transformer architecture paper ( https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762 ) left because they just get sick of the bureaucracy and all the internal infighting. At Nvidia, most of that is not there. Jensen is just incented to act and all that internal politics stuff isn’t there, which I think is very unique.

When I met him, he’s a very intimidating character, and I was in a room with him after he did the commencement speech at Caltech ( https://blogs.nvidia.com/blog/jensen-huang-caltech-commencement-address/ ).

He’s a student of history, that the big thing for a technology company is to start thinking you’re hot stuff and then start becoming complacent, he is so scared of that, so he tricks himself to not do that.
