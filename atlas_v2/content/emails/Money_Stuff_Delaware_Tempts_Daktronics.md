Delaware

See, when I was a corporate lawyer, many years ago, I thought that big 
companieswanted to be incorporated in Delaware. Most of them were, and are, and 
Delaware is in the business of catering to them. “Catering to corporations” 
means mostly catering to corporatemanagers, to founders and chief executive 
officers and boards of directors and controlling shareholders, since they are 
the ones who make decisions about where the company will be incorporated. So 
Delaware offers an attractive package to managers.The package is 
<https://bloom.bg/4aOSTDw>, roughly:

 * Delaware lets managers do a lot of stuff. There is a “business judgment 
rule” saying that, for the most part, courts will not second-guess managers’ 
decisions. So you can run your business without worrying too much that some 
random shareholder will sue you and a court will order you to do something else.
 * Delaware doesn’t let managers do anything they want. The managers have 
fiduciary duties to shareholders; they have to treat their shareholders fairly 
and put their interests first. If you do certain bad things, shareholders will 
sue you, and a courtwill order you to knock it off. This is good! For you, as a 
manager, it helps you credibly promise to investors that they will be treated 
well, which makes their shares more valuable, which makes them willing to 
invest in your company. If there wasno oversight over your relationship with 
shareholders, you’d have a hard time raising money. 
 * Delaware is predictable. Delaware has lots of corporate-law cases, so there 
are a lot of precedents saying what is and isn’t allowed. If you ask your 
lawyer if you’re allowed to do something, she can probably give you a pretty 
confident answer. Delaware has a specialized court — the Court of Chancery — 
for corporate disputes, with experienced judges who know a lot about corporate 
law. The chancery court moves quickly, so cases can be resolved quickly, and it 
doesn’t have jury trials, so you don’t have to worry about your merger being 
stopped by the unpredictable decision of some random laypeople. 
Of these three things, the third strikes me as by far the most important. The 
particular mix of rules in Points 1 and 2 — what is and isn’t allowed — isn’t 
that important; every state offers some reasonable mix of managerial discretion 
and shareholder rights, and any company can adapt to any set reasonable set of 
rules. What matters is that you know in advance what the rules are, so you can 
plan for them.

This has all become controversial recently, because of a few 
<https://bloom.bg/412ezcb> Delaware court decisions <https://bloom.bg/4gwJSR1> 
that (1) were surprising and (2) quitesignificantly limited the power of 
controlling shareholders. These decisions undermine the predictability point a 
bit — they were surprising — but they were also just economically bad for 
controlling shareholders. (One was the decision that Elon Musk couldn’t get a 
multibillion-dollar stock package that was approved by Tesla Inc.’s board of 
directors and shareholders, and he did really want that stock.) So it became 
rational for controlling shareholders to say “well, even if some other state is 
less predictable than Delaware, I’d rather roll the dice than be unable to run 
my company the way I want.” And soTesla moved to Texas 
<https://bloom.bg/3CAYu3P>, and we have talked about other companies that have 
moved out of Delaware <https://bloom.bg/4gpf7xa> or are considering it 
<https://bloom.bg/3WKgBuL>.

But you shouldn’t overstate this. It’s not like the story here is “Delaware is 
super-unfriendly to corporate management, and so companies are fleeing to 
anywhere else.” It’s that Delaware has seemed unfriendly to certain sorts of 
companies — often high-profile founder-controlled companies — and other states 
have seized the opportunity to position themselves as more founder-friendly and 
pick off some big companies.

We have discussed cumulative voting <https://bloom.bg/3EvcvRi> before: 
Basically, in cumulative voting, if there are eight directors up for election, 
the shareholders get eight votes per share, and can allocate those votes to any 
director(s) they want. If you really want to elect one director, you can give 
all eight votes to your favorite candidate. So an activist with a big minority 
stake might not be able to get a whole slate of directors elected, but it can 
probably getone director elected. As Alta Fox puts it 
<https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/915779/000153949725000241/n4695_x4-prec14a.htm>

Alta Fox believes that cumulative voting is an important shareholder right 
that protects the voice of independent minority shareholders.

Cumulative voting is a method of voting in corporate director elections that 
allows shareholders to allocate all their votes to one candidate or distribute 
them among multiple candidates, increasing the ability of one or a few minority 
shareholders to elect a faction of hand-picked director candidates that they 
believe will serve their particular interests, without broad shareholder support

So Daktronics wants to move to Delaware (to get rid of cumulative voting) and 
Alta Fox wants to stop it. Wetalked yesterday <https://bloom.bg/4gpf7xa> about 
a similar case: Tripadvisor Inc. wanted to leave Delaware and reincorporate in 
Nevada, arguably because it has a controlling shareholder and Nevada is less 
strict about reviewing transactions with controlling shareholders. Some 
Tripadvisor shareholders sued, asking a Delaware court to stop the move because 
it was bad for shareholders. Delaware declined. If you can get shareholders to 
vote to leave Delaware, you can leave, even if that means you can do stuff to 
shareholders that Delaware wouldn’t let you do.

Does the same logic work in South Dakota? Will a South Dakota court — actually 
a federal court in South Dakota — say that Daktronics can’t leave, because 
leaving would reduce shareholder rights? Or is there a limit on states’ ability 
to protect shareholder rights, where the limit is “the company can always 
leave”? I pretty much know the answer for Delaware; for South Dakota, who knows?

Pimco

How do you get into private credit? Like let’s say you have a pot of money and 
you want to start lending it directly to private equity sponsors to finance 
leveraged buyouts. How do you start? Probably you hire some former bankers with 
good relationships and have them call on private equity sponsors, saying “hi, 
we’d like to lend you money to finance your buyouts.” The sponsors are like 
“Sure, we can always use more money. Honestly we have a lot of people who want 
to finance our buyouts. But we’ll put you on the list.” And then they call you 
and say “hey, we’ve got a buyout deal going, want to lend us money? Full 
disclosure, we’ve called a few other people, and they’ll charge us 7%. Are you 
in?”

Are you in? I feel like the answer is “yes”? I mean, you could say no. But 
you’ve got that pot of money and you need to spend it; you’re looking to get 
into the private credit game and this is your chance. And if you say no, will 
the sponsor call you again? They’ve got a lot of people looking to lend them 
money, and what you bring to the table is … desperation? If you are very picky 
about financing only the best deals, if you do careful credit analysis and push 
to get a good return, the sponsors can just stop calling you. There are plenty 
of other people to call.

Those other people — the large firms that have been in the private credit game 
for a while — have advantages. They probably already lend money to the 
sponsors’ other deals, so they’ve got existing relationships and some 
negotiating leverage. Also they’re justbig; a sponsor that wants to do a lot of 
big buyouts will have to keep them in the mix. Those big leading private credit 
firmscan say no to deals; they can push hard on terms. You are trying to break 
into the business, and the easiest way to break in is to be accommodating. You 
give the sponsors good customer service, you say yes to everything and move 
quickly and don’t push too hard on rates or terms.

This is honestly a pretty standard way for a deep-pocketed latecomer to get 
into a business, [2]  <> but it has obvious problems. You are getting adversely 
selected; you are saying yes to the deals that the big incumbents have said no 
to. Maybe that’s fine. It sort of depends on your background and temperament. 
If your essential skill is “I raise money from investors and deploy it,” then 
you’re doing great: You’re raising a big pot of money and deploying it into the 
thing that investors want. If your essential skill is “I do good credit 
analysis,” though, this will hurt. You want to do credit analysis, to get alpha 
by doing good deals and avoiding bad ones; you don’t want to take bad deals 
just because you have money to spend.

(You can get around this problem, to some extent, by buying a big existing 
incumbent private credit firm, so that you don’t have to break into the 
business starting from zero. Buteveryone <https://bloom.bg/3WPZUhz> knows that 
<https://bloom.bg/4aQoW6j>, so those firms <https://bloom.bg/4aObIqp> are 
expensive.)

Rebalancing

You could have a simple model of the stock market that is like:

 * Most people have 60/40 portfolios, where they put 60% of their money in 
stocks and 40% in bonds. 
 * If stocks go up by 2% and bonds go down by 2%, then your 60/40 portfolio 
becomes a 61/39 portfolio. 
 * Therefore you have to sell some stocks and buy some bonds to get back to 
60/40. 
 * When you sell stocks, stocks will go down; when you buy bonds, bonds will 
go up. 
Obviously this is not literally true, but maybe it roughly describes the 
behavior of a lot of big investors. It is a simple theory of mean reversion: 
Every time the market moves, that will throw 60/40 portfolios out of balance, 
and they will come back into balance by tradingagainst the market move. When 
stocks go up, 60/40 investors are overweight stocks, so they sell stocks, etc.

If this were true, you might try to profit from it. “Whenever a 60/40 investor 
is going to buy stockstomorrow, I will buy them today, and then sell them 
tomorrow when they go up, and vice versa,” is the basic idea. When is a 60/40 
investor going to buy stocks tomorrow? If you take this theory super-literally 
then the answer is “if stocks are down today,” but in practice they won’t 
rebalanceevery day. If 60/40 investors have a rule like “rebalance once a 
month, so that we start each month with a portfolio that is exactly 60/40, and 
otherwise only rebalance if our portfolio is more than 62% stocks or less than 
58% stocks,” then you could trade against that rule. You buy stocks on the last 
day of the month, if stocks have been down that month, and otherwise you buy 
stocks if stocks have been down a lot and the 60/40 portfolios are far out of 
balance.

This is not investment advice, but here’s this <https://bloom.bg/3EDQL5t>:

Investors, who adjust their stock-bond portfolios like clockwork on regular 
schedules, are at the mercy of front-running by fast-money speculators.

Now, a group of academics claim they’ve put a price tag on the resulting cost 
to pension funds and other market participants known for their rebalancing 
activity: $16 billion a year.

Mechanical buying and selling by institutions to hold the mix of assets steady 
over time comes with a quantifiable downside, say researchers including 
Campbell Harvey of Duke University in a paper called The Unintended 
Consequences of Rebalancing. Much of it is the result of opportunistic 
competitors anticipating the trades and swooping in first, moving prices, they 
said.

Here is the paper 
<https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5122748#>, by Harvey, 
Michele Mazzoleni and Alessandro Melone; the intuition is roughly the one I 
laid out. “When stocks are overweight, funds sell stocks and buy bonds, leading 
to a decrease in equity returns of 17 basis points over the next day,” is the 
basic point, and the rebalancings are predictable:

We compute weight deviations from portfolio targets using two rule-based 
rebalancing approaches, Threshold and Calendar, that reflect the investment 
policies of different institutions. The Threshold approach adjusts positions 
when portfolio weights exceed predetermined distances from targets, reflecting 
the idea that allowing for portfolio drifts within defined ranges helps 
minimize transaction costs. Furthermore, many institutional investors have 
regular cash flow needs at the beginning of every month (see, e.g., Etula, 
Rinne, Suominen, and Vaittinen, 2020). For example, mature pension funds often 
sell assets at month-end to raise cash for member benefit payments. The 
Calendar approach captures these scheduled rebalancing activities. Both 
Threshold and Calendar are easy to compute, available in real-time, and 
applicable for any frequency with available return data. The Threshold signal 
captures faster intra-month rebalancing, while the Calendar signal captures 
slower month-end rebalancing. ...

A back-of-the-envelope calculation using our predictability results estimates 
that the rebalancing costs borne by institutional investors can exceed 8 bps 
per year. For a market potentially exceeding $20 trillion in size, rebalancing 
pressures could translate into an annual cost of $16 billion, or about $200 per 
U.S. households each year. …

Finally, mechanical rebalancing offers certain investors the opportunity to 
front-run the predictable trades of large funds. To explore the potential 
economic value of these frontrunning strategies, we construct a managed 
portfolio that replicates the trades of an investor anticipating rebalancing 
activities. … The managed portfolio constructed using these rebalancing signals 
delivers significant positive alphas and achieves a Sharpe ratio exceeding 1 
over the 1997 to 2023 sample period.

The best part is that they went and presented this result to big pension 
managers, who were like “yeah sure of course”:

Many pensions acknowledged that they were aware of this phenomenon. When we 
explained that potential front-runners could exploit such predictability, one 
pension replied, “We know about that.” Others agreed. When we suggested a more 
dynamic rebalancing policy might reduce the potential for front-running, one 
pension remarked, “It is easier for us to task our alpha desk with addressing 
this predictability than to try to convince our investment committee to change 
our rebalancing policy.”

In summary, pensions in our roundtable sample rebalance mechanically based on 
Calendar and Threshold rules. They understand these policies induce 
predictability and believe that traders will front-run their rebalancing. At 
least some of the funds appear to front-run their own rebalancing (and 
potentially that of others). Finally, the funds perceive changing rebalancing 
policies as very challenging given institutional constraints.

“At least some of the funds appear to front-run their own rebalancing”! Really 
this is a story about division of labor. If you run a pension fund targeting a 
60/40 portfolio, you will buy 60% stocks and 40% bonds, and then stocks and 
bonds will go up and down and you will be out of balance, and it will cost you 
money to trade to get back in balance, and it will cost youattention to do that 
trading every day. You are just running a pension fund, you are pretty indexed 
and passive, you are not a market-timing day trader, you have no idea when the 
best time to trade is. “Once a month, unless stuff gets really out of whack” is 
a good heuristic. [3]  <>

You could do better — and maybe save 8 basis points — but at some cost in time 
and attention and internal processes, and you could also do worse. Why bother? 
Let someone else do it. Let some hedge fund that is set up to think about this 
stuff make the 8 basis points. Or let your “alpha desk” — the people at your 
fund whoare market-timing day traders — make the money. 


Antigravity

This is not investment advice, but it’s a useful heuristic: If someone tells 
you that he has invented an antigravity propulsion device that the Department 
of Defense wants to buy, but he needs some capital from you to complete the 
prototype, you should be skeptical,because <https://bloom.bg/3ECiweF> “such 
technology would violate the known laws of physics.” But if the amount of 
capital that he needs from you istwo hundred dollars, then you should be very, 
very skeptical, because that violates the known laws of economics. Not to get
too Chicago on you 
<https://www.chicagobooth.edu/review/beyond-lamppost-theory-economic-policy>, 
but if he really had a viable antigravity device, he would have gotten $200 
from someone other than you.

[The owner] couldn’t believe her luck. Had she really chanced upon an
