More on Humane
--------------

Benjamin Sandofsky, a former Apple employee, wrote a blog post entitled, Why You Can’t Build Apple with Venture Capital ( https://www.sandofsky.com/humane/ ).

Only hubris can explain Humane’s dumbest product decision: a mandatory $24/mo subscription that uses a separate phone number. Set aside the money, nobody wants to manage two numbers! I have two email addresses and regret it whenever I fill out a form. I can picture the Humane product meeting in my head. “When we released the iPhone, we didn’t design it as an accessory for your current phone. It had to be so amazing that users will be ready to go all-in. We cannot compromise Ai Pin by making it an accessory. We must burn the ships.” The flaw in this reasoning is that everything before the iPhone sucked. People were happy to switch to a smartphone, aside from $600 price tag which was hard to swallow.

Today we are so reliant on smart phones that abandoning them would upend our lives. My brokerage account uses a bespoke authentication app, so without my phone and that very specific app, I can’t access that money. That would be solved if my brokerage firm ported their authenticator to the Ai Pin, but that will never, ever happen. It’s a credit to the ridiculous momentum of the first iPhone that Apple convinced so many developers to learn a completely new set of skills, and those developers have now spent 16 years in this ecosystem. Why waste time and energy learning a new platform when it doesn’t look like there’s going to be a market for their apps?

A better strategy for AI Pin 1.0 would have been to release it as an accessory that pairs with your iPhone. Drop the cell connection to improve battery life and bring down costs. If users bring their own carrier, there’s fewer complaints about dropped connections. While we’re cutting features, drop the camera. How many people want to take photos that they can’t frame ahead of time? Save money on parts, and use that space to fit more battery. Lower costs opens things up to more users, which entice third-party developers to get into the ground floor of the next big thing. Instead, Humane tried to cosplay as Apple, adopting its “My way or the highway” attitude without any of Apple’s leverage that makes it work…

I blame the team’s nostalgia. They clearly wants to recreate the Apple from 2007, but that’s impossible under a venture capital constraints and without the momentum of Apple. Contrary to what Imran, Ken, and I’m sure many others at Humane believe, the iPhone didn’t begin with their work in the 2000’s on Project Purple. It began in 1976 with the Apple computer, and the decades of goodwill it built up in consumers. The project was spearheaded by a guy ready to waste billions in iPod revenue if it helped achieve his vision, and he answered to nobody. It came together at the perfect point in time, when everyone knew the power of the Internet, but there wasn’t a way to carry the whole experience in your pocket. You can’t replicate all these factors in a few years, no matter how much money a VC throws at you.

Sandofsky makes a lot of good points, but he misses an important one: the iPod worked with both Windows and the Mac, and, crucially, so did the iPhone. In fact, a Windows or Mac computer was required to even set up the first several generations of iPhones (the first iPhone to not require iTunes on a Windows or Mac computer was the iPhone 4S in 2011). Just as offloading library management to iTunes allowed the iPod user interface to be simplified, offloading things like contact, photo, and music syncing to a computer allowed the iPhone to be much more usable. Then, over time, Apple built up the infrastructure (particularly iCloud) that allowed the iPhone to stand alone.

Humane, however, by virtue of trying to replace a phone, dramatically increased the number of things it might do poorly, while crowding out the focus — both in terms of hardware and software — necessary to do one thing exceptionally well.

Limitless

From The Verge ( https://www.theverge.com/2024/4/15/24130832/limitless-ai-pendant-wearable-meetings ) :

The Limitless Pendant doesn’t exactly scream “AI.” As Dan Siroker, the CEO of the company behind the new device, lifts it up to show me over Zoom, the round, rubbery-looking gizmo reminds me more of an old-school clippable Fitbit. But what Siroker is actually showing me is a device that can be clipped onto your shirt or worn on a string around your neck that is meant to record everything you hear — and then use AI to help you remember and make sense of it.

The Limitless Pendant is part of the whole Limitless system, which the company is launching today. (Oh, and in case you’re wondering: yes, it’s very much a reference to the movie.) Siroker’s last AI product, Rewind, was an app that ran on your computer and would record your screen and other data in order to help you remember every tab, every song, every meeting, everything you do on your computer. (When the company first teased the Limitless Pendant, it was actually called the Rewind Pendant.) Limitless has similar aims, but instead of just running on your computer, it’s meant to collect data in the cloud and the real world, too, and make it all available to you on any device. Rewind is still around, for the folks who want the all-local, one-computer approach — but Siroker says the cross-platform opportunity is much bigger.

At the same time, I’m a bit more nervous about Limitless, which is basically Rewind, but stored in the cloud. Yes, there is a solid encryption story ( https://www.limitless.ai/#privacy ) , but still, that’s some pretty sensitive data to be storing anywhere else, and it’s not like I actually use it that much…and at the same time, the only reason why I would even consider the new offering, which will almost certainly have much better AI capabilities, is because the company proved itself with a much simpler product to start. Just as importantly, the company proved to itself that there was a market for AI-augmented remembering.

It’s the same thing with the Limitless Pendant, which will record your voice continuously, and the voices of others if they verbally assent to being recorded. I am definitely dubious — we will have to see how well this works in practice, and I’m not super enthusiastic about a world where everyone around me is constantly recording everything — but I also see the utility, thanks in part to the company’s original software product.

Moreover, while the Limitless Pendant is a wearable like the Humane AI Pin, instead of trying to replace your phone, it does one thing — record what you say so you can review it later — which is a use case and market that the company has already proven out. That simplicity of purpose, meanwhile, means the Pendant is, relative to the Humane product, a much smaller and lighter device, with a claimed 100 hours of battery life, all for a much lower cost of $99.

What is not listed is replacing your phone…or is it? “More Hardware”, many iterations down the road, if and when the Limitless becomes so popular that it becomes a platform, could absolutely include a device able to replace your phone. The critical point, though, is that such a product, should it ever come to be, ought to be just as obvious a next step as the iPhone was beyond the iPod. Until then Limitless is doing well in building up their use case step-by-step, building trust and reputation, and working with what already exists.

The iPhone Integration Barrier
------------------------------

One thing that makes the Limitless Pendant very clever is that while it will interface with an app on your phone, it doesn’t actually need a consistent connection to your phone; it’s simply recording (and bookmarking, if you press the button), with said recording uploaded to the app when it has a connection. This gets around all of the various limitations that Apple places on 3rd-party hardware maintaining a consistent connection to your iPhone, which are a significant problem for would-be chat interfaces like Humane or the Rabbit R-1 ( https://stratechery.com/2024/ai-at-ces-the-rabbit-r1/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI0L2FpLWF0LWNlcy10aGUtcmFiYml0LXIxLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzE1OTUzODg3LCJpYXQiOjE3MTMzNjE4ODcsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBpLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiUnNldVlFOGpnSGVQcFdKZjFmQWI0QyIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.tFGCe_QJ_HUU4dHlp47mG3oocAdgbtqNiFFdNPGZcyiV9tEMRP_9d2HR57PATKUDO3NDPdkq7xEHdFh9APZ98EFr_jb8E92RnxDtoLtFd-bHrpH-DiAYTJDIXI6sSg9R-ljQLJhBsG0pvhk0JmZPOfvLEgsls1Qp934N0wL--k1i_MMjDS8MBDKxVW-x1zJqEsaGSo8YocYOuJrtfUFfjquJ578nTNdTstyJO-I1HASO8tY0tMr0zFYUxHpYJdcZgD66vrXkciZRvYYpGfroUjzGWxW82WZqA7rJ2Kq5px6LxUnpSrszjDQmIw5H1S5PYHxr-Lblpm8soNKz4LC6gQ ).

The other limitation Limitless faces is that they cannot record everything on your phone screen like they can on a Mac, due to limitations built into iOS. These limitations exist for a very good reason, of course: Apple purposefully and legitimately designed iOS to be far more locked down in terms of the capabilities available to 3rd-party apps for the obvious reason that it was built in the 2000s, when the dangers of malware and viruses and bad actors was far more apparent than in the halcyon days of the decades prior, when the only focus was on increasing the capabilities available to developers and users.

At the same time, notice that “Agents” item on Limitless’ roadmap: because their Mac or Windows application can “see” the whole screen, the potential for the app to “learn” how to do things is much greater; this is what the R-1 Rabbit says it is doing on the server:

The device, though, is only part of the value proposition: I mentioned that all of the actual compute is happening on the cloud, in this case via agents. The idea is that Rabbit has created what they call a “Large Action Model” which is trained on graphical user interfaces to understand their different components, what buttons do, how content is organized on a website, etc. What that means is that when a user makes a request — “Play Taylor Swift” — the cloud application actually navigates the Spotify interface (assuming you have logged in via OAuth on your “Rabbit Hole” website) and plays Taylor Swift.

Agent capability and sustainability are, needless to say, an open question, particularly in these early days. What is so enticing about the approach, though, is that it provides the promise of massive compatibility without having to actually recruit developers to your platform. Indeed, this might be the business case for the hardware: it’s the most interesting and eye-catching way for Rabbit to show off their “large action model”.

This can’t happen on your phone, though, because they are too locked down — again, for very good reasons! At the same time, are we sure that 3rd-party devices like Rabbit or Humane — or a future Limitless device — couldn’t have a better, more persistent connection to our phone?

This ties back to the DOJ case against Apple ( https://stratechery.com/2024/united-states-v-apple/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI0L3VuaXRlZC1zdGF0ZXMtdi1hcHBsZS8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTcxNTk1Mzg4NywiaWF0IjoxNzEzMzYxODg3LCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwaS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IlJzZXVZRThqZ0hlUHBXSmYxZkFiNEMiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.eioyutIsI87C1f1xiyWD3vrSxfrqlfPsWP-gU20nmRTb6XQ-MLVAtpmdNRLVfLH3vxkuieDPTcvpGndMVj0JgxKJXPUfv5g3HGV5ch4zVLOC5IcRdhvm5AUmYrl1y9_hbi8NttRwpzRXvcn5Z4ERUghDGqPxiNWhu95-ZdFMH7r3Jw8Ue1LuMyYiDNrUerEO0F_HtKEL7l2oazfkxY5Dve-WVvKgr46Mrmmy63VJ7lVqoHnTpB8shugBLJdxNr2BnVEYKo8-ldyciM-eNwMgmB332uEqIdNqNFtWBfCqkmHUNuiyCrvOPfCPOZQmBtVrTtDgWF6EkZ3B09Ob3cad4g ) , specifically the section alleging that Apple is unfairly using private APIs to deliver a superior smartwatch. From the complaint ( https://www.justice.gov/opa/media/1344546/dl?inline ) :

Apple’s smartwatch — Apple Watch — is only compatible with the iPhone. So, if Apple can steer a user towards buying an Apple Watch, it becomes more costly for that user to purchase a different kind of smartphone because doing so requires the user to abandon their costly Apple Watch and purchase a new, Android-compatible smartwatch…

Apple uses its control of the iPhone, including its technical and contractual control of critical APIs, to degrade the functionality of third-party cross-platform smartwatches in at least three significant ways: First, Apple deprives iPhone users with third-party smartwatches of the ability to respond to notifications. Second, Apple inhibits third-party smartwatches from maintaining a reliable connection with the iPhone. And third, Apple undermines the performance of third-party smartwatches that connect directly with a cellular network. In doing so, Apple constrains user choice and crushes innovation that might help fill in the moat around Apple’s smartphone monopoly.

The broader question of private APIs for external hardware, though, is a very big deal for tech innovation going forward. I quoted that blog post from Sandofsky extensively at the beginning because of his systematic takedown of Humane’s insistence on being independent of the iPhone, but the other question to raise is if Humane could have even functioned as an iPhone accessory? I don’t think so, and that strikes me as a big problem for innovation going forward.
