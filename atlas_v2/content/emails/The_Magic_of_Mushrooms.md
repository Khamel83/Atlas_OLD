The Magic of Mushrooms
Let’s Take a Trip
What do you think of when you think of psychedelics?
Maybe you think of hippies in the 1960s. Maybe you think of Phish concerts. Maybe you think about that one time in college. Maybe you just think: those are drugs, they’re illegal, they’re dangerous, and they’re bad. Just Say No.
To steal a phrase from Michael Pollan’s bestselling 2018 book on the benefits of psychedelics, I want you to Change Your Mind [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFULluwzAM_ZposyHJko9BQxGgW7cunQzaYh0hNmXoqOF-fZVkKECQ4PH4yDdDwsWH0-w-JpYjhtFZI1rZN1oNzBplRa975uL4HRA3cKtJISPb87S6GZLz9ERwLjW7GSVFq7vvjnOY2hkGrjqB7dTqFqToVMEVnhGydUgzGvzBcHpCtppbSnu8NG8X-V7sOI4aNvj1VM9-K4XrDWjB6svnUH04stXVU5ydz5EwxuozQEmR7GNtGbd7cUIPSnIly2XOSC65EHwQje4bWYtaU1iOAGKyeFF8W2Qd8xQTzPcHJQuGfJp8cLSU9v0GG67PRvl8LHHL5NI5IsG0on2Jkl4yPn9M546G8IgrpoThVXwo1fNByq5jhc36Iij9E_0Bz8qHMQ]. By the end of this essay, you’ll understand why we underestimate psychedelics, the promise they hold in solving our biggest mental health challenges, and the business model behind the first public psychedelics company in the US, COMPASS Pathways.
It’s easy to get excited about psychedelics. MDMA combined with therapy looks like it may cure PTSD in a large number of patients. Psilocybin with therapy is showing promise in fighting depression. And unlike traditional medication, psychedelics can cure while creating meaning. In a 2006 Johns Hopkins study [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFkMGKxCAQRL9mPIbWJDPx4GEv8xvBaJs0Y1TUbMjfrzNzWGgoqKIp6hldcY35UimWyo6CeSar-F1M_ThIZtVg-TROjMrsMuKuyauaD2TpWDwZXSmGzweAGNmmXA-uH-EhpRu4kWBACAuTvkstrAPD3j2zPixhMKjwF_MVAzKvtlpTufU_N_Fsd55nt8X0olB2tGQoYBfz2pKUsZQ5o0ddsDRDANybrJmco7qVVMhHcy0UumQdIyVAAOcgeT9Oveh4N4a8nlnzxeJtgH0VXTmWUrV5dSbuLKsQ6xIzhbXFr03v6D9B2zw33Y9A9Zox6MWj_eKoX4CfdfVKqAKexWOtmL_mm9EEUojHg7U2GxvK8F_0B52Ph60], 67% of participants who took psilocybin rated the experience either the most meaningful or among the top five most meaningful in their lives! As Matthew Johnson, the Associate Director of the Johns Hopkins Center for Psychedelic and Consciousness Research wrote [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFkMGugyAQRb-m7DCA2uqCxdu83zAIUyVVMMNY49-_sV28hADhzsy9HO8Ipoyn3XIhsRfAIQar76ar26YXwTZBd20nYhmeCLC6uFjCHcS2j0v0jmJOnw6lTCtmWzdd02rXBP80da9Uf2-0Cvc2eF_3znXi8hncHiIkDxbegGdOIBY7E23lVv_czC-v4ziq4rmI4pNtVkDeU-XzyqJDin4BvtEMCEWmLKecg4R3DNdcSbMjuZXTzxCAcxbJ3dLPLk0gz7yj3PIS6RIySuSSiT_CA0W0Rhmltep13Xa1qXTVJpwOdHoMcGvUOpmq7GMh519XHoE2ZRozxjSx_Jo57PIRGNHA57qnSOcAyY0LhC89-vL-wKBzA5vgKAsQAX4fL6Sd6o15PAS7hczk07_RH_TemWs], “Psychedelics are psychoactive substances that historically have attracted exaggerations of benefits as well as alarmism.”

The Psychedelic Renaissance
Because of long-held negative or recreational associations, most people are unaware that psychedelics are the most promising treatment for a wide range of mental health issues -- from depression to alcoholism to anorexia -- that we’ve ever seen. 
The psychedelic renaissance couldn’t have come at a better time. The world desperately needs innovative solutions to mental health disorders. The worldwide numbers are staggering. 
And those numbers are pre-COVID. According to a JAMA Network Survey [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxVUctu2zAQ_BrxFoOiLIk68OCkTRogTnvrkeBjJbPhQ-Cjhv--lBygLUCQy1nMDLmjRIYlxBtbQ8qoJIjcaNYOhHb9cUKaHXVLe4pM4nMEcMJYlmMBtBZpjRLZBL8zMCY9ujCg89wLMkI_q5l2I9ZKTqOQo9TDSAeMNh8uijbgFTD4DfEWPCDLLjmvqelODXmu65dwwkO-hvhxUMFtSCjRC5v-b4YVfEXmYq2I2SgL9UbGEbfHoemeS3Y8VaKCpvvyYoMUtiGP30DYfKnF-_efdT8LUzUe30zKDRk2ihJuFWbxlTTLiY5UaCIfvp5Pr2_86XT-cXp9eecEE8zxxHHHMeG4_-Q60Ka4ytyH9QlmiBuEOdVY44FoqR_-USZ0whS3yLBNtG3x1HY97cihPfQ-LtcoWqmhOWK3kEMqMmWh9rmgyHzIMkTjl9r-uAgHdm_UfHg9XfEm3zh4IS3oe3T5HvaeRL6twDxck4VcH3kHtzwpnkidI6puOtSf-L9GfwAGH7wR], depressive symptoms have tripled during the pandemic.
The total direct and indirect costs of the mental health epidemic are expected to reach $6 trillion by 2030, up from $2.5 trillion in 2010, according to the World Economic Forum. The Lancet Commission estimates that number to be as high as $16 trillion when you include the loss of productivity, and spending on social welfare, education, and law and order. Despite the huge need, the last real innovation in the fight against mental illness was the release of Prozac in 1988.
After fifty years in the dark, though, psychedelics are once again getting the chance to shine. Led by public figures like Michael Pollan, Tim Ferriss, and even Joe Rogan, and leading institutions like Johns Hopkins, NYU, Berkeley, and Imperial College London, therapeutic psychedelics are going mainstream again: 
On election night, Oregon voted to legalize psilocybin [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFkEmOhDAMRU9T2QVlgCpYZNGbvgbKYCCqDChDI_r0HYpFS5Gt5Nv-ztOywBrTKfaYC6oZ0myNoE828qGfkBG9oeMwIpvnJQF4aZ0oqQLaq3JWy2Jj-HQQwga0iReXPX0tymjF9cCfGqYJ-LNfOCPQt5LLZ5bVWAgaBPxAOmMA5MRWyp4f_OvBvts5jqPTQelOR9-ujDDSEqUtkL6FmNraAStoOmS82JQLzqX9BpeIHazS2V_AXq5WY1_zlmL0GcuMfWu9KzMGmQGbVFfs5JG7rXiHrLjMKCUT5cPIWUe7IaT1SJIqA4-e-JV1uao2Qr-v9VASIRYVkw1rk9-b9OA-QmM0t-xrsOWcIUjlwNz4yg38Q6OcO4gAR3ZQCqT78WI6komx1ws1NxMb-vBv9AcRqpdr] for mental health treatment in supervised settings. Washington, DC voted to decriminalize it. 
The FDA granted Breakthrough Therapy Designation, which they grant to therapies that have shown great promise and clinically significant improvement over available therapies, to three trials using psychedelics to treat mental health indications, one with MDMA and two with psilocybin, the active ingredient in magic mushrooms. 
Last week, Johns Hopkins released a new study [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFkUGOpDAMRU9T2YFCKLpgkUVv-hooJL8hUyRBsRnE7SdUlTSSJVv-sr79bA1jTvnUWyIWOyGP3unmS_Vtdx-E03fX9F0vPI2_GQjGr5rzDrHt0-qtYZ_ia0JK1YlFA5N0bWecedgJrVPK3RvIXyuHL2ftIC6f0ezOI1po_EU-U4RY9cK80a39vqmfEsdx1Evanj5SgPPWR9Qpz0WJOOiTckrhU1YZKwzhkjY67QKHsl_FGYYDIleH56XayK_JnpOP14Av9lQF8yflymHLICrnVMS7Oyta0kHCayWVbBo5NG3Xt6pu6i7m-cimmRxudxlmVdM-ERv7rG0KIuuYeErZx7nIz8UErC-hEBtLDnv0fI6IZlrh3jD5jf_Fhs8N-jppBTPyu3kR7uWg1OMhiptL5RHxv9E_Z3Gh2A] that showed that treatment with psilocybin and supportive psychotherapy produced rapid and large reductions in depressive symptoms in adults with major depression, with a magnitude of effect four times larger than antidepressants’.
At this point, you might be thinking: dope. This sounds amazing. Give people shrooms! But this isn’t a healthcare newsletter. In September, COMPASS Pathways [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFUMGOhSAQ-xo5GkBROXDYy_6GQZlV8hAIDGv8-0U9bDLJZNo0nXbVCFtIl4ohIykZ0myNYgOfOtFLYlRv2CQmYvP8kwAObZ3CVIDEsji7arTBPwpKuSC7YqMQ_SA5G9eBg2Sy52KZhgGMqMA4kNtn1sVY8Cso-IV0BQ_EqR0x5qb7avh3nTUcUeccNe6nvnJb74oSqzjllDEqWSemjresFT5tZ9JsMdD09Nh4m8uSUa-fW0SS8gGXkKzfKv3Z9QHuIWqAue6jeIvXDF4vDsybDd82nlfxiqA8nNkBIqQXvANPVHI-jqS6mVB78f9GfyYOdCE] (NASDAQ: CMPS [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFUMtqxDAM_Jr4GGzn5Rx8KAu9FQr9gOCHNjHr2MFWuuTv6ySHgkCPQTMaGYUwx3TILWYke4Y0OStZz0XTtSOxsrVMdIK4PD0TwKqcl5h2INuuvTMKXQzXBqW8I4tsG9ZqxgfDoe9bxQWwngmh7fBkHaMjOXUmtVsHwYCEX0hHDEC8XBC3XDUfFf8scdPXJq6lyRjNK5fi8fX9Q5zklFNWyFjTiYbXrO5Cmt9JMW2hauk68zrvOqMyr5OBJBki6phcmAv8WtQK_gKKylTyugeHxwRBaQ_29of3R65z8dhABnhnD4iQ7uFpWtCR82EgRc3G8pvwL_QHmmV05A]) IPO’d and became the first public company with a psilocybin-based product. The War On Drugs
In this time of vicious political division in the United States, I think there’s one thing that all Americans can agree on: Richard Nixon was a real dick. 
In addition to being the Watergate guy, in 1971, Nixon declared a War on Drugs [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFkMGOhCAQRL9muI0BFMUDh73sbxiQHocdBdPAGv9-25nDJoSudKUo8mZbYEl4mj3lwmoGnII3ope6Vd3IvOm80EqzkKcHAmw2rKZgBbZXt4bZlpDiO8G5VOxp3Oh66AfbidbPXav7h1R29NKO2vdSeXb1TLb6AHEGA7-AZ4rAVvMsZc-39usmv-kcx9HYDZAq4o5pQci5SbiQFXKukEnMGLYQ7Xr_qbmEGWiFsCcslym50DR4f-mBrk5JLUYSEH2Iy_2wePdYl3yPdXOAV4gFIzklBR9Fq3QrG9GoiMuBVjgPt45vi2xydbnY-dXMaWNoYiouIT1J9utJn17fBgGaaG41hnJOEK1bwX_YlQ_tN4py7mAiHHmFUgA_ywuo5qOUw8CozSfiHv-L_gCLF5Rk] that has, among other evils, cost the United States over $1 trillion, disproportionately targeted Black people, and led to an arrest for drug possession every 25 seconds.
The War on Drugs cost millions of American lives and prevented tens of millions more from their Constitutional right to the pursuit of happiness. It’s the reason that we lump all Schedule One drugs together as “bad and dangerous” despite their vastly different profiles, while doctors happily write prescriptions for the opioids that kill 128 Americans every day [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFUMuOwyAM_JpyjIA0LTlw2Mv-RgTYTdDyiMA0m79f2h5WsmSPR_ZoxhnCNZdT77kSaxXL4kGLm1TjdJ0Z6CsINSnm6_IoiNH4oKk0ZHuzwTtDPqf3BedyYps2kxjVLFBZK0H2PxLR2Rs4UGjG24O9dBbTwGNyqPGJ5cwJWdAb0V4v49dFfvc6jmNw4IY1PzuC0vqABXLFDnH3gNG7PvoE-DtsFAPzWnLJheCzGCc1ykEMUyrrUYywgJcrj6scarOVjPsZXI6s6JTJ5uLT2umfzUQMb6KbW3qPLXk6F0zGBoSPb_ok9bZB54464VEDEmH5LF9hKD5Leb-zrga5Z5b-hf4AFGp_pg]. 
So why’d he do it? 
In 1955, a New York banker named R. Gordon Wasson went to Mexico in search of a god-like mushroom, and detailed his experiences in a 1957 Life Magazine article called “Seeking the Magic Mushroom [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFUMuOwyAM_JpyRGCSlhw47GV_I4LgJmh5ROBslL9f2h5WsmRrRtY8Fku4lnqZvTRiR8M6B2_kHbQah4l5M3ipR81Cm58VMdkQDdUD2X64GBZLoeT3hxAwss0AqIfDJyzOD1ppYaWGQXkB8u7V5Dx76cz28AHzggZ_sV4lI4tmI9pv6usG333O8-Qh2TVkW4Plpa4v0LZWcj9ieCLfKLFgQICQUkxSjVoBl3zMdT2rlc7jbRBpBd4O18guP3wpiVWTC7lSQ147_bPZhPFN9Dxz3-nIga4Zs3UR_Scqfcp5O6drR5PxbBGJsH7AV34tJoDHg3U1X3pN-V_oD8yUeg4],” bringing the experience into the western consciousness. He also brought psilocybin into the lab. He sent mushroom samples to Albert Hofmann, a Sandoz scientist who had previously discovered LSD, and in 1958, Hofmann isolated the chemical structure of the active compound. By 1960, Sandoz released Psilocybin pills under the name Indocybin. 
With Indocybin, scientists and psychologists began doing real research on psychedelics’ potential benefits. According to the Beckley Foundation [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFUMtuxCAM_Jrl1ghI2CUHDr30NyIeToKWQASmUf6-bHKoZMljj8Yjj9UIS8qn2lNBUgvkyTvFnlz2YhiJU4NjUkjiyzRngE37oDBXIHs1wVuNPsVLQSkXZFVSMGl7I9zryUQvZmmYGAewz9kBE4KTj8-kq_MQLSj4hXymCCSoFXEvj_77wX9aHcfRGbDvAOecanSXUZfy0rhiL_EHVVNQN1y-NsA1udKWe_Eh2dP42AbiFaecMkZH1gvZ8451IublyJoZB4-Bbgvv7jv23dm0kaxiQpOyj0uj36veIFxEe3lqfavR4zlB1CaAu9PAO7_rOTx3UBGOEgAR8r38RCTpyPnrRZqbSy3J-G_0By5hh2s], the number of scientific studies published on psilocybin grew from five in 1958 to forty in 1968. The American Psychologists Association focused on LSD research, specifically a paper titled Personality change associated with psychedelic (LSD) therapy [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFUMmOwyAM_Zpya2QgCzlw6GV-I2JxUtQEInCmyt8PbQ4jWbL1nqy3OEO4pHzqPRViR8E8Ba95L5Ts2pF53XquOsVCmeaMuJmwasoHsv2wa3CGQorfDwDRsadWIFGNspfWcul7L3Ac_AzSub41wszsozOZwweMDjX-Yj5TRLbqJ9FebvJxEz919nK6iNSY3TQpLxXJ6FL29eBj39-BDwB3AM6CFiCAcxi57JQUDW-6mJd3Ntx6vLWwLaIphy1k3KtxaWNZx0Q25RCXSr-eZsP1S9RMU93bEQOdE0ZjV_RXXLoK-rqnc0cd8V1WJMJ8gZ8OFIxCDAOraj7VquK_0B9NFHk2] at its 1964 meeting. 
But psychedelics jumped from the lab and into the counterculture. In 1960, Harvard clinical psychologist Timothy Leary started the Harvard Psilocybin Project [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFUEGOwyAQe005RkBKGw4c9rLaY3-ABpimbAlEA2mV3y9tDyuNZI0ty7I9NJwL7WYttbGtItkYjDjJaVRHzYI5BjGpicVqr4S4QEym0YZs3VyKHlos-e3gXCp2M1qCVojd4oQM6PSIboTpdNIQrlx1X8-xsIWI2aPBB9JeMrJkbq2t9TB-HeR3P8zDM97jiiHCUGju1Ovv8AP0AAr2UmMqfncx2wuVX_SNRSO55EJwLUY1jXIQg8o0PwmEC3g48mWWQ91cbeDvgy8LI5NLc4Vinrt8v8GC6S30erbjsuXYdosZXMLwad4-W72LtH1Fk_FZE7aG9CFfc0xcS3k-s54WSl8t_wf9AU-pgL4] with Richard Alpert (who would later become Ram Dass) using psilocybin they ordered from Sandoz. The Project undertook some legitimate research, like the Marsh Chapel Experiment in which divinity students were administered the drug in a chapel to determine whether it could facilitate profound religious states (ten out of ten self-reported that it did), but largely devolved into the epicenter of recreational psilocybin and LSD use.
Harvard shut down the project in 1962, but Leary continued his work spreading the word about mushrooms and LSD. In 1966, he famously told America’s youth to “Turn on, tune in, drop out.” Despite pleading from serious researchers who feared that Leary was going to “wreak havoc on all of us doing LSD work all over the nation,” Leary pushed on, like the friend who gets too high and gets everyone in trouble. According to Pollan, Leary said things like “LSD is more frightening than the bomb,” and “The kids who take LSD aren’t going to fight your wars. They’re not going to join your corporations.” 
The FDA didn’t like the sound of that, ordering in 1966 that researchers halt all work with psychedelics. Neither did Tricky Dick. In 1970, in large part to slow down the anti-war counterculture movement, the Nixon government passed the Controlled Substances Act, which classified psilocybin and LSD as Schedule One drugs, which have no medical use and a high potential for abuse. In 1971 Richard Nixon declared the War on Drugs and labeled Leary “the most dangerous man in America.”
When Harper’s writer Dan Baum tracked Nixon’s domestic policy chief John Ehrlichman down in 1994, Erlichman admitted [https://email.mg2.substack.com/c/eJxFUMuOwyAM_Jpy24hHaMmBw172NyIeJkElEBnSKvv1S9vDSpYfY1ujGWcaLAVPvZfayFEB5-g1u3Il5DgRr0fPlFQk1jkgwGZi0g0PIPthU3SmxZLfH5RySVY9jXCzwYVAg5mEdMoEp64TFUzwYKUlL57ZHD5CdqDhAXiWDCTptbW9XsT3hf_0WA3ugHUouPTJoFvjA3rHKbv2QseeEiwmxV_4iu3LpNQREjWn_YTRiQmpBB_YIDMuTzTMeriMdFv4UA9bm3H3wZWNoM6l2YIxL319X80G6b3oAudetyPHds6QjU3gP9rbx623lHbuoDM8a4LWAD_gyxBFJ85vN9LZfOm-5X-iP1rafps] that the War on Drugs was an indirect way to target the two enemies of the Nixon administration: the antiwar movement and Black people. “Did we know we were lying about drugs? Of course we did.” 
In a total narc move, the US also pushed other countries to follow its drug scheduling guidelines. The 1971 UN treaty, the Convention on Psychotropic Substances, largely adopted the US scheduling, grouping psilocybin with drugs like heroin and crack cocaine and effectively halting research on its benefits worldwide. 
Despite being built atop a lie, the War on Drugs and its associated propaganda worked for decades. It’s why we’re just getting back to psychedelics now. 
When I was in 7th grade, we had to read a book. I forget what it was called or what it was about; the only thing I remember is that the main character’s older brother did acid once, lived in a flop house, and had frequent flashbacks in which spiders crawled all over his body for years.
It surprised me to find out many years and “no acid for me, thanks!”s later that LSD and mushrooms are the two safest drugs out there on the active/lethal dose and dependence potential scale. All weekend, I was drinking beers and coffee; turns out I would have been much safer tripping. 
The War on Drugs was like the Food Pyramid on crack, tricking generations of Americans into making unhealthy decisions despite the best intentions. Nixon started it, and Raegan picked up where he left off, waging an all-out media offensive. In 1987, the Partnership for a Drug-Free America dropped the classic “This is Your Brain on Drugs” commercial.
