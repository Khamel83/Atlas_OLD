Good evening and welcome back to Wall Power. I’m Marion Maneker—and I’ll be your Virgil through the next week of auction hell.

Some call it Gigaweek. Okay, no one really calls it Gigaweek. That’s just a term I made up several years ago when the traditional structure of two separate weeks of art sales—one for impressionist and modern, another for contemporary—collapsed into a single exhausting week and the total sales broke above $1 billion. The first sales kick off tomorrow night at Sotheby’s with the Sydell Miller estate and the various-owners sale of modern art. I’ll have those results in Tuesday’s edition of this email, and will share my first impressions of the season then, too.

In the meantime, if you want to get regular updates from me during the sales, sign up for the SMS channel ( https://puck.news/wall-power-sms/?utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ), where I’ll be texting out anything noteworthy. Afterward, I’ll try to send out a summary and results. And, as always, if there’s something you think I need to know, you can text me at 917.825.1391 or use that same number on Signal, if you’re worried about anonymity.

Tonight’s main story concerns the latest happenings in the fight for control of Artnet—a boardroom comic opera that’s currently experiencing a mysterious intermission.

But first…

* That Cattelan token is bananas: When last we checked in on the memecoin version ( https://www.coingecko.com/en/coins/comedian?utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) of Maurizio Cattelan’s Comedian, it seemed like a harmless bit of fun. Sure, the market cap of the crypto token had reached $20 million, but the volatility suggested that might just be a blip. After all, the crypto community apparently frowned upon the involvement of Sotheby’s Michael Bouhanna in launching the coin. More relevant, perhaps, was the fact that crowds
weren’t showing up to view the duct-tape-and-banana work, which originally sold for $120,000 in 2019. The artwork changed hands earlier this year for a number that is likely above $2 million. The buyer promised the artwork would not be flipped, but the current sale was announced just a few months later. On Wednesday, we’ll learn the fate of Cattelan’s Comedian when it’s auctioned at Sotheby’s with an estimate of $1 million.

We don’t have to wait to see what happens with the memecoin. Early last week, the token—dubbed Comedian (Ban)—jumped in trading from a market cap of $29.5 million to $95 million and eventually $235 million, before settling around $175 million. Don’t get caught up in that market cap number. It makes a nice headline, but no one is going to take out the whole of the token float. So it isn’t worth anything. But that doesn’t stop folks from trading a few hundred to a few thousand dollars on the memecoin’s
momentum.

Everyone is having fun and some of the trades are turning into real money. I saw trades at $30,000, $21,000, and $18,000. Not lose-your-shirt money for some people, but still nothing to lose lightly. And, of course, such high levels of trading volume each day from such small trades means a lot of people are keeping tabs on the auction of a banana duct-taped to a wall. Maybe some of them will actually bid?

Real Money for Photorealism

John Kacere, Untitled (with White Slip), from 1989, at Gratin Gallery

In the late 1960s and early 1970s, a loose group of painters began creating images that mimicked the flatness and reflections on surfaces captured by photographs. The critical and market appraisal for these painters, who became known as the photorealists, has waxed and waned for some time. Recently, however, there have been stirrings on the market side. Richard Estes, the best-known among the group, made three of his four highest prices at auction last year. Work by Robert Bechtle, who died in 2020, was shown ( https://www.gladstonegallery.com/exhibition/10771/robert-bechtle-2023/installation-views?utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) at
Gladstone Gallery in Chelsea last year, too. (Posthumous shows are usually more directional, or a sign of support for an artist’s estate.) A small diner still life by Ralph Goings was sold for more than twice the estimate in October at Sotheby’s. A much larger painting of a ketchup bottle ( https://www.bonhams.com/auction/29521/lot/10A/ralph-goings-1928-2016-ketchup-close-up-44-x-60-in-1117-x-1525-cm-painted-in-1990/?utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) is on offer at Bonhams with an estimate of $180,000. In 2007, a similar image in almost the same size was sold for $481,000 at auction. The current estimate is a big step back, part of the retreat I’ve been trying to describe. But it’s also an enticement which could
draw some of the attention on Estes back to Goings if it sells really well.

Also at Bonhams is a painting ( https://www.bonhams.com/auction/30364/lot/124/john-kacere-1920-1999-joelle-89-39-78-x-60-14-in-1012-x-1531-cm-painted-in-1989/?utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) by John Kacere, who never liked to be grouped with the photorealists and has a less wholesome visual vocabulary than Goings, Robert Cottingham, Estes, and some of the others. Kacere began his career as an abstract painter, but found inspiration in close-ups of women, with particular preference for a posterior point of view. Like the pop artist Mel Ramos, who painted pinups with what we now call consumer packaged goods, Kacere’s
paintings are uncomfortable and arresting. The viewer has to wonder whether the uncanny quality of Kacere’s work comes from the photorealist fidelity or the obvious fetishism.

The Kacere at Bonhams probably won’t reach a high value, but it may offer further evidence of photorealism’s rising tide. Loic Gouzer recently auctioned a Kacere for more than $250,000, a record price. And, on November 21, the Grand Street gallery Gratin ( https://www.gratin.com/?utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) will open a show of eight more paintings and drawings by Kacere gathered from various consignors and on offer at prices ranging from $35,000 to $250,000.

The titanic struggle for control of Artnet, the media and data company that provides a pivot point for the entire art market, was scheduled to come to a head at tomorrow’s annual general meeting. Artnet is controlled by the Neuendorf family, which owns 26 percent of the company’s shares. A slightly larger percentage of the shares, which are traded on the German stock exchange, are owned by Rudiger Weng, who’s been campaigning to win a seat on the three-person board of directors. Weng believes he finally has the votes, which would subsequently allow him to oust C.E.O. Jacob Pabst, the 52-year-old son of Hans Neuendorf, the company’s 87-year-old founder. But late last week, the meeting was canceled without warning.

This isn’t the first time the Neuendorf family has used delays and deflection to retain
control of the company. In 2012, when another group of investors were trying to grab the reins, Neuendorf replaced himself as C.E.O. with his son. Since then, his daughter and two other sons have taken up executive roles—though one son has since left the company.

In 2020, the company’s annual general meeting—a requirement for listed firms in Germany—was postponed because Pabst claimed to be ill. By that point, however, Weng had already begun to amass his substantial position in the company. He agitated for change in 2021, 2022, and 2023—but he couldn’t convince enough shareholders to cross the 50 percent threshold. Now, after delays in the release of the company’s 2023 financials and last week’s rug pull, investors may finally be getting fed up.

The curious thing about last week’s canceled A.G.M. was the reason. Instead of putting off the investors’ forum by claiming illness—which might have been hard to believe, but also hard to refute—the company said the delay was being caused by a problem with their auditors, the same firm that produced the 2023 financials. “The general
meeting is being canceled,” wrote Sophie Neuendorf, who handles the company’s investor relations, “because the auditing firm […] has informed the Company that it is not available for a further audit.”

This is somewhat unusual: Artnet got a new auditor last year, as German law requires companies to swap auditors every five years. But it appears the new auditor is relinquishing its lucrative assignment after completing its review of Artnet’s 2023 financials. That report revealed several transactions involving entities linked to family members, including the C.E.O.’s wife. The report also showed that Hans Neuendorf was still being paid six-figure consulting fees, even though Weng told me he’d agreed to stop after 2022. In addition, the auditors reported family members were paid $200,000 or more in related-party payments. There were also more than $2 million in loans from both Galerie Neuendorf and an Artnet executive, as well as a private
party in New York.

So why did the auditors bail on Artnet? “Following a complaint from a key shareholder about the costs,” the company said in a statement, “the auditor informed Artnet’s management that they would not be able to carry out the audit for the 2024 financial year.” Not surprisingly, Weng told me there’s more to the story. After all, he was the key shareholder. And he says the auditors had made it clear two months ago, in September, that they wouldn’t be sticking around.

Both sides agree that the audit wasn’t a pretty process. It “took longer than anticipated,” Artnet said in a statement, “as it was a first-time audit and presented additional challenges associated with evaluating companies with a strong technological backbone.” Weng said the process was delayed by Pabst’s foot-dragging and the board’s lack of cooperation, leading the auditors to submit invoices that were twice the estimate.

Both
sides were unhappy with the auditor’s fees. What happened next is disputed. Weng says Pabst asked for his help with the auditors. “I discussed the topic with him,” Weng told me, “and he asked me whether I could support him. Which I did with a letter to the auditors. And Jacob thanked me for doing it. I wanted to pave a way for him to negotiate a better price.” But a representative of the company says Pabst never asked Weng to write to the auditors.

Awaiting a Showdown

It’s not at all clear why Artnet believes an issue with auditors was reason enough to cancel its shareholder meeting, let alone days before the event. Even if you believe Artnet’s version of events, the auditors quit three weeks ago. Why not inform shareholders right away, allowing them time to cancel plans to travel to Berlin?

Weng made clear in a statement last Thursday that he, too, was annoyed by the delay, describing the cancellation as “unlawful and made in an abusive manner” and revealing that his company has begun the legal process to compel the meeting.

It’s hard to know if Weng has the votes to change the company’s leadership, but the company’s stock price has gone up 30 percent in the last month, perhaps in anticipation of his showdown with the Neuendorfs at the now-canceled meeting. The stock bump is a bit of a mystery otherwise, considering that, last week, Artnet released a dour financial report for the third quarter: Overall revenue fell 5.4 percent in the first nine months of 2024, attributable to a 3.8 percent decline in media revenue and a 5.8 percent drop in revenue from the sale of art. Gross profit was down 12 percent. Even in good years, Artnet has struggled to turn much of a profit—the source of much of the friction between the shareholders and the Neuendorfs. But for the first nine months of 2024, operating income fell to negative €1.35 million and operating cash flow was
down 45 percent.

Yet the stock price remains at a nine-month high, which isn’t great news for the Neuendorfs, who previously enlisted passive investors to buy the company’s stock at depressed prices and vote with their bloc. If the stock isn’t budging after the grim financials, it means that shareholders are sticking around; they haven’t given up on the company, but they may have given up on the Neuendorfs.
