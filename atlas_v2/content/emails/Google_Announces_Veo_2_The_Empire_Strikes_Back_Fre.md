Google Announces Veo 2
----------------------

From TechCrunch ( https://techcrunch.com/2024/12/16/google-deepmind-unveils-a-new-video-model-to-rival-sora/ ) :

Google DeepMind, Google’s flagship AI research lab, wants to beat OpenAI at the video-generation game — and it might just, at least for a little while. On Monday, DeepMind announced Veo 2, a next-gen video-generating AI and the successor to Veo, which powers a growing number of products across Google’s portfolio. Veo 2 can create two-minute-plus clips in resolutions up to 4k (4096 x 2160 pixels). Notably, that’s 4x the resolution — and over 6x the duration — OpenAI’s Sora can achieve.

It’s a theoretical advantage for now, granted. In Google’s experimental video creation tool, VideoFX, where Veo 2 is now exclusively available, videos are capped at 720p and eight seconds in length. (Sora can produce up to 1080p, 20-second-long clips.) VideoFX is behind a waitlist, but Google says it’s expanding the number of users who can access it this week.

Eli Collins, VP of product at DeepMind, also told TechCrunch that Google will make Veo 2 available via its Vertex AI developer platform “as the model becomes ready for use at scale.” “Over the coming months, we’ll continue to iterate based on feedback from users,” Collins said, “and [we’ll] look to integrate Veo 2’s updated capabilities into compelling use cases across the Google ecosystem … [W]e expect to share more updates next year.”

The overarching reason to write that piece, though, is that Dall-E 2 blew my mind.

What is interesting in retrospect is that the image blow-my-mind moment actually happened before the text blow-my-mind moment, which was the ChatGPT release in November 2022. That’s the opposite order from how gaming and the Internet progressed, but that is because the contraints were different: gaming was limited by the ability to display graphics, which came later than the ability to display text, and the Internet was limited by bandwidth and standards development.

The constraint with blow-your-mind generative AI, on the other hand, is coherence: does the final product make sense? In this images actually have a big advantage: first, the amount of data necessary to make a compelling model has proven to be much less, and second, an image is just a moment in time; there are still certain “rules” that early image generation struggled with, like hands or text, but even the mistakes could be forgiven as creative license. Text, on the other hand, not only needs to have the proper word order and grammar (the “rules”), it also needs to have a beginning, middle, and end that is consistent; it was this overall coherency that made ChatGPT the second big blow-your-mind moment in generative AI.

This is all a preamble to say that the third blow-your-mind moment in generative AI — this time for video — has arrived. There are a bunch of examples on X demonstrating Veo 2’s capabilities, but I liked this post that compared the various video generation models’ depiction of slicing a steak:

What is so impressive is the coherence: Veo 2 is the only model that actually keeps track of the scene as it progresses. The knife-wielder moves in ways that makes sense, slices once cut stay where they should be, etc.; every other model is impressive in that they exist and point to what might be possible, much like Dall-E 1 or GPT-2. Veo 2 actually does it.

The Empire Strikes Back
-----------------------

Veo 2 isn’t the only recent AI announcement from Google:

* Google announced Imagen 3 ( https://blog.google/technology/google-labs/video-image-generation-update-december-2024/ ) , the latest version of their image generation tool, and probably the best one available ( https://x.com/emollick/status/1868715099793338552?s=46 ).

* Google announced Gemini 2.0 and Gemini 2.0 Flash ( https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/compute/trillium-tpu-is-ga ) , which undergirds everything else.

The reason to focus on Veo 2, however, is not just that it strikes me as a seminal moment in generative AI history, but that it also is the most powerful manifestation of the advantages that Google has in this space. Think about the three pillars of generative AI:

* *Algorithms:* Google of course invented the transformer, but it certainly seems likely that the company figured out something important in terms of maintaining coherence (which I complained about with Sora ( https://stratechery.com/2024/chatgpt-pro-o1-pro-sora/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI0L2NoYXRncHQtcHJvLW8xLXByby1zb3JhLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzM3MDM2ODgyLCJpYXQiOjE3MzQ0NDQ4ODIsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBpLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiUnNldVlFOGpnSGVQcFdKZjFmQWI0QyIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.TTLGAlbrAffTiaYLYbxEXB2C2fktYlAft_NxHyQSFk0bBYVuO7wk6jY7Q6Hx5bl5x4UGGwUrRJdWuLVuuUq2AlbXzHctAsdb1GgQMS2QgHy6JUE14jBemsUjPW0lTguiFRRn3tEyImva2sq17_zNNOfviqf6OlmGoCQpte_xKEM-8XwKM30OOx6fZTmKYBdrQ9vjqRRXmypX6uDXWMFg9pQd9aXvC9vJYpah-A3i8nWNjFxOAezBUF_07Y7A8mzV2JUuEiTc4_IrGazK4mDVdDbZV7v08TmYDr5cOmZVcjv1f0VQ0jWRgHiU5anbxupLfXt_S8I4pIiXrq2DVaMFdQ ) ).
* *Compute:* Google is training and running inference on its own TPUs — which are increasingly developed in conjunction with its models (and Jeff Dean said at NeurIPS that he thinks there are massive more gains to had with further specialization ( https://x.com/jurajselep/status/1868458487744733651 ) ).
* *Data:* Data is shaping up to be the biggest obstacle for model development going forward; the single-greatest untapped resource is likely video generally, and YouTube in particular — which Google owns! I can certainly imagine this was an important resource for the development of Veo 2.

What we saw over the last week was all of these resources being brought to bear, and the results are impressive.

Still, the surprise is a matter of perspective: if you told someone in 2020 that Google would have a week like this, no one would be surprised; after all, Google has been the expected winner in AI for years (which is why OpenAI was founded in the first place ( https://www.theverge.com/2016/6/2/11837566/elon-musk-one-ai-company-that-worries-me ) ). In that light, what was surprising was how surprised Google seemed to be by ChatGPT and the floundering nature of their initial response; now, though, the company is locked in and starting to show it.

Free ChatGPT Search
-------------------

OpenAI’s 12 days, meanwhile, continued, with an announcement that many observers found relatively underwhelming; from Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-12-16/openai-rolls-out-chatgpt-search-features-to-all-users ) :

OpenAI is rolling out a search product to all users of its chatbot ChatGPT beginning on Monday, escalating its rivalry with Alphabet Inc.’s Google. The product, called ChatGPT Search, will be available to any user who is logged in with an account for the chatbot across the company’s mobile apps and website, the artificial intelligence startup announced at an event Monday. OpenAI previously made the search option available to paid subscribers…

As part of the expanded rollout, OpenAI users will be able to make the feature their default search engine via their web browser, the company said. OpenAI also demonstrated a new option that will let users ask questions — such as about activities to do in Zurich before Christmas — and have the chatbot respond verbally.

In the case of new subscription tiers, OpenAI is exploring how much it can maximize revenue per user, although as with all price increases, there is a corresponding reduction in their addressable market; in this case the move to open up search to free users is a clear step towards an advertising model, which is the largest addressable market possible.

What makes advertising so compelling and the inevitable end model for consumer tech companies is that you can, over time, continually increase your average revenue per user without a corresponding reduction in addressable market (because it’s still free-to-use). The key to doing that is both having a compelling ad unit and also effective targeting; I suspect the latter is why OpenAI wants users logged in (although I can imagine there are rate limit concerns as well, given the relative-to-search marginal costs involved in inference).

Of course Google is the best at monetizing search, but that is precisely why Altman can have reason to be confident: it is a lot easier to experiment and compete when you are building from zero than when you are protecting a cash cow; I for one just last week removed the Google app from my home screen in favor of (another) AI chatbot just because I think the experience is better (and, of course, search is easily accessible in Safari). Google, of course, has a chatbot competitor in Gemini, but Gemini usage is a net negative for Google, at least to the extent it reduces Google Search usage; Veo 2 and everything else is immaterial (outside of generative ads, which is a huge opportunity). Or, to continue the Star Wars analogy, Google’s reliance on traditional search revenue is its exhaust vent, i.e. its biggest potential vulnerability, even as it funds all of the company’s other advantages.

One additional note on OpenAI, and Sora specifically: I was finally able to make an account, and while the video generation was cool, I was pretty underwhelmed by the quality; it’s hard to imagine ever going to Sora.com once Veo is available.

That leads to an interesting question: why is Sora a separate site with its own account system? Part of it is certainly the extensive user interface that OpenAI built around the product, which wouldn’t necessarily fit into ChatGPT; another part, however, is almost certainly cost and capacity. OpenAI has built up sufficient infrastructure for ChatGPT to support its over 300 million weekly active users, but it can’t easily give those users Sora access with the flip of a switch.

These are good excuses, to be sure, but the reality is that these constraints — combined with the underwhelming nature of Sora relative to Veo — likely means the product is dead-on-arrival from a business perspective (assuming — and this is a big assumption — that Google makes Veo broadly available in the near future). It’s worse than the competition and doesn’t have any sort of distribution advantage or the brand awareness of ChatGPT; worse, all of the capacity going towards supporting Sora inference is capacity that can’t go towards ChatGPT and its attempt to disrupt Google. I think that OpenAI would be well-served in prioritizing ChatGPT going forward.
