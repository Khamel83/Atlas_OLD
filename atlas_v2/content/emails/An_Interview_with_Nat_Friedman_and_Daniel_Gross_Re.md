*Refresh our memory, what was the Vesuvius challenge and what’s the current status?*

*NF:* Thank you, Daniel for that cue. It started in 79AD when Mount Vesuvius erupted and spread a huge wave of hot gas and hot mud, the pyroclastic flow over the entire bay of Naples and completely buried the towns of Pompeii and Herculaneum. And as it turns out, there was a very fancy villa that was built by the father-in-law of Julius Caesar, just outside Herculaneum, enormous, opulent, and it was buried under 60 feet of mud.

When it was discovered by farmers digging a well in the 1700s, as they tunneled underground through its walls and rooms, mostly looting different statues and artifacts, a strange collection of ashen objects, carbonized lumps, was found, which turned out to be papyrus scrolls. What made this totally unique is that no other ancient library from that period had ever survived before. If you keep some papyrus in the Bay of Naples, it tends to degrade and rot from the humidity of the bay over 100 years or so. So the only ancient writings we have are the ones that were sequentially copied by a cascade of monks over the Middle Ages, and so no intact ancient library has survived.

Essentially this is it, but the mystery and difficulty of it is that they can’t be opened. They’re so fragile — I actually did this — if you carbonize a papyrus scroll and try to open it, they just flake apart in your hands and you can’t read them; the process of trying to open them destroys them. So there’s been now, since these were discovered in the 1700s, a nearly 300-year effort to try to open and read these things.

Last year at the beginning of the year, Daniel and I launched a project to try to crack this problem. The general approach was to scan the scrolls at really high resolution in 3D without opening them and without damaging them. To do this, a particle accelerator is used to get very high resolution, and then hopefully to take those scans and reduce this problem to a software problem where you can use computer vision and machine learning algorithms to virtually unwrap and read these scrolls.

I think they did break the static friction at least, but it’s clear that their aim is off and their aim is off for deep cultural reasons and organizational reasons. I’m sure the DeepMind team, the Gemini team really had nothing to do with this, it’s actually a shame that it happened.

*Yeah, I can’t imagine how frustrating that must be. Well, this is the other issue, they’re going to lose employees over this. “I built this incredible model and this other layer of the company was allowed to sabotage it. Why should I be spending all my time here?”*

*NF:* Well, and the challenge is that all those layers, who’s the common point that they report to? I think it’s probably Sundar and so he would have to actually exercise leadership. This is the challenge when you have companies that are sprawling like this, is that it’s very easy for things to fall between the cracks and you see the organizational divides.

Some of it I do also think is the youth of the field, we have this challenge that tone is a design problem, the industry is early, we’re missing the design tools, the Photoshop for personality, et cetera, the tools that make these things more obvious along the way. But this should have been obvious, this was way too conspicuous. And so yeah, I think it’s a leadership opportunity for someone. It could be Sundar, or it could be someone else.

Mistral, Evals, and OpenAI

*You mentioned Mistral, they just released a new large model this week. I haven’t written about them nearly enough, but number one, you already gave an initial take on the product generally, I also thought it was interesting that they also announced a new investor along the way, which was Microsoft. That’s a good response to the OpenAI situation is, look, what does Microsoft have? They have money and they can very explicitly hedge their bets both now that they’re in and also as a signal to OpenAI being like, “Look, we’re super dependent on you right now, and we’re going to try to make sure that’s not the case going forward”.*

*NF:* Yeah, I think Satya knows he’s got a lot of eggs in one basket and he’s very intelligently trying to make sure that’s not true. We saw him do this before. He partnered with Zuck on the initial Llama open sourcing and offered that via Azure. Now he’s got Mistral, Mistral’s the current open source darling and leader, and I think just executing with incredibly impressive speed and taste, as Daniel said.

*Why are they so much better than Llama?*

*NF:* Well, it’s interesting. I think they were originally the Llama team, and so I think they have a few things, they have the agility of a startup and I think that counts for a lot. Maybe they have some constraints that are helpful too. They have only so much capital, only so much compute, and so they’re solving for those constraints.

*And maybe they can’t hire as many fine-tuners.*

*NF:* Right? Well, definitely not. It’s definitely the case that, one of the things that we talked about earlier that they care about deeply is the quality of data and we know that they work very, very hard to clean their training data and to get effectively a compute multiplier out of doing that, that gives them a quality multiplier. But right now they’re punching way above their weight and it feels almost like a magic trick. Their new Mistral large model is performing really well on evals and they haven’t quite said what it is, maybe it’s an MOE mixture of Mistral mediums or something like that. But my gosh, it’s really impressive and so yeah, I think there’s just a lot of agility there and a really hardcore team with great taste and great judgment that so far is making really great decisions.

*How do evals work, by the way? Everyone puts up these test numbers. What is a good model? What is not?*

*NF:* Well, this is a funny topic, dear to my heart. It’s really funny to see, you have all these CEOs of all these companies that are trumpeting their MMLU number ( https://paperswithcode.com/paper/measuring-massive-multitask-language ) and MMLU is an eval that Dan Hendrycks ( https://twitter.com/danhendrycks ) developed himself when he was an undergrad. So you have basically CEOs of trillion-dollar companies talking about their score on an undergrad’s test, basically and this is the premier reasoning eval that’s out there.

I think if you just zoom way out and look at the progress in the AI field generally, we see incredible progress on models, incredible progress on alignment tools like RLHF. Products finally feel like they’re really going, we’re seeing a lot of products, even policy people are really spun up. But the one progress bar that seems the furthest behind is evals, and evals are basically the tests that you run on models to see what they can and can’t do and to see what their behavior is, so you have some idea of that before you ship them, and it’s this classic low prestige type of activity. It’s like benchmarks, right? But it has a huge impact in the industry. When new benchmarks come out, people want to perform against those.

I think we have a bunch of challenges right now. First is that there are very few good public evals and even the ones there are, like MMLU, they either aren’t really able to predict certain types of capabilities or they’re getting close to saturated. I mean, on MMLU, you have people scoring in the 80s now and with multi-shot maybe in the low 90s in some cases and so you have this saturated benchmark and in fact, all the benchmarks tend to saturate within a couple of years of being released. This is just really a missing thing.

I remember Andrej Karpathy ( https://twitter.com/karpathy ) told us once that the only eval he trusts is Twitter eval. After a model’s been shipped, you check the sentiment on Twitter a couple weeks in and see if people like it or not. But you have still the CEO of every company training these big models saying, “We’ve got to be at the top of the leaderboard.” By the way, what’s the leaderboard? The leaderboard is something a couple undergrads threw together, it’s Chatbot Arena ( https://huggingface.co/spaces/lmsys/chatbot-arena-leaderboard ) that the folks at Berkeley assembled.

*It doesn’t have the bits that Daniel has argued consistently are important, like the taste and tone and the way it feels.*

*DG:* And by the way, it’s not an easy problem to evaluate this with humans. Why should we think it’s easy to do that with models? We’re now at the point where we’re not talking about whether GPT-2 can write more than three paragraphs or not, the models have gotten good enough that the evals are a deep problem. Pearson is a huge company and part of what they do is create systems and methodologies that evaluate humans and yeah, this doesn’t exist for models and presumably it should.

*Is there a bit where for when it comes to OpenAI, are we underrating Sam Altman? Are we underrating someone else in that organization where there is just some sort of product sense that is manifesting that people can feel that maybe can’t be measured, but actually we still underrated its importance?*

*NF:* Greg Brockman ( https://twitter.com/gdb ) would be my vote. I think Greg actually is a major driver there and has fantastic taste.

*Famously of Stripe before, which is a company well-known in that regard.*

*NF:* Yeah, he was at Stripe, he did a lot of amazing things at Stripe. One of the things he did at Stripe that most people don’t remember, but an early recruiting exercise Stripe did was to launch a CTF [capture the flag] for security and it was a Greg Brockman production and it was beautifully put together and it really attracted super smart people and Greg has that incredibly good taste and judgment and ability to think in terms of products and shipping them. Look, I think he’s deep in the weeds also of their CUDA kernels and training code, but I think he’s very forceful and has really great product sense also and I’m sure there’s a lot of other people involved in that too. I think Sam also knows what that looks like when it’s going well, obviously, he’s been around so many startups, but if you were asking who’s underrated in this regard, I’d probably pick Greg.

*Well, it’s been a momentous few months. What is the big thing we should look for next? Is GPT-5 around the corner or do we not know? Is there going to be something that just pops up, like Groq did last week? Which again, a classic example of a company that’s been out for years and years but then just suddenly breaks through. I’m giving away the question, which is, “Who knows?”, but what do you guys see coming next?*

*DG:* Well, I’m curious now to hear what Nat will say, but maybe I’ll just throw out an idea. I think we’re in an odd tweener era, I think, where the models that we have today for them to be used, I think in truly economically valuable ways, they do need a product built around them and they need someone like with the time and attention that Nat and his team had at GitHub, to take the model and make it faster and smaller and really good for Copilot and that sort of thing. That’s just the paradigm that we’re in now and I sort of think that’s still true.

At the other end of the Rubicon is a world where these AI models that we have, act like agents and don’t really need an interface the same way that humans don’t. We interact with our peers over Slack and Gmail and that’s totally fine and if I had to guess at some point this year we make another step in that direction and we have more coworkers than copilots. That’s Nat’s phrase that I’m stealing here, that I think is quite prescient and that’ll be the big thing, I don’t know where it’ll come from. Maybe it’ll come from OpenAI, maybe it’ll come from Magic, maybe it’ll come from DeepMind, but I think that’s the big thing the industry is working towards. Would you agree, Nat?

*NF:* Yeah, I think that’s right, I totally agree. I think the thing that I’m interested in seeing is reasoning, basically, some better definition of reasoning, some way to measure it and some market improvements towards it, whether it’s the active reasoning Daniel was talking about or somehow a way of generating training data for the models that allow them to learn patterns of reasoning more directly, or a way of measuring reasoning ability in these models.

We try to tease it out now and see how well they’re reasoning, but some direct progress along this I think would be a very big deal and there’s a belief in the field that this may come from figuring out how to do reinforcement learning on text. I think that may be the path potentially, but in general, I think some direct way to improve reasoning in the models and then see that it’s actually improving and that we’re getting real logical thinking and good results.

*And this is all still staying in the broader transformer context, just working around the edges to put stuff back together?*

*NF:* Yeah, it seems like that’s how the computer industry has always evolved is you start with something and then you build on it and evolve it over time.

*NF:* Exactly.

*CISC was not the best approach, but once you’re two years ahead, you’re two years ahead.*

*NF:* Yeah. I think that’s likely to be the case here too.

*Path dependency is huge.*

*NF:* Yeah, we’re path dependent and people just find ways of adding on the things that are needed either in pre-training or post-training, or maybe they bolt on some model architectures. I don’t know, that’s where most of the research is. Most of the research is not fundamental new architectures and so just probabilistically, you’d expect extensions to be where the work actually happens and the progress gets made. But we’ll see. There are a few interesting and promising areas that are not transformers, that are being investigated, but I think the vector of progress that I’m paying most attention to is reasoning.

*DG:* Sounds great. Thanks for having us, Ben.
