This week’s Stratechery Interview is with Byrne Hobart ( https://x.com/ByrneHobart ). Hobart writes The Diff ( https://www.thediff.co/ ) , a newsletter about technological change as expressed in financial markets. Hobart is incredibly prolific, and abides by one of my favorite maxims ( https://www.thediff.co/manifesto/ ) : assume that people are smart and leverage that to figure out what you don’t know about the decisions they make.

Hobart, in partnership with Tobias Huber, has also written a new book that was released this week by Stripe Press: Boom: Bubbles and the End of Stagnation ( https://www.stripe.press/boom ). From the description:

From the Moon landing to the dawning of the atomic age, the decades prior to the 1970s were characterized by the routine invention of transformative technologies at breakneck speed. By comparison, ours is an age of stagnation; of slowing median wage growth, rising inequality, and decelerated scientific discovery. In Boom, Byrne Hobart and Tobias Huber take an inductive approach to this problem. They track some of the most significant breakthroughs of the past 100 years — from the Manhattan Project and the Apollo program to Moore’s law and Bitcoin — and reverse-engineer how transformative progress arises from the same dynamics that govern financial bubbles, bringing together small groups with a unified vision, vast funding, and surprisingly poor accountability. Bubbles, they conclude, aren’t all bad—in fact, they create the ideal conditions for transformative innovation. Integrating insights from economics, philosophy, and history, Boom provides a blueprint for accelerating innovation and a path to unleash a new era of global prosperity.

In this Interview we cover Hobart’s background and path to the newsletter business, and then dive into the book, which, as I note in the interview, is much more of an exhortation to a better future than a technocratic breakdown of the past. We discuss how he defines a bubble, break down some of the examples in his book, and examine how talent has flowed to different sectors over time. We also have an extended discussion about globalization and China, whether or not AI is in a bubble, and the ongoing vibe shift.

*Many examples of this sort of path.*

*BH:* So I ended up going to a state school on a full ride for a year, and I was starting to realize that it’s just not a great use of my time.

*“I want to be on the stock market!”*

*BH:* So I dropped out and moved to New York and eventually found a kind of random office job actually at a recruiting agency, which was not a really good job for me because I’m not an extrovert and I don’t like hassling people and sending lots of cold emails and doing cold calls and things like that. *(laughing) Can’t imagine why, talk about a dependency risk.*

*Yes, that’s the phrase I was looking for exactly.*

*BH:* From there I ended up getting a freelance job and a marketing agency that turned into a full-time job at that agency, and then I kind of was in the marketing world for a while doing SEO and social media stuff, generally in the white hat to gray hat range, but it was an interesting space and caused me to think a lot about search and learn a lot about search. So pieces on things like, “Here are all the tricks that Yelp uses to rank well for the names of businesses”, and, “Here are all the pieces of evidence we have that LinkedIn and Facebook are doing this brutal battle to rank first for people’s full names”.

Through writing that blog, I up getting on the radar of a hedge fund, and what I was told after I got hired, was that there was a portfolio manager at this hedge fund, SAC Capital, he emailed a ton of people asking them he needed a new analyst, he was asking them for recommendations, and he said it was really weird that two different people he knew recommended the same college dropout with no financial experience, and that was me. So I talked to them and I think that was my first experience of just being in a room with exceptionally smart people and being kind of the slow one and I realized this when I was explaining something about how Google rankings worked, and I could just see them get it and instantly get bored and instantly want me to move on the next thing.

*And you’ve been trying to walk people over this line all this time, I know what you mean.*

*BH:* Yeah.

*I know that exact experience you’re talking about and I think because I have a pretty similar — it’s completely different than yours, but I was an English teacher and the Internet, it’s kind of like the standardized test point, it’s a sort of a pure meritocracy when you’re running analysis online and you’re either going to get picked up or you’re not and then you get invited into these rooms and into these meetings and you’re like, “Whoa, I wasn’t familiar with that”, I did not go to Harvard, to your point.*

*BH:* Yeah, so that was really fun. I worked there for a couple years, ended up getting laid off, and then I did a brief detour into the crypto world. I worked at 21.co, Balaji Srinivasan’s thing ( https://x.com/balajis ) , which was also really fun, was actually a room with smarter people, which was a really weird disconcerting experience after I was sort of getting used to the idea that, “Okay, if you get into the hedge fund industry, you get to talk to people who their brains are a little bit over-clocked, maybe it’s the Adderall, but they’re also just really smart” and then going to a tech company that is hardcore crypto people and chip designers and people like that in 2015, yeah, it was more of that.

New York is a weirdly great city to be either really rich or really broke, and it’s actually reasonable in a lot of spaces in between. You do have public transportation, a lot of things to do, a lot of people and people visit all the time, so it’s good to have central locations like that.

I went back into the finance world doing basically data-driven equity research, this is some of what I had done on the hedge fund side was finding interesting data sets that we can use to predict company fundamentals, build models of fundamentals based on those data sets, and then at the fund it was use that to predict what the stock will do. Then for the data providers, it was sell research reports and data feeds to funds, and then they will predict what the stock will do. That was pretty fun actually. So that’s based on alternative data, it is a really good way to understand pretty much the gap between what do the accounting financials say and what are the true underlying economics of the business because over the long, long run, they have to tie out, your cumulative cash flows do have to sum to your cumulative net profits over a sufficiently long period, or you got your accounting numbers wrong. But over the short term, the accounting numbers are not necessarily a great guide to how the business works.

I had a couple of Medium pieces on things like California and housing ( https://byrnehobart.medium.com/peak-california-7cf97baecaf0 ) and I was looking back at the stories of early Airbnb and thinking about how if real estate had been as expensive then as it was at the time that I was writing in 2018, 2019 — Airbnb, they probably just would’ve run out of money and they probably would’ve been designers at Facebook or something, but they would not have actually been able to build Airbnb, it was a very close thing.

So I’ve written a couple of these pieces, and then I was thinking about my career progression, and it felt like there was a lot that was fun about the job, but also there’s a lot more money in predicting the next quarter, and there’s just a lot less demand for deep research into long-term company fundamentals and just a lot less demand for thinking about their economic models and understanding them, except in the sense that it’s instrumentally useful in that it makes it a little bit easier to predict the next quarter.

So yeah, I decided I should find something else to do and take a break. I really started the newsletter as just a way to keep busy during the break in a way to make sure that people didn’t forget that I existed, because I know this happens sometimes where you’re in an industry for a while and everyone knows you, you’ve got lots of friends, people are always asking you to hang out, and then if you’re not actively working, people just have fewer reminders of your existence, they don’t necessarily reach out. And after a while, people kind of vaguely remember who you were, but you’re not really there anymore. So I wanted to avoid that, and I figured newsletters are just a good way to have my name in people’s inbox every couple days, that was originally my plan. It was Monday, Wednesday, Friday.

So that takes us to late 2019, early 2020, I had actually a very extended interview process with a hedge fund that I really wanted to work for and got to the very end of the process, and they said it was a close decision, but no, but I had done some research as part of that interview process, and I thought that was fun too. That was fun to write this stuff up and kind of build a mental model of something I hadn’t really worked on before and so I decided I’m just going to keep doing that, I’m going to sort of larp as having the job that I’d like to have, which is just spend a lot of time reading, talk to interesting people and write up thoughts periodically on what’s going on in the world.

So I was doing the newsletter and then in yeah, January and February of 2020 started seeing more and more alarming headlines out of China and then out of other places about this novel respiratory infection and I was thinking, “I’m probably not going to be going on very many job interviews in March and April of 2020, but people don’t seem all that worried, so why don’t I just turn this into a paid product? I bet people will buy annual subscriptions, they’ll completely regret this when the economy collapses and they’re looking for every last dollar, but at least they’ll have a little bit of money and something to do as the world falls apart”. So yeah, I turned on paid subscriptions in February of 2020, and then the COVID era was just a really, really good era, as I’m sure you recall.

Early COVID was amazing for newsletter writers. I think part of what happened was people, they anchored their workday to when they leave for work, and if leaving for work just means you open your laptop and you’re at work now, they actually have this extra chunk of time, and it’s a good place to do things that are kind of work-like, but not strictly work, and reading a newsletter is one of those things. And in fact, I was looking back, I think I subscribed to Stratechery for the first time, and I believe April of 2020. I had been reading the free issues for a long time, but yeah, I became a paid subscriber just then.

*Your customers are already biased to give you the benefit of the doubt, they’ve already given you money.*

*BH:* Yeah.

The Boom Thesis

*Amazing. Well, anyhow, speaking of prolific — I mean, your background is particularly interesting to me. Again, I think there’s a lot of commonalities, but it is a great newsletter, but we do want to get to the book. The book is called Boom: Bubbles and The End of Stagnation ( https://www.stripe.press/boom ). Give me the high level overview and thesis, what’s the elevator pitch? And don’t worry, I have lots of questions that are getting too specific, so we will dive more into it, but you give me the overview.*

*BH:* Yes, it is that bubbles, for lack of a better word, are good.

Bubbles are this weird financial phenomenon where asset prices move in a way that does not seem justified by economic fundamentals. A lot of money pours into some industry, a lot of stuff gets built, and usually too much of it gets built and a bunch of people lose their shirts and a lot of very smart, sophisticated people are involved with the beginning, a lot of those people are selling at the peak, and a lot of people who are buying at the peak are less smart, less sophisticated, but they’ve been kind of taken in by the vibe and they’re buying at the wrong time and they lose their shirts, and that’s really bad.

Then there has been this effort to sort of rehabilitate them and say, well, that is true, they do waste some money, on the other hand, they build really useful infrastructure. The U.S. still has really excellent freight rail infrastructure because we built way too many railroads during various speculative manias in the 19th century. England, the UK also has pretty good railroads, and it’s for a similar reason, or at least they had, for a long time, a very good rail network for a pretty similar reason and then when you look more recently, something like YouTube, YouTube is hard to imagine if the telcos had not wasted hundreds of billions of dollars building out infrastructure for which there was no demand in the late ’90s, or basically no demand.

You’ve written a lot about this idea of fixed investment and very low marginal cost for providing services and that’s really, really great when you have a monopoly or quasi-monopoly and you have network effects and things. It’s really, really bad when you and all of your competitors have done the same fixed investment covering the same areas and the marginal cost is zero, so that’s where the prices go.

*Yeah, that’s the telecom story ( https://stratechery.com/2023/an-interview-with-craig-moffett-about-charter-vs-disney-and-the-path-dependency-of-the-communications-industry/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIzL2FuLWludGVydmlldy13aXRoLWNyYWlnLW1vZmZldHQtYWJvdXQtY2hhcnRlci12cy1kaXNuZXktYW5kLXRoZS1wYXRoLWRlcGVuZGVuY3ktb2YtdGhlLWNvbW11bmljYXRpb25zLWluZHVzdHJ5LyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzM0Nzc4NDQ3LCJpYXQiOjE3MzIxODY0NDcsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBpLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiUnNldVlFOGpnSGVQcFdKZjFmQWI0QyIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.I3VVES_LhI0_mVI2Xg00R7VK-HwEkD_dvfrg8YPR3zy4X2PaLfAc5HeYRiQaaXBh9KonsU55aypYplGffjY8czezLqkpUYbnMyWCyjm7tTNcHiqPYdMZPTVENAxQF5Z3xVMfiNClBC3TCu1QDir4joO62Zvk9uXaelL9VejlrikoF_1xvp7GJiFNey19So3tg3ZP1qVvpafHh3vjapf8-gGJgfs5qlsMUdHCGBNt1TgQ6QQ2CdLk41h9HVbCfPP-eJvPpFvtVmZbvuEivu7OP0wGH3sYSvrT1Lxsfnn8i7MdlQ8fBbrbS2pCUmP25r3shV8wJt6QkZlm9dhgVL8g8Q ) in a nutshell.*

*BH:* Yeah, so that’s one piece. Bubble Examples

*So one of the best examples of this was your first example, which was the Manhattan Project ( https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manhattan_Project ) and this was a really interesting example to start out with, because I don’t think if you ask anyone — anyone listening to this interview, if I told you, “Yeah, Byrne Hobart wrote a book about bubbles and he talked about building the atomic bomb”, they’d be like, “What? Safety-ism and Risk

*Well, I think part of what makes the job challenging that you’re telling in your book, and to go back to the stagnation bit, what you put your finger on is the problem in our society is safety-ism. Safety-ism isn’t just the poor decisions that we saw in the COVID era, but safety-ism up and down the stack, like corporate decision-making. There’s this idea where, and it’s hard to explain this because what you’re worried about is the future, the future that doesn’t happen and what happens in that case and so you have all these locally optimized risk avoidance choices that increases our collective risk of stagnation, whereas what you want is more local risk taking to reduce that collective risk. That inversion, does that capture what your concern is here?*

*BH:* Yeah. One way to think about it is that the eternal existential risk is we don’t make any progress, we run out of new ways to increase output for given level of input, and we have certain finite resources that get depleted over time.

A lot of those are physical. If we don’t increase productivity just across the economy, next year, the oil that we extract is a little bit more expensive to extract, and so is the copper, and you’re either digging a little bit deeper or you’re digging in places where you’re a little bit more likely to get shot at or something, you’re taking some additional risks. And meanwhile, if we haven’t done any energy transition, it’s a little bit warmer in the summer, there’s a little bit less snow in the winter every time.

But then there’s also the intangible things that get depleted, because one of the really valuable resources that countries have, and that the U.S. in particular has, is this surplus of some combination of reasonably high social trust and just this sense of you can actually accomplish things. You can move up in the world, you can start a company, and it could actually work, and if it doesn’t, maybe you’re unlucky, but it is mostly your fault and your decision. *What I’m interested in, maybe this is just my American coming through, is I’m not particularly concerned about what the government does or does not do. *BH:* I think that’s true. I think one of the interesting differences just rhetorically between say Biden Democrats and Trump and his people is that the Biden Democrats, it’s a group of genuinely smart, well-meaning people, but they are interacting a lot with abstractions, and it feels like the big triumph is when you allocate money towards some goal. When you try to measure economic progress, you are looking at things like GDP-per-capita and total factor productivity, and those are really important metrics, but when you want to inspire someone about the future, you don’t punch in a bunch of compounding numbers in a calculator and say that you will get so many more utils in 20 years if things go right. And also a lot of the people who build really cool stuff during bubbles, they build the stuff they were dreaming about building when they were kids and kids don’t dream of maximizing GDP per capita, kids dream about flying cars.
