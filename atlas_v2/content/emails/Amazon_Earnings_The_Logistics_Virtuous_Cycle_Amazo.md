Amazon Earnings
---------------

From Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-10-26/amazon-reports-revenue-that-tops-estimates-on-strong-web-sales ) :

Amazon.com Inc. Chief Executive Officer Andy Jassy gave investors much of what they wanted this earnings season: robust sales and profit growth along with a hint that the cloud division earnings machine is regaining momentum. While third-quarter revenue at Amazon Web Services, the cloud computing unit, fell just short of projections, Jassy said the business is stabilizing. The company signed several new deals with customers that took effect this month, and demand for generative artificial intelligence is likely to boost the division well into the future, he said Thursday on a conference call after the results were released. The shares gained sharply after his comments and were up about 6% as the markets opened on Friday. The stock has gained almost 50% this year…

Cloud unit sales increased 12% to $23.1 billion, a growth rate that was “just enough to keep the goblins away,” analysts at Jefferies said in a note to clients. It was slightly higher than the previous period, marking the first quarter-to-quarter rise in AWS revenue growth in almost two years.

The business’s operating income of $6.98 billion was about $1.3 billion more than analysts expected. AWS, which generally accounts for more profit than the rest of the company combined, reported the highest operating margin since the first quarter of 2022. On the call, Jassy acknowledged that some companies are still seeking to cut their spending on rented computing power and software, a phenomenon that has hobbled growth at AWS, along with rivals Microsoft Corp. and Alphabet Inc. But many clients are now turning to running new projects on Amazon’s servers, Jassy said.

I provided a bit of a dramatic preview of Amazon’s earnings in last week’s Update ( https://stratechery.com/2023/google-earnings-microsoft-earnings-ai-leverage/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIzL2dvb2dsZS1lYXJuaW5ncy1taWNyb3NvZnQtZWFybmluZ3MtYWktbGV2ZXJhZ2UvIl19LCJleHAiOjE3MDEzNDkzMDksImlhdCI6MTY5ODc1NzMwOSwiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcGkucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCBlbnRpdGxlbWVudHMiLCJzdWIiOiJSc2V1WUU4amdIZVBwV0pmMWZBYjRDIiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.WLfNE7WuMPd80GzY-1Zbl5eJPfBt4LitSBQZfo03vJ68Tyw9X3YkEm9Xppbp6fL-JOQ8qEb8S00i4XpqfvjmxKBoO05DxaFxAd3O-xXOb3lp4a5Fns85oYt_TgHWvx46T2_cBgeLJ-ZpRqTPBT9O7Fjt22D3VvZusPG0fj8Ct6COp6PUc6bj3IgvoOfOMTPcl1qLvKad6arcMaju2Hbq51bWkeqtruC8hxUoyXovnb3qQMw82kgg9Ed4bs6-0vk8I2-evIlUXS25gajCBXCcF6SToxSXzvpYQ8lW1cgkdmiG4lyRySi56qHqt6PM10qo8L4OXzpYqYHuIqV1bfbrzg ) about Google and Microsoft, writing:

All of that noted, the combination of Google and Microsoft’s results make Amazon’s earnings tomorrow very interesting indeed. If AWS looks like Google than it suggests that Microsoft has a meaningful advantage in AI infrastructure; if AWS is done talking about optimizations and ready to talk about realized AI growth then Google’s results will deserve even more scrutiny.

Jassy spent a lot of time talking about generative AI, but when asked specifically, “How should we think about the timing, just to drive some tangible monetization there?”, CFO Brian Olsavsky replied:

What I would tell you is that we have been surprised at the pace of growth in generative AI. Our generative AI business is growing very, very quickly, as I mentioned earlier. And almost by any measure, it’s a pretty significant business for us already. And yet I would also say that companies are still in the relatively early stages.

What he didn’t provide was a number; I think it’s safe to say that it is a lot less than Microsoft’s claim that AI is driving an incremental three points of growth. Still, at least AWS stabilized overall, matching last quarter’s 12% growth rate, off of the largest base of the three major providers; that means that only Google’s growth meaningfully declined.

That noted, Jassy emphasized that this is early days for AI, and that “customers want to bring the models to their data, not the other way around”; of course this observation is self-serving, but it also strikes me as correct. To take things back to Google, though, this only heightens concerns that its core cloud business is slowing. Yes, it’s AI offerings may be compelling, but it is a taller order to get companies to switch for your AI offerings than to simply get them to start using your offerings with data you already host. Moreover, that slowing may be in part due to AI; Doug O’Laughlin noted on Fabricated Knowledge ( https://www.fabricatedknowledge.com/p/weekly-earnings-asml-lrcx-tsmc-nod ) in the context of reviewing TSMC’s earnings:

They called out further strength in HPC, which grew 6% QoQ. We know that Nvidia is driving a large part of this, and I want to highlight the disparity between their results and Nvidia’s guide. Nvidia guided their outlook to grow 16% revenue quarter over quarter, and while they are not the entirety of HPC, it means that the rest of HPC revenue declined quarter over quarter. There is a 1-month mismatch, but this likely means that data center cannibalization is very real, and this is the proof. Watch out, AMD.

AMD reports later today, but this context matters for the cloud providers too: if new growth increasingly comes from AI then it is going to be to the advantage of companies that already have the biggest cloud businesses, which means AWS, then Azure, with Google Cloud Platform only just barely profitable. Again, these are very early days, but this is something that bears watching.

The Logistics Virtuous Cycle
----------------------------

While Jassy spent a lot of time on generative AI, he opened his prepared remarks by talking about Amazon’s logistics overhaul:

I’ll start with our stores business. Our move earlier this year from a single national fulfillment network in the U.S. to 8 distinct regions represented one of the most significant changes to our fulfillment network in our history. This change has gone more smoothly and made more impact than we optimistically expected. And you can see the benefits in many forms. Regional fulfillment clusters with higher local in-stock levels and optimized connections between fulfillment centers and delivery stations mean shorter distances and fewer touches to get items to customers.

Shorter travel distances and fewer touches mean lower cost to serve. But perhaps most importantly, shorter distances and fewer touches mean that customers are getting their shipments faster. We remain on pace to deliver the fastest delivery speeds for Prime customers in our 29-year history. And as I talked about last quarter, we know how important speed of delivery is to customer satisfaction and buying behavior. A good example is the significant growth we’re seeing in consumables and everyday essentials. When customers are getting items as quickly and conveniently as they are now from Amazon, they’re going to consider us more frequently for more of their shopping needs.

As we’ve shared the last few quarters, we’ve re-evaluated every part of our fulfillment network over the last year. The first substantial rearchitecture centered on the regionalization change. We obviously like the results but don’t think we fully realize all the benefits yet and we continue to make steady improvements in fine-tuning the placement algorithms to enable even more in-region fulfillment and to further increase consolidation into fewer shipments. We’ve also identified several substantial changes to our inbound processes that we believe could have a significant impact on our cost to serve and speed of delivery. We have a long way before being out of ideas to improve cost and speed. The team is really humming on this and I’m proud of the way they’re inventing and executing together.

Multiple regions with simpler distribution resulting in faster speeds and lower prices may sound like an obvious win, but it is the sort of change that is only possible because of massive volume and, most importantly, massive investment.

First off, this is the sort of risk-taking that should be rewarded, not punished by regulators ( https://stratechery.com/2023/ftc-sues-amazon-fba-and-prime-pricing-punishment/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIzL2Z0Yy1zdWVzLWFtYXpvbi1mYmEtYW5kLXByaW1lLXByaWNpbmctcHVuaXNobWVudC8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTcwMTM0OTMwOSwiaWF0IjoxNjk4NzU3MzA5LCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwaS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IlJzZXVZRThqZ0hlUHBXSmYxZkFiNEMiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.JeQkdB4JfYFdRC_N3tRrpht14132QGx2GZ2DQnPCs_SCCOWbGiuH_J9MXypZNXZv0neKVIeWtqd2XkeQylIEOc8FQhEZQKmZIGnq-uHAriX7RsnDfELB7GHU0HKUFSzpQ1QFsXUs0Az5Ze9KkG8sgB2AFVJFWUgqmODGiSS0_DHiyl0tAn0DJuPshAC0VCJyp-nohryopZbfrJXUj_K8WNdb5dTPIWsJXtaBTSjtAFv0JKKU3wel1-7Dsu5qhrVT6NBdWixdQX5Nhnotljy0jy1zHVWpx7Hjt1t4oa2eqEQ5xDP3FqrqHDi4lvazhhBSWqMxDFugISUrUKqu4YCmeQ ). Second, these are gains that ought to compound in a virtuous cycle: more volume means more leverage on those investments, and increased density of deliveries decreases the marginal cost of a single delivery, which ought to increase the number of marginal assets (i.e. delivery drivers), increasing speed, accelerating the cycle.

Amazon Aggregator Ads
---------------------

It’s more than that, though: increased speed driving more purchases on Amazon also drives Amazon’s advertising business. Jassy explained in response to a question about the ad business, which grew 25% year-over-year:

I think on the ad front, well, there’s a lot I’m excited about on the ad side as well. I think that it’s interesting what’s happened in our ads business as if you look around the industry, most advertising-heavy companies have struggled growth-wise as the economy has been difficult. And while we see companies being more cautious on the ad side and the top-of-funnel products, things like display and a little bit of video, we’re still seeing a lot of strength in the lower-funnel ad products like sponsored products. And I think in these types of economies, we have fared pretty well in part because we have a number of owned and operated properties that have very large volumes that advertisers and brands want to get in front of. Even in a harder economy, there’s going to be a lot of e-commerce purchasing. So people want to be in front of our customers in our marketplace.

Owned-and-operated properties that command customer attention — i.e. demand — is the characteristic of an Aggregator, and advertising has always been the most compelling Aggregator business model; it’s the chief characteristic of what I called the Super Aggregators ( https://stratechery.com/2017/the-super-aggregators-and-the-russians/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDE3L3RoZS1zdXBlci1hZ2dyZWdhdG9ycy1hbmQtdGhlLXJ1c3NpYW5zLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzAxMzQ5MzA5LCJpYXQiOjE2OTg3NTczMDksImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBpLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiUnNldVlFOGpnSGVQcFdKZjFmQWI0QyIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.XCqbqspfaFova12C_GR3Va4YLmcpPZc-mcd7jv1GclsBFwn5Iki9Q_EKVu4jAuQpwGsjXUj9mGTHDEhnz2kFjca96mmUdR_42URHWFAhQd--b3fmJbnZYqDKJyvGJ8bS4n6qWpf5FeH7xhjxTOvfvMp12_oInv605fUUDYpgToB6ELr8Lbq9St9NKveQqX-XEs07pC1YVnjDWhP21H7CHcKZ-VfpBjMcLMgYMudMS315cTKs5Nds2vjqgFL4TD3bvtv3ynIRoWP2pJ0CXgDmpcm1ZTy0tEBA8EWf7sb8JtlRLE1-avsokQiRGQ1I7tie1t2PP7pKhnMLch0joRH5aw ) :

Super-aggregators not only have zero transaction costs when it comes to users and content, but also when it comes to making money. This is at the very core of why Google and Facebook are so much more powerful than any of the other purely information-centric networks. The vast majority of advertisers on both networks never deal with a human (and if they do, it’s in customer support functionality, not sales and account management): they simply use the self-serve ad products like the one pictured above (or a more comprehensive tool built on the companies’ self-serve API).

Amazon doesn’t break out their advertising expenses, but it does operate a self-serve model for its 3rd-party merchants, and I think it’s safe to say the margin is very healthy; let’s say 80%, just as an estimate. If that is true then that means Amazon’s advertising business produced $9.6 billion in profit last quarter. That significantly outpaces AWS’s $7.0 billion in profit, and is roughly equivalent to Amazon’s overall profit of $9.8 billion. In other words, even if you removed AWS from the equation, Amazon would still be profitable thanks to advertising.

The key thing to remember, though, is that advertising doesn’t stand alone: the reason why Amazon commands that demand is because of all of the investment it has made in not just Amazon.com but also things like delivery, and the more that delivery drives demand, the more ads Amazon can sell. It’s a very elegant model, with far more long-term upside than a mere retail operation.
