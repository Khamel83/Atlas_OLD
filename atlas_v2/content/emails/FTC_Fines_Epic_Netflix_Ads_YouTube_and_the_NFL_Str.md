Epic indeed. Almost enough to make you think gaming platforms have a customer-focused interest in enforcing privacy rules and controlling in-game payments.

This is indeed why my preferred App Store policy ( https://stratechery.com/2022/googles-spotify-deal-the-ideal-app-store-approach-spotifys-south-korea-option/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJlbnQiOnsidXJpIjpbImh0dHBzOi8vc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkuY29tLzIwMjIvZ29vZ2xlcy1zcG90aWZ5LWRlYWwtdGhlLWlkZWFsLWFwcC1zdG9yZS1hcHByb2FjaC1zcG90aWZ5cy1zb3V0aC1rb3JlYS1vcHRpb24vIl19LCJleHAiOjE2NzQyMTg2NjEsImlhdCI6MTY3MTYyNjY2MSwiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9zdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIiwic3ViIjoiUnNldVlFOGpnSGVQcFdKZjFmQWI0QyIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.JVXbz1Lg4VMxWabZ9wiN-IA1iMZPE3JRcLcgG07ZdlBR2aSJQlToFYcRWJpJbmb1FWQmBq12PO0KqUdCa6dLcBIO556KrcOF2Z9kkxA7TAy4-Dst_rs2zmGzn93HyJrxQc4n0Nd8SePeueFrObgWgE9iv_vc1t8KhTbHEbApafDd3JHVaDqcXmdwpc5bFb07FePXwrFzS39QYZznmZvKB2JXhBQKM_2Euap8ynUT1o2FTndgaZI1LwDC-V02lDCsr46bib3HAIPz11fr3Lc7CqkYVqIzM4p89KTkRjnus935n499qT1TAsznplWN6ml8DVF2K7wQUJ8DTdI3_VDUBA ) has always been that Apple (and Google) retain control of the purchasing experience for games, while loosening the rules for everything else.

From a user perspective, it makes sense that payments for what is likely a cross-platform service is handled by that service; it also makes it more likely that payments are even accessible in the first place (i.e. you can’t buy Kindle books on iOS at all). Games, on the other hand, are by-and-large consumed on the device in question, and also provide more fertile ground for abuse (or are likely to be accessed by children); in that case it is attractive to have the platform owner in control of payments.

As opposed as I am to Apple’s absolutist approach, it is always worth remembering the very real benefits that have come from the App Store. To take just Fortnite as an example, a purchase made through in-app purchase would have been, unquestionably, a purchase; in this case the fact that Apple controls refunds, not the developer, would have been a feature, not a bug. Or consider the case of subscriptions, where multiple entities still let people subscribe online but require a phone call to unsubscribe; subscriptions made through the App Store are easily accessible and cancellable. More broadly, as I have noted many times in the past, the App Store arrived at a time when the general population had been conditioned by a decade of malware to never install anything from the Internet; suddenly people were willing to download and install apps willy nilly.

Now of course I think that Apple’s limitations on apps that compete directly with them, like Spotify and Kindle, are anticompetitive; that the insistence on dictating whether an app can link to its own website or try a new business model are stifling for innovation; and refusing to share customer information with developers (or podcasters!) while insisting that developers pay for the privilege of making Apple’s devices more attractive is unfair. This is a useful reminder, though, that none of this stuff is binary; indeed, a way to frame my ongoing critique of Apple is that I am frustrated that a subset of their policies are binary in the opposite direction, even as there is real customer value — and long-term developer value in terms of customer trust — being provided.

Netflix Ads
-----------

Last week from Digiday ( https://digiday.com/future-of-tv/netflix-lets-advertisers-take-their-money-back-after-missing-viewership-targets/ ) :

For as quickly as Netflix brought its ad-supported tier to market, the streamer’s advertising business is off to a slow start. Netflix is falling short of ad-supported viewership guarantees made to advertisers and allowing advertisers to take their money back for ads that have yet to run, according to five agency executives. The specific shortfall amounts vary by advertiser, but in some cases, Netflix has only delivered roughly 80% of the expected audience, said the executives. A Netflix spokesperson declined to comment.

Yesterday from the Wall Street Journal ( https://www.wsj.com/articles/netflixs-ad-supported-tier-was-its-least-popular-plan-in-launch-month-analytics-firm-estimates-11671539939 ) :

Netflix’s new ad-supported plan was the least popular tier of its service in November, the first month in which the streaming giant offered it, according to subscription analytics firm Antenna. The plan accounted for 9% of new Netflix sign-ups in the U.S. during the month. Some 57% of subscribers to the ad-supported tier in the first month were people re-joining the service or signing up for the first time, while 43% downgraded from pricier plans, according to Antenna.

These results, assuming they are accurate, are certainly disappointing, but I don’t think they are surprising; I wrote in an Update ( https://stratechery.com/2022/netflix-earnings-netflix-doubles-down-netflixs-ad-tier/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJlbnQiOnsidXJpIjpbImh0dHBzOi8vc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkuY29tLzIwMjIvbmV0ZmxpeC1lYXJuaW5ncy1uZXRmbGl4LWRvdWJsZXMtZG93bi1uZXRmbGl4cy1hZC10aWVyLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNjc0MjE4NjYxLCJpYXQiOjE2NzE2MjY2NjEsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCIsInN1YiI6IlJzZXVZRThqZ0hlUHBXSmYxZkFiNEMiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.RfyR2ax6CznOzhjSWm9hAKG5fDZlUJj8r-tdvNY6V1lcks1lkiLWkZAKJkVbkauf9Okq-UmWL5OCike0e_F0dB-Jp65p3M0AEpIQdDHuT2x4SpEYr6r3tn5T6UyipJKsHUDC5zincqN58ZhaKg2Qrm0gT4M8_JTehLFIRf6CnogUh7jqFl0YqWcwTBOojYsy6swbhxUfa6EpisVZghLLmg0bMU4pBne4tU86i9Xe1nS1cS8y7uqJmksDUCdZHulUbfNydhSLrsKl9Ss1DMsiO-B9525mV3CN17bHA2QhdMREq5qwnuG3HQCHt2GGkkJZjkJM8EqnQVn5aKztWXORbA ) after Netflix’s announcement of its initial ad-supported offering that such a narrow offering seemed like a pretty unrealistic bet on growth, given how important scale is to ads; I also noted that the likely explanation was that Netflix would add more tiers over time. Indeed, from CNBC ( https://www.cnbc.com/2022/12/06/netflix-ceo-says-streamer-likely-to-offer-multiple-ad-supported-tiers.html ) earlier this month:

For viewers who don’t want to see commercials, Netflix already offers multiple plans ranging in price from $9.99 a month to $19.99 a month. And the company will likely do the same for its ad-supported model as the business grows, Sarandos said at the UBS TMT conference. “We have multiple tiers today, so it’s likely we’ll have multiple ad tiers over time, but nothing to talk about yet,” Sarandos said. “And the product itself will evolve, I suspect, pretty dramatically, but slowly, gradually.”

I think this is totally reasonable; indeed, as I noted in that Update the reality is that Netflix has moved quite fast over the last year (Co-CEO Reed Hastings now wishes the company had built out advertising years ago ( https://www.cnbc.com/2022/11/30/netflix-ceo-reed-hastings-on-advertising-turnaround-focused-on-google-facebook.html ) ). The most likely outcome here is that as Netflix increases their offering and as customer awareness grows that volume will follow.

That noted, it would be interesting if Netflix’s (still) default status — i.e. most people have Netflix plus some other streaming service — means customers are more resistant to ads; after all, it’s one thing to encounter adds only occasionally because you only use a service occasionally. If you use a service a lot, though, ads may be a bigger burden. Or, alternatively, maybe customers just can’t be bothered to downgrade — until the next price hike, that is. That is when having those ad-supported tiers will make a big difference ( https://stratechery.com/2022/why-netflix-should-sell-ads/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJlbnQiOnsidXJpIjpbImh0dHBzOi8vc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkuY29tLzIwMjIvd2h5LW5ldGZsaXgtc2hvdWxkLXNlbGwtYWRzLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNjc0MjE4NjYxLCJpYXQiOjE2NzE2MjY2NjEsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCIsInN1YiI6IlJzZXVZRThqZ0hlUHBXSmYxZkFiNEMiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.tnPjBf2_T88NsxW3ZJPuxcliXI9TRPrO3-W1gLq17usbCU6enP61iINPWUsWiYqPRVKhVZWzMl2p4yowFXn5jTw9imi0wBh8jcdxapDDwv7L-fDHO_caqgkFCjlS36SdBGIAjcp8a2fuJo9pd_Z_vNmKfwSziNCNX-qdkS29dnSi3x8VlLHwSPPU19PuXy9oya0N8lyhdOS8bz2AHgr9BfuBgHO62nC3uIdxDZrJuMUJXVL9Zk38vfhdRtXWehuWbW8slD9Vs9lB2jAGcNbk37X3zE21a7xhPbrVImIrABlyLty_nf_8eB7OmF1muqXK1FKUeHeb1hHDD9QjE7KNEw ) (and it will help with the password-sharing crackdown as well).

YouTube and the NFL
-------------------

From the Wall Street Journal ( https://www.wsj.com/articles/nfl-in-advanced-talks-with-googles-youtube-for-sunday-ticket-rights-deal-11671579236 ) :

The National Football League is in advanced talks to give Google’s YouTube exclusive rights to NFL Sunday Ticket, a subscription-only package that allows football fans to watch most Sunday afternoon games, people familiar with the matter said. An agreement could be reached as early as Wednesday, following a meeting of NFL owners, who approve rights deals…Sunday Ticket allows subscribers to view all out-of-market Sunday afternoon games, meaning games that don’t involve their local teams.

A potential move of Sunday Ticket to YouTube is further evidence of major sports migrating from traditional TV, which has been hit by cord-cutting, to streaming, and tech companies that are willing to spend heavily on content. Amazon.com Inc. has its own NFL deal, while Apple Inc. streams some Major League Baseball games and has a new deal for Major League Soccer. Amazon and Apple also kicked the tires on Sunday Ticket…Under the scenario being discussed, NFL games would be available to be streamed on two subscription services, YouTube TV and YouTube Primetime Channels, next season.

Primetime Channels launched in November; from The Verge ( https://www.theverge.com/2022/11/1/23433617/youtube-primetime-channels-streaming-tv-movies ) :

Streaming services are coming to YouTube. The company is rolling out a new feature called Primetime Channels that will bring shows and movies from more than 30 services directly into the YouTube interface. It’s a big bet for YouTube that it can be the cable bundle of the future and that its unparalleled audience will make streaming services buy into the idea.

YouTube has signed up 35 partners for the launch, from big-name streaming services like Paramount Plus and Epix to niche offerings like The Great Courses and Magnolia Selects. (Another service, NBA League Pass, is coming soon.) Each one will operate essentially like any other YouTube channel, with a curated homepage and a bunch of videos. Those videos will show up in the Movies and TV section of the YouTube app, as well as in search results, recommendations, and elsewhere around the platform. You’ll be able to leave a comment and like or dislike the video — all that’s missing is view counts.

Really, the movies and shows only have one distinguishing characteristic: a neon green button that says “Watch now” if it comes from a service you subscribe to or “Pay to watch” if you’re not signed up.

Primetime Channels is, in broad strokes, similar to Amazon Prime Video Channels or Apple TV Channels: it is a way to leverage YouTube’s reach and distribution to sell subscriptions. What is notable is the extent to which premium shows are, from a user-perspective, treated no differently from user-generated video. That means that not carrying RSNs is a rational decision: sure, hardcore sports fans won’t choose an MVPD, but that is counterbalanced by being able to offer a lower price relative to cable; cable, meanwhile, can’t afford to lose subscribers who care about RSNs because of their high fixed costs. The end result is that while vMVPDs are slowing the rate of cord cutting, they are only doing so for national networks like ESPN; RSNs are losing far more customers, to the point where some are teetering on bankruptcy, potentially wrecking the economic model for high inventory leagues like professional basketball and football.

If NFL Sunday Ticket were only available on YouTube TV, though, that would be a big incentive for sports fans to switch to YouTube TV. Yes, you lose regional sports networks, but you get all of the NFL, and in this case, you don’t need to get a satellite dish to do so. That is still a risk, but the fact that NFL Sunday Ticket will be easily acquired as a standalone subscription in Primetime Channels means that sports fans can have it all: both regional sports and all of the NFL. That gives RSNs and the leagues that depend on them a bit more breathing room to figure out the future (or, more likely, to kick this very significant can down the road).
