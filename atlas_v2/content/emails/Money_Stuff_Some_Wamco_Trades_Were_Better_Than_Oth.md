Cherry-picking

It is nice, in investing, to have a one-day lookback. If you could buy a stock 
first thing in the morning, see how it does over the course of the day, and 
then at 4 p.m. decide either to keep it (because it went up ) or to cancel the 
trade (because it went down) — that would be nice, for you. If you plan to hold 
the stock for a year, it wouldn’t bethat nice — one day of returns probably 
won’t affect your overall performance that much — but it helps. [1]  <>

This option is not generally available to you, though Wall Street derivatives 
structurers do spend a certain amount of time trying tosneak one-day lookbacks 
into their trades 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2023-04-11/goldman-got-some-good-swaps-deals?cmpid=BBD112624_MONEYSTUFF&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=241126&utm_campaign=moneystuff>
. But if you are an investment manager there is a way to approximate it. It is:

 * You run two investment funds, Good Fund and Dumb Fund. 
 * You call your broker in the morning and buy some stocks. Your broker 
doesn’t ask which fund you are buying for; that sort of back-office detail can 
be figured out later. 
 * At 4 p.m., you see which of the stocks went up and which went down. 
 * Now you turn to the back-office detail of deciding which fund bought which 
stocks. 
 * Good Fund, you decide, bought the stocks that went up. Dumb Fund bought the 
stocks that went down. [2]  <>
You give Good Fund a lookback, at the expense of Dumb Fund. This is usually 
called “cherry-picking.” It is obviouslyless nice than having a pure lookback. 
If you do this, Good Fund will look good (it will keep buying stocks that go 
up) but Dumb Fund will look bad (it will keep buying stocks that go down). You 
manageboth funds. If Dumb Fund underperforms, that is bad for you: Money will 
flow out of it, you will earn less in fees, etc. It is all pretty zero-sum. 
Also it is illegal!

Still you could find situations where it made sense. (But still be illegal!) 
That situation might look a bit like this:

 * Dumb Fund is bigger than Good Fund: The losses that you allocate to Dumb 
Fund will be a smaller portion of its overall returns, while the gains that you 
allocate to Good Fund will matter more. [3]  <> 
 * Dumb Fund’s investors are paying less attention. Maybe Dumb Fund is 
managing lots of money for retail investors who are only vaguely aware of what 
is going on, while Good Fund is a small hedge fund with very engaged limited 
partners who really care about performance. 
 * Good Fund pays you higher fees than Dumb Fund. Maybe Good Fund is a hedge 
fund that pays performance fees, while Dumb Fund is a long-only fund that 
doesn’t, so you can get paid more for the same performance at Good Fund than at 
Dumb Fund. 
 * You put your personal money in Good Fund, so when it goes up you benefit 
directly.
Yesterday US prosecutors 
<https://www.justice.gov/usao-sdny/pr/former-chief-investment-officer-global-bond-investment-firm-charged-over-600-million>
 and theSecurities and Exchange Commission 
<https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2024-187> brought a cherry-picking 
case 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-11-25/ken-leech-sued-by-sec-over-alleged-cherry-picking-scheme?cmpid=BBD112624_MONEYSTUFF&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=241126&utm_campaign=moneystuff>
 against Ken Leech, the former co-chief investment officer of Western Asset 
Management Co. From the SEC announcement:

The SEC’s complaint alleges that from at least January 2021 through October 
2023, Leech placed trades with brokers and then routinely waited until later in 
the trading day to allocate the trades among clients in the portfolios he 
managed. According to the complaint, Leech’s delay between placing and 
allocating trades gave him the opportunity to observe price movements, and then 
disproportionally allocate trades at a first-day gain to favored portfolios and 
trades at a first-day loss to disfavored portfolios. As alleged, Leech 
allocated hundreds of millions of dollars of net first-day gains to favored 
portfolios, which also benefited Leech personally, and a similar amount of net 
first-day losses to disfavored portfolios.

The SEC complaint 
<https://www.sec.gov/files/litigation/complaints/2024/comp-pr2024-187.pdf> says 
that Leech “was part of a team of multiple portfolio managers for Core and Core 
Plus,” two Wamco portfolios with “broad market strategies with benchmarks tied 
to the Bloomberg US Aggregate Index” and with combined assets of, at times, on 
the order of $160 billion. He was also the lead portfolio manager for Macro 
Opps, a Wamco strategy that ran as much as $13 billion with “a macro-oriented, 
global strategy that did not adhere to a specific benchmark.” Macro Opps was 
smaller but paid more:

The management fee was between approximately 5 and 45 basis points of 
portfolio market value for the Core and Core Plus portfolios Leech managed, 
with the majority of the portfolios paying 30 basis points or less. The 
management fee was between approximately 40 and 115 basis points of portfolio 
market value in the Macro Opps portfolios Leech managed, with the majority of 
the portfolios paying 60 basis points or more.

Also, the criminal indictment 
<https://www.justice.gov/d9/2024-11/u.s._v._leech_indictment.pdf> notes that 
“WAMCO produced scorecards that attributed accounts and net revenue to each 
portfolio manager. Those scorecards credited LEECH with all of the net revenue 
from Macro Opps, but divided the net revenue from accounts following the Core 
Strategies among different portfolio managers.” Macro Opps was his fund, while 
Core was a shared responsibility.

So he would wake up in the morning and trade Treasury futures and options:

Leech typically began trading around 5 a.m. Pacific Time (“PT”) (i.e., 8 a.m. 
Eastern Time (“ET”)), and traded all day, generally placing dozens of trades 
per day.

He would do these trades in an omnibus account, which “allows an investment 
adviser to buy and sell securities and commodities on behalf of multiple 
clients without telling the applicable broker the specific client for which a 
trade is intended at the time of the trade.” And then he’d figure out the 
allocations later:

Most of Western Asset’s portfolio managers used its electronic trading system 
and pre-allocated trades prior to trading and/or allocated trades shortly after 
they placed a trade, consistent with the firm’s instructions and training.

But Leech typically did not use the electronic trading system or specify trade 
allocations in draft tickets when placing trades.

Instead, Leech predominately placed trades by calling his contacts (i.e., 
registered representatives) at brokerage firms. ...

As Leech’s trades were filled, brokers issued trade confirmations that were 
typically received on a rolling basis throughout the day by Leech and his 
dedicated trade assistant (“Trade Assistant A”), as well as by other personnel 
in Western Asset’s trade operations group.

Typically, after Trade Assistant A received numerous trade confirmations, 
Trade Assistant A would attempt to contact Leech by phone to obtain allocations 
for his trades.

Trade Assistant A often did not hear back from Leech for several hours.

But then around the end of the day he’d go through the trades and allocate 
some to Core and Core Plus, and others to Macro Opps. By that time he would 
know which of the trades went up and which went down. And the ones that went up 
were disproportionately allocated to Macro Opps, says the SEC:

During the Relevant Period, as reflected in Western Asset’s trade blotter, 
Leech allocated more than $600 million of trades at net first-day gains to 
Favored Portfolios, and more than $600 million of trades at net first-day 
losses to Disfavored Portfolios.

Statistically, the probability that these differences in first-day returns 
occurred by random chance is less than one in one trillion.

“Favored Portfolios” here means Macro Opps; “Disfavored Portfolios” means Core 
and Core Plus.

It is a strange sort of cherry-picking? “The probability that these 
differences … occurred by random chance” is low, but the two strategies were
different, so the null hypothesis is not really “he allocated trades to them 
randomly.” And neither of these strategies was really a day-trading strategy: 
These positions were generally held for a while, so it’s not clear how much the 
first-day gains mattered. The SEC says:

Although these first-day gains and losses were primarily unrealized (i.e., the 
positions remained open rather than being sold off at the end of the day), they 
nonetheless benefitted Favored Portfolios and disadvantaged Disfavored 
Portfolios because they frequently permitted Favored Portfolios to end the day 
at a gain, and starting a trading position with a gain rather than a loss 
ultimately increased the likelihood of Favored Portfolios’ successful 
performance.

Also Leech had personal money in both funds, though he increased his personal 
allocation to Macro Opps and decreased his allocation to Core and Core Plus 
over this period.

Which was a mistake. Macro Opps did not do particularly well during this 
period: It was down a little bit in 2021, and down 21% in 2022. Clients were 
not impressed. From the indictment:

Between 2021 and October 2023, S. KENNETH LEECH II, the defendant, made a 
seriesof poor investment decisions that disproportionately affected Macro Opps, 
causing significant losses in the fund that led LEECH to seek to improve its 
performance, even at the expense of clients in the Core Strategies:

Between 2021 and 2023, LEECH made a series of investment decisions that led to 
losses across the portfolios he managed, including a large position in Russian 
debt that was nearly wiped out soon after Russia invaded Ukraine in early 2022 
and a significant investment in Credit Suisse debt that also lost all value 
when Credit Suisse collapsed in early 2023. Although those losses affected many 
of WAMCO's strategies, including the Core Strategies, they caused particularly 
acute problems for Macro Opps, in large part because Macro Opps had more 
exposure to some of the losing investments. Over the period described above, 
the assets under management for Macro Opps fell by approximately 80%, dropping 
to a low of under $3 billion.

This severe drop in the value of Macro Opps-which LEECH had advertised as 
reflecting what he claimed were WAMCO's best ideas-caused LEECH and the Macro 
Opps team to pay particularly close attention to improving the daily 
performance of that strategy. During this time of extreme drawdowns in Macro 
Opps, WAMCO and LEECH engaged in a Macro Opps “retention campaign” to convince 
clients to keep their investments in Macro Opps. WAMCO attempted to convince 
its clients to stay invested in Macro Opps by claiming that Macro Opps 
typically recovered quickly after suffering losses.

I don’t know that the Macro Opps investors felt like they were the favored 
funds. The theory here is something like “Leech made bad long-term investment 
decisions for Macro Opps, but tried to partially make up for it by allocating 
the trades with one-day gains to Macro Opps.” The one-day gains did not make up 
for the bad decisions. I feel like if I were running two investment funds, and 
one was melting down, I would probably favor theother one?

Anyway I suppose the useful lesson here is “don’t wait until the end of the 
day to allocate your trades, because even if you do it honestly it’s gonna look 
bad.” The SEC quotes Wamco compliance trainings:

In a June 2018 compliance training, Western Asset’s Chief Compliance Officer 
(“CCO”) warned employees to finalize “allocations and the trade ticket promptly 
after the trade execution.” He explained, “this is another thing that we know 
that the regulators are doing, they’re looking at time stamps. So, if they get 
a sense that you executed first thing in the morning, but we didn’t allocate 
until the end of the day, they may come to us … and wonder … ‘you only 
allocated after you saw what the market did.’” ...

In a training conducted in June 2022, Western Asset’s CCO stated that in going 
through the daily trade process, employees should finalize trade allocations 
“promptly after the trade is executed.” Then he described the following as a 
“bad facts scenario”: “Let’s say you execute that trade at 8am, the market 
moves, you post that allocation at noon.” He further explains, “how do we know 
whether that allocation posted at noon was influenced by the market move? How 
do we know that you … saw the market move in a particular way, and you didn’t 
tweak that allocation with that knowledge in your mind? How do you prove it?” 
Further, he said “the risk that we have is longer the longer the trade sits 
between execution and posting.”

In the olden days, when everyone did trades by phone and wrote up paper 
tickets at the end of the day, it would be reasonable to say “we allocate our 
trades at the end of the day and if we do a little cherry-picking, well, who 
can prove it?” Whereas in the modern world where “Western Asset’s electronic 
trade management system allowed portfolio managers to prepare draft trade 
tickets and specify an allocation instruction even before placing a trade,” you 
look a bit like a dinosaur doing all of your trades by phone and making your 
assistant chase you for allocations at the end of the day. And the burden might 
be on you to prove that youweren’t cherry-picking.

Quant Olympics

One theory that you sometimes hear about Renaissance Technologies is that it 
employs very smart researchers to come up with trading strategies, but its real 
advantage is in data. Renaissance has had a long time to accumulate and analyze 
and carefully scrub a lot of data sources, so if you want to build a 
quantitative trading strategy at Renaissance, you can use sophisticated 
machine-learning techniques to extract signals from that data, and those 
signals will work. But if you were to quit Renaissance and go out on your own, 
you might be just as smart and just as good at machine learning, but your data 
won’t be as good, so your signals won’t work. You will apply your sophisticated 
techniques to not-quite-right or not-quite-complete data, and they will 
generate incorrect answers. Conversely, Renaissance can hire smart scientists, 
computational linguists, etc., who are good at machine-learning techniques but 
know nothing about finance, because it can give them pristine financial data 
and let them get to work.

You could extrapolate a model from this in which a quantitative hedge fund 
does three essential things:

 * It acquires and scrubs financial and financial-adjacent data. 
 * It employs researchers who analyze that data to find signals that predict 
financial returns. 
 * It implements those signals by trading securities.
Steps 1 and 3 are essential financial functions that the fund does as an 
institution, and that its researchers couldn’t do on their own. Step 2 is sort 
of pure scientific research, which relies on the curiosity and creativity and 
whimsy of the researchers. The researchers are generally paid pretty well and 
signed to strict nondisclosure agreements, but you could imagine a different 
model. There are a lot of curious, creative, whimsical, numerate people 
interested in scientific research, and money. You have the data and the 
trading, which are hard to replicate; could you just outsource the research?

Bloomberg’s Justina Lee and David Ramli report on WorldQuant’s International 
Quant Championship 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-11-25/at-singapore-hosted-quant-olympics-competitors-pitch-new-investing-strategies?cmpid=BBD112624_MONEYSTUFF&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=241126&utm_campaign=moneystuff>
:

The contestants are ranked by the returns on their hypothetical strategies, 
culminating in this two-day shindig — part rave, part Shark Tank episode — at 
the Equarius Hotel in Singapore. The Ruff Boys emerge victorious on Sept. 17, 
sharing in the $20,000 prize money.

“Every week I spent about 50 hours on this,” said team member Chong-Sheng 
Huang. “It’s not about the prize money or the internship. I purely wanted to 
experience the taste of being first.”

These quant Olympics aren’t just about discovering a few bright kids. It’s 
part of founder Igor Tulchinsky’s latest spin on his long-standing vision for 
beating the markets — a crowdsourcing platform called BRAIN where virtually 
anyone, from students to off-duty engineers, can submit alphas and potentially 
get paid. …

“We are exploring creating our own data sets,” in addition to the exploding 
amount out there, said Nitish Maini, WorldQuant’s chief strategy officer. “Data 
has story in it, and to capture that story you have to analyze data and develop 
signals. To help us ingest much of the large amount of data that’s out there, 
we need a big talent pool.”

On BRAIN, any user can simulate strategies from over 125,000 data fields, 
ranging from trading volumes to dividend yields. All the data sets are cleaned 
for consumption and made anonymous so that a user has no idea what each 
security is. In true quant fashion, it’s not about understanding a company, but 
rather crunching the data in their various iterations and combinations to find 
statistical relationships.

Yes see it turns out that you can get university students to (1) find useful 
alphas for you and (2) say “it’s not about the money, I purely wanted to 
experience the taste of being first.” Whereas you probably have to pay someone 
to clean the data. No one is doing that for glory.

Incidentally a lot of sophisticated trading firms stereotypically find 
researchers atmath competitions, and I wonder which way is better. Would you 
rather hire quant researchers from a quant research competition (so you know 
they are good at quant research), or from a math competition (so you know they 
are good at math, and you are perhaps casting a wider net)?

Private wealth

I feel like, if you made a graph of private wealth clients, with “desirability 
to the bank” on the y-axis and “hairiness” on the x-axis, it would mostly go up 
and to the right? The hairier the client is, the more private wealth services 
you can probably sell them, and the more they will pay. Somewhere there is 
probably a billionaire who sold a company and now sits on the beach, gives a 
little to charity, and has all her money in Treasuries; her private wealth 
manager is desperately bored. Whereas if you are a billionaire who is 
politically-involved-ish, a Russian-ish oligarch-ish, own-ish some disputed-ish 
assets, have a need to hide-ish some of your assets, etc., then your private 
wealth manager will never be bored and will always be billing. “I need to sell 
my yacht by this afternoon, for reasons, but it’s currently drifting off the 
coast of North Korea,” you will frantically text your adviser, and she can 
charge you anything she wants.

But obviously, at the far right end of the graph, the hairiest clients are 
extremelyundesirable. The hairiest clients lead to money laundering convictions

In-seat promotions

Investment banks are, as far as I can tell, somewhat unusual employers in that 
everyone who works therehas two titles 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2023-06-13/naked-short-seller-gets-in-trouble?cmpid=BBD112624_MONEYSTUFF&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=241126&utm_campaign=moneystuff>
. One is a standardized title in the bank’s seniority hierarchy: analyst, 
associate, vice president, managing director, etc. The other is some sort of 
functional job description, “short-term yen rates options trader” or “head of 
investment banking coverage for the cardboard packaging industry” or “research 
analyst responsible for ball bearings manufacturers” or whatever.

Some of those functional jobs align with some level of the seniority 
hierarchy: The Global Head of Investment Banking will probably have the top 
title (partner, senior managing director, etc.), while someone running a tiny 
trading book will generally have a fairly junior title. But the alignment is 
not complete. Some fairly important trading books can be run by associates or 
managing directors, just depending on who’s available and who’s good and how 
long they have stayed in their seat.

Meanwhile many of the standardized titles are pretty strictly linked to 
seniority, so you can get promoted to the next title almost automatically after 
a set number of years. So you can be given some trading book or investment 
banking coverage universe as an associate, and then become a vice president and 
then a managing director while still doing the same job.

Or, at Citigroup Inc., not 
<https://www.ft.com/content/f5732fa2-4876-4595-b5e8-96d3cd6ef50f>:

Citigroup is slashing the number of year-end promotions it usually awards, as 
it seeks to hold costs down amid a long-term restructuring to shrink headcount 
and right the plodding bank.

Managers have been told that as many as 2,000 Citi employees could receive a 
bump to their pay and title in the next month, down from about 8,000 in 
previous rounds, four people familiar with the decisions said, cautioning that 
those decisions are not final.

The change relates to “in-seat promotions”, in which the bank awards pay 
increases and better titles without changing employees’ roles or responsibility.

In the past few weeks, unit heads have held meetings with their team members 
to manage expectations about promotions, two of the people said. The staffers 
were told promotions this year were in most cases only available to individuals 
who are taking on new roles or responsibilities.

“Promotions are only available to people taking on new roles or 
responsibilities” seems like a pretty normal message in most businesses, but in 
investment banking it is demoralizing.
