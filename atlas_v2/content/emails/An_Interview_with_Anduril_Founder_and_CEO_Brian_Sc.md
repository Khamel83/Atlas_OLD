Good morning,

This Daily Update interview is another installment of the Stratechery Founder series; as a reminder, one of the challenges in covering startups is the lack of available data. My solution is to go in the opposite direction and interview founders directly, letting them give their subjective overview of their companies, while pressing them on their business model, background, and long-term potential.

Today’s Daily Update Interview is with Anduril ( https://www.anduril.com/ ) co-founder and CEO Brian Schimpf ( https://twitter.com/SchimpfBrian ). Schimpf went to Cornell, where he competed in the DARPA Grand and Urban Challenges, before joining Palantir and rising to Director of Engineering. Then, in 2017, Schimpf founded Anduril with Palmer Luckey, Trae Stephens, Matt Grimm, and Joe Chen, with the mission of “Transforming US & allied military capabilities with advanced technology.”

In this interview we talk about Schimpf’s journey and what exactly that mission entails. Topics include why now is the time for a software-based defense contractor, despite software’s bad fit with the military procurement process; Lattice, Anduril’s software system, and its relation to traditional hardware products; Anduril’s potential as a diffuser and conduit for consumer market innovation; and other topics like production, China, and AI.

Background

*Brian Schimpf, welcome to Stratechery.*

*Brian Schimpf:* Hey, thanks for having me on. Really appreciate being here.

*Before we dive into Anduril, tell me more about yourself and your background. I am kind of curious: when you were a little kid and you had to sketch out your dreams in class, did you write down I want to be a defense contractor?*

*BS:* Yeah, I’m not sure a weapons maker was super high on the list when I was a kid. I’ve been coding since I’ve been 12. I’ve been an engineer forever, both my parents were engineers, my grandfather was an engineer, it’s just sort of in the blood. Went to school, did self-driving cars, I started in computer science, thought the people were a little too weird for me, so I ended up doing operations research. But I found self-driving cars, loved it, it was awesome. It was a cool time to be working in tech where over a very short period of time you went from nothing working to you just had amazing progress in the field in just five, seven years.

*What timeframe?*

*BS:* 2005-2007 range was when I worked on it the most. So really at kind of that DARPA Grand Challenge ( https://www.darpa.mil/about-us/timeline/-grand-challenge-for-autonomous-vehicles ) , Urban Challenge stage, when those were first getting going.

*Did you work on the Grand Challenge?*

*BS:* I did. So at Cornell, we did the Grand Challenge, which a bunch of undergrads, we did terrible on the desert race thing and then we did the urban race. We were one of six cars that finished. Weren’t in the top two obviously, but we were happy we were able to get through the whole race and successfully do it and it was a blast, it was a really fun experience.

*What were the changes in technology that were happening then? Because at this point, it’s somewhat ancient history, it’s like fifteen, sixteen years ago, but people weren’t really talking about self-driving cars back then, what was happening that was making it possible? You mentioned there was all that progress in a short amount of time.*

*BS:* Yeah, it’s interesting, it feels quite a bit different to the ways the technology you can ride today, where at the time I remember even trying to get enough compute to run and cool at the back of a car was a challenge. Lidar was not really existent, so you really had to kind of bolt these things together, computer vision didn’t work. I think the interesting thing was just the progress and robotics as a discipline made a lot of progress, you just had a community of people figuring out how to engineer these systems and make them work. It was a bit interesting, whereas I got to look back in the last five, ten years and there’s been this massive change in terms of buildability of GPUs, computer vision, machine learning, all of it has substantially changed how you think about what you can do with software holistically. At the time none of that was working yet, it was a bit of a different time, so it was a lot of just grind it out, engineering, and getting a community of humans together that just scrum on a problem rather than any kind of discreet technology changes that enabled it to start to go.

*Do you think that’s an experience that has helped you today, where you had to barely get it to work and in some respects, for folks approaching the problem today it’s too easy or is stuff just changed so fundamentally that you’re kind of starting from the same place?*

*BS:* I think the lessons I learned were that no problem is insurmountable, you can just dig in and go, you can just do it, you could just get brilliant people together and start solving these problems. I mean also this was pre-SpaceX, it was not obvious at the time what extent a lot of these things could be re-invented or “Did it really take 50 years to make any of these companies big”? So for me, a big lesson was these problems are just very solvable. There’s a lot of these grind it out, do the right thing problems that are really key.

Since then, I’ve looked at software, I’ve worked in software most of my career after. That changed a lot in the last fifteen years where a lot of it was figuring out how do you adopt and figure out how to apply the best-in-breed technologies — just take the best and open source, machine learning, all of this and actually figure out novel applications and smart ways to actually utilize the technology. And for me, the learning around “How do you actually build these compelling products, solve these problems and use the tools available faster than anybody else?”, became the dominant lesson for me over the last fifteen years or so.

*So what did you do after the early self-driving cars?*

*BS:* 2007 I was kind of, “Should I go do a PhD, this stuff’s kind of fun?”, there was no real self-driving car companies at the time, Waymo wasn’t a thing yet. A company called Palantir reached out, they were just getting started a couple of years in and I interviewed and I was like, “This is great”. At the time, as an East Coast kid, it was relatively hard to get into Silicon Valley, it just really wasn’t that common at the time; everyone was going into banking and finance and that sort of world. So I was like, “This seems great. I’m in,” and it ended up being a fantastic experience.

So I started out working on the government space there, that’s where I really got very enamored with the mission set, what problems people are trying to solve, the challenges of it, how passionate all the users are. It’s a calling for these folks, you don’t do national security as a job, it is a passion, and it was just a really compelling experience. Then I did a lot on the commercial side as well. Built up some great products there, some really cool technology. Got to see how a lot of different industrial companies operate, it was a fantastic experience. I was there for about ten years and then started Anduril with some folks from Palantir and with Palmer Luckey ( https://twitter.com/PalmerLuckey ).

The Anduril Opportunity

*So Anduril was founded in 2017 by yourself and, as you mentioned, most prominently Palmer Luckey, who most people have probably heard the most of. He had recently been removed from Facebook for what from the outside looked pretty much for being a Trump supporter ( https://www.wsj.com/articles/why-did-facebook-fire-a-top-executive-hint-it-had-something-to-do-with-trump-1541965245 ) , then you all founded Anduril, and at this time I think the sentiment about probably Washington broadly but certainly about defense contractors in Silicon Valley was pretty negative. Why then? What was the key moment then that made it the right time to start Anduril?*

*BS:* Yeah, I mean I think the timing on these things really does matter. I think what we saw was one, the pace of what Silicon Valley was able to accomplish both in the enterprise software world, which is sort of obvious, but the hardware world as well was becoming so much faster than what DOD [Department of Defense] was able to accomplish. So one, it seemed very ripe from just the pace, practices, how the competition operated, it just seemed like a very disruptable space.

The second was you have to time these things in DOD with the right urgency and generational change, and the threat of China and how that is playing out was one of these moments where you’ll see substantial shifts in behavior in how the Department of Defense invests, programs turns over, they just mobilize in a very different way when there’s that urgency around the problem.

*Well, it’s kind of like in tech, there always is a paradigm shift that drives new companies, but in tech we think of the mobile phone, but in the case of national defense, it’s like “New primary opponent”, that is the paradigm shift that matters.*

*BS:* 100%. That’s exactly right, which is there’s always a motivating factor that has sort of shifted the calculus, and you get this new equilibrium on the other side of it, and if you can time that right, take advantage of where you have that arbitrage on tech and really lean into it, you can do very, very well.

I think the other part that we had going in was the knowledge of “How do you sell into the government?”. That is not an obvious thing, very few people understand this, the sales cycles are bizarre and specific and it’s quite different. It’s not selling into even enterprise software, the closest analogs might be selling to oil and gas.

*Walk me through it. What is this bizarre sales cycle like?*

*BS:* Yeah, so the first thing you got to understand is there are very few buyers. So you think of an enterprise SaaS company, I’m selling data software, I’ve got hundreds of buyers of varying scales, and it’s kind of statistical which ones are ready to buy at any given time. But with very few buyers, they’re telling you when they’re going to buy what, and so you’ve got to see where they’re going to go, be competitive in advance, invest to show that you have that credibility, and then be able to get it at the right time to win that decades-long program, that’s how these things turn and go. The way we’ve had success with it is you’ve got the early adopter folks, and a lot of this looks a lot like enterprise software world, you got your special operations crew —

*Right. They all have their innovation teams that have their little budget and they can spend wherever they want.*

*BS:* Exactly.

*It’s like the experimental advertising budgets or something like that.*

*BS:* Exactly. Right, so you’re not making a business out of that, but you can get traction, you can build evidence of why you’re credible. You can build relationships, you can do all these things, so you kind of start there. You get the early adopter folks, you start getting operational experience.

But really these are quite lengthy requests for proposal cycles, bids, multi-year contracts, it is a very complex process, especially as you get up to higher and higher-end systems where these are maybe a three-to-five year long procurement process. It’s very, very substantial, but all along you got to convince them that you have the tech, you’ve invested the right way and you can scale.

What we tried to do is innovate on the business model. It’s just so obvious when you’re used to the commercial world where — crazy idea, company invests in technology it believes needs to exist, and we’ll move the market, and then once the technology works, sells it — that seems very obvious, that’s how we operate. We use a ton of internal research and development money to fund technologies that need to exist.

On the government side, that’s very atypical. What is typical there is the government pays you for all the research and development and companies are extending very little investment in IP into these programs, and that’s largely a product of the incentives. If you are not compensated for your IP and you’re only compensated for your labor and materials, then well guess what? You’re not going to invest, it doesn’t really help, certainly not beyond what would win need the contract. So for us, these are typically very long deals, but we’ve gotten a pretty good machine of how do we think about this three, five years out where we need to be.

*You’ve talked about this idea of “build to the mission”, which I think sort of relates to this. Now you talk about this R&D bit about how traditional defense contractors underinvests in R&D because they’re waiting for the government to give them money, and then effectively they get to tell them what to spend their R&D on. But that’s your differentiator, right? You’re actually spending the R&D upfront and anticipating what those requests are, is that the way to think about it?*

*BS:* Yeah, I think there’s two pieces to this. So certainly that’s a huge part of this, which is when we look at the space, we kind of say, “What are the capabilities that are going to move the needle for potential conflict in Taiwan?” Or what would Ukraine need or what does the Air Force need for next generation technologies? Some of those are within our reach, they make sense and we can kind of time them out. Some of those, maybe not right. But we’re probably not going to build a next generation bomber on our own dollar today.

*Did you anticipate that the Air Force is going to need a “Find My” token ( https://www.nytimes.com/2023/09/18/us/missing-f35-military-jet-charleston.html ) or maybe that wasn’t on the radar?*

*BS:* That is a pretty wild story. And I think that one was the Marine Corps, to give credit to the Air Force.

*Yes, that’s right. It was the Marine Corps, you’re right, I apologize!*

*BS:* Yeah, pretty wild. But yeah, so we largely looked at where does the military need to be? What are the technologies that need to exist and how do we invest get there?

Software and Military R&D

*BS:* I think the second part, again, that’s taking a playbook from Silicon Valley and how enterprise tech works, and how a lot of these modern technology companies are built. You invest in core software platform that is very general purpose and enables you to accelerate every other derivative thing you do with this, so we’ve built a core platform we call Lattice ( https://blog.anduril.com/anduril-unveils-lattice-for-mission-autonomy-8e0c5fa0e94b ). It’s really all about operation of teams of robotic and autonomous systems, that’s really what we do at the core of it, and that’s paid huge dividends. It enables us to solve very hard problems very quickly in a way that very few other companies can either solve the hardware problem or the software problem — basically none can solve both together.

*I mean, this is, I think one of the most interesting points, and it sort of ties into all this. When you think about a defense contractor, you think about hardware, weapons of war. We sort of joked about that at the very beginning, and the prompt to have you on Stratechery now was the announcement of this new drone fighter Fury ( https://www.anduril.com/fury/ ) , which we’ll get to in a little bit. But what you’re saying, and I think rings true to me, is that hardware is probably the wrong frame of reference for thinking about Anduril and why you are fundamentally different than other defense contractors.*

*BS:* Yeah, I think that’s right. I mean, look, at the end of the day, the DOD, the Department of Defense, they know how to buy hardware. They can count the number of planes, they know why it costs money, they can count the number of missiles. There’s a very clear understanding of what a price for that should be, what you’re paying for and what you’re getting, it’s very obvious. The government does not believe it will run a factory to produce these things on the whole. There’s some things they produce, which is very random, but the software side is not that today.

So the software side, it is constantly a discussion and debate around how much of the government own the IP, what should the structures be, how do you get the incentives you want out of industry? It’s a really tricky problem. Where you look at it from the government’s perspective, they’re sort of like, “Well, it doesn’t cost you to make marginal copies of this, so why am I paying you?” And you look at it from the software companies perspective-

*Software is a pure R&D cost. It’s not a marginal cost, which doesn’t match up with the way government is paid for stuff traditionally.*

*BS:* Exactly. So you just have this massive mismatch, and that is a real challenge. So by doing the hardware side, we’re in a lot of ways able to wrap it in metal. The software is often the, “Why us?”. It’s like we’re able to solve problems in an interesting and novel way because we can make them smarter, but it’s very hard to price that, understand that, know how that fits, but the hardware side enables us to solve this.

The second piece of this is the hardware of what has been built to date is not designed around how do I have the right software APIs, control, be able to deploy modern machine learning models into it, it’s just not how it works. It’s very hard-coded, it’s very baked, it’s very designed for manual control of these things, and so when you want to apply all these advanced software techniques, it falls apart. So designing hardware that’s open and API-driven from the beginning, that has enabled us to be extremely effective of how we tie these together as an interesting family of systems.

*So let me see if I have what you just articulated right from a big picture perspective, I want to tie it all together. So you are a Silicon Valley startup, and you are able then to get investors that understand the idea of investing huge upfront amounts in R&D to build a software stack knowing that you can monetize in the long run, so that lets you build this differentiated operating system, basically Lattice, that then in the long run will let you produce very highly capable weapons that are much more capable relative to their marginal cost, which is highly differentiated. You could sell it to the military, “Look, you can get this weapon for relatively cheap, that’s incredibly capable because we have this ability.” It’s because you did the R&D because you’re a software company, you have this massive go-to-market advantage in the long run for any weapons platform you want to build. Is that the good one-minute summary there?*

*BS:* That’s basically spot on and that’s exactly how we think about the business.

*I mean, it sounds a lot like Palantir ( https://stratechery.com/2023/an-interview-with-palantir-cto-shyam-sankar-and-head-of-global-commercial-ted-mabrey/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIzL2FuLWludGVydmlldy13aXRoLXBhbGFudGlyLWN0by1zaHlhbS1zYW5rYXItYW5kLWhlYWQtb2YtZ2xvYmFsLWNvbW1lcmNpYWwtdGVkLW1hYnJleS8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTY5Nzg4MTk2NiwiaWF0IjoxNjk1Mjg5OTY2LCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwaS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IlJzZXVZRThqZ0hlUHBXSmYxZkFiNEMiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.kHZjg_ZIoHyNlg1nk7s6SOuMU4EQdM0QBxKudAG8u9-Ea6-hMZbDLgBNx-st8cUa1nvWfI0PglUX5Ky4o0NSKlrda8K79iSp9R2yub2GPUPZxaoEpu3xiObwST3vEVvH9qbczu1RwuWsupFxje5Uqte6OKX3Dn_sIMwnLzstOpFN9YMi41GDJ2WiuXniKMzmCnjuJ0CSLehDNfmBHkis6lPGNnWO9hTlQEJxZCjE9izvVQe4eoBsk3d54W_kuH_TJpPdQO6r_dj25DaEh81N44ozKFmrHIfyhp2PBaXNswN_JkwsZwCgzcFQ305F1y1nustoXsr_wLglXODM-f2dNg ) , right?*

*BS:* Yeah.

*Was that very much an inspiration for how you were thinking about that or is this just the obvious thing to do?*

*BS:* No, it’s very much similar sort of play in that regard. I think with Palantir, we were always selling pure software but a similar sort of thesis. We’ve got this amazing data and analytics platform, we can solve your problems.

*Customize on top of that, whatever it be.*

*BS:* Exactly right, pretty straightforward. The hardware side has been a huge unlock compared to being boxed in a lot of ways and limited in scope of what you can do. We’re able to address a huge percentage of the $800 billion DOD market and it would not surprise me if we see trillion dollar defense budgets in the next three or four years and so the addressable space you can get to once you start getting into those hardware platforms is massive, and that’s really been the key unlock we’ve had, which is that, “Look, at the end of the day, it’s like the missiles, the planes, the ships, those are what you need to fight a war”. We look at what’s being consumed in Ukraine, it’s missiles, right? It’s weapons.

*They’re consumables basically.*

*BS:* They’re consumables.

*They need to be replaced all the time.*

Anduril Products

*Walk me through the product suite real quick, just at a high level. I made a list here: ALTIUS ( https://www.anduril.com/hardware/altius/ ) , Anvil ( https://www.anduril.com/hardware/anvil/ ) , Dive-LD ( https://www.anduril.com/hardware/dive-ld/ ) , Dust ( https://www.anduril.com/capability/land/ ) , Fury ( https://www.anduril.com/fury/ ) , Ghost ( https://www.anduril.com/hardware/ghost-autonomous-suas/ ) , Sentry ( https://www.anduril.com/hardware/sentry/ ). I guess we could get into all the specific use cases, but is there a sense where these are all the same thing, they just are adapted to different environments?*

*BS:* I kind of group it into two or three big categories of technology we work on. So one is where we started, which is I would say these sort of land-based force protection systems. So this is counter-drone, border security, all these capabilities. You’re typically having a suite of sensors and things to defeat drones, be it Anvil knocking a drone out of the sky, jamming capabilities, all of that. So we have this kind of ability to sense what’s going on, react to it, and then deploy some countermeasure to it. That’s the crux of what we do on a lot of these counter-drone enforcement products.

*Does this all happen automatically or to what extent are humans in the loop here?*

*BS:* You can do it automatically, but in reality with the DOD, defensive is one of the only places they would ever allow something to go full automatic. But in practice, they really don’t because the consequences of getting something wrong are just too high, these things are very serious. So what we’ve tried to do is automate the most kind of mechanical portions of this. So steering a camera around, “Is this a drone or a bird?”, “What types of weapons would be effective against this drone?” — we’ve tried to automate those steps so that it’s very straightforward. Lattice

*So I want to dive a little bit more into Lattice. *BS:* That’s right. Another area we’re seeing huge return right now is the 5G community invested so heavily in software-defined radios (SDR). When you look at how the defense market worked prior to about three to five years ago, you had to custom tape-out chips, you had to make all these unique things, like it was a $100 million investment to get next-generation capabilities. Now, I just got to stay on the latest Xilinx and analog devices, SDRs, faster than anyone else, marry it up with GPUs and have the best software and packaging for this, and I’ve got the most cutting-edge system at a very low dollar invested, comparatively.

And so, it’s just a very different time. Recognizing the fact that technology innovation is happening incredibly fast, whoever can apply it fastest is going to have the advantage, is really where we’re at. Then, hopefully in the long run, we can also change the DOD procurement systems to recognize this fact as well, which is constant tech refresh, constant maturation, that’s the goal. We just want this, “Buy the new iPhone every year, don’t lock in an iPhone from ten years ago — like, what are you doing?”. Figuring out that model is really the key for the future.

*Yeah, and to your point, it’s obviously a better way to do it, but this is where having China as a way to change the old guard and change the views was a really essential catalyst to make this possible.*

Production

*One more question from that earlier question. Where do you make products? You talked about scalability and the idea is you’re going to make a consumable. You talked about the fact that China has massively more industrial capacity. Obviously, you’re not making products in China. Where are they made and how much have you had to invest in building out this supply chain capability?*

*BS:* The strategy we’ve taken is twofold. So, one is there’s a large amount of consumer and commercial manufacturing capacity within the US, so you can find a lot of companies that are making things for consumer electronics and industrial electronics, and all these things. That doesn’t have to be defense-unique, there’s a lot of parts here that could be farmed out to modern supply chain on this. So, we’ve been able to take advantage for piece part manufacturing, PCBA manufacturing, composites, various things. Taking advantage of the industrial base that does exist. We use COTS [commercial-off-the-shelf] parts whenever possible. So, if we can just get a camera, let’s just buy a camera, why are we making a custom camera if we don’t need to? We’ve been able to take advantage of the commercial industrial supply base in a very big way.

The second though is then you get to the final assembly configuration and “How do I actually pull this together?” A lot of the unique parts we’re trying to drive into manufacturing are more around how do we think about this as this sort of, we don’t need to spend a huge amount to bring up a line. We can very quickly go from prototype into production through kind of continuous process of maturing up work instruction, supply chain, hardening each piece at a time as we continuously move through this cycle, and that’s very hard. Getting that manufacturing system working, so that you can do this fast introduction of prototypes, minimize your ramp time, and have constant change in iteration as you go, is a very complicated process. So, we’ve invested heavily in that.

*Does that mean building your own assembly sites? Or are you trying to build an operating system across other ones?*

*BS:* Yeah.

*So you do have your own final assembly sites?*

*BS:* Yeah. We’ve got manufacturing in SoCal and Atlanta, and Massachusetts. We’ve got manufacturing locations in a number of spots right now and so our view is, “Hey, we’ll do full assembly tests, manufacturing that’s high value, we can get a lot of lift out of doing this. But everything else we can pull out, let’s take advantage of the huge supply chain that already exists in the States.”

*Yep, that makes sense.*

AI

*The last question, this is sort of an obvious one, is AI generally. I am curious, you talked about China and Ukraine being big spurs for the Pentagon changing their view. Palmer did say in an article a couple of weeks ago ( https://breakingdefense.com/2023/09/palmer-luckey-anduril-ipo-chatgpt-interview/ ) that ChatGPT actually made selling a lot easier. Is that something that you’ve noticed as well, just a broad awareness of this? I mean there’s obviously been tons of talk about AI ( https://www.nytimes.com/2023/09/13/technology/silicon-valley-ai-washington-schumer.html ) and the whole thing with China and chips and all that sort of thing in Washington, has there been a step change though over the last year that has made your job easier in some respects?*

*BS:* The feeling of urgency and “We are running out of time to make a difference on the Taiwan scenario” is palpable. It has changed dramatically in the last — I think there’s a part of that, which is just time is running out. Xi is a little bit unstable, the bad facts on the economy in China don’t work in favor of this going well. So, I think there’s a little bit of that.

I do think the AI piece has kind of put a clearer marker on the competition that exists between China and the US. First it was TikTok and then the AI piece, CHIPS Act was shortly after all that, and the AI pieces really has crystallized for folks that we are in a unique time where long-term consequential advantage will be established, and we need to secure that, but the stakes are high.

So, when the stakes are high, the desire for someone like Xi to mobilize against this becomes really consequential and so you end up in this very tricky, precarious time where people believe tech is a huge advantage both from a commercial and industrial base as well as defense base. Xi is getting more desperate, and this matters more than it ever has. That is a palpable feeling in DC right now, people do not believe they’re in a slow race here.

*AI is sort of a catch-all term, but I think one of the most interesting things about generative AI in particular is it’s really highlighted the difference between probabilistic and deterministic computing. You go back to the way hardware platforms worked or you go back to your self-driving fifteen years ago, and it was pretty deterministic, like “If A, do B” programming. Whereas AI, particularly in the generative perspective is probabilistic, and that’s why it can get stuff wrong, and it’s also why I can be more capable and creative in those things. How does that apply, that dichotomy or that difference, if at all, to what you are doing? Is it super important that you’re still pretty deterministic, or is this why it’s important to keep the human in the loop? How much does that impact what you’re working on?*

*BS:* Predictability matters, but predictability is a bounded thing. When you employ a weapon, you want to know what it’s going to do, but you want to know what it’s going to do within bounds. The DOD has always worked with a kind of stochastic lens to these things where with a weapon, they talk about the error bounds — how accurate is it going to be, how it engages. They talk about with targeting, “What’s the uncertainty around this?” and so, they’ve always had this notion of bounding the uncertainty on these things in very practical ways that you can train to an 18-year-old enlisted guy. It’s actually a pretty good system and so the reality is they’ve always dealt with uncertainty. When you deal with sensor systems, it is always statistical by nature, and so there’s always noise in your returns, so that has always been the case.

When I think you start talking about autonomy systems making decisions now and the bounds become more fuzzy, you’ve got to reset those bounds, and what I’m willing to let the systems do. The reality of how it’s going to shake out is it will just be bounded in a different way. We know that when I release this autonomous fighter jet, it’s going to stay in this sort of box, doing these things, and it will engage according to these rules that I have programmed. I think that will be true, and to the extent that generative AI fits in those bounds, which it very much can, I can generate plans constrained within these inputs. I can use generative AI to evoke APIs that are inherently bounded, and so I think there’s a lot of applicability for thinking through things within limits and how you activate the system in predictable ways and in transparent ways. That’s where I think these are going to get most readily applied.

The reality is most of the DOD’s job is descriptive, and so a lot of the descriptive AI pieces have been much more beneficial, like computer vision models, machine learning models, all these kind of classifiers. Very little of the DOD job is inherently creative. There’s some pieces around the art of war and how you plan maneuvers and these different things where I do think there is a lot of applicability. But that implies to an extent you’ve been able to digitize and training it on context and understanding — which historically has not been digitized.

So I think there’s just a mismatch there for now, but there’s still a ton of applicability on this on consuming information and intelligence gathering, and capturing new materials. There’s all these great applications of it but from the, “I’m going to find the adversary and attack them,” it’s the sort of thing that’s going to require a large degree of thoughtfulness, precision of how we proceed on that.

*Is there a Big Six now? Has Anduril graduated to that level? Or what is going to be necessary for you to get there and what might prevent you from becoming the Big Six as opposed to the Big Five?*

*BS:* The key for us is if we can get some of these major big strategic platforms adopted, employed, and rolled out, that is what’s going to move the needle the most. So if you’ve got the pedigree and the mass to be able to produce, scale and deliver these next-generation capabilities and probably even more importantly, the DOD believes that it’s possible they know how to employ these, and they know what they want out of them. Where we’re kind of just going through the first generation of this right now, they don’t even know how to fight with these right now, and so the idea of how would you even use it is hard. If we get through that, I think we will see this avalanche of new platforms wanting to go more autonomous, cheaper, all of that and that’s really when I think we’ll leapfrog into the top tier.

*And that’s when all the network effects of software and the dispersion advantage that you talked about would really come to bear?*

*BS:* You got it. That’s exactly the thesis.

*Very good. Well, Brian Schimpf, it was great talking to you and I look forward to observing how Anduril evolves. And I hope we can talk again soon.*
