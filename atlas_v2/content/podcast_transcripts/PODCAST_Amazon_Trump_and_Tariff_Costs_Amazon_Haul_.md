The shopping site will display how much of an item’s cost is derived from tariffs – right next to the product’s total listed price. The new pricing display will be implemented in the coming weeks as Trump’s tariffs begin to affect most imported products.It’s a bit of a risky move for Amazon. Going on offense against Trump-imposed tariffs may cause its 300 million active customers to direct their anger toward the administration and not the retailer. But it could also irk Trump, who isn’t afraid of retaliating. That has been a boon to businesses like Temu and Shein, which sell Chinese-made products to U.S. consumers and then ship them directly to customers, without paying any duty along the way.Amazon almost certainly saw Temu and Shein as a threat, so late last year they launched Amazon Haul, a direct competitor; I wondered about their motivation in that Update:It’s hard to understand the point of this new offering if it’s not leveraging thede minimisloophole; indeed, my theory at the time (which I can’t find a link to — it may have been on Sharp Tech) is that Amazon was launching this program for the express purpose of drawing attention to this loophole in the hope it might be closed. After all, Amazon rather enjoys its position as the preeminent retailer of cheap Chinese goods, particularly if they are stored in their warehouses and delivered with Prime…I suspect that when Amazon says “we also hear from customers” they mean “we look nervously at Temu and Shein’s rising sales numbers”; Haul, interestingly enough, is only available on mobile, where Temu and Shein derive their sales. In other words, I still believe in my original thesis: Amazon would love for this loophole to be closed; a world where everyone pays the same import duties is one where Amazon’s delivery network can be much more effectively brought to bear.Temu, meanwhile, is doing exactly what Punchbowl reported: showing tariff costs to customers. FromCNBC:Chinese e-tailer Temu has started adding “import charges” of about 145% in response to President Donald Trump’s tariffs. The fees, which began cropping up over the weekend after price hikes went into effect on Friday, cost more than the individual products consumers are buying and can more than double the price of a typical order.For example, a summer dress sold on Temu for $18.47 will cost $44.68 after $26.21 in import charges are added to the bill, a 142% surcharge, a CNBC analysis shows. A child’s bathing suit priced at $12.44 will cost shoppers $31.12 when the $18.68 import charge is taken into account, a staggering 150% fee. A handheld vacuum cleaner listed at $16.93 now costs $40.11 when factoring in an import charge of $21.68, which is a roughly 137% markup.This is almost certainly how we arrived at this story: if we assume that Amazon Haul:Was created to be a Temu and Shein competitor by:Taking advantage of thede minimisexception then:Amazon Haul executives figured it made sense to copy Temu’s tariff disclosure.The problem for Amazon — again, assuming their explanation is true, and I think it makes perfect sense — is that the report in question didn’t distinguish between Amazon proper and Amazon Haul; everyone, including the White House, assumed that the reported plans, such as they existed, was for the former, but I bet it was for the latter.Political LessonsSo what are the lessons here?The first one is in fact in the Punchbowl report, specifically the line that “Amazon didn’t respond to a request for comment.” This was obviously a mistake; Amazon at a minimum needed to ensure that the report properly identified Amazon Haul was considering this move, not Amazon proper.The second lesson is about how corporate incentives can become misaligned. Amazon Haul executives are charged with building Amazon Haul and competing with Temu and Shein; it’s natural that their decision-making would be centered on that specific KPI, and in that sense it makes sense to copy Temu’s tariff disclosure. The problem is that this is counter to the interest of Amazon’s overall business.The third lesson is about the danger of getting cute in an attempt to make political points: if I’m right in my assumption that the purpose — or at leastapurpose — of Amazon Haul was to highlight the existence/abuse of thede minimisexception, then what we ended up with is a bit of a corporate political stunt that totally backfired on the company.The most important lesson of all, however, is the point I made inAmerican Disruption, extending the argument I originally made inInternet 3.0 and the Beginning of (Tech) History: we are entering the political era, and executives have to view everything they do through that lens. From “American Disruption”:Internet 3.0 is about politics. This is the era when countries make economically sub-optimal choices for reasons that can’t be measured in dollars and cents. In that Article I thought that Big Tech exercising its power against the President might be a spur for other countries to seek to wean themselves away from American companies; instead it is the U.S. that may be leaving other countries little choice but to retaliate against U.S. tech.This episode is another variation: in the end we had the U.S. government threatening retaliation against a U.S. company for disclosing the cost of tariffs. Is this a good thing? Absolutely not. Is it reality? Unfortunately yes, and companies need to internalize that this risk vector exists. This actually ties all of those lessons together:Companies need to be dramatically more pro-active in terms of managing PRCompanies need to communicate up-and-down the chain the importance of considering political risk in decision-makingCompanies need to carefully consider programs that increase political riskAgain, this is all terrible, both in terms of principle and in terms of economics. All three of these points are contrary to efficiency and effective management, and of course a terrible abuse of the role of government and rule of law. It is, however, reality, and Amazon won’t be the last company to accidentally step on a political mine. Some of these may in the end be unavoidable, but executives would do well to recognize and start ameliorating the risk.This Update will be available as a podcast later today.
