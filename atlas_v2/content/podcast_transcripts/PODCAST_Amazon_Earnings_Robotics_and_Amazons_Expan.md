The company said it plans to spend around $75 billion on capex this year and more next year as part of a push to build generative AI services…Shares of Amazon — which had risen more than 20% this year — rose over 5% in after-hours trading following its results Thursday.Amazon followed in the path ofGoogleandMicrosoftwith bullish AI-related growth and the promise of further spending; CEO Andy Jassy said onthe earnings call:AWS grew 19.1% year-over-year and now stands at a $110 billion annualized run rate. We’ve seen significant reacceleration of AWS growth for the last four quarters.Here’s the chart of that growth:It’s worth noting that the declining growth rates in 2022 were attributed to some combination of COVID overhang and customers wanting to reduce their costs; Amazon executives regularly assured investors throughout the year that growth would return, which is to say it’s unclear what percentage of this growth is actually a recovery from that slowdown and what is attributable to AI specifically. Jassy did say:The AWS team continues to make rapid progress in delivering AI capabilities for customers in building a substantial AI business. In the last 18 months, AWS has released nearly twice as many machine learning and GenAI features as the other leading cloud providers combined. AWS’s AI business is a multibillion-dollar revenue run rate business that continues to grow at a triple-digit year-over-year percentage and is growing more than 3 times faster at this stage of its evolution as AWS itself grew, and we felt like AWS grew pretty quickly.The use of “run rate” suggests to me that this is a business that just crossed $500 million in quarterly revenue; which would be about 1.82% of AWS’s business for the quarter. A triple-digit year-over-year percentage suggests that AI was about $50 million a year ago, which would mean that the core AWS business grew a very respectable 17%. In short, it looks like both stories are true.Everyday Essentials and Amazon’s First Party Retail BusinessWhat is particularly notable to me is just how profitable the North American retail business has become:Amazon’s logistics network was also dramatically overbuilt during COVID, and the combination of growing into and rationalizing that capacity was a big drag on earnings; now, though, revenue growth has significantly exceeded expenses growth for nearly two years, leading to big profits. Notice, though, that their growth rates are re-converging. It will be interesting to see if Amazon can maintain a gap in growth rates; even if they keep them even, though, the gap that was built-up over the last two years should continue to be a very profitable one.The challenge for Amazon is keeping up this profitability given the phenomenonI touched on last quarter, wherein Amazon’s unit growth is outpacing its revenue growth as its one-or-same-day delivery convinces more customers to buy so-called “Everyday Essentials” from Amazon. As I noted last quarter, accessing this category is a big win for Amazon:This strikes me as a big deal: Amazon is saying that their increase in delivery speed has increased their total addressable market, specifically that they are taking share in terms of “everyday essentials” — think classic CPG items like shampoo or detergent or paper towels…Now these items are low margin, and Amazon has paid the price over the last few years for the build-out necessary to achieve this level of service; there’s a reason, though, that Morton went back to that old Jeff Bezos quote, “Your margin is my opportunity.” The march to achieveThe Amazon Taxcontinues apace, and it’s a big deal that the company may have unlocked an entirely new category to collect on.This quarter brought new details about how exactly Amazon will access this market without blowing up its profitability. The first way is exactly what you would expect: Amazon believes that being involved in more purchase decisions will ensure they get the big-ticket items too. Jassy said in answer to an analyst question:When you look at the split between revenue growth and unit growth, you do see some impact of the lower ASP products that we’re selling as well as some of the trade down that consumers are doing. But we take that as a real positive, seeing the growth in everyday essential categories, which are really predicated on speed. So you have to have fast delivery to be able to sell the those products to customers. And when you do, it results in a stickier consumer relationship, higher orders, building larger baskets, which help our ship economics, and repeat orders are stronger. So those are all positive signs, and we’ll take any short-term degradation in ASP because what we’re focused on primarily is free cash flow here. And we see the ability to unlock all new elements of the consumer spend.Second, most of these everyday essentials — and in contrast to larger-ticket items — are first-party, not third-party. Jassy said in response to a question about the declining share of third-party unit mix:Third-party sellers, yes, the percent of paid units was 60% in Q3. And if you look over the last two years, it’s — essentially, it was at 59%, stepping up to 60%, stepping up to 61% pretty much every second quarter. So what we’re seeing is still in a tight band between 59% and 61%. But what we’re seeing is the impact of the everyday essentials. 3P demand is still strong and unit volumes are strong. It’s just the everyday essentials tend to skew more to 1P than 3P.To step back, first-party means that Amazon buys the product from the manufacturer and then sells it to the customer. This means that both the entire price of the item and the cost of the item are on the income statement. Third-party means that the seller lists their product on Amazon, and either handles fulfillment themselves or, more likely, pays to have Amazon hold their inventory and fulfill orders. Those third-party sellers could be the manufacturers, or they could be another wholesaler or retailer.There are a number of reasons why it makes sense that everyday essentials are more likely to be first-party rather than third-party. First, everyday essential manufacturers are probably less likely to be equipped to be retailers in their own right; think CPG companies like P&G and Unilever that are used to distributing their goods through retailers, both online and off. Second, by cutting out any other middlemen Amazon keeps all of the available margin, which is beneficial given that these are relatively cheap items. Third, because these are everyday essentials, Amazon likely wants total control over inventory so they can assure they are distributed across their network to enable same-day or one-day delivery. Fourth, given the very high turnover in these items, there probably isn’t much money to be made from warehouse charges anyways. Fifth, these items are probably less likely to command as much advertising dollars, another way in which Amazon makes money off of third-party sellers; indeed, even if advertising dollars were available, capturing the full benefit of these items entails making them as easy to buy as possible.The third detail about this business was the talking points Amazon shared about a new facility in Shreveport, Louisiana that just launched that is far more automated than anything Amazon has built yet. Jassy said in his prepared remarks:We continue to innovate in robotics to speed delivery, lower cost to serve, and further improve safety in our fulfillment network. We recently launched our 12th-generation fulfillment center design with the first building launching in Shreveport, Louisiana. This is the first facility that incorporates our newest robotics inventions that simplify stowing, picking, packing, and shipping processes. Thus far, this new design reduces fulfillment processing time by up to 25%, increases the number of items we can offer for same-day or next-day delivery and is expected to drive a 25% improvement in our cost to serve during peak within this next generation facility. Though we believe we have more expansive automation and robotics than other retail peers, it’s still early days in how much automation we expect in our fulfillment network.Jassy added in an answer later in the call:The reason why we’re trying to have more robotics and automation in our fulfillment network is it allows us to ship more quickly, to ship more cost effectively, and to make conditions even safer for our fulfillment teammates than what they already have today. And I think something that the team has done in a very disciplined way, and I talked about this a little bit in my annual letter, my shareholder letter is just they have been very thoughtful about defining what are the primitive foundational building blocks that you need, that you can then use in lots of different combinations to build additional automation/robotics capabilities down the road, so we can move even more quickly with the next generation of robotics capabilities. And that team has invested in those and they’re already working on the next generation.This is almost certainly the most important component in terms of controlling expense growth in the long run. Every involvement of human pickers or packers is a marginal cost that provides a floor in terms of what Amazon can sell profitably; to the extent that Amazon can continue to replace humans with robots, whose marginal cost is basically electricity, is the extent they can offer ever lower-priced everyday essentials profitably. Of course they still have to deliver them, but that is where the self-driving cars and drones come in!What is interesting is how this mirrors so many of Amazon’s other build-outs in the past. Way back when Amazon Logistics was just a rumor I wrote inan Update:It seems likely that Amazon will build out this network from the inside out, by first connecting its fulfillment centers with each other and with suppliers, leaving the more logistically challenging and expensive last mile delivery to whomever wants to fight for it. That doesn’t sound great for UPS’ already low e-commerce margins.That’s exactly what happened, and, over time, Amazon has pushed further and further into the last mile, which means that UPS and USPS are stuck with the least profitable deliveries while Amazon has internalized the rest.The way this is poised to play out in terms of Amazon’s sales is a little different. For many years  Amazon has been increasing the number of third-party sales, which really give Amazon the best of both worlds: they get way more selection, and they have much higher margins because they’re basically running a services business, charging for everything from warehousing to discovery via advertising to transactions to delivery. I don’t think they’re going to really move into those markets that are already dominated by third-party sellers. At the same time, I expect that this expansion of first-party goods will continue to increase, particularly as the marginal cost of fulfilling them drops thanks to robotics. The end result will be that everything with high turnover is first party, and everything with low turnover is third party, which is to say Amazon will maximize its profitability for both.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. As I noted last quarter, accessing this category is a big win for Amazon:This strikes me as a big deal: Amazon is saying that their increase in delivery speed has increased their total addressable market, specifically that they are taking share in terms of “everyday essentials” — think classic CPG items like shampoo or detergent or paper towels…Now these items are low margin, and Amazon has paid the price over the last few years for the build-out necessary to achieve this level of service; there’s a reason, though, that Morton went back to that old Jeff Bezos quote, “Your margin is my opportunity.” The march to achieveThe Amazon Taxcontinues apace, and it’s a big deal that the company may have unlocked an entirely new category to collect on.This quarter brought new details about how exactly Amazon will access this market without blowing up its profitability. The first way is exactly what you would expect: Amazon believes that being involved in more purchase decisions will ensure they get the big-ticket items too. Jassy said in answer to an analyst question:When you look at the split between revenue growth and unit growth, you do see some impact of the lower ASP products that we’re selling as well as some of the trade down that consumers are doing. But we take that as a real positive, seeing the growth in everyday essential categories, which are really predicated on speed. So you have to have fast delivery to be able to sell the those products to customers. So what we’re seeing is still in a tight band between 59% and 61%. But what we’re seeing is the impact of the everyday essentials. 3P demand is still strong and unit volumes are strong. It’s just the everyday essentials tend to skew more to 1P than 3P.To step back, first-party means that Amazon buys the product from the manufacturer and then sells it to the customer. This means that both the entire price of the item and the cost of the item are on the income statement. Third-party means that the seller lists their product on Amazon, and either handles fulfillment themselves or, more likely, pays to have Amazon hold their inventory and fulfill orders. Those third-party sellers could be the manufacturers, or they could be another wholesaler or retailer.There are a number of reasons why it makes sense that everyday essentials are more likely to be first-party rather than third-party. First, everyday essential manufacturers are probably less likely to be equipped to be retailers in their own right; think CPG companies like P&G and Unilever that are used to distributing their goods through retailers, both online and off. Second, by cutting out any other middlemen Amazon keeps all of the available margin, which is beneficial given that these are relatively cheap items. Third, because these are everyday essentials, Amazon likely wants total control over inventory so they can assure they are distributed across their network to enable same-day or one-day delivery. Fourth, given the very high turnover in these items, there probably isn’t much money to be made from warehouse charges anyways. Fifth, these items are probably less likely to command as much advertising dollars, another way in which Amazon makes money off of third-party sellers; indeed, even if advertising dollars were available, capturing the full benefit of these items entails making them as easy to buy as possible.The third detail about this business was the talking points Amazon shared about a new facility in Shreveport, Louisiana that just launched that is far more automated than anything Amazon has built yet. Jassy said in his prepared remarks:We continue to innovate in robotics to speed delivery, lower cost to serve, and further improve safety in our fulfillment network. We recently launched our 12th-generation fulfillment center design with the first building launching in Shreveport, Louisiana. This is the first facility that incorporates our newest robotics inventions that simplify stowing, picking, packing, and shipping processes. Thus far, this new design reduces fulfillment processing time by up to 25%, increases the number of items we can offer for same-day or next-day delivery and is expected to drive a 25% improvement in our cost to serve during peak within this next generation facility. Though we believe we have more expansive automation and robotics than other retail peers, it’s still early days in how much automation we expect in our fulfillment network.Jassy added in an answer later in the call:The reason why we’re trying to have more robotics and automation in our fulfillment network is it allows us to ship more quickly, to ship more cost effectively, and to make conditions even safer for our fulfillment teammates than what they already have today. And I think something that the team has done in a very disciplined way, and I talked about this a little bit in my annual letter, my shareholder letter is just they have been very thoughtful about defining what are the primitive foundational building blocks that you need, that you can then use in lots of different combinations to build additional automation/robotics capabilities down the road, so we can move even more quickly with the next generation of robotics capabilities. And that team has invested in those and they’re already working on the next generation.This is almost certainly the most important component in terms of controlling expense growth in the long run. Every involvement of human pickers or packers is a marginal cost that provides a floor in terms of what Amazon can sell profitably; to the extent that Amazon can continue to replace humans with robots, whose marginal cost is basically electricity, is the extent they can offer ever lower-priced everyday essentials profitably. Of course they still have to deliver them, but that is where the self-driving cars and drones come in!What is interesting is how this mirrors so many of Amazon’s other build-outs in the past. That doesn’t sound great for UPS’ already low e-commerce margins.That’s exactly what happened, and, over time, Amazon has pushed further and further into the last mile, which means that UPS and USPS are stuck with the least profitable deliveries while Amazon has internalized the rest.The way this is poised to play out in terms of Amazon’s sales is a little different. For many years  Amazon has been increasing the number of third-party sales, which really give Amazon the best of both worlds: they get way more selection, and they have much higher margins because they’re basically running a services business, charging for everything from warehousing to discovery via advertising to transactions to delivery. I don’t think they’re going to really move into those markets that are already dominated by third-party sellers. At the same time, I expect that this expansion of first-party goods will continue to increase, particularly as the marginal cost of fulfilling them drops thanks to robotics. The third detail about this business was the talking points Amazon shared about a new facility in Shreveport, Louisiana that just launched that is far more automated than anything Amazon has built yet. Jassy said in his prepared remarks: We continue to innovate in robotics to speed delivery, lower cost to serve, and further improve safety in our fulfillment network. We recently launched our 12th-generation fulfillment center design with the first building launching in Shreveport, Louisiana. This is the first facility that incorporates our newest robotics inventions that simplify stowing, picking, packing, and shipping processes. Thus far, this new design reduces fulfillment processing time by up to 25%, increases the number of items we can offer for same-day or next-day delivery and is expected to drive a 25% improvement in our cost to serve during peak within this next generation facility. Though we believe we have more expansive automation and robotics than other retail peers, it’s still early days in how much automation we expect in our fulfillment network. The reason why we’re trying to have more robotics and automation in our fulfillment network is it allows us to ship more quickly, to ship more cost effectively, and to make conditions even safer for our fulfillment teammates than what they already have today. And I think something that the team has done in a very disciplined way, and I talked about this a little bit in my annual letter, my shareholder letter is just they have been very thoughtful about defining what are the primitive foundational building blocks that you need, that you can then use in lots of different combinations to build additional automation/robotics capabilities down the road, so we can move even more quickly with the next generation of robotics capabilities. And that team has invested in those and they’re already working on the next generation. This is almost certainly the most important component in terms of controlling expense growth in the long run. Every involvement of human pickers or packers is a marginal cost that provides a floor in terms of what Amazon can sell profitably; to the extent that Amazon can continue to replace humans with robots, whose marginal cost is basically electricity, is the extent they can offer ever lower-priced everyday essentials profitably. Of course they still have to deliver them, but that is where the self-driving cars and drones come in! It seems likely that Amazon will build out this network from the inside out, by first connecting its fulfillment centers with each other and with suppliers, leaving the more logistically challenging and expensive last mile delivery to whomever wants to fight for it. That doesn’t sound great for UPS’ already low e-commerce margins. That’s exactly what happened, and, over time, Amazon has pushed further and further into the last mile, which means that UPS and USPS are stuck with the least profitable deliveries while Amazon has internalized the rest. The way this is poised to play out in terms of Amazon’s sales is a little different. For many years  Amazon has been increasing the number of third-party sales, which really give Amazon the best of both worlds: they get way more selection, and they have much higher margins because they’re basically running a services business, charging for everything from warehousing to discovery via advertising to transactions to delivery. I don’t think they’re going to really move into those markets that are already dominated by third-party sellers. At the same time, I expect that this expansion of first-party goods will continue to increase, particularly as the marginal cost of fulfilling them drops thanks to robotics. The end result will be that everything with high turnover is first party, and everything with low turnover is third party, which is to say Amazon will maximize its profitability for both.
