So this gets to the presence question, to take this back to a point that you made at the beginning, you call it teleportation. What’s exciting about this vision, no pun intended, is this is one that actually you can communicate about VR and you can particularly communicate it after someone has done a VR demo where you talk about this idea of being at a sports game, for example, and feeling like you’re there. One of my contentions is people are overthinking the production capabilities of this. I’m perfectly fine just sitting on the sideline, I don’t even need a play-by-play announcer, I don’t need a scoreboard, I can look up at the scoreboard. So much of sports broadcasting is making up for the fact you are not there and if you’re there, it’s different. And how much better would it be? What’s the limitation? That I can’t talk to my friends during the game. Well, what if I look to the side and my friend’s there and they’re talking during the game and it’s coming in my ear along with the sounds of the game? I do feel like Apple’s focus on the entertainment experience versus Meta’s, I think, more focused on the gaming experience has led to markedly different trade-offs. At the same time, Meta is the social media company and I don’t want them to combine because I think competition is good for the market as a whole, but it does feel like there’s an aspect of both are going to have critical pieces to make this work and it’s not clear if they can get the part that the other one is good at. HB:That’s right. So it’s really interesting. I think to put it in very simple blunt terms, Quest is about gaming, they’re going to have to step up in productivity, and a big part of it has to do with launching a headset that has at least 2.5K displays so that you can see text and work in VR. HB:You just need the resolution, so I think there’s some work to do there, they’ll catch up. They may or may not need tablet apps, there’s some good work to do there for them to catch up. Apple is really well-positioned in productivity and letting people work single-player mode in virtual reality or mixed reality, and they’re making this huge longterm bet in immersive video, particularly for live sports, which I think is very promising, but it will take a lot of time for them to get there. That, I question as something that Meta can get into in the next decade. HB:Yeah and these are going to be carve-out rights. We’re not talking about them doing the same thing they did with MLS in all of the other sports because that’s not viable or possible, but put yourself in the shoes of the NBA, or choose your league, we’re talking about net new revenue above and beyond all of the existing rights that they sell, and subscription services, and so on. We’re talking about net new revenue here to let people feel like they’re there at the game. How much money are you willing to pay to sit courtside without even all the cool production things that they’re going to do to make that experience almost better than being at the game. HB:I think Apple is the only company in the world that can make all of those things happen at the same time. They’ll get a tremendous amount of exclusivity, I think, in exchange. So that I don’t think is something that’s going to be readily available for Meta to take on with the quality and immersiveness that Apple will. There’s still going to be some experience like you havetoday on XTADIUM, but that’s going to be Apple’s play, and it’s great. It’s going to get average people interested in virtual reality, and they’ll get this headset, and then the next thing you’ll see is they’re trying other things, they’ll teleport with their grandma, and off we go. Is immersive video really the path here? I think the question about the Vision Pro, you returned yours, as you said, I still have mine. I did put it on this weekend for the first time in awhile — after I let it charge because the charge had run out — and I used it to watch, actually, my favorite activity, and my primary use case for the Vision Pro is watching F1. The reason is because F1 is usually not on at a time that I can watch it live, so I’m avoiding social media the whole time, and so it’s actually a place where I don’t want to have my phone with me and there’s a bit where being able to sit there and the big screen really is better. I have a relatively small TV in my house here, I can go sit on the porch, and I can have the big screen, and I can just enjoy that experience, but would I enjoy that experience if I had a home theater? I wouldn’t, this is obviously cheaper in that regard, but when it comes to almost anything else, particularly watching sports, I want to have social media there, I want to have the experience, but if I was there in 3D, wow, that’s really compelling. Apple TV Plus makes a lot more sense now as an avenue for immersive video. Is this just going to be a matter of Apple building this out and paying for it, and that is going to get the critical mass, and that will get developers, and that will finally unlock the use cases we’re talking about? HB:I think there’s a really significant chance that it could be. When you think about raw volume shipment, how many millions of units can Apple ship on a given year in a given market, like the US, I think live sports would unlock a lot more demand than any combination of productivity, or casual gaming, or even VR gaming could. So even though it’s a thin wedge use case wise, I think it could be a huge demand driver. So if I were Apple, I would be betting massively on this, and I think they are. It’s technically very complicated because a dual 8K format, very, very difficult to do as a live broadcast. HB:It’s a broadcast pipeline issue, from how I understand it. Just being able to put all of that together in real time and then encode it and stream it is quite a lot. The stream itself is about 50 megabits per second, so it’s not terrible, and a lot of people have more than enough home broadband for that, but the question is, can you do it all live? That feels like a solvable problem for someone like Apple. What about productivity? That, as I noted, has been a disappointment for Vision Pro. I would like the Quest environment, in many respects, but with the Apple Vision Pro screens, but is that wishful thinking in that there’s just a price issue or are there real fundamental trade-offs that are happening that are going to be an issue in the long run? HB:I don’t think there are any fundamental issues. Projecting more than one macOS screen in 5K resolution is relatively non-trivial because of just existing protocols, but it can be done. Worst case scenario, you can mirror and lose some resolution or do something hacky where you almost do foveated rendering of the transmission from your Mac. There’s some complicated latency challenges there. HB:Yeah and the macOS team has to be bought into doing this, which is another internal navigation issue that I’m sure that they have to solve, as well, because I’ve heard the macOS team is not that supportive of Vision Pro, but I think that’s solvable, and I think the single-player version of productivity in VR using Vision Pro is going to be a lot — the macOS single-player version of it is going to be great for a lot of people. I think a lot of people are going to be into the iPad as a computing device encapsulated in the Vision Pro thing where all you’re using are iPad apps and Safari windows. You’re not actually carrying your MacBook Pro with you, I think a lot of people can do all of their work, all of their compute, a lot of their tasks just using an iPad Pro, where you can have a lot of different iPad screens up at the same time, so I think that’s a very possible use case. Are any of these $4,000 worth of equipment? Not for most people, which is why I think this is going to be a slow progression. HB:Yeah, I think an Oculus Pro 2 with equivalent display or close to equivalent display, but cutting down weight tremendously and trying to price it below $2,000, which they are now allowed to do because Apple cracked the price envelope. You’re no longer forced to stay under $1,000. HB:Yeah, and they got so much heat from the world when they launched a product at $1,500, but now, if they launch a $1,500 product, it’s going to look very cheap. So I think launching a product with a micro-OLED display, at least 2.5K, hopefully close to 4K, real focus on ergonomics, bring that weight down, allow people to have a headset strap that does not have a built-in battery so we can have the battery on the side, so if you are okay with having a wire out, you can remove that 70 grams of battery weight from the headset, I think they can get down to 400 grams that way, I think it’s doable. HB:And catch up with Apple and everything else, pass through, add gaze-and-pinch, but take it to the next level, partner with Microsoft. There’s a whole bunch of things that they can do there, and ship teleportation. Allow people to scan their room and teleport people into it using super high-quality, human-like avatars. That technology exists, they just have to get to a point where it’s shippable, and they can. And they need to get over themselves in that regard, I think they overreacted. It’s one of those things, when I talk about that vision, that presence experience that I had, that was with legless avatars that didn’t have expressions. This is what’s hard to articulate. You get the sense of presence without it being perfect, and I think they felt embarrassed that people were making fun of these avatars and Mark Zuckerberg in Paris or whatever, and you just got to ship it because that’s the only way people are going to get it. HB:Check it out. It’s new technology that allows you to use cameras and depth sensors to scan a room in a really, really high degree of fidelity three dimensionally. So you can essentially quickly look around, scan a room with your headset or with your phone, and that becomes a 3D model that lives in the cloud, and you can then share it with others. So even if your avatar isn’t perfect yet, I think this habit of teleporting is going to be a defining characteristic of some virtual reality headset that will launch. I think Meta has the ability to be the first one to ship that headset, they just got to get it out the door. I do think that they’re also the ones that can ship human-like avatars first because they’ve been working on this for a very long time. Is there a possibility all this amounts to just a sideshow? Does it end up never really amounting to anything in the long run? HB:Yeah, it’s a collection of use cases always, and it’ll matter more to some people than others, but to me, it is the kind of technical achievement that leads to a type of experience that, for the few people that try it and talk to their friends, will be so meaningful that it justifies all of the technology, the discomfort, the new competing paradigms and so on. I really do feel, again, I’m biased because I worked in this for very many years, but I do think that, in the fullness of time, this idea of teleporting, like you and I could have a much richer human interaction if we were doing this in the same room, even if we weren’t physically in the same room, but if we felt like we were in the same room, I do think that that speaks to humanity and just, I don’t know, I think it gives us a better life. It has tremendous impact over a lot of different things, including education, including quality of life for the elderly, and so on and so forth and I think it could exist sooner than people think. You were in the news, I think, when COVID came around and forreleasing a COVID test kit, but where is that at right now? HB:Detect is a consumer molecular diagnostics company. I, many years ago, thought that I’d learned a whole bunch about the consumer hardware business and thought that there were some lessons there that we could bring into the healthcare world, which needs as much help as it can get. So COVID was a great motivation and a regulatory window to be able to do that, to build a team of people that had been in the consumer hardware world for some time, locking hands with scientists and biochemists, and essentially build technology that allows people to run diagnostics tests that would otherwise go to a centralized laboratory in the comfort of their own home. That’s what we’ve been building at Detect for the past four years, and it’s fun. It’s going to take a while for that world to materialize because it’s a very heavily regulated business and there’s a lot of new technologies that have to exist, but in the future, in the not so distant future, we’re going to find ourselves doing the thing we did during COVID and testing ourselves at home for a lot more things and a lot more often than I think we can imagine today. Anything that doesn’t require taking your own blood will get done first, and then blood will take some more time because blood is blood, but anything that could be learned from looking at your saliva, from looking at your nose, from looking at your genitals, your urine, and there’s a lot that can be learned there, especially as it relates to infectious diseases, we will be able to tell you at home. So I’ve been dedicating the last couple of years to this crazy new adventure. Is hardware ultimately, though — no matter what it is, and this goes back to the voice bit early on, your work in voice that ended up at the big companies — can there ever be a truly meaningful hardware tech startup or is it all R&D for the largest companies in the world? HB:I think it can. And to me, the healthcare world is a pretty great candidate for this because you have insurance companies paying for things, obviously where it makes sense for them to pay for things, which means that you’re relying a lot less on people taking money out of their pockets, which means cost of customer acquisition can be a very different ballgame. So I think the unit economics of consumer hardware in the healthcare world actually can be much more compelling than in the traditional world where you’re paying a couple hundred dollars of customer acquisition costs, all things considered, to get somebody to pay $500 out of pocket, and then you’re in the red before you know it. And I do agree, it’s hard to be a startup in that world, but in healthcare, I think things are a bit different, or can be. One of my theses has been thatthe enterprise use case is actually the most important, precisely because trial is such a problem, whereas if you can sell a much smaller number, it’s a much more difficult sell job, but if you can sell a company into, with remote work, hybrid work, this sort of thing, to get their employees headsets, or you want to have an open office, but you still want developer productivity, put them in a headset, and then people get the experience, and then they want it at home. This is what happened with the PC, the PCs had to start in the enterprise. Unlike the mobile phone, is that how this might play out or is it going to be a consumer use case first? HB:I think it absolutely can play out that way, and I love the thought behind some of the work that Microsoft is doing with Teams and withMicrosoft Mesh, which is precisely this idea of you’re in a meeting with a bunch of people over video, now you’re going to work on a thing, and it just makes more sense for you to do that in an immersive environment because you can have more surfaces to collaborate, or maybe you’re actually working on a 3D model, or there are 3D authoring tools that you can benefit. So you’re sitting at your desk in a video call, just like you and I are right now, “Oh, let’s jump in.” You take your headset, put it on, and jump in, continuing that interaction that you were having with people in that meeting, but now you’re going into an immersive environment. That jumping in and out of VR on an as-needed basis for hyper-collaboration value is a huge deal, I think it’s the work version of teleportation, in a way. There’s a beauty to that tool in that I think people really could be feeling like they are in the same physical space with others. They don’t have to spend all day in that physical space, but what it will take is seamless integration between the tools that we use for 2D collaboration today, getting on a Zoom call, getting on a Teams call, and actually jumping in to work on a thing together and not feeling like that transition is super jarring. Otherwise, people won’t want to do it. Very good. Well, we should touch base again as this continues to develop. I think it’s one of those things where, if you look out 100 years, yeah, of course VR is a thing, but it’s the question with all tech, how long is it actually going to take? And in some respects, it feels very near, and then sometimes you get the details and it feels so far away. So I guess we’ll have to wait and see how it plays out.
