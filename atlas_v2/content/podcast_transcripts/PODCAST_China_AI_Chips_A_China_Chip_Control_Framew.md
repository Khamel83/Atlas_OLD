First, onFriday:The US commerce department has started issuing licences to Nvidia to export H20 chips to China following weeks of inaction, after chief executive Jensen Huang met President Donald Trump at the White House. A US official told the Financial Times the bureau of industry and security, the arm of the commerce department that oversees export controls, had begun to issue licences for the H20.Nvidia designed the chip for the Chinese market after the Biden administration imposed export controls on more advanced artificial intelligence chips. In April, the Trump administration appeared to take a tougher line by telling Nvidia it could not sell the H20 to China. Trump reversed course after Huang visited the White House and directly lobbied the president. But Nvidia was frustrated that three weeks after the decision, the administration had not started issuing the licences. Huang visited the White House on Wednesday and held another meeting with the president. Two days after the meeting, the commerce department starting issue the licences, according to people familiar with the decision.Second,on Saturday:Chinese officials have told experts in Washington that Beijing wants the Trump administration to relax export restrictions on high-bandwidth memory (HBM) chips, according to several people familiar with the matter. US Treasury secretary Scott Bessent has led three rounds of trade negotiations with China over the past three months. One person said the Chinese team, headed by vice-premier He Lifeng, had raised the HBM issue in some of those negotiations. The US Treasury declined to comment. Ahead of an August 12 deadline for the US and China to reach a trade deal and avoid reimposing high tariffs, US commerce secretary Howard Lutnick this week said the administration would probably extend the ceasefire by 90 days.Beijing has been frustrated at US export controls since President Joe Biden in 2022 unveiled measures to damage Chinese efforts to buy or make advanced AI chips. In 2024, he banned HBM exports to China to hamper Huawei and Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation (SMIC), a Chinese chipmaker.The recent focus on export controls to China has been on the H20 chip Nvidia designed for the Chinese market after Biden banned the export of more advanced chips. The Financial Times reported that the US approved H20 export licences on Friday after Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang met Trump. But people familiar with the matter said China is much more worried about HBM controls because they seriously constrain the ability of Chinese companies, including Huawei, to develop their own AI chips.Third,on Sunday:Nvidia and AMD have agreed to give the US government 15 per cent of the revenues from chip sales in China, as part of an unusual arrangement with the Trump administration to obtain export licences for the semiconductors. The two chipmakers agreed to the financial arrangement as a condition for obtaining export licences for the Chinese market that were granted last week, according to people familiar with the situation, including a US official. The US official said Nvidia agreed to share 15 per cent of the revenues from H20 chip sales in China and AMD would provide the same percentage from MI308 chip revenues. Two people familiar with the arrangement said the Trump administration had not yet determined how to use the money.The Financial Times reported that the commerce department started issuing H20 export licences on Friday, two days afterNvidia chief executive Jensen Huang met President Donald Trump. The US official said the administration had also started issuing licences for AMD’s China chip. The quid pro quo arrangement is unprecedented. According to export control experts, no US company has ever agreed to pay a portion of their revenues to obtain export licences. But the deal fits a pattern in the Trump administration where the president urges companies to take measures, such as domestic investments, for example, to prevent the imposition of tariffs in an effort to bring in jobs and revenue to America.A lot of attention is being paid to the third story, given its, shall we say, unusual nature; it’s almost certainly unconstitutional. During the Constitutional Convention southern states, whose economies were heavily dependent on international exports, were concerned that a federal government, dominated by the north, would seek to fund itself on the back of those exports; that’s why Article I, Section 9, Clause 5 states:No Tax or Duty shall be laid on Articles exported from any State.The Supreme Court has allowed very limited exceptions to this clause: there can be a broad-based tax that happens to cover exports (like, say, a corporate income tax),but not taxes specifically focused on exports or explicitly export-oriented activities, andthere can be user fees(like, say, harbor duties), but those fees cannot bead valorem— i.e. in proportion to the value of the goods in question. An import tax specifically targeted at to-be-exported goodsis also unconstitutional. In short, of all of the Trump tariff decisions, this is the most likely to ultimately be struck down.Then again, someone needs to actually make the case to have this overturned, which, presumably, would be Nvidia or AMD. Given they want an export license, it’s possible they simply decline to sue, and it’s not clear who else might have standing; perhaps Nvidia or AMD shareholders? In other words, if Nvidia and AMD ultimately don’t object to this 15% (because access to the Chinese market is more important to them), it might stand, at least for a while.The entire issue, however, is by-and-large a distraction that seems to be magnifying one particular error people are making with regards to most tariff stories specifically: the revenue that would come from this arrangement goes to the federal government, not Trump personally; the argument that Huang has made to Trump isn’t about personally paying him off — and I thinkhis argumentis important. Indeed, that’s why the 15% is particularly annoying: above and beyond the constitutionality, or lack thereof, it’s a distraction from what I think is a shift towards a better approach for China chip policy.A China Chip Control FrameworkSome of what I am going to articulate is a repeat of February’sAI Promise and Chip Precariousness, but I thought it was worth revisiting the topic for two reasons:First, I think that was one of those Articles that was too early; it’s directly applicable to these Nvidia questions, and I want to re-anchor it in people’s minds now that they are thinking about the issues I touched on.Second, the high-bandwidth memory question is a useful clarifier and delineation point in terms of figuring out where to draw the line in terms of China exports.To that end, let me do my best to reduce a 5,300 word Article —which you should read— to a set of bullet points.First, I do not subscribe to the view of imminent AI take-off, in which one AI starts recursively improving itself, effectively taking over the world. I do believe this was a view that took hold in the Biden administration, which drove a number of what I considered poor decisions, includingseveral aspects of the various chip bansandthe executive order on AI.Second, even if I did believe in AI take-off, said take-off would still be dependent on massive numbers of chips manufactured by TSMC in Taiwan. That means the optimal response by China from a game theory perspective would be to destroy TSMC specifically and Taiwan generally. This, needless to say, would be catastrophic for everyone.Third, I believe that chips are incredibly difficult in every facet, from design to manufacturing to programming. This, by extension, creates a “water runs downhill” effect, which is to say that Chinese companies will, whenever possible, choose the option that is easier, cheaper, and faster, even in the face of government dictates to do the opposite.With these assumptions in mind, there are a number of options for the U.S. government to consider when it comes to China and AI:The first option is to try and cut off everything: no chips, no access to TSMC, no semiconductor manufacturing equipment. This would be difficult, but at least intellectually coherent. It also raises two long-term risks: first, that China decides that if they can’t have AI, then no one can, and attacks Taiwan (and other parts of the semiconductor supply chain); second, that China at some point gains the ability to build advanced chips on their own, not only rendering U.S. attempts at control null and void, but creating the conditions to undercut U.S. technological dominance throughout the world.The second option is to try and cut off the most advanced capabilities: no advanced Nvidia chips, and no access to leading-edge TSMC manufacturing to make alternatives. This, by-and-large, was the Biden approach, and while it had the same goals and risks of the first option above, it added intellectual incoherency: specifically, banning Chinese access to leading edge chips and manufacturing, while still giving them access to semiconductor manufacturing equipment, both provided companies with the motivation to pursue leading-edge manufacturing (to get the best chips) and a massive leg-up in doing so (by both being able to procure large portions of the supply chain, and also getting equipment to copy).The third option is the one I favor: basically do the exact opposite of the Biden administration. Specifically, sell China advanced chips, and, at least for now, allow Chinese companies — including Huawei — to contract with TSMC for manufacturing; at the same time, ban the sale of all semiconductor manufacturing equipment to China.The fourth option was basically U.S. policy until halfway through the first Trump administration: sell China anything and everything, from chips to semiconductor manufacturing equipment.I believe the ideal scenario in the long run (1) maintains U.S. dominance in chips, (2) avoids an attack on TSMC and Taiwan, and (3) delays the rise of an alternative Chinese chip supply chain as long as possible, if not indefinitely. That means three or four; I think three preserves the most leverage for the longest time, while reducing the most risks.Specifically, this is where the “water runs downhill” effect comes in. First, I believe that every Chinese company would, if they could, use Nvidia chips. Therefore, by selling them Nvidia chips, you will relieve the market pressure and potential ecosystem effects that could both pull Chinese-built alternatives into the market and make them widely desirable. Critically, chips are unique — particularly Nvidia chips — in that allowing their export doesn’t threaten their software moat, but rather deepens it; the network effects of software make this debate fundamentally different from other China-export stories.Second, some companies, like Huawei in particular, will still pursue this market; however, Huawei would, if they could, manufacture their chips at TSMC. Allowing them to do so will remove market pressure from the Chinese chip supply chain to build up an alternative; furthermore, allowing Chinese companies like Huawei to manufacture chips with TSMC makes China dependent on TSMC and Taiwan, which dramatically reduces the risk that China will simply bomb them out of existence.Third, stopping the sale of semiconductor equipment to Chinese manufacturers will, combined with the reduction of private company market demand, make it nearly impossible for them to move further down the learning curve than they have come so far, which, in the long run, will increase dependence on U.S. chips and TSMC manufacturing.The Chinese government will, to be sure, continue to push for and fund an alternative semiconductor supply chain that is fully independent from the U.S. This die can’t be uncast, which is why I’m out on the fourth option. I’m skeptical, however, that they will be very successful without the market forces that the U.S. is engendering through our current bans on chips and advanced manufacturing; to put it another way, if China wants market pressure to build more advanced chips, let them impose it themselves by banning Nvidia chips or access to TSMC, instead of us doing it for them.I recognize that this recommendation entails China building advanced AI; moreover, that advanced AI may very well end up competing with U.S. AI. I will also freely admit that this AI will be relevant militarily. The problem facing the U.S. government, however, is that you don’t just get to stomp your foot and dictate reality to a country like China: they have the ability to act as well — including the ability to just make AI available to no one, by bombing Taiwan — and in light of that fact it is incumbent on our leaders to carefully think through the short, medium, and long-term effects of the policy options in front of them and make hard choices.Whither HBMThis is where the high-bandwidth memory ban is very interesting, and a useful pressure point in terms of applying my framework. High-bandwidth memory is a specific type of 3D-stacked memory chip that is particularly critical for making AI chips. HBM is very difficult to make, and very expensive; in fact, the cost for HBM on a Grace Blackwell chip exceeds the amount that Nvidia pays to TSMC for the GPU die.To figure out how the U.S. could proceed, there is another way to distill my argument above. Assuming you have accepted my argument that China should be allowed to develop AI:The U.S. should prefer that China develop AI on U.S.-basedsystems.If China wishes to build their own system, the U.S. should prefer that the components are manufactured with U.S. allies.If China wishes to build their own manufacturing, the U.S. should not aid China in any way.The reason why I think the HBM question is interesting is that before I wrote this Update I assumed that I was going to come down in support of the continued ban on HBM: if China wants access to HBM then they can buy U.S.-based systems (i.e. Nvidia and AMD chips that we would allow to be sold). However, that creates a very large incentive for China to create its own HBM supply chain, and many of the learnings necessary to create HBM would be applicable to logic. That makes the question more nuanced.Basically, if you want to drive Chinese dependency on Nvidia specifically, leaning on the CUDA software moat as a point of control, then you should support the HBM ban; if what you are most concerned about is a manufacturing moat and the semi supply chain, then you should support the sale of HBM chips. I can see both arguments, but ultimately I would fall on the side of being OK with the sale of HBM chips to China, with, obviously, important givebacks on the Chinese side, particularly around things like rare earth metals.It’s the rare earth metals point, by the way, that is almost certainly the most important explanation as to why Trump is being so “soft” on China. The fact of the matter is that while the U.S. has important points of leverage over China, they do over us as well. Far too many people in this debate seem to operate as if the U.S. is the only actor in the world, with every other country, including China, operating as mere props. That’s simply not true, and accepting that is the first step to a cogent policy that preserves what leverage we still have, while minimizing the risks that too many are too unwilling to contemplate.This Update is also available as a podcast. I believe the ideal scenario in the long run (1) maintains U.S. dominance in chips, (2) avoids an attack on TSMC and Taiwan, and (3) delays the rise of an alternative Chinese chip supply chain as long as possible, if not indefinitely. That means three or four; I think three preserves the most leverage for the longest time, while reducing the most risks. Specifically, this is where the “water runs downhill” effect comes in. First, I believe that every Chinese company would, if they could, use Nvidia chips. Therefore, by selling them Nvidia chips, you will relieve the market pressure and potential ecosystem effects that could both pull Chinese-built alternatives into the market and make them widely desirable. Critically, chips are unique — particularly Nvidia chips — in that allowing their export doesn’t threaten their software moat, but rather deepens it; the network effects of software make this debate fundamentally different from other China-export stories. Second, some companies, like Huawei in particular, will still pursue this market; however, Huawei would, if they could, manufacture their chips at TSMC. Allowing them to do so will remove market pressure from the Chinese chip supply chain to build up an alternative; furthermore, allowing Chinese companies like Huawei to manufacture chips with TSMC makes China dependent on TSMC and Taiwan, which dramatically reduces the risk that China will simply bomb them out of existence. Third, stopping the sale of semiconductor equipment to Chinese manufacturers will, combined with the reduction of private company market demand, make it nearly impossible for them to move further down the learning curve than they have come so far, which, in the long run, will increase dependence on U.S. chips and TSMC manufacturing. The Chinese government will, to be sure, continue to push for and fund an alternative semiconductor supply chain that is fully independent from the U.S. This die can’t be uncast, which is why I’m out on the fourth option.
