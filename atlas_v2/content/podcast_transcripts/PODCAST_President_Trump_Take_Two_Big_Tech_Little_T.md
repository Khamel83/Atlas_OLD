US stocks surged, with S&P futures climbing 2.3%; the dollar posted its biggest gain against major currencies since 2020; Treasury bonds tumbled, sending benchmark yields up more than 0.1 percentage point; and Bitcoin soared to a record.All the votes aren’t in yet, but the swings send a clear signal that investors believe a second Trump administration is on its way and will look a lot like the first one: a stream of policies (tax cuts, deregulation, tariffs) that will simultaneously stoke economic growth, corporate profits and inflation.I wasn’t sure if there would be results to cover this morning, but Amazon and Apple earnings will have to wait; I’m grateful, in a way, that the results were definitive, and that it is plausible to start considering the implications of a second Trump administration without any intervening uncertainty. The single most important thing to keep in mind is that this isn’t all theoretical: Trump was already president, which should be the starting point in these discussions.Big Tech, Little Tech, Chips, and HardwareIt’s foolish, at this point, to think of tech as being a singular thing; to that end, I’m lumping the potential impact of a Trump administration into four buckets: Big Tech, Little Tech (including crypto), Chips, and Hardware.Big Tech:While the Big Tech companies obviously benefit the most from positive market sentiment, it’s not clear that they are major beneficiaries from a second Trump administration; they very well may be losers. Look no further than Trump’s first administration: it was the Trump administration that initiated the default search payment lawsuit thatGoogle recently lost, and thelawsuit against Facebook for illegally acquiring Instagram and WhatsApp.Moreover, Trump has long expressed personal animosity against Big Tech, which he sees as being in the bag for the Democratic party, and towards Meta in particular, thanks to the money CEO Mark Zuckerberg donated to voting initiatives in 2020 that Trump believes financed Democratic get-out-the-vote operations. Apple CEO Tim Cook famously got on very well with Trump, and successfully excluded Apple products from Chinese tariffs; a potential blanket tariff, however, which Trump has pledged to implement, would hurt badly.All of this animus could be amplified by the presence of Vice President-elect J.D. Vance, who told Joe Rogan onhis appearance last weekthathe “hates big tech”thanks to the moderation/censorship decisions it makes with its platforms. His critique goes deeper than that, as I explained inan Update when he was selected: Vance believes that Meta and Google in particular are impervious to competition and stifle innovation, and he has spoken positively about the Biden administration’s approach to antitrust, including complimenting FTC Chair Lina Khan by name. To the extent that Vance has a say might be the extent to which Big Tech’s antitrust problems get worse, not better (as they likely would have under a Harris administration).Little Tech:The “Little Tech” moniker was coined by Marc Andreessen and Ben Horowitzearlier this yearas they ramped up their political lobbying efforts, and was the basis fortheir Trump edorsement. Frommy Update at the time:The issues that Andreessen Horowitz are concerned about range from increased regulation on things like blockchain and AI to the aforementioned freeze on acquisitions, as well as taxes on unrealized gains; what is pertinent to this discussion, though, is the distinction being drawn between “Big Tech” and “Litle Tech”. I think it’s an important distinction to draw!Consider AI regulation: “Little Tech” is in favor of no limits on open source models, while “Big Tech” wants regulations that cement their distribution advantages. You can see the same thing in recent discussions about EU regulations: while “Big Tech” didn’t like GDPR, it cemented their advantage; the same thing is going to happen with the DMA. It’s a good rule of thumb to assume that while no one wants more regulation, regulation does in the end only deepen the moat for “Big Tech”.This is where it’s important to remember that antitrust is distinct from other types of regulation: the latter restricts competition, while the goal of the former is to increase it. It follows, then, that “Little Tech” wants more antitrust enforcement. Consider something like the App Store: Apple is fine with rules around security and privacy; what “Little Tech” wants is side-loading and alternate payment methods.All that noted, the acquisition question is the one area where “Big Tech” and “Little Tech” are on the same page: the former wants to be able to buy interesting new companies, and the funders of the latter want to get a return on their investment. I do think that “Little Tech” is currently the most enthused by Vance’s appointment; his remarks below, though, suggest he may actually help keep the liquidation spigots turned off.I wanted to include this excerpt both for the explanation of little tech, and also that bit distinguishing between types of regulation. If you wanted to make things more complicated, you could also add one more type to antitrust (which spurs competition) and safety/red tape (which restricts it): rules of the road regulation, which sets the boundaries of competition. This is particularly pertinent in crypto, where the Trump administration is going to be a massive wind in the sails of the industry.First, current SEC Chair Gary Gensler, crypto public enemy number one, will be removed. Second, what was frustrating for crypto companies about Gensler specifically and the Biden Administration generally was the lack of specificity in terms of rules; I noted whenStripe bought Bridgethat regulation would actually be bullish for the sector:This news may be enough to spur usage in its own right, and, critically, regulation around stablecoins, which Stripe would very much welcome, both because it has a strong interest in the coins being stable, and also because regulatory uncertainty is a major obstacle to adoption.That regulation may be coming soon, and it’s likely to be regulation in which crypto advocates, which were major supporters of both Trump andthe campaign to replace Ohio Senator Sherrod Brown, another major crypto opponent, with a crypto entrepreneur, have significant say. That this will happen on top of the major technical advancements in crypto I noted in that Update, along with the Stripe validation of the space, could been major boom times are ahead.AI, meanwhile, runs the risk of being strangled by the safety/red-tape variety of regulation, particularly the Biden executive order on AI, whichI strongly opposed; Trump pledges to repeal it, which I consider good news.It’s the acquisition question that is the big one, and where, as I noted, Big Tech and Little Tech’s interests are aligned. I think that acquisitions are an essential part of the venture capital ecosystem that has driven so much growth, but this is where you have to go back to the first Trump administration: I wroteFirst, Do No Harm, warning against the impending de facto ban on acquisitions, after attending an antitrust conference where theTrumpDepartment of Justice was heavily featured. It’s not clear this is going to get better.Chips:Whoa boy, now we’re getting complicated! I could write thousands of words about this subject, but at a high level, let me note three things:TSMC just started production of 4nm chips in Arizona; this was a Trump initiative.Trump thinks the CHIPS Act was a terrible deal; instead of giving money to chip companies like Intel, Trump thinks a tariff should have been applied to Taiwanese chips.Trump has accused Taiwan of stealing the U.S. chip industry and then leveraging said chip industry into demanding protection from China.I think these three points are quite coherent, and either correct or directionally correct.It is a good thing for the U.S., particularly from a national security perspective, to have near leading-edge chips made in the U.S. It is problematic from a U.S. perspective that TSMC’s engineers are mostly based in Taiwan, along with the leading edge, but the situation is better now than it was.I also think the CHIPS Act was poorly formulated, andhave said so repeatedly. Moreover, I agree that the correct solution is to stimulate demand, not supply. I think the most efficient way to do that would be to guarantee demand for Intel in particular, because they need demand to get off the ground, but tariffs in theory could have the same effect by giving U.S.-made chips a major price advantage. The problem is that I don’t think this would be enough to help Intel (chips are too high-risk and too low-cost to change suppliers), but it might be enough to force TSMC to put leading edge fabs on American soil.U.S. chip companies, starting with Fairchild Semiconductor opening an assembly plant in Hong Kong, were amongst the first industries to globalize. Yes, the Taiwanese government helped TSMC get in the game, and continues to subsidize them via a currency that is kept artificially low, which gives TSMC a price advantage, but Trump goes too far when he says “stole.” At the same time, I agree that Taiwan under-invests in its defense and operates as if the U.S. will save them for free, thanks in part to its dependence on TSMC.None of these things are very polite to say out loud, but that doesn’t make them untrue, directionally anyways. Meanwhile, Trump was also the one who initiated chip bans against ZTE and Huawei; it was Biden that extended the ban to all high-performant AI chips to China period, along with the equipment necessary to make them.The chip ban as a concept worries me, because it both decreases the cost of invading Taiwan (China already lost leading-edge chip access), and increases the cost of waiting (because the U.S. could develop superior AI military capabilities over time). At the same time, I think that China will be less likely to do something reckless or provocative with Trump as President. The bigger question is if Trump will seek to secure some sort of grand deal that covers advanced chips, Taiwan’s status, trade, etc. I’m not sure what exactly that would look like, but the reality is that Taiwan does have leverage for the exact reasons Trump identified: they make the best chips.Hardware:This is the area where I am most intrigued by the possibilities of a Trump administration. I was very disheartened over the weekend whenMeta’s nuclear plans were waylaid by a rare bee species, andAmazon’s nuclear plans were thwarted by regulators. Just two more things that won’t be built, along with everything from houses to factories and everything in-between, thanks to decades of regulation that, even if passed with the best of intentions, has massive interleaving societal costs. To that end, the number one thing I would like to see from a Trump Administration is a continuation of the rollback in regulation that was one of the big successes of his first tenure.Still, I think Trump can go further, particularly if Republicans control Congress: I would like to see the President given the power to unilaterally remove, or at least streamline, regulations around major projects or economic areas that will make it possible to actually build new things, particularly factories. I think there are a ton of opportunities in this space, particularly if Trump is as aggressive with tariffs as he promises to be, but there needs to be a way to cut through all of the red tape that has accumulated. I also think this is a much more viable path to economic growth and trimming the deficit via increased tax proceeds than trying to cut down government expenses, which will be exceptionally difficult, subject to endless lawsuits, and time-consuming.Elon Musk’s TriumphThere was aStraussian readingofElon Dreams and Bitter Lessonsthat I was more explicit about onSharp Tech; I said in that Article:The Tesla bet, though, is that Waymo’s approach ultimately doesn’t scale and isn’t generalizable to true Level 5, while starting with the dream — true autonomy — leads Tesla down a better path of relying on nothing but AI, fueled by data and fine-tuning that you can only do if you already have millions of cars on the road. That is the connection to SpaceX and what happened this weekend: if you start with the dream, then understand the cost structure necessary to achieve that dream, you force yourself down the only path possible, forgoing easier solutions that don’t scale for fantastical ones that do.This is one way to think about Musk’s decision to fully immerse himself in this election, including what appeared to be manipulation of the “For You” algorithm, funding a massive GOTV operation, and campaigning on the ground in Pennsylvania on a near daily basis for the last few weeks. Musk identified the U.S. regulatory state as a fundamental limiter on SpaceX in particular, and a threat to himself and his businesses generally, and instead of playing both sides, went all in on getting one particular candidate elected, knowing that an all-in bet would have the highest possible pay-off. This tweet summarized it well:Let it be noticed that once he decided that this race was existential, Elon basically just set up camp in PA and sat there until it flipped.This is how he does everything.Find the one lever that moves the world and then just go jump up and down on it, relentlessly— Isaiah Taylor – making nuclear reactors (@isaiah_p_taylor)November 6, 2024To say this was high-risk is a massive understatement; ever since Musk bought Twitter he has been hit by an onslaught of judicial and administrative actions, including having his shareholder-approved pay packageretroactively undone, and being sued forSpaceX not employing refugeeseven though, as a national security entity, it can’t. It seems likely all of that would have gotten much worse had Trump lost.At the same time, one of the best ways to succeed is to go all in; this is one of the advantages startups have versus incumbents: they have nothing to lose. That Musk operated this way given he has a lot to lose is remarkable; it’s also why this tweet is true:Historically, when Elon goes all-in, he tends to win.— Galois Capital (@Galois_Capital)November 5, 2024Musk clearly knew the stakes; his celebratory tweet was short and sweet:Game, set and match— Elon Musk (@elonmusk)November 6, 2024What is fascinating is how this fundamentally transforms any attempt to evaluate the Twitter acquisition. From a business perspective it’s a massive failure, and might always be: Musk paid too much for Twitter as it was, and in the intervening years the flight of advertisers from the platform has made it worth even less. From a Musk Inc. perspective, however, X played a pivotal role in ensuring that the incoming administration will do whatever Musk needs at the exact moment that SpaceX is gaining the capabilities to actually make a trip to Mars, if only the FAA in particular will give him the freedom to do so. That alone is almost certainly worth $44 billion to Musk!This, more broadly, gets at why Musk is so hard to discuss: a focus on his accomplishments, particularly Tesla and SpaceX, are so significant that it seems reasonable to excuse the means; at the same time I am sympathetic to those so offended by the means that they oppose him in principle. What cannot be denied, however, is that he is consequential.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. It follows, then, that “Little Tech” wants more antitrust enforcement.
