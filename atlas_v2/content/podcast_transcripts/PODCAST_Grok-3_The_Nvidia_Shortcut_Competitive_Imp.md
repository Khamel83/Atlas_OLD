DeepSearch is a reasoning chatbot that expresses its process of understanding a query and how it plans its response. It includes options for research, brainstorming and data analysis, the demonstration showed. Musk’s team also said it intends to release a voice-based chatbot “as soon as possible.”Grok 3appears to be one of if not the best-performing base model in the world; it istopping the usual benchmarks(butnot if you includeo3), andtops all categories with the highest ever score in LMArena, anincreasingly difficult featas models have converged in theGPT-4class. Andrej Karpathy is not an objective observer — he is the former Senior Director of AI at Tesla — buthis review on Xis worth a read; some highlights:The impression overall I got here is that this is somewhere around o1-pro capability, and ahead ofDeepSeek-R1, though of course we need actual, real evaluations to look at…The impression I get of DeepSearch is that it’s approximately around Perplexity DeepResearch offering (which is great!), but not yet at the level of OpenAI’s recently released “Deep Research”, which still feels more thorough and reliable…As far as a quick vibe check over ~2 hours this morning,Grok 3+ Thinking feels somewhere around the state of the art territory of OpenAI’s strongest models (o1-pro, $200/month), and slightly better than DeepSeek-R1and Gemini2.0Flash Thinking. Which is quite incredible considering that the team started from scratch ~1 year ago, this timescale to state of the art territory is unprecedented.This gets at the firstGrok 3takeaway: it is simultaneously surprising and not surprising. Start with the latter: xAI famously built a 100,000 GPU data center in Memphis (andnow has 200,000GPUs), and used20,000 GPUsa good number of them to trainGrok 3; that’s the largest cluster ever and, sure enough, it resulted in a top tier model. That’s not surprising given it is what scaling laws predicted, but it is comforting, as there was a question as to whether scaling laws had hit a wall.What is surprising is that xAI is only 19 months old: the company has gone from incorporation to largest GPU cluster in the world to arguably the best model in less time than it has taken OpenAI to go fromGPT-4 to GPT-5(albeit with substantial updates along the way, including a big4oupdate just this past weekend). That is certainly a testament to both Elon Musk and his team —just ask Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang— and to the fact that not only are models commoditizing, but so is the knowledge of how and the wherewithal to build them.The Nvidia ShortcutxAI’s success is also a big win for Nvidia, and this story is in many respects the flipside of the DeepSeek one. I wrote inDeepSeek FAQ:There are real challenges this news presents to the Nvidia story. Nvidia has two big moats:CUDA is the language of choice for anyone programming these models, and CUDA only works on Nvidia chips.Nvidia has a massive lead in terms of its ability to combine multiple chips together into one large virtual GPU.These two moats work together. I noted above that if DeepSeek had access to H100s they probably would have used a larger cluster to train their model, simply because that would have been the easier option; the fact they didn’t, and were bandwidth constrained, drove a lot of their decisions in terms of both model architecture and their training infrastructure. Just look at the U.S. labs: they haven’t spent much time on optimization because Nvidia has been aggressively shipping ever more capable systems that accommodate their needs. The route of least resistance has simply been to pay Nvidia. DeepSeek, however, just demonstrated that another route is available: heavy optimization can produce remarkable results on weaker hardware and with lower memory bandwidth; simply paying Nvidia more isn’t the only way to make better models.It remains, however, a very good way to make better models! The inverse of DeepSeek slaving away over optimizations for months or years is xAI simply buying the best possible chips complete with the best possible networking that makes a20,000 GPUmassive cluster usable in mere weeks or months, and training a better model.Moreover, there is that bit I noted above about commoditization, not just in terms of models but also the knowledge of how to build these models; this isn’t completely accurate — xAI has an excellent and experienced team — but I still think it’s fair to say thatGrok 3reaffirms that the limiting factor in having a leading edge model is having access to Nvidia chips. In this respect DeepSeek is the exception that proves the rule: you can have a cracked team that works around things like the chip ban, but the best path to success continues to run through Nvidia.Competitive ImplicationsI did get access toGrok 3(by being an X Premium Plus subscriber), but not the thinking and search modes; I also have to say that I find myself usingGrok 2quite a bit, almost entirely via its integration into the X app. It turns out being plugged into a pre-existing distribution channel is very useful!I also never use it anywhere else; for me,product matters, and ChatGPT remains my product of choice. I am also curious to what extent ChatGPT Deep Research’s advantage over other competitors like Google, Perplexity, and now xAI are due simply too3still being the best model versus OpenAI spending more time crafting a better tool. Regardless, the impetus for OpenAI to focus onbeing a consumer tech companyis as strong as ever; OpenAI is the only AI lab to have organically built its own distribution channel (and they really, really need to get an advertising product out the door, to ensure they are serving free customers the best possible models).This also explains whyOpenAI is tying up with Oracle and Softbankand trying to become a for-profit; xAI raised$6 billion last Novemberand actually owns its own GPUs. Musk also said on the livestream that xAI is building outa 1 million GPU cluster, although that’s going to take quite a bit more moneythan even the $10 billion xAI is reportedly raising.xAI’s success also helps explain this story fromBloomberg:OpenAI co-founder Ilya Sutskever is raising more than $1 billion for his startup at a valuation of over $30 billion, according to a person familiar with the matter — vaulting the nascent venture into the ranks of the world’s most valuable private technology companies. Greenoaks Capital Partners, a San Francisco-based venture capital firm, is leading the deal for the startup, Safe Superintelligence, and plans to invest $500 million, said the person, who asked not to be identified discussing private information. Greenoaks is also an investor in AI companies Scale AI and Databricks Inc. The round marks a significant valuation jump from the $5 billion that Sutskever’s company was worth before, according to Reuters, which earlier reported some details of the new funding. The financing talks are ongoing and the details could still change.First off, a disclaimer: Daniel Gross,a frequent Stratechery Interview guest, is the CEO of SSI; that noted, I don’t know anything about SSI beyond what is public, which is very little! To that end, how can a company that is less than a year old, which has pledged to not release a product until it has achieved safe superintelligence, raise so much money at such a steep valuation increase? Well, xAI explains why: it is absolutely viable to have started late and — provided you have the funds — catch up quickly, and of course SSI’s talent speaks for itself.SSI is also, I would imagine, a big Nvidia customer — again, I don’t know that to be true, but what else might the money be going towards? — which brings this conversation back to chips. On one hand, chips remain the gating factor to the “easiest” route to a frontier model; on the other hand, this is why DeepSeek matters: they’re not the only route to something that is at least competitive. The debate on the value of an attempted export ban remains an open one!What does seem over is the idea of there being one model to rule them all, but for one fact: why else are all of these companies spending so wildly on the prize? The reason is the belief that there is a prize — AI that recursively improves itself — in which second place is the proverbial set of steak knives. And, given thatGrok 3reaffirms compute scaling, the willingness to spend in the face of yet another legitimate contender has probably only increased.This Update will be available as a podcast later today.
