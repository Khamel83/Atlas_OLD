The chip titan has been on a roller-coaster over the past few months after the Trump administration moved to limit sales of chips to the Chinese market and then cleared the way for multibillion-dollar deals for processors in the Middle East.Nvidia, which has emerged as one of the world’s most-valuable companies because its chips provide the computing power needed in a global AI arms race, posted another quarter of record-breaking sales on Wednesday. Revenue reached $44.06 billion for its fiscal first quarter, a 69% increase that was curtailed by Washington’s new limits on China chip sales. The company was unable to ship $2.5 billion of its H20 processors and projected $8 billion in lost revenue for the current quarter due to the policy…Still, the results showed the strength of demand for Nvidia’s products in the rest of the world. And while the company’s forecast for the quarter ending in July was below Wall Street’s projections, the stock rose following the results, as many analyst estimates didn’t fully reflect the previously announced loss of business from the China limits. Nvidia’s market capitalization of $3.3 trillion is now second only to Microsoft — and ahead of Apple.Last year I expressed concernthat inference workloads were not increasing as a proportion of Nvidia sales:It’s worth noting, though, that while the company reported that 40% of its chips were used for inference, that isthe same percentage as last quarter. Granted, that is a large increase in absolute terms (by-and-large matching Nvidia’s revenue increase), and of course training large models requires ever more GPUs, but the arrival of meaningful AI workloads will be marked by an increasing percentage of chips being used for inference. Assuming, of course, that inference workloads will be run on Nvidia chips.Well,this callCEO Jensen Huang and CFO Colette Kress were adamant that inference workloads were sky-rocketing; a sampling:Kress:AI workloads have transitioned strongly to inference, and AI factory buildouts are driving significant revenue.Kress:We are witnessing a sharp jump in inference demand. OpenAI, Microsoft and Google are seeing a step function leap in token generation. Microsoft processed over 100 trillion tokens in Q1, a five-fold increase on a year-over-year basis.Huang:Reasoning is compute-intensive, requires hundreds to thousands of times more tokens per task than previous one-shot inference. Reasoning models are driving a step-function surge in inference demand. AI scaling laws remain firmly intact, not only for training, but now inference too requires massive scale compute.Huang:The first positive surprise is the step function demand increase of reasoning AI. I think it is fairly clear now that AI is going through an exponential growth, and reasoning AI really busted through concerns about hallucination or its ability to really solve problems, and I think a lot of people are crossing that barrier and realizing how incredibly effective agentic AI is and reasoning AI is. So, number one is inference reasoning and the exponential growth there, demand growth.Huang:Training demand continues to rise with breakthroughs in post training and like reinforcement learning and synthetic data generation, but inference is exploding. Reasoning AI agents require orders of magnitude more compute.This is all great news for Nvidia specifically and AI generally, but what was missing was an update on that “percentage of chips used” number: it certainly sounds like it is more than 40%, but it would have been nice to receive concrete information that that number has in fact increased.Nvidia’s China ArgumentBeyond that — or perhaps in line with that — the upside surprise for investors is that Nvidia is growing just fine without selling to China. Again, though, I’m not sure why this is a surprise: Nvidia’s sales have been gated not by demand but by supply for a long time now, and it seems likely that will continue. Huang said:Well, we have more orders today than we did at the last time I spoke about orders at GTC. However, we’re also increasing our supply chain and building out our supply chain. They’re doing a fantastic job. We’re building it here onshore in the United States, but we’re going to keep our supply chain quite busy for many more years coming.So why, then, was Huang so impassioned both in this call andin my interview with him two weeks agoabout being shut out from China? I think an interesting place to start is with this tweet from Christopher Balding, who is a very strident China critic:This has been the story of every CEO that has gone to China. How do these idiots not get this story yet?https://t.co/M2OH0wjZJQ— Blume Industries CEO Balding 大老板 (@BaldingsWorld)May 21, 2025What I believe Balding is referring to is the pattern in which a multinational moves manufacturing to China at the cost of a joint venture or technology transfer, only to see their marketshare competed away by Chinese competitors benefitting from their technology. This is, of course, the exactoppositeof what is happening to Nvidia: the company’s marketshare in China is falling because of the U.S. government’s export controls, not because of Chinese government policy.Moreover, unlike the vast majority of other product categories, one would in fact expect Nvidia to maintain its marketshare if it were able to compete. The difference, of course, is software: Nvidia chips are not just about GPUs, but about the CUDA software layer, which has network effects. Nvidia not only has the best performing chips on the market — matched, at times, in pure performance by AMD — they also have by far the largest ecosystem of software and developers, which makes them the first choice for everyone, if they can buy them!Huang’s argument is that not only does Nvidia want to sell to China (obviously), but that the U.S. should want him to sell to China too; from the call:China’s AI moves on with or without U.S. chips. It has to compute to train and deploy advanced models. The question is not whether China will have AI, it already does. The question is whether one of the world’s largest AI markets will run on American platforms. Shielding Chinese chipmakers from U.S. competition only strengthens them abroad and weakens America’s position. Export restrictions have spurred China’s innovation and scale. The AI race is not just about chips. It’s about which stack the world runs on. As that stack grows to include 6G and quantum, U.S. global infrastructure leadership is at stake.It seems clearly in America’s interest for China to have a dependency on a U.S. software platform; then again, it also seem to be in America’s interest to haveChina dependent on Taiwan for chips, but that argument is not winning the day either.The U.S. has based its policy on the assumption that China cannot make AI chips. That assumption was always questionable and now it’s clearly wrong. China has enormous manufacturing capability. In the end, the platform that wins the AI developers win AI. Export controls should strengthen U.S. platforms, not drive half of the world’s AI talent to rivals.This is the root of Nvidia’s concern, and why they devoted scarce capacity to making chips like the H20 for the Chinese market: China is the market where CUDA’s network effect is the most likely to be broken, just as the network effects of countless other U.S. tech companies have been broken. The difference with the latter is that it was China that broke them via the Great Firewall; the U.S. is the one doing the breaking in the case of Nvidia.On DeepSeek: DeepSeek and Qwen from China are among the best open-source AI models. Released freely, they’ve gained traction across the U.S., Europe and beyond. DeepSeek-R1, like ChatGPT, introduced reasoning AI that produces better answers, the longer it thinks. Reasoning AI enables step-by-step problem solving, planning and tool use, turning models into intelligent agents. Reasoning is compute-intensive, requires hundreds to thousands of times more tokens per task than previous one-shot inference. Reasoning models are driving a step-function surge in inference demand. AI scaling laws remain firmly intact, not only for training, but now inference too requires massive scale compute. DeepSeek also underscores the strategic value of open-source AI. When popular models are trained and optimized on U.S. platforms, it drives usage, feedback and continuous improvement, reinforcing American leadership across the stack. U.S. platforms must remain the preferred platform for open-source AI. That means supporting collaboration with top developers globally, including in China. America wins when models like DeepSeek and Qwen runs best on American infrastructure.This is another self-serving argument that, to be clear, doesn’t make it any less valid. From Huang and Nvidia’s perspective, seeing open source models standardize on a non-Nvidia architecture is very bad, particularly given the fact that open source is likely to be the largest market in the long run; from the U.S.’s perspective Nvidia is the only plausible hedge in terms of retaining some sort of control over open source AI. That may not be possible, to be clear, but the other danger is that to the extent that Chinese open source AI models become the standard is very much the addressable market for Chinese chip manufacturers, significantly weakening one of the U.S.’s most enduring industrial strengths.It is worth noting that there are a lot of U.S. companies and Nvidia customers that would be happy to see the CUDA moat broken down; to that end, if the only goal is lower prices, then spurring Chinese competition is an excellent way to do just that. Indeed, as Balding noted, that’s exactly what we have seen in industry after industry. The blind pursuit of lower prices, however, has its own strategic downsides; that politicians decry those downsides even as they create the conditions for those downsides in our crown jewel companies and industries seems very short-sighted.NVLink FusionFromBloomberg:Nvidia Corp. Chief Executive Officer Jensen Huang outlined plans to let customers deploy rival chips in data centers built around its technology, a move that acknowledges the growth of in-house semiconductor development by major clients such as Microsoft Corp. and Amazon.com Inc…To date, Nvidia has only offered complete computer systems built with its own components. This change gives data center customers more flexibility and allows a measure of competition, while still keeping Nvidia technology at the center. NVLink Fusion products will provide customers the option to use their own central processing units with Nvidia’s AI chips, or pair Nvidia silicon with another company’s AI accelerator.This bit of news from Computex two weeks ago is a reminder that CUDA isn’t Nvidia’s only moat: the company is also dominant in networking, particularly the ability to link multiple chips together so that they function as a single large chip; the key to this so-called “scale-up” capability is NVLink.There are two parts of this announcement. The first part is that Nvidia will license its chip-to-chip (C2C) technology to enable custom configurations; for example, Grace Hopper was an Nvidia ARM CPU and an Nvidia Hopper GPU, while Grace Blackwell is an Nvidia ARM CPU and two Nvidia Blackwell GPUs. If you don’t want that configuration, then you’re out luck — or at least you were, because now you can license Nvidia’s C2C technology to make any number of combinations (and it doesn’t have to be Nvidia chips that are being combined, although Nvidia is obviously confident that you’ll choose their GPUs).The second part of this announcement is that Nvidia will sell an NVLink Fusion chiplet. A chiplet is a discrete chip that is placed on the same interposer as other chips, like a CPU and a GPU; in this case it is a chiplet for networking, that allows you to scale up a system with multiple discrete computers in a way that only Nvidia has mastered.This announcement is a little bit riskier than the first, which has a clear connection to selling more Nvidia GPUs by enabling more useful configurations; in this case Nvidia is selling one of its biggest differentiators to potential GPU competitors who cannot scale up effectively (and, crucially, note that Nvidia is selling hardware in this case; you don’t actually get the secret sauce, licensed or otherwise). I suspect Nvidia has several motivations here:First, theUALink consortium was incorporated last yearwith the express goal of building an NVLink alternative; Nvidia is making a bid to peel off supporters of that alternative by giving them the good stuff right now while UALink is stuck in committee meetings.Second, Nvidia is almost certainly charging a very healthy price for these chiplets; if there are going to be competitors, Nvidia might as well take some of their profits.Third, these might end up being the only products that Nvidia can sell into China — and ifChina is going to build AI using slower and less efficient chips, then they certainly need the best networking they can get.All the same, I do find the NVLink Fusion chiplet part of this announcement surprising, given what a differentiator NVLink is for Nvidia’s chips. Huang, however, wants to preserve and extend his control and reach in the ecosystem broadly, instead of prioritizing winning in the short term, which is to say he appears to be practicing what he preaches.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. Huang:Reasoning is compute-intensive, requires hundreds to thousands of times more tokens per task than previous one-shot inference. Reasoning models are driving a step-function surge in inference demand. AI scaling laws remain firmly intact, not only for training, but now inference too requires massive scale compute. Huang:The first positive surprise is the step function demand increase of reasoning AI. I think it is fairly clear now that AI is going through an exponential growth, and reasoning AI really busted through concerns about hallucination or its ability to really solve problems, and I think a lot of people are crossing that barrier and realizing how incredibly effective agentic AI is and reasoning AI is. So, number one is inference reasoning and the exponential growth there, demand growth. Huang:Training demand continues to rise with breakthroughs in post training and like reinforcement learning and synthetic data generation, but inference is exploding. Reasoning AI agents require orders of magnitude more compute. This is all great news for Nvidia specifically and AI generally, but what was missing was an update on that “percentage of chips used” number: it certainly sounds like it is more than 40%, but it would have been nice to receive concrete information that that number has in fact increased. Beyond that — or perhaps in line with that — the upside surprise for investors is that Nvidia is growing just fine without selling to China. Again, though, I’m not sure why this is a surprise: Nvidia’s sales have been gated not by demand but by supply for a long time now, and it seems likely that will continue. Huang said: Well, we have more orders today than we did at the last time I spoke about orders at GTC. However, we’re also increasing our supply chain and building out our supply chain. They’re doing a fantastic job. We’re building it here onshore in the United States, but we’re going to keep our supply chain quite busy for many more years coming. So why, then, was Huang so impassioned both in this call andin my interview with him two weeks agoabout being shut out from China? I think an interesting place to start is with this tweet from Christopher Balding, who is a very strident China critic: What I believe Balding is referring to is the pattern in which a multinational moves manufacturing to China at the cost of a joint venture or technology transfer, only to see their marketshare competed away by Chinese competitors benefitting from their technology. This is, of course, the exactoppositeof what is happening to Nvidia: the company’s marketshare in China is falling because of the U.S. government’s export controls, not because of Chinese government policy. Moreover, unlike the vast majority of other product categories, one would in fact expect Nvidia to maintain its marketshare if it were able to compete. The difference, of course, is software: Nvidia chips are not just about GPUs, but about the CUDA software layer, which has network effects. Nvidia not only has the best performing chips on the market — matched, at times, in pure performance by AMD — they also have by far the largest ecosystem of software and developers, which makes them the first choice for everyone, if they can buy them! China’s AI moves on with or without U.S. chips. It has to compute to train and deploy advanced models. The question is not whether China will have AI, it already does. The question is whether one of the world’s largest AI markets will run on American platforms. Shielding Chinese chipmakers from U.S. competition only strengthens them abroad and weakens America’s position. Export restrictions have spurred China’s innovation and scale. The AI race is not just about chips. It’s about which stack the world runs on. As that stack grows to include 6G and quantum, U.S. global infrastructure leadership is at stake. It seems clearly in America’s interest for China to have a dependency on a U.S. software platform; then again, it also seem to be in America’s interest to haveChina dependent on Taiwan for chips, but that argument is not winning the day either. The U.S. has based its policy on the assumption that China cannot make AI chips. That assumption was always questionable and now it’s clearly wrong. China has enormous manufacturing capability. In the end, the platform that wins the AI developers win AI. Export controls should strengthen U.S. platforms, not drive half of the world’s AI talent to rivals. This is the root of Nvidia’s concern, and why they devoted scarce capacity to making chips like the H20 for the Chinese market: China is the market where CUDA’s network effect is the most likely to be broken, just as the network effects of countless other U.S. tech companies have been broken. The difference with the latter is that it was China that broke them via the Great Firewall; the U.S. is the one doing the breaking in the case of Nvidia. On DeepSeek: DeepSeek and Qwen from China are among the best open-source AI models. Released freely, they’ve gained traction across the U.S., Europe and beyond. DeepSeek-R1, like ChatGPT, introduced reasoning AI that produces better answers, the longer it thinks. Reasoning AI enables step-by-step problem solving, planning and tool use, turning models into intelligent agents. Reasoning is compute-intensive, requires hundreds to thousands of times more tokens per task than previous one-shot inference. Reasoning models are driving a step-function surge in inference demand. AI scaling laws remain firmly intact, not only for training, but now inference too requires massive scale compute. DeepSeek also underscores the strategic value of open-source AI. When popular models are trained and optimized on U.S. platforms, it drives usage, feedback and continuous improvement, reinforcing American leadership across the stack. U.S. platforms must remain the preferred platform for open-source AI. That means supporting collaboration with top developers globally, including in China. America wins when models like DeepSeek and Qwen runs best on American infrastructure. This is another self-serving argument that, to be clear, doesn’t make it any less valid. From Huang and Nvidia’s perspective, seeing open source models standardize on a non-Nvidia architecture is very bad, particularly given the fact that open source is likely to be the largest market in the long run; from the U.S.’s perspective Nvidia is the only plausible hedge in terms of retaining some sort of control over open source AI. That may not be possible, to be clear, but the other danger is that to the extent that Chinese open source AI models become the standard is very much the addressable market for Chinese chip manufacturers, significantly weakening one of the U.S.’s most enduring industrial strengths. It is worth noting that there are a lot of U.S. companies and Nvidia customers that would be happy to see the CUDA moat broken down; to that end, if the only goal is lower prices, then spurring Chinese competition is an excellent way to do just that. Indeed, as Balding noted, that’s exactly what we have seen in industry after industry. The blind pursuit of lower prices, however, has its own strategic downsides; that politicians decry those downsides even as they create the conditions for those downsides in our crown jewel companies and industries seems very short-sighted. Nvidia Corp. Chief Executive Officer Jensen Huang outlined plans to let customers deploy rival chips in data centers built around its technology, a move that acknowledges the growth of in-house semiconductor development by major clients such as Microsoft Corp. and Amazon.com Inc… To date, Nvidia has only offered complete computer systems built with its own components. This change gives data center customers more flexibility and allows a measure of competition, while still keeping Nvidia technology at the center. NVLink Fusion products will provide customers the option to use their own central processing units with Nvidia’s AI chips, or pair Nvidia silicon with another company’s AI accelerator. This bit of news from Computex two weeks ago is a reminder that CUDA isn’t Nvidia’s only moat: the company is also dominant in networking, particularly the ability to link multiple chips together so that they function as a single large chip; the key to this so-called “scale-up” capability is NVLink. There are two parts of this announcement. The first part is that Nvidia will license its chip-to-chip (C2C) technology to enable custom configurations; for example, Grace Hopper was an Nvidia ARM CPU and an Nvidia Hopper GPU, while Grace Blackwell is an Nvidia ARM CPU and two Nvidia Blackwell GPUs. If you don’t want that configuration, then you’re out luck — or at least you were, because now you can license Nvidia’s C2C technology to make any number of combinations (and it doesn’t have to be Nvidia chips that are being combined, although Nvidia is obviously confident that you’ll choose their GPUs). The second part of this announcement is that Nvidia will sell an NVLink Fusion chiplet. A chiplet is a discrete chip that is placed on the same interposer as other chips, like a CPU and a GPU; in this case it is a chiplet for networking, that allows you to scale up a system with multiple discrete computers in a way that only Nvidia has mastered. This announcement is a little bit riskier than the first, which has a clear connection to selling more Nvidia GPUs by enabling more useful configurations; in this case Nvidia is selling one of its biggest differentiators to potential GPU competitors who cannot scale up effectively (and, crucially, note that Nvidia is selling hardware in this case; you don’t actually get the secret sauce, licensed or otherwise). I suspect Nvidia has several motivations here: All the same, I do find the NVLink Fusion chiplet part of this announcement surprising, given what a differentiator NVLink is for Nvidia’s chips. Huang, however, wants to preserve and extend his control and reach in the ecosystem broadly, instead of prioritizing winning in the short term, which is to say he appears to be practicing what he preaches.
