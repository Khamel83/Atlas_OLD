You can add the show using the link at the bottom of this email.On to the Update:OpenAI’s Spring EventFromCNET:OpenAI, the startup that kicked off the generative AI era with a massively popular chatbot, is set to reveal what it calls “Spring Updates” to its ChatGPT and GPT-4 models.That’ll take place in a livestream on Monday, the day before Google hosts its annual I/O conference, at which artificial intelligence will likely play a commanding role. Google has its own gen AI offerings, including the Gemini chatbot and what it calls Search Generative Experience.I wanted to start with this excerpt from over the weekend, to echo the point made in the second paragraph: there definitely seems to be a pattern to OpenAI’s announcements. Specifically, the AI startup seems pretty determined to overshadow Google whenever possible.On March 14, 2023, OpenAI released GPT-4 on the same day that Google launched generative AI features for Google Workspace.On February 15, 2024, OpenAI announced Sora on the same day that Google launched Gemini 1.5 and its 1 million-token context window; Sora has yet to ship.Yesterday, with only a few days notice, OpenAI announced its “Spring Updates” that included the GPT-4o model, the day before Google I/O.The first example might be a bit of a stretch, but the other two definitely feel like an intentional strategy, and frankly, I get it: OpenAI recognizes that Google is its biggest threat, particularly in the consumer arena where the startup has the biggest brand in the space, and it wants to do everything it can to ensure the tech giant doesn’t get any buzz.The strategy worked to perfection with Sora: Google didn’t get nearly the credit it deserved for Gemini 1.5, which was subsequently swamped by Gemini’s politicized image generation; this week might be a bit of a tougher slog, as Google appears poised to launch an upgraded chatbot of their own that will likely be very impressive. Still, the comparison point will be to GPT-4o, and that’s better for OpenAI than the alternative.GPT-4oSo about the announcement; fromBloomberg:OpenAI is launching a faster and cheaper version of the artificial intelligence model that underpins its chatbot, ChatGPT, as the startup works to hold on to its lead in an increasingly crowded market. During a livestreamed event on Monday, OpenAI debuted GPT-4o. It’s an updated version of its GPT-4 model, which is now more than a year old. The new large language model, trained on vast amounts of data from the internet, will be better at handling text, audio and images in real-time. The updates will be available in the coming weeks.Asked a question verbally, the system can reply with an audio response in milliseconds, the company said, allowing for a more fluid conversation. In a demonstration of the model, OpenAI researchers and Chief Technology Officer Mira Murati held a conversation with the new ChatGPT using just their voices, showing that the tool could talk back. During the presentation, the chatbot also appeared to translate speech from one language to another almost instantaneously, and at one point sang part of a story upon request.There was a bit of grumbling on the Internet about the fact that OpenAI did not announce GPT-5, i.e. a completely new model from the ground up; GPT-4o does appear to be a better version of GPT-4, but my impression is that it is indeed based on the same foundation as GPT-4. Frankly, though, I would have loved this announcement even if GPT-4o weren’t the overall improvement in model quality it seems to be.Go back to last fall and the launch of ChatGPT’s voice mode; I wrote inan Update:The key to ambient computing, though — and this was the point I was trying to tease out with the opening about TMT and virtualizing space, time, and interactivity — is that there needs to be something to interact with continuously. No human can fill this role: yes, you can communicate with anyone anywhere, but not on-demand 24/7. Media can’t fill this role either: you can listen to or watch something at any time or any place, but it’s not interactive. Software is always available, but there is friction in terms of figuring out what app to use, actually launching it, interacting with it, etc. This is where generative AI is so compelling: it is the essential technological underpinning of continuous ambient computing.That then leads to my second point: there is a necessity for new hardware to enable this sort of frictionless ambient computing. You can get an idea of what that ambient computing is like with chatbots — this is why the ChatGPT voice application was so compelling to me — but the app paradigm is not the right one. There is a hardware breakthrough waiting to happen just like the Internet created the conditions for the smartphone breakthrough to happen. In that context, Meta’s hardware investments are suddenly drastically more compelling than they might have seemed previously, and it makes sense that OpenAI is exploring the space.Set aside — for a moment — that this excerpt is about hardware; the broader point I was driving at is the important ofspeed. Speed has always been the killer feature for basically every product type in every category, particularly in the consumer space; my contention is that the importance of speed increases in direct correlation with the intuitiveness of the experience. It’s one thing to feel the delay in interacting with an app subsequent to pulling a phone out of your pocket and unlocking it; it’s another to feel the delay while attempting to hold a conversation with a chatbot.This is why the old voice interaction mode for GPT-4 was both enticing and frustrating. There was clearly something there, as I noted inAI, Hardware, and Virtual Reality:You have obviously been able to chat with ChatGPT via text for many months now; what I only truly appreciated after talking with ChatGPT, though, was just how much work it was to type out questions and read answers. There was, in other words, a human constraint in our conversations that made it feel like I was using a tool; small wonder that the vast majority of my interaction with ChatGPT has been to do some sort of research, or try to remember something on the edge of my memory, too fuzzy to type a clear search term into Google.Simply talking, though, removed that barrier: I quickly found myself having philosophical discussions including, for example, the nature of virtual reality. It was the discussion itself that provided a clue: virtual reality feels real, but something can only feel real if human constraints are no longer apparent. In the case of conversation, there is no effort required to talk to another human in person, or on the phone; to talk to them via chat is certainly convenient, but there is a much more tangible separation. So it is with ChatGPT.The problem is how slow everything was, for reasons OpenAI explainedin a blog post about GPT-4o:Prior to GPT-4o, you could use Voice Mode to talk to ChatGPT with latencies of 2.8 seconds (GPT-3.5) and 5.4 seconds (GPT-4) on average. To achieve this, Voice Mode is a pipeline of three separate models: one simple model transcribes audio to text, GPT-3.5 or GPT-4 takes in text and outputs text, and a third simple model converts that text back to audio. This process means that the main source of intelligence, GPT-4, loses a lot of information—it can’t directly observe tone, multiple speakers, or background noises, and it can’t output laughter, singing, or express emotion.GPT-4o, on the other hand, is trained to understand and render speech natively, resulting in a drastically improved experience; from the same post:GPT-4o (“o” for “omni”) is a step towards much more natural human-computer interaction—it accepts as input any combination of text, audio, and image and generates any combination of text, audio, and image outputs. It can respond to audio inputs in as little as 232 milliseconds, with an average of 320 milliseconds, which is similar to human response time in a conversation. It matches GPT-4 Turbo performance on text in English and code, with significant improvement on text in non-English languages, while also being much faster and 50% cheaper in the API. GPT-4o is especially better at vision and audio understanding compared to existing models.I strongly recommend watching OpenAI’s26-minute presentation; the entire thing was compelling. What stood out above everything, though, was the speed: speed changes everything, particularly when it comes to verbal interaction. To that end, this little snippet inadvertently makes the point:A few notes on this clip:First, I included a full minute so you can see some GPT-4o interaction, in case you didn’t take my advice and watch the full presentation.Second, the “little snippet” I was referring to was the admission that the demo was using wired Internet: the takeaway is that OpenAI knows just how important low latency and immediate responsiveness is in delivering this user experience.Third, the video is a little glitchy, for reasons unknown; the glitchiness makes the point about the importance of a great user experience in the opposite direction.There are, to be clear, lots of other improvements in GPT-4o, including vastly improved image generation (including actually being able to reproduce text in images, generate 3D art, and more), better coding, etc. All of those are important, but in my estimation pale in comparison to the most important improvement: speed, eternally tech’s killer feature.OpenAI and AppleAgain fromBloomberg:Apple Inc. has closed in on an agreement with OpenAI to use the startup’s technology on the iPhone, part of a broader push to bring artificial intelligence features to its devices, according to people familiar with the matter.The two sides have been finalizing terms for a pact to use ChatGPT features in Apple’s iOS 18, the next iPhone operating system, said the people, who asked not to be identified because the situation is private. Apple also has held talks with Alphabet Inc.’s Google about licensing that company’s Gemini chatbot. Those discussions haven’t led to an agreement, but are ongoing.There is alotto unpack here.First, to go back to the previous point, the single most remarkable — and, in my opinion, the most important — aspect of GPT-4o is its speed; certainly a huge part of this improvement is due to model changes, but are there broad-based infrastructure improvements as well? Will GPT-4o be more widely deployed to reduce latencxy?Second,the reason I completely bought into the Apple-Gemini rumorsis that the iPhone promises to bring a massive amount of usage to whatever Chatbot it partners with; my assumption is that only Google had the infrastructure to handle that level of usage. Does OpenAI and Microsoft?Third, one of the other important GPT-4o revelations is that it will be available to free users, not just paid users (free users are currently offered GPT-3.5 Turbo). That too requires a massive increase in capacity for OpenAI.Fourth, is this actually more evidence thatGoogle is going for its moonshot? I notedlast monththat Google’s recent reorganization promoted Pixel above Android in the corporate hierarchy, lending credence to the possibility that Google might seek to differentiate its phones with Pixie, its rumored new chatbot, instead of making it broadly available to the Android ecosystem, with the goal of taking on the iPhone. This strategy would, if internally consistent, entail not giving Gemini to Apple.Fifth, are we seeing OpenAI embrace being a consumer company? Note this paragraph from Sam Altman onhis blog:Our initial conception when we started OpenAI was that we’d create AI and use it to create all sorts of benefits for the world. Instead, it now looks like we’ll create AI and then other people will use it to create all sorts of amazing things that we all benefit from.First off, I welcome the articulation of AI as tool instead of overseer, a themeI touched upon yesterday. More broadly, though, this entire release feels like a consumer product release: yes, the underlying model is better, but its not GPT-5, and that’s not the focus; the real improvements are in the user experience and the actual ChatGPTproduct. That is what it takes to win in consumer, to a much greater extent than enterprise.Moreover, this approach seems in-line with an Apple partnership. I mentioned the hardware limitation earlier; as fast as GPT-4o appears to be, there is still the hassle of taking out your phone, unlocking it, launching an app, and entering voice mode; what if that functionality was available in the same way Siri is available today?On the flipside, what does Apple have to offer ChatGPT? Distribution is certainly massive, and Apple may be paying for access; they also provide a very wide funnel to premium ChatGPT memberships. At the same time they are also a company with a lot of cash: is OpenAI’s sudden dramatic increase in inference capabilities purely a function of model improvement, or is Apple paying up to make sure that ChatGPT is more performant than ever, to the extent that it is actually a viable alternative to Google’s Gemini for Apple’s needs?I don’t know the answers to all of these questions; some will be answered today at Google I/O, some next week at Microsoft Build, and some in June at WWDC. What is clear is that OpenAI is fully committed to ensuring it plays a role in whatever comes next.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. You can get an idea of what that ambient computing is like with chatbots — this is why the ChatGPT voice application was so compelling to me — but the app paradigm is not the right one. There is a hardware breakthrough waiting to happen just like the Internet created the conditions for the smartphone breakthrough to happen. In that context, Meta’s hardware investments are suddenly drastically more compelling than they might have seemed previously, and it makes sense that OpenAI is exploring the space. Set aside — for a moment — that this excerpt is about hardware; the broader point I was driving at is the important ofspeed. Speed has always been the killer feature for basically every product type in every category, particularly in the consumer space; my contention is that the importance of speed increases in direct correlation with the intuitiveness of the experience. It’s one thing to feel the delay in interacting with an app subsequent to pulling a phone out of your pocket and unlocking it; it’s another to feel the delay while attempting to hold a conversation with a chatbot. You have obviously been able to chat with ChatGPT via text for many months now; what I only truly appreciated after talking with ChatGPT, though, was just how much work it was to type out questions and read answers. There was, in other words, a human constraint in our conversations that made it feel like I was using a tool; small wonder that the vast majority of my interaction with ChatGPT has been to do some sort of research, or try to remember something on the edge of my memory, too fuzzy to type a clear search term into Google. Simply talking, though, removed that barrier: I quickly found myself having philosophical discussions including, for example, the nature of virtual reality. It was the discussion itself that provided a clue: virtual reality feels real, but something can only feel real if human constraints are no longer apparent. In the case of conversation, there is no effort required to talk to another human in person, or on the phone; to talk to them via chat is certainly convenient, but there is a much more tangible separation. So it is with ChatGPT. Prior to GPT-4o, you could use Voice Mode to talk to ChatGPT with latencies of 2.8 seconds (GPT-3.5) and 5.4 seconds (GPT-4) on average. To achieve this, Voice Mode is a pipeline of three separate models: one simple model transcribes audio to text, GPT-3.5 or GPT-4 takes in text and outputs text, and a third simple model converts that text back to audio. This process means that the main source of intelligence, GPT-4, loses a lot of information—it can’t directly observe tone, multiple speakers, or background noises, and it can’t output laughter, singing, or express emotion. GPT-4o (“o” for “omni”) is a step towards much more natural human-computer interaction—it accepts as input any combination of text, audio, and image and generates any combination of text, audio, and image outputs. It can respond to audio inputs in as little as 232 milliseconds, with an average of 320 milliseconds, which is similar to human response time in a conversation. It matches GPT-4 Turbo performance on text in English and code, with significant improvement on text in non-English languages, while also being much faster and 50% cheaper in the API. GPT-4o is especially better at vision and audio understanding compared to existing models. I strongly recommend watching OpenAI’s26-minute presentation; the entire thing was compelling. What stood out above everything, though, was the speed: speed changes everything, particularly when it comes to verbal interaction. To that end, this little snippet inadvertently makes the point: There are, to be clear, lots of other improvements in GPT-4o, including vastly improved image generation (including actually being able to reproduce text in images, generate 3D art, and more), better coding, etc. All of those are important, but in my estimation pale in comparison to the most important improvement: speed, eternally tech’s killer feature. Our initial conception when we started OpenAI was that we’d create AI and use it to create all sorts of benefits for the world. Instead, it now looks like we’ll create AI and then other people will use it to create all sorts of amazing things that we all benefit from. First off, I welcome the articulation of AI as tool instead of overseer, a themeI touched upon yesterday. More broadly, though, this entire release feels like a consumer product release: yes, the underlying model is better, but its not GPT-5, and that’s not the focus; the real improvements are in the user experience and the actual ChatGPTproduct. That is what it takes to win in consumer, to a much greater extent than enterprise. Moreover, this approach seems in-line with an Apple partnership. I mentioned the hardware limitation earlier; as fast as GPT-4o appears to be, there is still the hassle of taking out your phone, unlocking it, launching an app, and entering voice mode; what if that functionality was available in the same way Siri is available today? On the flipside, what does Apple have to offer ChatGPT? Distribution is certainly massive, and Apple may be paying for access; they also provide a very wide funnel to premium ChatGPT memberships. At the same time they are also a company with a lot of cash: is OpenAI’s sudden dramatic increase in inference capabilities purely a function of model improvement, or is Apple paying up to make sure that ChatGPT is more performant than ever, to the extent that it is actually a viable alternative to Google’s Gemini for Apple’s needs? I don’t know the answers to all of these questions; some will be answered today at Google I/O, some next week at Microsoft Build, and some in June at WWDC. What is clear is that OpenAI is fully committed to ensuring it plays a role in whatever comes next.
