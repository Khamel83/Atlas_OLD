I thought thatthe most recent episodewas an absolute treat.On to the Update:Llama 3.1FromBloomberg:Facebook parent company Meta Platforms Inc. debuted a new and powerful AI model that Chief Executive Officer Mark Zuckerberg called “state of the art” and said will rival similar offerings from competitors like OpenAI and Alphabet Inc.’s Google. The new model released Tuesday, called Llama 3.1, took several months to train and hundreds of millions of dollars of computing power. The company said it represents a major update from Llama 3, which came out in April.“I think the most important product for an AI assistant is going to be how smart it is,” Zuckerberg said during an interview on the Bloomberg Originals series The Circuit with Emily Chang. “The Llama models that we’re building are some of the most advanced in the world.” Meta is already working on Llama 4, Zuckerberg added…Meta uses its Llama models to power its AI chatbot, called Meta AI, which operates inside its apps, including Instagram and WhatsApp, and also as a separate web product. Zuckerberg said that Meta has “hundreds of millions” of users for its chatbot, and expects it will be the most widely used chatbot in the world by the end of the year. He expects that others outside of Meta will use Llama to train their own AI models.This release is a big deal for a whole host of reasons, many of them detailed onMeta’s blog postannouncing the release. Start with performance; the largest version of the model, with 405 billion parameters, is state-of-the-art:The smaller models, meanwhile, appear to be best-in-class:Second, Meta trained this model in the most straightforward way possible (as opposed to OpenAI’s more complicated mixture-of-experts approach for GPT-4); this entails significantly more computer resources, but also leverages those resources in a more stable and scalable manner. In other words, Meta trained Llama like a big company with lots of resources, while OpenAI trained GPT-4 like a startup.Third, Meta released a collection of tools and sample apps in addition to the models, and is proposing a “Llama Stack”, a standardized set of tools and interfaces for building critical yet undifferentiated components necessary to deploy Llama.Fourth, Llama 3.1 is widely available immediately: developers can download the model weights directly, or, more realistically when it comes to the largest model, acces Llama on a host of cloud providers:All of this is linked to the most important fact about Llama 3.1: it is “open source” (as always, I put that in quotes; Llama isn’t releasing the “source code”, which is effectively the data used to train Llama 3.1, because it almost certainly doesn’t have a license to a good portion of it;I preferthe term “open weights”).This decisionwas a master-stroke: all of the evidence I have seen suggests that Llama is the standard for all AI applications outside of direct engagements with OpenAI and Microsoft; there is a good chance that Meta controls the future of AI for every entity that is not a tech company.Zuckerberg on Open Source AIMeta CEO Mark Zuckerberg has no etymological compunctions about the definition of “open source”; he wrote a post entitledOpen Source AI Is the Path Forward. It is very much worth reading in full, and opens like this:In the early days of high-performance computing, the major tech companies of the day each invested heavily in developing their own closed source versions of Unix. It was hard to imagine at the time that any other approach could develop such advanced software. Eventually though, open source Linux gained popularity — initially because it allowed developers to modify its code however they wanted and was more affordable, and over time because it became more advanced, more secure, and had a broader ecosystem supporting more capabilities than any closed Unix. Today, Linux is the industry standard foundation for both cloud computing and the operating systems that run most mobile devices — and we all benefit from superior products because of it.I believe that AI will develop in a similar way. Today, several tech companies are developing leading closed models. But open source is quickly closing the gap. Last year, Llama 2 was only comparable to an older generation of models behind the frontier. This year, Llama 3 is competitive with the most advanced models and leading in some areas. Starting next year, we expect future Llama models to become the most advanced in the industry. But even before that, Llama is already leading on openness, modifiability, and cost efficiency.Zuckerberg goes on to explain why open source (open weights) is the best option for developers, for Meta, and for the world. For developers:Every organization needs its own models, and they can start with Llama; to that end, Meta has updated the Llama license to allow it to be used for training other models, as long as those model names start with the word “Llama”.Developers need to avoid being locked into a closed vendor.Companies need to protect their data.Developers need a model that is efficient and affordableDevelopers should want to direct their time and energy to an ecosystem that will be the industry standard.For Meta:Investing in Llama ensures that Meta has access to the best technology for its products.There is no risk in open sourcing because the industry is very competitive already.Meta isn’t cannibalizing any of its revenue streams by open-sourcing Llama (this is a nice bit of jujitsu on the investor critique thatMeta doesn’t monetize its models directly).Meta has a proven track record of saving a lot of money by open-sourcing its complements, particularly in the data center.For the world:Open source will democratize the benefits of AI.Open source will better protect against unintentional harm by virtue of being more transparent and testable.Open source will better protect against intentional harm from small scale actors by proliferating advanced detection and protection systems.I want to call out the part about nation-state actors specifically:The next question is how the US and democratic nations should handle the threat of states with massive resources like China. The United States’ advantage is decentralized and open innovation. Some people argue that we must close our models to prevent China from gaining access to them, but my view is that this will not work and will only disadvantage the US and its allies. Our adversaries are great at espionage, stealing models that fit on a thumb drive is relatively easy, and most tech companies are far from operating in a way that would make this more difficult. It seems most likely that a world of only closed models results in a small number of big companies plus our geopolitical adversaries having access to leading models, while startups, universities, and small businesses miss out on opportunities. Plus, constraining American innovation to closed development increases the chance that we don’t lead at all. Instead, I think our best strategy is to build a robust open ecosystem and have our leading companies work closely with our government and allies to ensure they can best take advantage of the latest advances and achieve a sustainable first-mover advantage over the long term.I could not agree with this paragraph more strongly. The reality is that China almost certainly has access to all of Silicon Valley’s most advanced technology, and there is little point in trying to undo that; the U.S. will never out-China China. What the U.S. can do is lean heavily into innovation — which requires more openness, not less — and unlock capabilities at a rate that China with its state-centric system simply can’t keep up with.Kamala Harris, Europe, and Tech InnovationOn that note, there was, based on email feedback to last week’s series of Updates about politically-adjacent topics likemy explanation for why some parts of tech started questioning their Democratic Party allegiance, some misunderstanding of how I think about Stratechery and what I write. Vance’s views on antitrust, I do feel compelled to offer some sort of comment on Kamala Harris becoming the de facto Democratic nominee. In short, I think she has an opening in terms of tech: obviously she is from San Francisco, but more pertinently, Vance’s views on tech antitrust, to the extent they indicate how a Trump administration will approach the topic, give a lot of room for Harris to triangulate.Specifically, while I analyzed the Andreessen Horowitz distinction between “Big Tech” and “Little Tech”, I did note that they did have some interests in common:All that noted, the acquisition question is the one area where “Big Tech” and “Little Tech” are on the same page: the former wants to be able to buy interesting new companies, and the funders of the latter want to get a return on their investment. I do think that “Little Tech” is currently the most enthused by Vance’s appointment; his remarks below, though, suggest he may actually help keep the liquidation spigots turned off.To that end, Harris has an obvious angle to gaining significant support in tech: pledge to turn back on the acquisition spigot that has been shut off by Biden’s FTC and DOJ appointees, so that “Big Tech” can buy features and “Little Tech” can generate liquidity for their investors (seeFirst, Do No Harm). As I noted in last week’s Updates the Democrats have not so much driven some number of tech luminaries to be Trump acolytes as they have freed them to pursue their own self interest; the nomination of Vance, though, opens up the opportunity to meet that self interest in a very concrete way.All that noted, I’m skeptical; the most concerning aspect about the Harris nomination is that (1) she was the Biden administration’s AI czar and (2) President Biden issued an executive order about AI that I believecompletely missed the mark, sacrificing innovation for control and, by extension, lock-in from closed proprietors. I go back to the Zuckerberg excerpt above: the U.S. is not going to lead the world by regulating innovation; that is the surest possible recipe for losing to China; the U.S.’s enduring advantage is freedom, which spurs innovation, and it’s frustrating that Harris was the czar of an approach that has already gotten this wrong.Of course it could be worse; news brokea week agothat Meta’s latest models wouldn’t be available in the E.U.; now we know why.
