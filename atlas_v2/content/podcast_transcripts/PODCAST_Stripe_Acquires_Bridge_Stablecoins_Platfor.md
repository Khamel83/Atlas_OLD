The deal, firstreportedby Bloomberg News on Oct. 16, is still subject to regulatory approvals and closing conditions. The parties expect to complete the transaction in coming months, according to a separatepostfrom Bridge’s X account. Stripe will pay $1.1 billion, according to a person familiar with the matter who asked not to be identified discussing private information. A spokesperson for Stripe declined to comment on a purchase amount.Stablecoins are typically designed to track the value of currencies or assets such as dollars one-for-one and today are primarily used as bridge currencies in and out of the crypto economy. “Stablecoins are room-temperature superconductors for financial services,” Collison wrote on X. “Thanks to stablecoins, businesses around the world will benefit from significant speed, coverage, and cost improvements in the coming years. Stripe is going to build the world’s best stablecoin infrastructure.”This isn’t the first Stripe crypto-related news this year; fromBloombergin April:Stripe Inc. is bringing back crypto payments by allowing merchants using its platform to accept stablecoins for online transactions. “There’s been a lot of technical improvements happening in crypto,” John Collison, co-founder and president said Thursday at the payments processing company’s annual conference in San Francisco. The option should be available this summer, he said.Transaction speeds have increased in crypto, while fees have gone down as crypto finds “real utility,” according to Collison. He said that stablecoins, which are cryptocurrencies pegged to a fixed asset like the dollar, are becoming “more stable” and demonstrated a crypto payment on Stripe using Circle’s USDC token during his presentation. Stablecoins have continued to gain traction in crypto and fintech, with PayPal Holdings Inc. introducing its PYUSD stablecoin last year.Stripe enabled Bitcoin payments about 10 years ago, with Collison noting that the experience back then was “pretty terrible.” The service ended in 2018 in part due to a lack of customer demand.Stripe’s crypto payment integration is, according toThe Information, provided by Paxos; this acquisition is probably bad news for that relationship in the long run, but the opportunity Stripe is targeting with Bridge is much broader than simply collecting crypto payments.StablecoinsHere is the full Collison tweet that Bloomberg quoted above:Stablecoins are room-temperature superconductors for financial services. Thanks to stablecoins, businesses around the world will benefit from significant speed, coverage, and cost improvements in the coming years. Stripe is going to build the world’s best stablecoin…https://t.co/6yKi7OKXVT— Patrick Collison (@patrickc)October 21, 2024Superconductors were on the mind last summer with the alleged discovery of LK-99, andI wrote at the time:Superconductors conduct electricity with perfect efficiency, which has all kinds of positive outcomes: you could install solar panels in the desert, for example, and transport the electricity thousands of miles away. You could build perfect batteries. You could build magnetically levitating trains that move without friction. You could build fusion reactors. You could build quantum computers. Superconductors do exist, but only at high pressure and extremely low temperatures that aren’t viable for widespread use; it’s that room-temperature (and room-pressure) bit that is potentially extraordinary about LK-99.Of course LK-99 turned out to be, if not a fraud, then a wildly over-optimistic misinterpretation of an impure sample; that’s a sentence that could just as easily be used for a lot of crypto.Longtime readers will know, however, that I have consistently remained interested in crypto, for an easily distilled reason: digital scarcity is fundamentally interesting. The digital part means that crypto is programmable and perfectly portable like everything else on the Internet; most of the Internet, though, is about abundance, given the infinitely replicable nature of digital, while crypto, thanks to the structure of blockchains, allows for scarcity, without any sort of centralized verifier or clearinghouse.Stablecoins distill crypto to these most interesting bits. Unlike Bitcoin, stablecoins do not have intrinsic value downstream from a network effect, and unlike Ethereum, they are not set up to execute smart contracts or other applications; rather, their value is right there in the name: they are stable representations of value — usually the U.S. dollar. That stability can be maintained (or attempted to be maintained) in a number of ways, including algorithmically, through crypto-based collateral, or, most simply, through dollar-denominated assets like U.S. Treasuries. The specifics don’t matter, for reasons I’ll get to in a moment, but assume the dollar-denominated asset version.What remains is a synthetic currency that is digital but scarce, with all of the affordances that allows for, including the ability to move money frictionlessly (thus Collison’s analogy). The analogy I think of is to the Internet itself:Physical goods are scarce, but while you can scale up from hand-delivery, you still have to pay a lot for a delivery service, and if you cross borders you have to deal with customs.Information used to be delivered in person, then via physical media like letters or newspapers, but now it is purely digital and freely distributed and duplicated all over the world; it is abundant.Dollars right now are more akin to physical goods than they are to information: you can deliver it by hand, or even via delivery services like ACH or SWIFT, but the need for verification and confirmation flows introduce a ton of friction. Moreover, you can’t actually do anything with dollars at rest, other than watch them deflate. Stablecoins solve these problems: you can transfer them like information, while preserving scarcity, while blockchains provide verification and confirmation that scale from the smallest transactions to the biggest; meanwhile, they also earn a return while at rest thanks to the assets backing them.Now the assets backing them part is tricky: the two biggest stablecoins — Tether and USDC — have facedallegations of questionable accountingandan outright (temporary) loss of its dollar pegrespectively, but the key thing to know about this acquisition is that Stripe is not acquiring a stablecoin, but rather the infrastructure to leverage them.Platform of PlatformsBridge is a pretty mysterious company witha supremely unhelpful webpage; however,Fortune profiled the companyjust a couple of months ago:A startup called Bridge, cofounded by Square and Coinbase alumni Zach Abrams and Sean Yu, wants to take stablecoins to the next level. Drawing on their extensive experience in fintech, Bridge aspires to build a future global payment network around stablecoins in order to challenge everything from Swift to credit cards…The challenge for many companies that want to use stablecoins is building on-ramps and off-ramps to move fiat currency into and out of the crypto ecosystem, while also facilitating transfers across different types of stablecoins and blockchains. Dollar-backed options like USDC and Tether may be the most popular, but there are countless others with pegs to the Mexican peso or a basket of assets—and a single stablecoin might be issued across a dozen different blockchains…The pitch is simple. “We built Bridge as a low-level set of APIs that would enable companies to use a stablecoin rail without thinking about it,” as Abrams put it. But why would companies want that?As crypto advocates like to point out, moving around fiat currency is slow and expensive—you often need to wait for bank operating hours for large transactions, and cross-border payments can come with massive fees and interminable waits. Stablecoins, at least in theory, offer low fees and instant settlement. In Bridge’s ideal future, stablecoins will operate as the global payments rail, and Bridge will allow developers to seamlessly integrate. In that sense, you can think of it as a Web3 analog to Stripe, which helps businesses accept online payments, or Plaid, which makes it easy to connect an app to a bank account.Right now the primary use case for stablecoins is for transactions between crypto users, particularly for decentralized finance (DeFi) applications. Things like loans and leverage and whatnot require that the underlying asset be, well, stable; Bridge, however, is focused on abstracting stablecoins away so that its benefits can be reaped for non-crypto use cases.The most obvious use case is cross-border money flows, particularly to countries that are underbanked and have unstable currencies. Scale AI, for example, which hires and needs to pay data labelers around the world, uses Airtm, which is built on Bridge, to pay contractors in stablecoins; Scale AI wires money to Airtm, and the contractors get stablecoins on the other end immediately, with no cross-border complications or slow-downs. It’s not a perfect end-to-end solution, but for tech-savvy people in countries with unstable currencies that’s a feature, not a bug — they’d rather hold their money in dollars, even synthetic ones.What is interesting to think about is how more of this end-to-end infrastructure might be built out over time.Earlier this yearI interviewed the founder and new CEO of dLocal, which is attacking the space from a different direction: dLocal’s focus is on buildingOne APIfor large multinationals to use to offer services to emerging markets, while dLocal does all of the legwork to integrate local payment methods; the Bridge approach is the exact opposite. Yes, it is all about the API, but that API is the plumbing for financial services companies to be built on both sides, whether that be for getting fiat money into the system (usually in developed markets) or getting it out (usually in emerging markets). It’s unbundling the process, while removing the cross-border challenges via stablecoins.The Bridge API is, by all accounts, very well done and easy-to-use, and critically, the company has made a lot of progress on the regulatory front. An anonymous writer namedSIGNALwrote onConviction.finance:Zach has managed to do the (near) impossible, obtain solid licenses in the 2 major jurisdictions: the United States and Europe, where they are both permitted to transmit large sums of money and exchange currencies on behalf of users, essentially, a ramp. One, for the Eurozone (EU/EEA) is located in Poland, and another for the US DE C-corp is located in Berkeley, California. The US C-corp has a money transmitter license (MTL) approved in 22 states, but don’t fret, it looks like they’re far from done; in 2024 alone, Ohio, North Dakota, North Carolina, New Mexico, Maine, Louisiana, Illinois, and the District of Colombia has issued them a MTL [Note: The Fortune article above says that Bridge has MTL licenses in 48 states]…The downstream effects of their B2B flow, their large settlement limits, great compliance, and speed cannot be understated. The consumer-facing routes used by auxiliary non-related entities are a huge quality of life improvement right now, but for institutional use, I believe Bridge is going to play a huge, if not the ultimate role, in ensuring funds are never stuck on or offchain.That regulatory work may very well be worth the $1.1 billion Stripe paid in-and-of-itself, particularly if it comes attached to two years of work and a strong team. There is certainly no question that Bridge is aligned with the overall Stripe mission; what I articulated above is basically theplatform of platformsstrategy Stripe is pursuing in traditional payments.What is interesting to consider, however, is if Stripe anticipates that the stablecoin market will become very hot before they would have a chance to build-instead-of-buy.The biggest potential catalyst would be a major market debuting a central bank digital currency. Remember, Bridge is stablecoin agnostic; yes, the big public coins like Tether or USDC are what will enable a lot of the use cases for now, and yes, you can imagine companies wanting their own internal stablecoins to reap the benefits for internal processes, and yes, stablecoins present an intriguing and vastly more scalable alternative to “For the Benefit Of” accounts that undergird fintechs (the blockchain gives you the ledger for free!), but any sort of formalized stablecoin would probably do more to accelerate the space than anything else, and Stripe will be ready. Or, this news may be enough to spur usage in its own right, and, critically, regulation around stablecoins, which Stripe would very much welcome, both because it has a strong interest in the coins being stable, and also because regulatory uncertainty is a major obstacle to adoption.And, I should note, there are the more long-range fantastical outcomes that crypto advocates have long predicted, like micropayments or AI-agent usable currencies, all of which require new payment rails. Crypto generally and stablecoins specifically remain the most likely candidates to achieve those sorts of outcomes, even if they are still a long ways in the future. The future, though, both short, medium, and long-term, is what this acquisition is about, and I think Stripe buying a $1 billion option on that future is a reasonable bet.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. You could build perfect batteries. You could build magnetically levitating trains that move without friction. You could build fusion reactors. You could build quantum computers. Superconductors do exist, but only at high pressure and extremely low temperatures that aren’t viable for widespread use; it’s that room-temperature (and room-pressure) bit that is potentially extraordinary about LK-99.Of course LK-99 turned out to be, if not a fraud, then a wildly over-optimistic misinterpretation of an impure sample; that’s a sentence that could just as easily be used for a lot of crypto.Longtime readers will know, however, that I have consistently remained interested in crypto, for an easily distilled reason: digital scarcity is fundamentally interesting. The digital part means that crypto is programmable and perfectly portable like everything else on the Internet; most of the Internet, though, is about abundance, given the infinitely replicable nature of digital, while crypto, thanks to the structure of blockchains, allows for scarcity, without any sort of centralized verifier or clearinghouse.Stablecoins distill crypto to these most interesting bits. Unlike Bitcoin, stablecoins do not have intrinsic value downstream from a network effect, and unlike Ethereum, they are not set up to execute smart contracts or other applications; rather, their value is right there in the name: they are stable representations of value — usually the U.S. dollar. That stability can be maintained (or attempted to be maintained) in a number of ways, including algorithmically, through crypto-based collateral, or, most simply, through dollar-denominated assets like U.S. Treasuries. The specifics don’t matter, for reasons I’ll get to in a moment, but assume the dollar-denominated asset version.What remains is a synthetic currency that is digital but scarce, with all of the affordances that allows for, including the ability to move money frictionlessly (thus Collison’s analogy). The analogy I think of is to the Internet itself:Physical goods are scarce, but while you can scale up from hand-delivery, you still have to pay a lot for a delivery service, and if you cross borders you have to deal with customs.Information used to be delivered in person, then via physical media like letters or newspapers, but now it is purely digital and freely distributed and duplicated all over the world; it is abundant.Dollars right now are more akin to physical goods than they are to information: you can deliver it by hand, or even via delivery services like ACH or SWIFT, but the need for verification and confirmation flows introduce a ton of friction. Moreover, you can’t actually do anything with dollars at rest, other than watch them deflate. Stablecoins solve these problems: you can transfer them like information, while preserving scarcity, while blockchains provide verification and confirmation that scale from the smallest transactions to the biggest; meanwhile, they also earn a return while at rest thanks to the assets backing them.Now the assets backing them part is tricky: the two biggest stablecoins — Tether and USDC — have facedallegations of questionable accountingandan outright (temporary) loss of its dollar pegrespectively, but the key thing to know about this acquisition is that Stripe is not acquiring a stablecoin, but rather the infrastructure to leverage them.Platform of PlatformsBridge is a pretty mysterious company witha supremely unhelpful webpage; however,Fortune profiled the companyjust a couple of months ago:A startup called Bridge, cofounded by Square and Coinbase alumni Zach Abrams and Sean Yu, wants to take stablecoins to the next level. Drawing on their extensive experience in fintech, Bridge aspires to build a future global payment network around stablecoins in order to challenge everything from Swift to credit cards…The challenge for many companies that want to use stablecoins is building on-ramps and off-ramps to move fiat currency into and out of the crypto ecosystem, while also facilitating transfers across different types of stablecoins and blockchains. Dollar-backed options like USDC and Tether may be the most popular, but there are countless others with pegs to the Mexican peso or a basket of assets—and a single stablecoin might be issued across a dozen different blockchains…The pitch is simple. “We built Bridge as a low-level set of APIs that would enable companies to use a stablecoin rail without thinking about it,” as Abrams put it. But why would companies want that?As crypto advocates like to point out, moving around fiat currency is slow and expensive—you often need to wait for bank operating hours for large transactions, and cross-border payments can come with massive fees and interminable waits. Stablecoins, at least in theory, offer low fees and instant settlement. In Bridge’s ideal future, stablecoins will operate as the global payments rail, and Bridge will allow developers to seamlessly integrate. In that sense, you can think of it as a Web3 analog to Stripe, which helps businesses accept online payments, or Plaid, which makes it easy to connect an app to a bank account.Right now the primary use case for stablecoins is for transactions between crypto users, particularly for decentralized finance (DeFi) applications. Things like loans and leverage and whatnot require that the underlying asset be, well, stable; Bridge, however, is focused on abstracting stablecoins away so that its benefits can be reaped for non-crypto use cases.The most obvious use case is cross-border money flows, particularly to countries that are underbanked and have unstable currencies. Scale AI, for example, which hires and needs to pay data labelers around the world, uses Airtm, which is built on Bridge, to pay contractors in stablecoins; Scale AI wires money to Airtm, and the contractors get stablecoins on the other end immediately, with no cross-border complications or slow-downs. It’s not a perfect end-to-end solution, but for tech-savvy people in countries with unstable currencies that’s a feature, not a bug — they’d rather hold their money in dollars, even synthetic ones.What is interesting to think about is how more of this end-to-end infrastructure might be built out over time.Earlier this yearI interviewed the founder and new CEO of dLocal, which is attacking the space from a different direction: dLocal’s focus is on buildingOne APIfor large multinationals to use to offer services to emerging markets, while dLocal does all of the legwork to integrate local payment methods; the Bridge approach is the exact opposite. Yes, it is all about the API, but that API is the plumbing for financial services companies to be built on both sides, whether that be for getting fiat money into the system (usually in developed markets) or getting it out (usually in emerging markets). It’s unbundling the process, while removing the cross-border challenges via stablecoins.The Bridge API is, by all accounts, very well done and easy-to-use, and critically, the company has made a lot of progress on the regulatory front. An anonymous writer namedSIGNALwrote onConviction.finance:Zach has managed to do the (near) impossible, obtain solid licenses in the 2 major jurisdictions: the United States and Europe, where they are both permitted to transmit large sums of money and exchange currencies on behalf of users, essentially, a ramp. One, for the Eurozone (EU/EEA) is located in Poland, and another for the US DE C-corp is located in Berkeley, California. The US C-corp has a money transmitter license (MTL) approved in 22 states, but don’t fret, it looks like they’re far from done; in 2024 alone, Ohio, North Dakota, North Carolina, New Mexico, Maine, Louisiana, Illinois, and the District of Colombia has issued them a MTL [Note: The Fortune article above says that Bridge has MTL licenses in 48 states]…The downstream effects of their B2B flow, their large settlement limits, great compliance, and speed cannot be understated. The consumer-facing routes used by auxiliary non-related entities are a huge quality of life improvement right now, but for institutional use, I believe Bridge is going to play a huge, if not the ultimate role, in ensuring funds are never stuck on or offchain.That regulatory work may very well be worth the $1.1 billion Stripe paid in-and-of-itself, particularly if it comes attached to two years of work and a strong team. There is certainly no question that Bridge is aligned with the overall Stripe mission; what I articulated above is basically theplatform of platformsstrategy Stripe is pursuing in traditional payments.What is interesting to consider, however, is if Stripe anticipates that the stablecoin market will become very hot before they would have a chance to build-instead-of-buy.The biggest potential catalyst would be a major market debuting a central bank digital currency. Remember, Bridge is stablecoin agnostic; yes, the big public coins like Tether or USDC are what will enable a lot of the use cases for now, and yes, you can imagine companies wanting their own internal stablecoins to reap the benefits for internal processes, and yes, stablecoins present an intriguing and vastly more scalable alternative to “For the Benefit Of” accounts that undergird fintechs (the blockchain gives you the ledger for free!), but any sort of formalized stablecoin would probably do more to accelerate the space than anything else, and Stripe will be ready. Crypto generally and stablecoins specifically remain the most likely candidates to achieve those sorts of outcomes, even if they are still a long ways in the future. Stablecoins solve these problems: you can transfer them like information, while preserving scarcity, while blockchains provide verification and confirmation that scale from the smallest transactions to the biggest; meanwhile, they also earn a return while at rest thanks to the assets backing them. Now the assets backing them part is tricky: the two biggest stablecoins — Tether and USDC — have facedallegations of questionable accountingandan outright (temporary) loss of its dollar pegrespectively, but the key thing to know about this acquisition is that Stripe is not acquiring a stablecoin, but rather the infrastructure to leverage them. A startup called Bridge, cofounded by Square and Coinbase alumni Zach Abrams and Sean Yu, wants to take stablecoins to the next level. Drawing on their extensive experience in fintech, Bridge aspires to build a future global payment network around stablecoins in order to challenge everything from Swift to credit cards… The challenge for many companies that want to use stablecoins is building on-ramps and off-ramps to move fiat currency into and out of the crypto ecosystem, while also facilitating transfers across different types of stablecoins and blockchains. Dollar-backed options like USDC and Tether may be the most popular, but there are countless others with pegs to the Mexican peso or a basket of assets—and a single stablecoin might be issued across a dozen different blockchains… As crypto advocates like to point out, moving around fiat currency is slow and expensive—you often need to wait for bank operating hours for large transactions, and cross-border payments can come with massive fees and interminable waits. Stablecoins, at least in theory, offer low fees and instant settlement. In Bridge’s ideal future, stablecoins will operate as the global payments rail, and Bridge will allow developers to seamlessly integrate. In that sense, you can think of it as a Web3 analog to Stripe, which helps businesses accept online payments, or Plaid, which makes it easy to connect an app to a bank account. Right now the primary use case for stablecoins is for transactions between crypto users, particularly for decentralized finance (DeFi) applications. Things like loans and leverage and whatnot require that the underlying asset be, well, stable; Bridge, however, is focused on abstracting stablecoins away so that its benefits can be reaped for non-crypto use cases. The most obvious use case is cross-border money flows, particularly to countries that are underbanked and have unstable currencies. Scale AI, for example, which hires and needs to pay data labelers around the world, uses Airtm, which is built on Bridge, to pay contractors in stablecoins; Scale AI wires money to Airtm, and the contractors get stablecoins on the other end immediately, with no cross-border complications or slow-downs. It’s not a perfect end-to-end solution, but for tech-savvy people in countries with unstable currencies that’s a feature, not a bug — they’d rather hold their money in dollars, even synthetic ones. What is interesting to think about is how more of this end-to-end infrastructure might be built out over time.Earlier this yearI interviewed the founder and new CEO of dLocal, which is attacking the space from a different direction: dLocal’s focus is on buildingOne APIfor large multinationals to use to offer services to emerging markets, while dLocal does all of the legwork to integrate local payment methods; the Bridge approach is the exact opposite. Yes, it is all about the API, but that API is the plumbing for financial services companies to be built on both sides, whether that be for getting fiat money into the system (usually in developed markets) or getting it out (usually in emerging markets). It’s unbundling the process, while removing the cross-border challenges via stablecoins. The Bridge API is, by all accounts, very well done and easy-to-use, and critically, the company has made a lot of progress on the regulatory front. An anonymous writer namedSIGNALwrote onConviction.finance: Zach has managed to do the (near) impossible, obtain solid licenses in the 2 major jurisdictions: the United States and Europe, where they are both permitted to transmit large sums of money and exchange currencies on behalf of users, essentially, a ramp. One, for the Eurozone (EU/EEA) is located in Poland, and another for the US DE C-corp is located in Berkeley, California. The US C-corp has a money transmitter license (MTL) approved in 22 states, but don’t fret, it looks like they’re far from done; in 2024 alone, Ohio, North Dakota, North Carolina, New Mexico, Maine, Louisiana, Illinois, and the District of Colombia has issued them a MTL [Note: The Fortune article above says that Bridge has MTL licenses in 48 states]… The downstream effects of their B2B flow, their large settlement limits, great compliance, and speed cannot be understated. The consumer-facing routes used by auxiliary non-related entities are a huge quality of life improvement right now, but for institutional use, I believe Bridge is going to play a huge, if not the ultimate role, in ensuring funds are never stuck on or offchain. That regulatory work may very well be worth the $1.1 billion Stripe paid in-and-of-itself, particularly if it comes attached to two years of work and a strong team. There is certainly no question that Bridge is aligned with the overall Stripe mission; what I articulated above is basically theplatform of platformsstrategy Stripe is pursuing in traditional payments. The biggest potential catalyst would be a major market debuting a central bank digital currency. Remember, Bridge is stablecoin agnostic; yes, the big public coins like Tether or USDC are what will enable a lot of the use cases for now, and yes, you can imagine companies wanting their own internal stablecoins to reap the benefits for internal processes, and yes, stablecoins present an intriguing and vastly more scalable alternative to “For the Benefit Of” accounts that undergird fintechs (the blockchain gives you the ledger for free!), but any sort of formalized stablecoin would probably do more to accelerate the space than anything else, and Stripe will be ready. Or, this news may be enough to spur usage in its own right, and, critically, regulation around stablecoins, which Stripe would very much welcome, both because it has a strong interest in the coins being stable, and also because regulatory uncertainty is a major obstacle to adoption. And, I should note, there are the more long-range fantastical outcomes that crypto advocates have long predicted, like micropayments or AI-agent usable currencies, all of which require new payment rails. Crypto generally and stablecoins specifically remain the most likely candidates to achieve those sorts of outcomes, even if they are still a long ways in the future. The future, though, both short, medium, and long-term, is what this acquisition is about, and I think Stripe buying a $1 billion option on that future is a reasonable bet.
