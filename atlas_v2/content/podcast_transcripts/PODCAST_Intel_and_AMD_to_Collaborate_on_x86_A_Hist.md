The Waymo can request assistance, at which point a remote worker can provide said assistance, but the point of Level 4 autonomy is that the Waymo does operate on its own in all situations (although sometimes this does mean coming to a stop).On to the Update:Intel and AMD to Collaborate on x86From theWall Street Journal:Intel and Advanced Micro Devices have created an advisory group, including members from other technology companies such as Google, Microsoft and Meta Platforms, to shape the future of the x86 computer architecture, as the landscape transitions to dynamic artificial-intelligence workloads. “We are on the cusp of one of the most significant shifts in the x86 architecture and ecosystem in decades—with new levels of customization, compatibility and scalability needed to meet current and future customer needs,” Intel Chief Executive Pat Gelsinger said.The companies on Tuesday said the group would work to identify new ways to expand the x86 ecosystem by enabling compatibility across different platforms and simplifying software development. As a result, the group said it hopes to enhance customer choice and compatibility across hardware and software; simplify architectural guidelines to enhance software consistency and standardize interfaces; and enable better integration of new capabilities into operating systems, frameworks and applications.The founding members of the group are Meta, Microsoft, Broadcom, Dell, Alphabet-unit Google, Hewlett Packard Enterprise, HP Inc., Lenovo, Oracle and International Business Machines-unit Red Hat.The implications of this new group are somewhat obvious, but it is momentous enough to justify a trip down memory lane.A History of Intel and AMDThe origin story of the PC is so misunderstood that it was actually one of the very first things I wrote about on Stratechery, way back in May 2013 inThe Truth About Windows Versus the Mac:You’ve heard the phrase, “No one ever got fired for buying IBM.” That axiom in fact predates Microsoft or Apple, having originated during IBM’s System/360 heyday. But it had a powerful effect on the PC market.In the late 1970s and very early 1980s, a new breed of personal computers were appearing on the scene, including the Commodore, MITS Altair, Apple II, and more. Some employees were bringing them into the workplace, which major corporations found unacceptable, so IT departments asked IBM for something similar. After all, “No one ever got fired…”IBM spun up a separate team in Florida to put together something they could sell IT departments. Pressed for time, the Florida team put together a minicomputer using mostly off-the shelf components; IBM’s RISC processors and the OS they had under development were technically superior, but Intel had a CISC processor for sale immediately, and a new company called Microsoft said their OS — DOS — could be ready in six months. For the sake of expediency, IBM decided to go with Intel and Microsoft.The point of that Article is to note that Microsoft’s dominance of PC operating systems actually preceded the Mac; yes Windows came later, but Windows was backwards-compatible with DOS, and DOS was blessed by IBM. This was particularly pertinent in 2013 because everyone was pointing to Windows dominance over the Mac as evidence that Android would come to dominate the iPhone, but in fact their analogy was grounded in a misunderstanding of what actually happened in the early 1980s.Anyhow, the focus on operating systems is why I didn’t bother telling the entire story, which is that while IBM chose Intel to provide the PC’s processor, they were wary of being reliant on a single supplier (it’s notable that IBM didn’t demand the same of the operating system, which was probably a combination of not fully appreciating operating systems as a point of integration and lock-in for 3rd-party software, which barely existed at that point, and a recognition that software is just bits and not a physical good that has to be manufactured). To that end IBM demanded that Intel license its processor to another chip firm, and AMD was the obvious choice: the firm was founded by Jerry Sanders, a Fairchild Semiconductor alum who had worked with Intel’s founders, and specialized in manufacturing licensed chips.The chip used in the IBM PC was the Intel 8088, which was a cheaper 8-bit version of the 16-bit 8086; it’s the 8086 that gives the x86 instruction set architecture (ISA) its name. The ISA defines the lowest level interface between hardware and software; it defines the operations a processor can execute (like arithmetic, logic, etc.), data types, register operation, memory addressing, I/O management, etc. All software has to be decomposed to what is in the ISA; any chip that supports a particular ISA has to be able to execute everything defined in the ISA. In this case Intel came up with a new ISA for the 8086, and has carried that ISA through (with many modifications and additions, to be sure), ever since. That is x86 (ARM, for example, is a different ISA).AMD’s first chip under the IBM-mandated second-sourcing agreement was a clone of the 8088, with basically the exact same performance even though it came out three years later in 1982. Intel, meanwhile, released the 286 in 1982 as well, moving back to a 16-bit processor. It took AMD 2 years to follow with the Am286, but in the meantime AMD had meaningfully improved their manufacturing capability, which meant their clone could operate at 20MHz, a massive jump over Intel’s 12.5MHz. Moreover it was cheaper! This made AMD particularly attractive to the exploding IBM clone market: while the original reverse-engineered Compaq used Intel’s 8088, an increasing number of IBM PC clones gravitated to AMD.Intel saw the clones as an opportunity as well: the company launched its 386 processor with Compaq, not IBM, and declared it would no longer share its designs with AMD; it ended up taking AMD 5 years to reverse-engineer the 386, while the company sought arbitration to force Intel to share its design. In the meantime. Intel was suing companies like Cyrix and NEC for having reverse-engineered the 286. Intel failed to stop Cyrix with a patent lawsuit, and NEC with a copyright lawsuit, but the latter case did state that microcode (which actually implements the commands defined by the ISA) was copyrightable. Intel seized on that and sought an injunction against AMD’s impending Am386 processor; that case was stayed pending the resolution of AMD’s arbitration case, which meant the Am386 launched, and once again it was a great chip. It was faster and cheaper than the 386, and nearly as fast and alotcheaper than the new Intel 486.Finally, in 1995, after years of litigation and tens of millions of dollars of legal fees (there were more cases than what I just noted, including an AMD antitrust suit against Intel), the companies settled: AMD received a perpetual license to the microcode that implemented the x86 ISA in the 386 and 486, but would develop its own chips going foreward. Unfortunately AMD was fairly hobbled by this point, while Intel had reaped massive profits from the 386 and 486, which meant that AMD’s one-time manufacturing advantage was long gone; Intel had a lead it would hold until the mid-2010s.That wasn’t enough for Intel, though: the company engaged in a plethora of questionable behavior, including creating compilers that only enabled multimedia extensions on Intel chips, but not AMD ones, and paying “loyalty rebates” to OEMs that didn’t use AMD chips, most famously Dell. Intel ended up settling an antitrust lawsuit with AMD in the U.S. and was found guilty of abusing its market position by the European Commission, but by then AMD was severely weakened and, in 2009, spun off its manufacturing.Still, AMD had some big victories along the way, particularly their creation of the x86-64 ISA. This was, as the name suggests, an extension of the x86 ISA for 64-bit computing, but it didn’t come from Intel; Intel, obsessed with getting rid of AMD once-and-for-all, was busy trying to create an entirely new ISA for 64-bit processors called IA-64 (the chip was called Itanium). AMD instead proposed an ISA that had a “compatibility” mode, which meant a 64-bit chip could run 32-bit x86 applications; this approach won for the same reason that Windows beat the Mac, because the ability to run pre-existing software is worth far more than marginal performance improvements. Now Intel was forced to follow AMD and license x86-64 from them, which finally led to a resolution of their legal battles and full cross-license agreements.I recount all of this for three reasons: first, history is fun. Second, Intel’s history of playing dirty is a not insignificant barrier to their need for foundry customers; memories in Silicon Valley run deeper than silicon pipelines. Third, you can understand why an announcement about Intel and AMD collaborating to evolve x86 is a bit a shock. It’s not, however, a surprise.ARM and AIThe single most important driver of this new collaboration, which should lead to an evolution of x86-64 ISA to accomodate new use cases, actually didn’t involve AMD and Intel at all; in 2007 Apple launched the iPhone with an ARM processor. Smartphones faced fundamentally different constraints than PCs, because they operated primarily on battery power, which meant that efficiency mattered more than pure performance.I wrote above that “the ability to run pre-existing software is worth far more than marginal performance improvements”; that was true as long as the overall constraints of the system were the same, but the shift to efficiency as a priority suddenly meant that improvements were not at all marginal, but essential. That, by extension, meant that it was suddenly worth it to undergo the long and arduous process of rewriting every aspect of the software stack for a new ISA.This is a process that has been ongoing for 17 years now, and sufficient progress has been made to expand the ARM market from smartphones to servers and PCs. Yes, Intel and AMD are still competitors, but they are also on the same team when it comes to fending off internally-developed ARM processors in the public clouds (i.e. Amazon’s Graviton line of chips), and Qualcomm and potentially Nvidia in PCs. This is reality on the ground; to put it another way, this new advisory board is simply recognizing that reality. That’s why this announcement is a shock but not a surprise.There are probably other factors as well. Intel’s aforementioned foundry efforts means it behooves the company to be much more of a team player, particularly with the hyperscalers who have the capability and the volume to justify making not just ARM work, but also AMD. There are still slight differences between AMD64 and Intel64, even though both implement the x86-64 ISA, but those differences don’t matter anymore to the largest clouds. If anything AMD’s better performance over the last few years means that Intel needs to be wary of not being the standard. AMD, meanwhile, has always had dramatically weaker software capabilities than Intel; both will benefit from working together to evolve x86 and compete on vectors other than ISA compatibility.Still, this is a notable chapter in a fraught relationship that has helped define tech for 40 years; it also is a chapter that feels closer to the end of the book than the beginning. Looming over everything is AI, which runs not on a different ISA, but on a different kind of chip completely. I guess everyone wants closure at the end.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. After all, “No one ever got fired…”IBM spun up a separate team in Florida to put together something they could sell IT departments. Pressed for time, the Florida team put together a minicomputer using mostly off-the shelf components; IBM’s RISC processors and the OS they had under development were technically superior, but Intel had a CISC processor for sale immediately, and a new company called Microsoft said their OS — DOS — could be ready in six months. For the sake of expediency, IBM decided to go with Intel and Microsoft.The point of that Article is to note that Microsoft’s dominance of PC operating systems actually preceded the Mac; yes Windows came later, but Windows was backwards-compatible with DOS, and DOS was blessed by IBM. This was particularly pertinent in 2013 because everyone was pointing to Windows dominance over the Mac as evidence that Android would come to dominate the iPhone, but in fact their analogy was grounded in a misunderstanding of what actually happened in the early 1980s.Anyhow, the focus on operating systems is why I didn’t bother telling the entire story, which is that while IBM chose Intel to provide the PC’s processor, they were wary of being reliant on a single supplier (it’s notable that IBM didn’t demand the same of the operating system, which was probably a combination of not fully appreciating operating systems as a point of integration and lock-in for 3rd-party software, which barely existed at that point, and a recognition that software is just bits and not a physical good that has to be manufactured). To that end IBM demanded that Intel license its processor to another chip firm, and AMD was the obvious choice: the firm was founded by Jerry Sanders, a Fairchild Semiconductor alum who had worked with Intel’s founders, and specialized in manufacturing licensed chips.The chip used in the IBM PC was the Intel 8088, which was a cheaper 8-bit version of the 16-bit 8086; it’s the 8086 that gives the x86 instruction set architecture (ISA) its name. The ISA defines the lowest level interface between hardware and software; it defines the operations a processor can execute (like arithmetic, logic, etc.), data types, register operation, memory addressing, I/O management, etc. All software has to be decomposed to what is in the ISA; any chip that supports a particular ISA has to be able to execute everything defined in the ISA. In this case Intel came up with a new ISA for the 8086, and has carried that ISA through (with many modifications and additions, to be sure), ever since. That is x86 (ARM, for example, is a different ISA).AMD’s first chip under the IBM-mandated second-sourcing agreement was a clone of the 8088, with basically the exact same performance even though it came out three years later in 1982. Intel, meanwhile, released the 286 in 1982 as well, moving back to a 16-bit processor. It took AMD 2 years to follow with the Am286, but in the meantime AMD had meaningfully improved their manufacturing capability, which meant their clone could operate at 20MHz, a massive jump over Intel’s 12.5MHz. Moreover it was cheaper! This made AMD particularly attractive to the exploding IBM clone market: while the original reverse-engineered Compaq used Intel’s 8088, an increasing number of IBM PC clones gravitated to AMD.Intel saw the clones as an opportunity as well: the company launched its 386 processor with Compaq, not IBM, and declared it would no longer share its designs with AMD; it ended up taking AMD 5 years to reverse-engineer the 386, while the company sought arbitration to force Intel to share its design. In the meantime. Intel was suing companies like Cyrix and NEC for having reverse-engineered the 286. Intel failed to stop Cyrix with a patent lawsuit, and NEC with a copyright lawsuit, but the latter case did state that microcode (which actually implements the commands defined by the ISA) was copyrightable. Intel seized on that and sought an injunction against AMD’s impending Am386 processor; that case was stayed pending the resolution of AMD’s arbitration case, which meant the Am386 launched, and once again it was a great chip. It was faster and cheaper than the 386, and nearly as fast and alotcheaper than the new Intel 486.Finally, in 1995, after years of litigation and tens of millions of dollars of legal fees (there were more cases than what I just noted, including an AMD antitrust suit against Intel), the companies settled: AMD received a perpetual license to the microcode that implemented the x86 ISA in the 386 and 486, but would develop its own chips going foreward. Unfortunately AMD was fairly hobbled by this point, while Intel had reaped massive profits from the 386 and 486, which meant that AMD’s one-time manufacturing advantage was long gone; Intel had a lead it would hold until the mid-2010s.That wasn’t enough for Intel, though: the company engaged in a plethora of questionable behavior, including creating compilers that only enabled multimedia extensions on Intel chips, but not AMD ones, and paying “loyalty rebates” to OEMs that didn’t use AMD chips, most famously Dell. Intel ended up settling an antitrust lawsuit with AMD in the U.S. and was found guilty of abusing its market position by the European Commission, but by then AMD was severely weakened and, in 2009, spun off its manufacturing.Still, AMD had some big victories along the way, particularly their creation of the x86-64 ISA. This was, as the name suggests, an extension of the x86 ISA for 64-bit computing, but it didn’t come from Intel; Intel, obsessed with getting rid of AMD once-and-for-all, was busy trying to create an entirely new ISA for 64-bit processors called IA-64 (the chip was called Itanium). AMD instead proposed an ISA that had a “compatibility” mode, which meant a 64-bit chip could run 32-bit x86 applications; this approach won for the same reason that Windows beat the Mac, because the ability to run pre-existing software is worth far more than marginal performance improvements. Now Intel was forced to follow AMD and license x86-64 from them, which finally led to a resolution of their legal battles and full cross-license agreements.I recount all of this for three reasons: first, history is fun. Second, Intel’s history of playing dirty is a not insignificant barrier to their need for foundry customers; memories in Silicon Valley run deeper than silicon pipelines. Third, you can understand why an announcement about Intel and AMD collaborating to evolve x86 is a bit a shock. It’s not, however, a surprise.ARM and AIThe single most important driver of this new collaboration, which should lead to an evolution of x86-64 ISA to accomodate new use cases, actually didn’t involve AMD and Intel at all; in 2007 Apple launched the iPhone with an ARM processor. Smartphones faced fundamentally different constraints than PCs, because they operated primarily on battery power, which meant that efficiency mattered more than pure performance.I wrote above that “the ability to run pre-existing software is worth far more than marginal performance improvements”; that was true as long as the overall constraints of the system were the same, but the shift to efficiency as a priority suddenly meant that improvements were not at all marginal, but essential. That, by extension, meant that it was suddenly worth it to undergo the long and arduous process of rewriting every aspect of the software stack for a new ISA.This is a process that has been ongoing for 17 years now, and sufficient progress has been made to expand the ARM market from smartphones to servers and PCs. Yes, Intel and AMD are still competitors, but they are also on the same team when it comes to fending off internally-developed ARM processors in the public clouds (i.e. Amazon’s Graviton line of chips), and Qualcomm and potentially Nvidia in PCs. This is reality on the ground; to put it another way, this new advisory board is simply recognizing that reality. That’s why this announcement is a shock but not a surprise.There are probably other factors as well. Intel’s aforementioned foundry efforts means it behooves the company to be much more of a team player, particularly with the hyperscalers who have the capability and the volume to justify making not just ARM work, but also AMD. There are still slight differences between AMD64 and Intel64, even though both implement the x86-64 ISA, but those differences don’t matter anymore to the largest clouds. If anything AMD’s better performance over the last few years means that Intel needs to be wary of not being the standard. AMD, meanwhile, has always had dramatically weaker software capabilities than Intel; both will benefit from working together to evolve x86 and compete on vectors other than ISA compatibility.Still, this is a notable chapter in a fraught relationship that has helped define tech for 40 years; it also is a chapter that feels closer to the end of the book than the beginning. Looming over everything is AI, which runs not on a different ISA, but on a different kind of chip completely.
