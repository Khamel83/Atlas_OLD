He initially believed the request was fake, but later realized the account belonging to the US national security adviser was genuine after the group discussed detailed plans for an attack on the Houthis, a militant group that has carried out numerous attacks on commercial vessels in the Red Sea.Goldberg didn’t publish the actual plans in the article. But he said Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth at one point shared a post that featured “operational details of forthcoming strikes on Yemen, including information about targets, weapons the US would be deploying, and attack sequencing.” Hours later, the attacks went ahead.I think this is a fascinating story with a very clear tech angle. So, up front, let me get the obvious caveats out of the way: yes, this was very stupid, and probably illegal — although the potential illegality is itself an interesting tech story.Signal: The Coke of Secure MessagingStart with Signal: given that Signal is a consumer app, I saw a lot of commentary decrying the obvious lack of security. In fact, I think the opposite is the case: Signal is actually themostsecure messaging app. I summarized the differences between encrypted messaging apps inthis Update about (the not-end-to-end-encrypted) Telegramlast summer:One thing that is important to note here is that there are real advantages that come from not being encrypted. Telegram chats, for example, have their full history easily accessible from any device you sign in with; sync — both in terms of ongoing messages, and history — is much easier (or even viable) when you don’t have to deal with encryption. It is also far more scalable: WhatsApp and Signal only recently increased their group sizes to 1,024 and 1,000 maximum users respectively, while iMessage is capped at 32.This does, as an aside, speak to some minute but important differences in security between the three apps:iMessage’s maximum group size is so small because every message in a group chat is actually sent directly between the sender and every person in the group chat; in other words, when you send a message to a 32 person group chat, you are actually sending 32 separate messages (and potentially more if some of those users are using iMessage on multiple devices). This is also the most secure implementation: there is both forward secrecy (breaking one message does not give you access to past messages) and self-healing (breaking one message gives you access to future messages, until you are off-line for a single message, at which point you have lost the chain).Signal uses what is called client-side fan-out: after an initial back-and-forth exchange with everyone in the group, including the exchange of “send keys” for everyone in the group, you send one message that is individually-encrypted for everyone in the group. This provides forward secrecy, but sacrifices self-healing; however, because the “send keys” are themselves encrypted, there is plausible deniability, in that you don’t know for certain who sent a particular message (Signal does maintain the structure of the group in encrypted form on its servers to maintain a consistent state for all users over time).WhatsApp is the most reliant on a central server, using what is called “server-side fan-out”: (encrypted) messages are sent to a central server (with “send keys” for everyone in the group) and are then distributed by the server to everyone. Part of this process entails maintaining the structure of the group centrally, and while there is a degree of plausible deniability, it does not go as far as Signal does.The actual specifics of how these various systems work are incredibly complex (and my apologies in advance if these simplifications got any details wrong); if you want to understand more I highly recommend these YouTube videos fromComputerphile:How Signal Instant Messaging Protocol Works (& WhatsApp etc)Double Ratchet Messaging EncryptionWhat’s Up With Group Messaging(which includes a quick note at the end that one way to compromise these groups is by automatically adding an additional “silent” member of the group)While iMessage’s design is the most secure (and thus the least scalable), it is closed source; Signal’s apps and protocol, on the other hand, are open source (and there is no server component that needs to be verified). In other words, we have an Andy Warhol Coke scenario here; fromThe Philosophy of Andy Warhol:What’s great about this country is that America started the tradition where the richest consumers buy essentially the same things as the poorest. You can be watching TV and see Coca Cola, and you know that the President drinks Coca Cola, Liz Taylor drinks Coca Cola, and just think, you can drink Coca Cola, too. A coke is a coke and no amount of money can get you a better coke than the one the bum on the corner is drinking. All the cokes are the same and all the cokes are good. Liz Taylor knows it, the President knows it, the bum knows it, and you know it.So it is with encryption: there really isn’t a more secure messaging product than Signal, even if it were designed by the NSA.What is interesting is that last bullet point in my excerpt: one way in which WhatsApp in particular could be compromised is if the server, which orchestrates the chat, were to insert a “silent” participant in the chat when it was established; this silent user, which could be obscured in the user interface (remember, WhatsApp is not open source), would participate in the continual key exchanges that undergird end-to-end encrypted messaging and thus be able to record everything in the chat.The reason I bring this up is that this is kind of what happened (but not exactly). Goldberg was added to the chat, albeit not silently — there was certainly a notification about his addition, and he would have been publicly listed as a chat participant. However, if you read the story, Goldberg was added at the same time a bunch of other participants were added, and it seems no one noticed; that means he effectively operated as a silent participant, and thus saw all of the messages until the time he exited the chat.Transparency Versus SecurityWhat is perhaps the most surprising detail about this episode, however, is that the violation was not about security, but rather transparency. In fact, the official policy of the Department of Defense is that officials uselesssecure means of communication. From a2023 Pentagon memoabout the “Use of Text Messaging on Mobile Devices and Records Management of Electronic Messages”:Effective immediately, when conducting government business DoD users of government-owned mobile devices and non-government-owned devices must use Microsoft Teams Chat for text messaging as the designated fully managed DoD Mobile Enterprise System. For use on government-owned mobile devices, Microsoft Teams Chat will be available as a managed application controlled by an enterprise management system. For use on a non-government owned mobile device, Microsoft Teams Chat will be used in accordance with DoD CIO memorandum of August 10, 2022, “Use of NonGovernment Owned Mobile Devices.” DoD service providers will configure Microsoft 365 to manage records created or received by Microsoft Teams Chat in accordance with applicable approved records schedules. Once configured, records created or received in Microsoft Teams Chat will be managed within the application and no additional copy of the record will be required.When mission needs or the effective conduct of DoD business cannot be adequately supported by Microsoft Teams Chat, SMS texting may be used in accordance with Do DI 8170.01. In such cases, a complete copy of the record must be forwarded to an official DoD electronic messaging account of the user within 20 days of the record’s original creation or transmission in accordance with Section 2911 of Title 44 U.S.C, and Component processes. The complete copy of the record includes the content of the message and required metadata, and the record must be retrievable and usable in compliance with the applicable retention schedule approved by the Archivist of the United States. DoD Component heads shall ensure that DoD users are provided guidance on their Component’s processes for forwarding complete copies of records originating in SMS texts. Additionally, in accordance with Do DI 8170.01 , DoD Component heads may approve the establishment of non-DoD controlled electronic messaging services accounts by authorized users for public communication related to assigned duties (e.g., recruiting) or any other purpose determined necessary and in the interest of DoD.While Microsoft Teams Chat is designated as the primary DoD capability for messaging, DoD Component Heads may approve the use of other messaging capabilities, provided the use of those alternative capabilities complies with DoDI 5015.02, DoDI 8170.01, and applicable DoD CIO memoranda. Service providers should collaborate with Component Records Officers to ensure compliance.Microsoft Teams isnotend-to-end encrypted, which is part of the point: the DoD’s primary concern is not message security but rather record retention. That, obviously, requires that the chat be recordable, which is impossible if the chat is end-to-end encrypted — indeed, that was the point I was making about Telegram in the above excerpt.The recommendation to use SMS as a fallback is particularly notable: SMS is not secure at all. In fact, the problem with theSalt Typhoon hackis that China had the ability to read pretty much every SMS message in America — which is why the U.S. governmentadvised citizens to use end-to-end encrypted apps like Signal!At the same time, there are certain advantages to using a centralized non-end-to-end encrypted solution like Microsoft Teams (Teams content is encrypted-in-transit, but the server has a key and can thus decrypt all conversations). A core capability of all enterprise software is access control; you could set up a chat in Teams that only allows users that have a certain level of classified clearance, for example. This would have made it impossible for Goldberg to join the chat. The problem, probably from the participants’ perspective, is that then they would have had to use Microsoft Teams!And, of course, there is that transparency bit; fromGoldberg’s article:There was another potential problem: Waltz set some of the messages in the Signal group to disappear after one week, and some after four. That raises questions about whether the officials may have violated federal records law: Text messages about official acts are considered records that should be preserved.There is no transparency requirement in the Constitution, and it really wasn’t a norm for the first 190 years of the United States; the first federal law focused on record-retrieval was the Freedom of Information Act of 1966, which was passed by Congress over the stated objections of President Lyndon B. Johnson (Johnson did sign the law which was passed unanimously, and thus veto-proof). In the intervening decades there has been a continuous tussle between the executive branch, which cites security concerns, and transparency advocates who argued that most of those concerns are a fig leaf for a desire to escape accountability.The transparency advocates have largely won that fight; the most pertinent law for this episode is thePresidential and Federal Records Act Amendments of 2014, which established the rule about all executive branch communications, including those conducted via private email, be preserved within 20 days. This has been by-and-large extended to messaging — note the 20 day requirement in the Pentagon memo about SMS — although there have not been any formal laws about messaging.It’s worth considering — completely independently of this case — if the push for transparency has gone too far, particularly in a world of not just email but ubiquitous text messaging. It was one thing to mandate transparency and the preservation of records when writing things down had a marginal cost: you only put things on paper when decisions, which were litigated in person or over the phone by voice, had been made. Today, however, debate is just as likely to happen by text, which in the digital era has no marginal cost; is it actually healthy for officials to know that every single thought or opinion they might have — even if they end up changing their mind ordisagree and commit— must be saved for posterity?This too is a tech angle, and not just in terms of the technology: Microsoft was famously nailed by the Justice Department primarily because most of its internal deliberations happened via email. You could certainly make the case that this is a good thing, particularly if you think a company whose emails are used in court did in fact commit a crime, but you also are creating perverse incentives wherein the larger a company gets the more it is incentivized to dramatically decrease the efficiency of their communication, which often kills debate completely, leading to worse decisions.And, to take this full circle, there are the security considerations: executive branch officials perhaps overstated their case when they wanted official records locked up forever, but are we really sure that we want ongoing conversations to be happening on services that are less secure than Signal? Again, none of this is to dismiss the stupidity of this particular case, but that’s why I find the story so interesting: there are a lot more ramifications beyond one military operation that raise legitimate systemic questions about how the government should operate in the digital age.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. From a2023 Pentagon memoabout the “Use of Text Messaging on Mobile Devices and Records Management of Electronic Messages”: Effective immediately, when conducting government business DoD users of government-owned mobile devices and non-government-owned devices must use Microsoft Teams Chat for text messaging as the designated fully managed DoD Mobile Enterprise System. For use on government-owned mobile devices, Microsoft Teams Chat will be available as a managed application controlled by an enterprise management system. For use on a non-government owned mobile device, Microsoft Teams Chat will be used in accordance with DoD CIO memorandum of August 10, 2022, “Use of NonGovernment Owned Mobile Devices.” DoD service providers will configure Microsoft 365 to manage records created or received by Microsoft Teams Chat in accordance with applicable approved records schedules. Once configured, records created or received in Microsoft Teams Chat will be managed within the application and no additional copy of the record will be required. When mission needs or the effective conduct of DoD business cannot be adequately supported by Microsoft Teams Chat, SMS texting may be used in accordance with Do DI 8170.01. In such cases, a complete copy of the record must be forwarded to an official DoD electronic messaging account of the user within 20 days of the record’s original creation or transmission in accordance with Section 2911 of Title 44 U.S.C, and Component processes. The complete copy of the record includes the content of the message and required metadata, and the record must be retrievable and usable in compliance with the applicable retention schedule approved by the Archivist of the United States. DoD Component heads shall ensure that DoD users are provided guidance on their Component’s processes for forwarding complete copies of records originating in SMS texts. Additionally, in accordance with Do DI 8170.01 , DoD Component heads may approve the establishment of non-DoD controlled electronic messaging services accounts by authorized users for public communication related to assigned duties (e.g., recruiting) or any other purpose determined necessary and in the interest of DoD. While Microsoft Teams Chat is designated as the primary DoD capability for messaging, DoD Component Heads may approve the use of other messaging capabilities, provided the use of those alternative capabilities complies with DoDI 5015.02, DoDI 8170.01, and applicable DoD CIO memoranda. Service providers should collaborate with Component Records Officers to ensure compliance. Microsoft Teams isnotend-to-end encrypted, which is part of the point: the DoD’s primary concern is not message security but rather record retention. That, obviously, requires that the chat be recordable, which is impossible if the chat is end-to-end encrypted — indeed, that was the point I was making about Telegram in the above excerpt. The recommendation to use SMS as a fallback is particularly notable: SMS is not secure at all. In fact, the problem with theSalt Typhoon hackis that China had the ability to read pretty much every SMS message in America — which is why the U.S. governmentadvised citizens to use end-to-end encrypted apps like Signal! At the same time, there are certain advantages to using a centralized non-end-to-end encrypted solution like Microsoft Teams (Teams content is encrypted-in-transit, but the server has a key and can thus decrypt all conversations). A core capability of all enterprise software is access control; you could set up a chat in Teams that only allows users that have a certain level of classified clearance, for example. This would have made it impossible for Goldberg to join the chat. The problem, probably from the participants’ perspective, is that then they would have had to use Microsoft Teams! There was another potential problem: Waltz set some of the messages in the Signal group to disappear after one week, and some after four. That raises questions about whether the officials may have violated federal records law: Text messages about official acts are considered records that should be preserved. There is no transparency requirement in the Constitution, and it really wasn’t a norm for the first 190 years of the United States; the first federal law focused on record-retrieval was the Freedom of Information Act of 1966, which was passed by Congress over the stated objections of President Lyndon B. Johnson (Johnson did sign the law which was passed unanimously, and thus veto-proof). In the intervening decades there has been a continuous tussle between the executive branch, which cites security concerns, and transparency advocates who argued that most of those concerns are a fig leaf for a desire to escape accountability. The transparency advocates have largely won that fight; the most pertinent law for this episode is thePresidential and Federal Records Act Amendments of 2014, which established the rule about all executive branch communications, including those conducted via private email, be preserved within 20 days. This has been by-and-large extended to messaging — note the 20 day requirement in the Pentagon memo about SMS — although there have not been any formal laws about messaging. It’s worth considering — completely independently of this case — if the push for transparency has gone too far, particularly in a world of not just email but ubiquitous text messaging. It was one thing to mandate transparency and the preservation of records when writing things down had a marginal cost: you only put things on paper when decisions, which were litigated in person or over the phone by voice, had been made. Today, however, debate is just as likely to happen by text, which in the digital era has no marginal cost; is it actually healthy for officials to know that every single thought or opinion they might have — even if they end up changing their mind ordisagree and commit— must be saved for posterity? This too is a tech angle, and not just in terms of the technology: Microsoft was famously nailed by the Justice Department primarily because most of its internal deliberations happened via email. You could certainly make the case that this is a good thing, particularly if you think a company whose emails are used in court did in fact commit a crime, but you also are creating perverse incentives wherein the larger a company gets the more it is incentivized to dramatically decrease the efficiency of their communication, which often kills debate completely, leading to worse decisions. And, to take this full circle, there are the security considerations: executive branch officials perhaps overstated their case when they wanted official records locked up forever, but are we really sure that we want ongoing conversations to be happening on services that are less secure than Signal? Again, none of this is to dismiss the stupidity of this particular case, but that’s why I find the story so interesting: there are a lot more ramifications beyond one military operation that raise legitimate systemic questions about how the government should operate in the digital age.
