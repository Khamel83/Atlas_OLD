In other words, these cuts may not necessarily be a sign that the Vision Pro is a massive failure, but they probably are a sign that it is not a massive success, either.This seems obvious anecdotally; Mark Gurman asked inhis Bloomberg newsletter, “Remember the Vision Pro?”, and reported that demos are down in retail stores, which are only moving “a handful in a whole week”. Meanwhile, various developers havereportedonpodcaststhat app usage is extremely low. What is the most puzzling, meanwhile, is the fact that Apple seems to have forgotten about the Vision Pro as well,at least as far as original content is concerned.Meta, obviously, hasn’t forgotten about VR, nor have I, as exemplified by yesterday’s ArticleMeta and Open; still, while that Article used the Horizon OS announcement as a launching point, the topic was really about when being “open” makes sense, using Meta as an example. That’s why I didn’t address the elephant in the room: does “open versus closed” matter if no one cares?In fact, I think it matters a great deal; it may matter so much that I might need to make another challenge to Professor Clayton Christensen’s model of disruption.Modular Products and Defining New MarketsIn 2013’sWhat Clayton Christensen Got Wrong, I attempted to explain why Silicon Valley’s favorite business school professor kept getting Apple wrong.The original theory of disruption, now known as new market disruption, was detailed in Christensen’s seminal paperDisruptive Technologies: Catching the Waveand expanded on in the classic bookThe Innovator’s Dilemma. Based primarily on a detailed study of the disk drive industry, the theory of new market disruption describes how incumbent companies ignore new technologies that don’t serve the needs of their customers or fit within their existing business models. However, as the new technology, which excels on completely different attributes than the incumbent’s product, continues to mature, it eventually takes over the market.This remains an incredibly elegant and powerful theory, and I fully subscribe to it…It is Christensen’s second theory of disruption — low-end disruption — that I believe is flawed. Christensen first described this theory inDisruption, Disintegration and the dissipation of differentiability, and expanded on it inThe Innovator’s Solution. It is this theory that is at the basis of Christensen’s critique of Apple quoted above.Briefly, an integrated approach wins at the beginning of a new market, because it produces a superior product that customers are willing to pay for. However, as a product category matures, even modular products become “good enough” — customers may know that the integrated product has superior features or specs, but they aren’t willing to pay more, and thus the low-priced providers, who build a product from parts with prices ground down by competition, come to own the market. Christensen was sure this would happen with the iPod, and he — and his many adherents — are sure it will happen to the iPhone.You can read my argument in full at that link, but briefly, I argued that:Customers aren’t “rational” in the way that businesses are.Not every attribute that matters to customers — like the user experience — is easily documented and measured.Modular providers can not become “good enough” on all the attributes that matter to the buyers.Notice, though, that I was still buying into Christensen’s overall framing about integrated products winning in the beginning, followed by modular products winning in the end; my argument was basically that Apple’s integrated approach would keep on winning. However, by the time I wrote that Article in September 2013, though, I had already written the Article I quoted from yesterday,The Truth About Windows Versus the Mac, which made the case that Microsoft had already won the PC market before the Mac even showed up! Christensen, I would note, subscribed to the false view of history I was refuting. FromThe Innovator’s Solution:The same sequence occurred in the personal computer wave of disruption. During the early years, Apple Computer — the most integrated company with a proprietary architecture — made by far the best desktop computers. They were easier to use and crashed much less often than computers of modular construction. Ultimately, when the functionality of desktop machines became good enough, IBM’s modular, open-standard architecture became dominant. Apple’s proprietary architecture, which in the not-good-enough circumstance was a competitive strength, became a competitive liability in the more-than-good-enough circumstance. Apple as a consequence was relegated to niche-player status as the growth explosion in personal computers was captured by the nonintegrated providers of modular machines.Again, this is just flat-out wrong: DOS-based IBM computers and “IBM-compatible” DOS-based computers were dominant before the Mac even launched; Windows’ subsequent dominance was directly downstream from being backwards compatible with DOS and the huge software ecosystem that had already been built up before Steve Jobs ever had the first Mac say hello.This, by extension, means that the theory was wrong when it came to PCs: the IBM PC was modular! It had an Intel processor, a Microsoft operation system, a BIOS from IBM, and supported 3rd-party software from the beginning. I suppose you could make the case that customer demand for the PC was driven by integrated solutions that came first, like the Apple II, Commodore, MITS Altair, etc.; maybe a stronger case is that the integrated System/360 had to be built before modular PCs could even be a thing, but at that point you can just go back to the more compelling theory of low end disruption to explain why PCs took over the world.In fact, you could go further in terms of taking on the theory of low-end disruption, and make the case that modularity was in fact essential to the PCs success, as it enabled the exploration of use cases that made the platform popular. I’m not sure if I’d go that far, to be fair: a lot of that exploration had already happened on those integrated products I listed above, but then again, those were modular in the parts that mattered, i.e. they freely allowed 3rd-party software.Smartphones, meanwhile, never needed a reason to exist: they were, first and foremost, cellular phones, which billions of people at the time of the iPhone’s release already owned; the iPhone was “just” a better cellular phone, at least to start, which gave it the runway to become so much more. To that end, I wrote in yesterday’s Article:The headset market is the opposite of the smartphone market: Meta has been at this for a while, and has a much larger developer base than Apple does, particularly in terms of games. It’s not overwhelming like Microsoft’s DOS advantage already was, to be sure, and I’m certainly not counting out Apple, but this also isn’t the smartphone era when Apple had a multi-year head start.In response to the question I posed above — does anyone care about VR? — I would generalize this observation to note that one of the big differences between PCs and smartphones is that PCs had to figure out how to make people care, while people already cared about phones. By extension, the fact that Meta is taking meaningful steps to be more open, while Apple is taking its more integrated and closed approach, arguably makes it more likely that Meta will win, simply because it increases the odds that Meta’s ecosystem figures out how to make people care before Apple does.TikTok AcquirersFromThe Verge:A bill that would force China-based company ByteDance to sell TikTok — or else face a US ban of the platform — is all but certain to become law after the Senate passed a foreign aid package including the measure. It now heads to President Joe Biden, who already committed to signing the TikTok legislation should it make it through both chambers of Congress. The House passed the foreign aid package that includes the TikTok bill on Saturday. Once signed by the president, ByteDance would have up to a year to complete a sale of TikTok or face an effective ban for the platform in the US. The bill gives ByteDance an initial nine months and gives the president discretion to extend it another three should there be progress toward a deal. Still, legal challenges could possibly delay enforcement.I made the case for banning TikTok back in 2020 inThe TikTok War; meanwhile, I suspect there will be plenty of time to get into the upcoming legal fight, what ByteDance will do if they lose that legal fight, and what would actually be entailed in separating TikTok from its parent company. For now, though, let’s assume that the bill is signed, is found to be legal, and that ByteDance decides to sell (i.e. is allowed to do so by the Chinese government, which, well, gets to the reason for the ban in the first place): who should buy it?First off, I assume that most of the consumer Internet giants are out, given that they aren’t allowed to buy anything these days (whichisn’t good for innovation). That’s unfortunate for TikTok as a business, because Meta is obviously the best acquirer if the goal is to maximize profitability; that is also a situation where I clearly side with the regulators!Meanwhile, Oracle is going to be mooted as a potential acquirer, given the fact they are already working with TikTok on storing data outside of China, but that strikes me as a very poor outcome. There is no reason to expect Oracle to be good at running a consumer advertising-based Internet business. Microsoft might again be interested: the company certainly has the motivation to acquire a large consumer Internet business, and they do have an ad business, and could also make the case that AI will be essential to TikTok in the long run; perhaps they should be put down as the favorite.However, I think the two best candidates to acquire TikTok are Amazon and — here comes the ultimate dark horse! — Walmart. The reason comes back to App Tracking Transparency, of all things: the most effective route to effective digital advertising is owning both ad inventory and ad conversion, i.e. the actual point of purchase. This gives you free reign to directly link seeing an ad and buying a product, just like Facebook used to do pre-ATT. In this case, TikTok has the inventory, while Amazon and Walmart have the conversion (Shopify is another possibility, but TikTok may simply be too big to swallow); one company owning both would have an incredibly powerful integration that would make both TikTok and the associated e-commerce business significantly more profitable than they would be on their own.This Update will be available as a podcast later today.
