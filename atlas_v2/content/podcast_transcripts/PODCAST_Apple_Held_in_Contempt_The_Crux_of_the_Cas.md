Epiccase is obviously a huge deal, and about a topic that Stratechery has covered long before it reached mainstream attention.Second, while I anticipate writing more about these developments in the future, there is a specific angle on the latest ruling that I think is being missed by much of the coverage.Third, on Monday, May 12 I will be traveling; I had planned on writing my Update for that day over the preceding weekend, but I figured given points one and two, I might as well do it this weekend (and just take May 12 off). So, uh, yeah, see you on Monday, but not on May 12.So there we are. On to the Update:Apple Held in ContemptFromBloomberg:Apple Inc. violated a court order requiring it to open up the App Store to third-party payment options and must stop charging commissions on purchases outside its software marketplace, a federal judge said in a ruling that referred the company to prosecutors for a possible criminal probe. US District Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers sided Wednesday with Fortnite developer Epic Games Inc. over its allegation that the iPhone maker failed to comply with an order she issued in 2021 after finding the company engaged in anticompetitive conduct in violation of California law. Gonzalez Rogers also referred the case to federal prosecutors to investigate whether Apple committed criminal contempt of court for flouting her 2021 ruling. The US attorney’s office in San Francisco declined to comment.Apple responded in a statement to multiple media outlets:We strongly disagree with the decision. We will comply with the court’s order and we will appeal.Apple updated its developer guidelines Thursday night; from9to5Mac:“The App Review Guidelines have been updated for compliance with a United States court decision regarding buttons, external links, and other calls to action in apps. These changes affect apps distributed on the United States storefront of the App Store,” Apple said in an email to developers on Thursday night.Here are the full changes to the App Store Guidelines with today’s revisions:3.1.1: Apps on the United States storefront are not prohibited from including buttons, external links, or other calls to action when allowing users to browse NFT collections owned by others.3.1.1(a): On the United States storefront, there is no prohibition on an app including buttons, external links, or other calls to action, and no entitlement is required to do so.3.1.3: The prohibition on encouraging users to use a purchasing method other than in-app purchase does not apply on the United States storefront.3.1.3(a): The External Link Account entitlement is not required for apps on the United States storefront to include buttons, external links, or other calls to action.Epic says thatFortnite is coming back to the App Store, butJohn Gruber is rightthat there is nothing in this decision that says that is the case; in fact,the original decision, which washeld up on appeal, confirmed that Epic violated its license agreement and remains banned.What is allowed is for apps to link out to the web to complete transactions, using the method, language, and links of their choosing; they do still have to include an in-app purchase option, but can, in light of this ruling, bury that on some other screen. Spotify, for one, saysit has already submitted an app update implementing the ruling:Yesterday, a U.S. federal judge ordered Apple to stop its illegal behavior and loosen its iron grip on its App Store, finding that the company was in “willful violation” of a 2021 injunction. This means that Apple will now have to stop restricting developers’ ability to communicate with their customers free of charge. While other governments around the world have taken steps against Apple’s harmful practices, this is, by far, the most consequential action to date—and it delivers the benefits that all consumers deserve around the world.This is a great day for Spotify users in the U.S. We have just submitted a Spotify app update to Apple, which includes certain features we can now bring to our American users. Once Apple approves our update, U.S. consumers:Can finally see how much something costs in our app, including pricing details on subscriptions and information about promotions that will save money;Can click a link to purchase the subscription of choice, upgrading from a Free account to one of our Premium plans;Can seamlessly click the link and easily change Premium subscriptions from Individual to a Student, Duo, or Family plan;Can use other payment options beyond just Apple’s payment system—we provide a wider range of options on our website; andGoing forward, this opens the door to other seamless buying opportunities that will directly benefit creators (think easy-to-purchase audiobooks)If all of this seems obvious and user-friendly, you’re right, and we agree — these are the kinds of improvements that any app should offer its users. The fact that we haven’t been able to deliver these basic services, which were permitted by the judge’s order four years ago, is absurd. The ruling made it clear that Apple deliberately abused its market power to intentionally harm others and benefit only itself. But thanks to this important ruling in the case between Apple and Epic Games yesterday, this should change “effective immediately” — those are the judge’s words.I do, as long-time readers know, completely agree with Spotify: Apple should have allowed links to the web for purchase a long time ago, particularly for non-game apps, both because it was the right thing to do, and also because the company was running the risk of something exactly like this happening: losing control on terms that were dictated to it by a regulator or a judge.Needless to say,almost everyone agrees with me, and is excited about this ruling. I suspect, however, that Apple may have more success on appeal than people think; basically, Apple acted so poorly and with such spite that I think Judge Gonzalez Rogers ruled out of righteous anger, and made at least one critical error.The Crux of the CaseJudge Gonzales Rogers latest ruling ishere, and is worth a read; let’s be clear about one thing: Apple acted badly, and the evidence that at least one executive perjured himself is compelling. Specifically, Judge Gonzales Rogers makes a compelling case that Apple’s decision-making in terms of how they might abide by her order to allow links to web-based payment alternatives was motivated by an attempt to limit competition, and that the company’s stated justifications were createdpost hoc— and that an Apple executive explicitly lied about that fact.However — and this is where I think Gonzales Rogers erred — it does not necessarily follow that Apple’s stated justifications were in fact invalid. I’m going to quote extensively from a section entitled “More Specific Findings of Civil Contempt”; I will omit references for readability, but will add back at least one critical footnote later in the Update:As described above, Apple assessed the external costs developers face when utilizing linked-out transactions, which generally ranged based on the size of the developer—the larger the developer, the better able to reduce the external costs of linked-out transactions. With that information, Apple selected a 3% discount on its 30% IAP commission that it knew was anticompetitive. In doing so, Apple willfully set a commission rate that in practice made all alternatives to IAP economically non-viable. The Court cannot conceive of how any reasonable mind interpreting this Court’s and the Ninth Circuit’s orders would find that structure permissible, because it forecloses competitive alternatives. That appears to have been the point. Business documents reveal that the internal justification was to maintain the existing anticompetitive revenue stream.This Court previously recognized that “[e]ven in the absence of IAP, Apple could still charge a commission on developers.” Apple was tasked with valuing its intellectual property, not with reverse engineering a number right under 30% that would allow it to maintain its anticompetitive revenue stream. Apple and Mr. Schiller knew this. Apple ignored this opportunity choosing instead to retroactively justify the desired end result.The AG report’s recommendation of a commission rate on link-out transactions as the basis for its commission determination is entirely manufactured, and Apple’s reliance thereon is a sham. As the 2025 hearing revealed, the AG’s report did not materially factor into Apple’s decision-making process. It was created as a show piece for the Court. The plan backfired.The real-time business documents which the Court ordered Apple to produce revealed the true business decision. Apple’s focus was on the impacts to its revenue as well as some analysis of the costs developers would face when implementing a link-out transaction. The AG report only appears in a public-facing presentation for the July 5, 2023 meeting where Apple decided to impose a 27% commission. Thus, the Court does not credit the AG report as support for a bottoms-up analysis of the value of Apple’s intellectual property.This right here is the crux of the entire case, both in terms of this decision and almost certainly Apple’s appeal. As I noted, Judge Gonzales Rogers makes a compelling case that Apple only created an internal evaluation of the value of its intellectual property after it had already decided to charge a 27% commission for purchases made outside of the App Store; given that, it’s understandable that the judge dismissed Apple’s justification and held the company in contempt. The problem is that by throwing out Apple’s implementation and declaring a free-for-all, the Judge implicitly declared that the value of Apple’s intellectual property was zero, and/or simply “took” Apple’s intellectual property.The Takings ClauseMy use of the word “took” is deliberate, because I am referring to theTakings Clauseof the 5th Amendment (the clause is emphasized):No person shall be held to answer for a capital, or otherwise infamous crime, unless on a presentment or indictment of a Grand Jury, except in cases arising in the land or naval forces, or in the Militia, when in actual service in time of War or public danger; nor shall any person be subject for the same offence to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb; nor shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself, nor be deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law;nor shall private property be taken for public use, without just compensation.It seems to me that Judge Gonzales Rogers just did exactly that: her latest ruling basically says that companies like Spotify are entitled to iOS APIs and App Store distribution without having to pay Apple anything.If this sounds like a new addition to the case, it is! The Takings Clause has not come up in any previous litigation precisely because Judge Gonzales Rogers’ original opinion acknowledged that Apple had the right to charge a commission; the issue under the California Unfair Competition Law was that Apple’s commission was much higher than was justified precisely because Apple foreclosed competition. To that end, what would have made much more sense would have been if Judge Gonzales Rogers lowered Apple’s proposed 27% commission to something significantly lower; to simply wipe it out completely is what prompts this discussion.Judge Gonzales Rogers did anticipate this point in her opinions; I mentioned a critical footnote earlier, which I will quote now (the footnote was inserted after “This Court previously recognized that…Apple could still charge a commission on developers.”):Apple emphasizes that California courts have declined to use the UCL as a ratemaking tool. (“[T]he control of charges, if it be desirable, is better accomplished by statute or by regulation authorized by statute than by ad hoc decisions of the courts.”). Apple also argues that the question of whether Apple’s commission appropriately reflects the value of its intellectual property is not an issue for injunction compliance, and that it is legitimate for a business to promote the value of its corporation for stockholders. Apple misses the point. The issue is that Apple flouted the Court’s order by designing a top-down anticompetitive system, in which its commission played a fundamental role.For the same reasons, the Court disagrees that requiring Apple to set a commission of zero constitutes and unconstitutional taking. For instance, as described infra Section IV, in the trademark context, “a party who has once infringed is allowed less leniency for purposes of injunction enforcement than an innocent party.” Apple does not have an absolute right to the intellectual property that it wields as a shield to competition without adequate justification of its value. Apple was provided with an opportunity to value that intellectual property and chose not to do so.This is correct: Apple’s intellectual property evaluation appears to have been a sham; that doesn’t, however, mean that its intellectual property isn’t valuable, and that Judge Gonzales Rogers isn’t taking it. Again, I do agree that Apple, given the benefits that accrue to it for owning a platform, would in fact make its valuable intellectual property available for free like Microsoft did back in the day; I also agree that Apple should have put forward an honest evaluation of its intellectual property value and used that to build a new commission number, instead of backing into a number that justified 27%. This is a company acting badly!At the same time, I strongly suspect it will fall to the Appeals Court — and perhaps the Supreme Court — to decide if a federal judge, using a state law, can compel permanent commercial access to proprietary infrastructure — backed by intellectual property — without paying for it, simply because the company acted duplicitously. Apple’s rate may be anticompetitive; it may also be the case that setting it to zero is equally illegal.There are a number of pertinent Supreme Court precedents that have bearing on this argument:The controlling precedent for the Takings Clause isPenn Central Transportation Co. v. New York City; this, in contrast to the bright line precedent ofLucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council, considers cases where the taking is only partial, not total. In this case, Apple still owns its IP, and can still monetize it through things like the developer fee, in-app purchases, etc.; however, Apple will argue that (1) the economic impact, (2) interference with investment-backed expectations, and (3) character of the government action will render this partial taking of the right to monetize IP used for web-based transactions illegal.Horne v. Department of Agricultureis, funnily enough, a case about raisins; the principle that applies, however, is that the Takings Clause does not just apply to land, but all private possessions (which includes intellectual property).Ruckelshaus v. Monsanto Co.held that forced disclosure of trade secrets (in this case, for regulatory approval) is a taking; this makes clear that the takings clause applies to something that is intangible and proprietary like intellectual property.Cedar Point Nursery v. Hassidheld that compelled access — even if it doesn’t result in compelled divesture — constitutes taking. In this case, just because Apple’s intellectual property is non-rivalrous doesn’t mean the company isn’t harmed by being forced to grant access to it.Apple is not guaranteed to win on this point;Penn Centralis a balancing test. Specifically:The economic impact on Apple must be weighed against the public interest the Court is enforcing—namely, California’s regulation of commercial conduct under the UCL.Apple should have been prepared for a decision like this and making decisions accordingly, both because of the anticompetitive justifications it was using internally, and also the long-running investigations into its business practices around the App Store.The government isn’t taking anything from Apple that Apple wouldn’t have given away anyways, it’s just taking the very reasonable step of stopping Apple from muzzling developers from informing users — to the users’ benefit! — about other payment options.Moreover, if Apple were to win, Congress would still have latitude to pass a law regulating commissions; the Courts are much more likely to defer to Congress’ right to balance the common good against Apple’s interests than they may be to balance a district judge’s unilateral decision.One other decision to keep in mind isQualcomm v. FTC; this was a case under U.S. antitrust law, and so not necessarily applicable to this case, which falls under California law; it was, however, decided by the ninth circuit, which fell firmly in favor of the right of a vertically integrated player to license their property on whatever terms they wished.Again, I share the general sentiment people have about this case:finally. When it comes to the law, however, details matter, and Apple still has cards to play. And, I would hasten to add, they also don’t have to play them: this anti-steering prohibition has been the single worst thing about the App Store for years; Apple might think it has the legal justification to hold onto it, but its refusal to consider the politics of its stridency is going to catch up to it at some point, and may already have.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. Thus, the Court does not credit the AG report as support for a bottoms-up analysis of the value of Apple’s intellectual property.This right here is the crux of the entire case, both in terms of this decision and almost certainly Apple’s appeal. As I noted, Judge Gonzales Rogers makes a compelling case that Apple only created an internal evaluation of the value of its intellectual property after it had already decided to charge a 27% commission for purchases made outside of the App Store; given that, it’s understandable that the judge dismissed Apple’s justification and held the company in contempt. The problem is that by throwing out Apple’s implementation and declaring a free-for-all, the Judge implicitly declared that the value of Apple’s intellectual property was zero, and/or simply “took” Apple’s intellectual property.The Takings ClauseMy use of the word “took” is deliberate, because I am referring to theTakings Clauseof the 5th Amendment (the clause is emphasized):No person shall be held to answer for a capital, or otherwise infamous crime, unless on a presentment or indictment of a Grand Jury, except in cases arising in the land or naval forces, or in the Militia, when in actual service in time of War or public danger; nor shall any person be subject for the same offence to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb; nor shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself, nor be deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law;nor shall private property be taken for public use, without just compensation.It seems to me that Judge Gonzales Rogers just did exactly that: her latest ruling basically says that companies like Spotify are entitled to iOS APIs and App Store distribution without having to pay Apple anything.If this sounds like a new addition to the case, it is! The Takings Clause has not come up in any previous litigation precisely because Judge Gonzales Rogers’ original opinion acknowledged that Apple had the right to charge a commission; the issue under the California Unfair Competition Law was that Apple’s commission was much higher than was justified precisely because Apple foreclosed competition. To that end, what would have made much more sense would have been if Judge Gonzales Rogers lowered Apple’s proposed 27% commission to something significantly lower; to simply wipe it out completely is what prompts this discussion.Judge Gonzales Rogers did anticipate this point in her opinions; I mentioned a critical footnote earlier, which I will quote now (the footnote was inserted after “This Court previously recognized that…Apple could still charge a commission on developers.”):Apple emphasizes that California courts have declined to use the UCL as a ratemaking tool. (“[T]he control of charges, if it be desirable, is better accomplished by statute or by regulation authorized by statute than by ad hoc decisions of the courts.”). Apple also argues that the question of whether Apple’s commission appropriately reflects the value of its intellectual property is not an issue for injunction compliance, and that it is legitimate for a business to promote the value of its corporation for stockholders. Apple misses the point. The issue is that Apple flouted the Court’s order by designing a top-down anticompetitive system, in which its commission played a fundamental role.For the same reasons, the Court disagrees that requiring Apple to set a commission of zero constitutes and unconstitutional taking. Apple was provided with an opportunity to value that intellectual property and chose not to do so.This is correct: Apple’s intellectual property evaluation appears to have been a sham; that doesn’t, however, mean that its intellectual property isn’t valuable, and that Judge Gonzales Rogers isn’t taking it. Again, I do agree that Apple, given the benefits that accrue to it for owning a platform, would in fact make its valuable intellectual property available for free like Microsoft did back in the day; I also agree that Apple should have put forward an honest evaluation of its intellectual property value and used that to build a new commission number, instead of backing into a number that justified 27%. This is a company acting badly!At the same time, I strongly suspect it will fall to the Appeals Court — and perhaps the Supreme Court — to decide if a federal judge, using a state law, can compel permanent commercial access to proprietary infrastructure — backed by intellectual property — without paying for it, simply because the company acted duplicitously. Apple’s rate may be anticompetitive; it may also be the case that setting it to zero is equally illegal.There are a number of pertinent Supreme Court precedents that have bearing on this argument:The controlling precedent for the Takings Clause isPenn Central Transportation Co. v. New York City; this, in contrast to the bright line precedent ofLucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council, considers cases where the taking is only partial, not total. In this case, Apple still owns its IP, and can still monetize it through things like the developer fee, in-app purchases, etc.; however, Apple will argue that (1) the economic impact, (2) interference with investment-backed expectations, and (3) character of the government action will render this partial taking of the right to monetize IP used for web-based transactions illegal.Horne v. Department of Agricultureis, funnily enough, a case about raisins; the principle that applies, however, is that the Takings Clause does not just apply to land, but all private possessions (which includes intellectual property).Ruckelshaus v. Monsanto Co.held that forced disclosure of trade secrets (in this case, for regulatory approval) is a taking; this makes clear that the takings clause applies to something that is intangible and proprietary like intellectual property.Cedar Point Nursery v. Hassidheld that compelled access — even if it doesn’t result in compelled divesture — constitutes taking. In this case, just because Apple’s intellectual property is non-rivalrous doesn’t mean the company isn’t harmed by being forced to grant access to it.Apple is not guaranteed to win on this point;Penn Centralis a balancing test. Specifically:The economic impact on Apple must be weighed against the public interest the Court is enforcing—namely, California’s regulation of commercial conduct under the UCL.Apple should have been prepared for a decision like this and making decisions accordingly, both because of the anticompetitive justifications it was using internally, and also the long-running investigations into its business practices around the App Store.The government isn’t taking anything from Apple that Apple wouldn’t have given away anyways, it’s just taking the very reasonable step of stopping Apple from muzzling developers from informing users — to the users’ benefit! — about other payment options.Moreover, if Apple were to win, Congress would still have latitude to pass a law regulating commissions; the Courts are much more likely to defer to Congress’ right to balance the common good against Apple’s interests than they may be to balance a district judge’s unilateral decision.One other decision to keep in mind isQualcomm v.
