That, though, is the risk of only providing one answer for the entire world.Ten Blue LinksThere is another point that occurred to me while recording another episode of Sharp Tech this morning (which will be released in a few hours): the Aggregator risks I wrote about yesterday are the exact same risks Google faces from a business model perspective.As long as Google was providing ten blue links, it could serve everyone, no matter their values; after all, the user decided which link to click.By providing ads alongside those ten blue links, Google deputized the end user to choose the winner of the auction it conducted amongst advertisers; those advertisers would pay a premium to win that auction because their potential customer was affirmatively choosing to go to their site, which both meant a higher chance of conversion and the possibility of building a long-term relationship that increased lifetime value.AI threatens both:Because AI reduces supply to one answer, which, if the subject rests on subjective values, can never satisfy everyone, Google risks upsetting a significant portion of demand, weakening its Aggregator position and potentially decreasing its leverage on costs.Because AI gives an answer instead of links, there is no organic place to put the auction decision; yes, Google could have affiliate links or put some ads alongside the AI answer, but both options are likely less attractive to advertisers and may command less of a premium.I’m reminded of that classic xkcd cartoon entitledDependency:Is this the Google version?This is perhaps going too far, but I’m reminded of an observation I made in 2019’sThe Value Chain Constraint:It is understandable why the Internet giants in particular move into seemingly adjacent territories: the growth imperative is strong, both for financial and strategic reasons, and the technology seems easy enough, particularly given the resources these companies bring to bear. And yet, the truth is that those massive resources do not stem, at least in the long run, from technical excellence, but rather integration in specific value chains that produces positive feedback loops and outsize profits.It follows, then, that without that integration, the positive feedback loops quickly disappear, along with the profits, which is the exact pattern we see again and again. Microsoft spent billions on phones and consumer Internet services, Amazon spent billions on Whole Foods, Google has spent billions on not just Google Cloud but a whole host of initiatives that have nothing to do with Search, Facebook has spent billions on Watch and VR, and now Apple is getting in the game with billions spent on Video, and the expected outcome of all these should be that they will fail.To be sure, failure takes time: these companies do have nearly unlimited resources thanks to their core business models, and the reckless optimism bred by structural success. And, I suppose, sometimes they can actually push products across the line to profitability, kind of. Bing, for example is profitable — if you exclude traffic acquisition costs, which makes my point.The reality is that technology has an amplification effect on business models: it has raised the Internet giants to unprecedented heights, and their positions in their relevant markets — or, more accurately, value chains — are nearly impregnable. At the same time, I suspect their ability to extend out horizontally into entirely different ways of doing business — new value chains — even if those businesses rely on similar technology, are more limited than they appear.Instead of Bing I might have used Google Cloud:it’s finally profitable(or at least the broader enterprise division, which includes Google Workspace, is), but at margins that don’t even come close to Google’s core business. Good on Google for sticking to it, but the long slog speaks to how hard it is for highly tuned and highly scaled business models like Search to extend to new areas. The big question about AI is if it is close enough to Search that Google can fold it in to its current business model, or if the shift away from ten blue links ends up breaking the entire thing.The Complicity FrameworkThis last section was actually written by a friend; he asked me to give it a once-over, and I thought it was worth including in Stratechery (with permission, of course), particularly in the context of yesterday’s Article. In yesterday’s conclusion I suggested that the best approach for Google and Meta was a personalized AI that was tuned using the same data used for personalized advertising. This is another solution to some of the same issues, particularly questions around “safety”.The AI safety debate is currently unproductive because its focus is on the wrong actor: AI. In fact, every AI interaction involves two actors: the AI and the human prompting the AI. The way to solve the “AI safety debate” is to render it moot by shifting perceived culpability away from AI to humans.To illustrate the spectrum over which this shift occurs, consider the following unpleasant example (chosen because it is politically neutral — everyone hates it!): the creation of an image of a bunny rabbit being tortured.Scenario 1: A user creates the image in MS Paint or Adobe Photoshop by handNo one would blame Microsoft or Adobe if a user spent hours creating our unfortunate picture using MS Paint or Photoshop. The perceived culpability would be completely with the artist.Scenario 2: A user generates a bunny rabbit using AI and then depicts torture in MS Paint or Adobe Photoshop by handThis picture takes much less time, and is probably of higher quality: the user might first ask AI to draw a bunny with realistic fur and features; then the user asks AI to create a realistic pair of pincers. After that the artist uses Photoshop to combine the two images to depict torture.This image is almost entirely AI generated, and yet I suspect there would be no AI safety issue: this scenario would be viewed similarly to the first one, in that the perceived culpability would be with the user despite the fact almost the entire image is generated by AI.Moreover, I think this perceived culpability would remain with the user no matter how high fidelity the AI becomes (i.e. a movie instead of an image); yes, there may be more complaints on the margins, but any reasonable critic would understand the user volition required to make our sordid image.There are two analogies that explain why this is my expectation: first, 3D printers can be used to create guns, but in fact are used for making literally anything else ~99.99% of the time; reasonable people accept that bad people will do bad things, but that is not worth banning the tool entirely. The second analogy is even closer to home: the Internet itself. There are obviously bad things that can happen when communities come together, but we have accepted as a society that the benefits of widespread connectivity outweigh the fact said connectivity could be leveraged for evil ends.Scenario 3: A user enters the prompt “a bunny being tortured” into Dall-E and gets an image of a bunny being tortured.I hope you bulked up your security, because the gate is being stormed in the morning! Obviously this is the fault of AI: how dare you release such evil upon the land?I’m obviously being hyperbolic, but only to a point: the mistake companies are making is completely removing user agency from the creation process, which means the perceived culpability is completely with the AI. Yes, the user is technically at fault because they are the one that entered the prompt, but text-to-image represents a level of abstraction that is a step change in perception.My strong suspicion, as illustrated by the tortured bunny example, is that increasing the number of user actions shifts the perceived culpability from the AI to the user.To make another analogy, consider the jurisprudence around entrapment: if a police officer hands out free heroin samples and then arrests the buyers it is clear entrapment and ruled unjust. The legal concept undergirding entrapment is that a legitimate arrest requires “the perpetrator’s natural disposition to commit the crime and not be induced by social pressure” (Sherman vs United Statesprovides a good example). However, that doesn’t mean entrapment can’t be legal: successful entrapment cases rely on the criminal consistently choosing the illegal path of their own free will. The key criteria for a successful prosecution is the number of individual discrete unprompted actions.If we apply this framework to AI, then perceived culpability moves from the AI to the user when the user performs a number of discrete unprompted actions that establish it is his/her will to create the end product.More speculatively, underlying the “natural predisposition” concept relied on in entrapment is an idea of the amount of work done. Successful entrapment cases appear to rely on the perpetrator having independently done a lot of work or undertaken effort to commit the crime (cross-referencing this conceptually to the legal concept of “premeditated” also provide oblique support for this line of reasoning). Likewise, it is clear that we do not blame an undergraduate text in chemistry for facilitating bomb-making, even though it contains all the information required, even as we take down websites (or AIs!) that repackage the exact same information in a simple-to-digest format. It turns out that effort — either on the part of the perpetrator or the facilitator — matter in our moral calculus around culpability.In summary, there are two drivers of complicity: 1. User choice and 2. User effort. As the total set of (1) and (2) increases, perceived culpability shifts from the AI (and its creator) to the end user.Applying the FrameworkThe practical application of this framework is that all AI tools (LLM, image-gen or others) need to ensure user complicity as they move from uncontroversial results to those that some sections of society might find unacceptable. Essentially they need to make the user clearly CHOOSE settings that enable bad content and then the users need to WORK HARD to get those outcomesIt is beyond the scope of this document to brainstorm UI and workflow mockups, although that speaks to the key point: this isn’t a model problem; it’s a product problem. Instead of relying solely on RLHF (with all the drawbacks therein), the UI and flow of the product should require user complicity to make the results more controversial. If the user has had to toggle many different settings, and now has a UI that, due to those toggle changes, displays explicit warnings, then the perceived culpability is different. Indeed, one could go even further by making the user have to “work at it” by requiring complex commands or by explicitly selecting one of multiple results that has an NSFW tag to get to their end goal.Another solution would be to make the user perform a thin RLHF overlay on the AI; the user might be prompted to answer a series of questions if they want access to less vanilla results that make it “my AI”. Again, by involving the user the moral onus moves: if I can make the AI say something inappropriate it is a “gotcha” I can post on social media (Gemini suffered from this). Conversely if it is My AI that I helped train (and visually branded as such), posting a screenshot of something inappropriate says something about me and my choices; in this case induced shame is an attractive product quality.ConclusionAttempting to set a particular boundary of safety is impossible given the heterogeneous nature of nation states, societies and political groups within those nation states and the fact that morality is dynamic (it changes every decade and the “past is a foreign country”). Moreover, the risk is not one-sided: people will be upset no matter what rules you place on the model; the only question is what set of people.This is a significant business risk.The correct framework is to move culpability onto the user via product design decisions that means the user is not “entrapped” by the AI but can only move to more controversial results via the application of choice and effort.My thanks to my anonymous friend! I think this is good advice, and not just because no one wants bad PR. Overly constricting AI is like any other form of censorship: sure, you may stop a lot of the bad stuff you don’t want, but you will also stop good stuff, particularly new and innovative things that are outside the norm, and which represent some of the biggest potential upsides of AI. It makes sense to me that the solution is not to treat users like children, but let them make choices in a way that makes it clear to everyone that AI is a tool, not an oracle.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. In yesterday’s conclusion I suggested that the best approach for Google and Meta was a personalized AI that was tuned using the same data used for personalized advertising. This is another solution to some of the same issues, particularly questions around “safety”.The AI safety debate is currently unproductive because its focus is on the wrong actor: AI. In fact, every AI interaction involves two actors: the AI and the human prompting the AI. The way to solve the “AI safety debate” is to render it moot by shifting perceived culpability away from AI to humans.To illustrate the spectrum over which this shift occurs, consider the following unpleasant example (chosen because it is politically neutral — everyone hates it!): the creation of an image of a bunny rabbit being tortured.Scenario 1: A user creates the image in MS Paint or Adobe Photoshop by handNo one would blame Microsoft or Adobe if a user spent hours creating our unfortunate picture using MS Paint or Photoshop. The perceived culpability would be completely with the artist.Scenario 2: A user generates a bunny rabbit using AI and then depicts torture in MS Paint or Adobe Photoshop by handThis picture takes much less time, and is probably of higher quality: the user might first ask AI to draw a bunny with realistic fur and features; then the user asks AI to create a realistic pair of pincers. After that the artist uses Photoshop to combine the two images to depict torture.This image is almost entirely AI generated, and yet I suspect there would be no AI safety issue: this scenario would be viewed similarly to the first one, in that the perceived culpability would be with the user despite the fact almost the entire image is generated by AI.Moreover, I think this perceived culpability would remain with the user no matter how high fidelity the AI becomes (i.e. a movie instead of an image); yes, there may be more complaints on the margins, but any reasonable critic would understand the user volition required to make our sordid image.There are two analogies that explain why this is my expectation: first, 3D printers can be used to create guns, but in fact are used for making literally anything else ~99.99% of the time; reasonable people accept that bad people will do bad things, but that is not worth banning the tool entirely. The second analogy is even closer to home: the Internet itself. There are obviously bad things that can happen when communities come together, but we have accepted as a society that the benefits of widespread connectivity outweigh the fact said connectivity could be leveraged for evil ends.Scenario 3: A user enters the prompt “a bunny being tortured” into Dall-E and gets an image of a bunny being tortured.I hope you bulked up your security, because the gate is being stormed in the morning! Obviously this is the fault of AI: how dare you release such evil upon the land?I’m obviously being hyperbolic, but only to a point: the mistake companies are making is completely removing user agency from the creation process, which means the perceived culpability is completely with the AI. Yes, the user is technically at fault because they are the one that entered the prompt, but text-to-image represents a level of abstraction that is a step change in perception.My strong suspicion, as illustrated by the tortured bunny example, is that increasing the number of user actions shifts the perceived culpability from the AI to the user.To make another analogy, consider the jurisprudence around entrapment: if a police officer hands out free heroin samples and then arrests the buyers it is clear entrapment and ruled unjust. The legal concept undergirding entrapment is that a legitimate arrest requires “the perpetrator’s natural disposition to commit the crime and not be induced by social pressure” (Sherman vs United Statesprovides a good example). However, that doesn’t mean entrapment can’t be legal: successful entrapment cases rely on the criminal consistently choosing the illegal path of their own free will. The key criteria for a successful prosecution is the number of individual discrete unprompted actions.If we apply this framework to AI, then perceived culpability moves from the AI to the user when the user performs a number of discrete unprompted actions that establish it is his/her will to create the end product.More speculatively, underlying the “natural predisposition” concept relied on in entrapment is an idea of the amount of work done. Successful entrapment cases appear to rely on the perpetrator having independently done a lot of work or undertaken effort to commit the crime (cross-referencing this conceptually to the legal concept of “premeditated” also provide oblique support for this line of reasoning). Likewise, it is clear that we do not blame an undergraduate text in chemistry for facilitating bomb-making, even though it contains all the information required, even as we take down websites (or AIs!) that repackage the exact same information in a simple-to-digest format. It turns out that effort — either on the part of the perpetrator or the facilitator — matter in our moral calculus around culpability.In summary, there are two drivers of complicity: 1. User choice and 2. User effort. As the total set of (1) and (2) increases, perceived culpability shifts from the AI (and its creator) to the end user.Applying the FrameworkThe practical application of this framework is that all AI tools (LLM, image-gen or others) need to ensure user complicity as they move from uncontroversial results to those that some sections of society might find unacceptable. Essentially they need to make the user clearly CHOOSE settings that enable bad content and then the users need to WORK HARD to get those outcomesIt is beyond the scope of this document to brainstorm UI and workflow mockups, although that speaks to the key point: this isn’t a model problem; it’s a product problem. Instead of relying solely on RLHF (with all the drawbacks therein), the UI and flow of the product should require user complicity to make the results more controversial. If the user has had to toggle many different settings, and now has a UI that, due to those toggle changes, displays explicit warnings, then the perceived culpability is different. Indeed, one could go even further by making the user have to “work at it” by requiring complex commands or by explicitly selecting one of multiple results that has an NSFW tag to get to their end goal.Another solution would be to make the user perform a thin RLHF overlay on the AI; the user might be prompted to answer a series of questions if they want access to less vanilla results that make it “my AI”. Again, by involving the user the moral onus moves: if I can make the AI say something inappropriate it is a “gotcha” I can post on social media (Gemini suffered from this). Conversely if it is My AI that I helped train (and visually branded as such), posting a screenshot of something inappropriate says something about me and my choices; in this case induced shame is an attractive product quality.ConclusionAttempting to set a particular boundary of safety is impossible given the heterogeneous nature of nation states, societies and political groups within those nation states and the fact that morality is dynamic (it changes every decade and the “past is a foreign country”). Moreover, the risk is not one-sided: people will be upset no matter what rules you place on the model; the only question is what set of people.This is a significant business risk.The correct framework is to move culpability onto the user via product design decisions that means the user is not “entrapped” by the AI but can only move to more controversial results via the application of choice and effort.My thanks to my anonymous friend! This is another solution to some of the same issues, particularly questions around “safety”.The AI safety debate is currently unproductive because its focus is on the wrong actor: AI. In fact, every AI interaction involves two actors: the AI and the human prompting the AI. The way to solve the “AI safety debate” is to render it moot by shifting perceived culpability away from AI to humans.To illustrate the spectrum over which this shift occurs, consider the following unpleasant example (chosen because it is politically neutral — everyone hates it!): the creation of an image of a bunny rabbit being tortured.Scenario 1: A user creates the image in MS Paint or Adobe Photoshop by handNo one would blame Microsoft or Adobe if a user spent hours creating our unfortunate picture using MS Paint or Photoshop. After that the artist uses Photoshop to combine the two images to depict torture.This image is almost entirely AI generated, and yet I suspect there would be no AI safety issue: this scenario would be viewed similarly to the first one, in that the perceived culpability would be with the user despite the fact almost the entire image is generated by AI.Moreover, I think this perceived culpability would remain with the user no matter how high fidelity the AI becomes (i.e. a movie instead of an image); yes, there may be more complaints on the margins, but any reasonable critic would understand the user volition required to make our sordid image.There are two analogies that explain why this is my expectation: first, 3D printers can be used to create guns, but in fact are used for making literally anything else ~99.99% of the time; reasonable people accept that bad people will do bad things, but that is not worth banning the tool entirely. The second analogy is even closer to home: the Internet itself. There are obviously bad things that can happen when communities come together, but we have accepted as a society that the benefits of widespread connectivity outweigh the fact said connectivity could be leveraged for evil ends.Scenario 3: A user enters the prompt “a bunny being tortured” into Dall-E and gets an image of a bunny being tortured.I hope you bulked up your security, because the gate is being stormed in the morning! Obviously this is the fault of AI: how dare you release such evil upon the land?I’m obviously being hyperbolic, but only to a point: the mistake companies are making is completely removing user agency from the creation process, which means the perceived culpability is completely with the AI. Yes, the user is technically at fault because they are the one that entered the prompt, but text-to-image represents a level of abstraction that is a step change in perception.My strong suspicion, as illustrated by the tortured bunny example, is that increasing the number of user actions shifts the perceived culpability from the AI to the user.To make another analogy, consider the jurisprudence around entrapment: if a police officer hands out free heroin samples and then arrests the buyers it is clear entrapment and ruled unjust. The legal concept undergirding entrapment is that a legitimate arrest requires “the perpetrator’s natural disposition to commit the crime and not be induced by social pressure” (Sherman vs United Statesprovides a good example). However, that doesn’t mean entrapment can’t be legal: successful entrapment cases rely on the criminal consistently choosing the illegal path of their own free will. The key criteria for a successful prosecution is the number of individual discrete unprompted actions.If we apply this framework to AI, then perceived culpability moves from the AI to the user when the user performs a number of discrete unprompted actions that establish it is his/her will to create the end product.More speculatively, underlying the “natural predisposition” concept relied on in entrapment is an idea of the amount of work done. Successful entrapment cases appear to rely on the perpetrator having independently done a lot of work or undertaken effort to commit the crime (cross-referencing this conceptually to the legal concept of “premeditated” also provide oblique support for this line of reasoning). Likewise, it is clear that we do not blame an undergraduate text in chemistry for facilitating bomb-making, even though it contains all the information required, even as we take down websites (or AIs!) that repackage the exact same information in a simple-to-digest format. It turns out that effort — either on the part of the perpetrator or the facilitator — matter in our moral calculus around culpability.In summary, there are two drivers of complicity: 1. User choice and 2. User effort. As the total set of (1) and (2) increases, perceived culpability shifts from the AI (and its creator) to the end user.Applying the FrameworkThe practical application of this framework is that all AI tools (LLM, image-gen or others) need to ensure user complicity as they move from uncontroversial results to those that some sections of society might find unacceptable. Essentially they need to make the user clearly CHOOSE settings that enable bad content and then the users need to WORK HARD to get those outcomesIt is beyond the scope of this document to brainstorm UI and workflow mockups, although that speaks to the key point: this isn’t a model problem; it’s a product problem. Instead of relying solely on RLHF (with all the drawbacks therein), the UI and flow of the product should require user complicity to make the results more controversial. If the user has had to toggle many different settings, and now has a UI that, due to those toggle changes, displays explicit warnings, then the perceived culpability is different. Indeed, one could go even further by making the user have to “work at it” by requiring complex commands or by explicitly selecting one of multiple results that has an NSFW tag to get to their end goal.Another solution would be to make the user perform a thin RLHF overlay on the AI; the user might be prompted to answer a series of questions if they want access to less vanilla results that make it “my AI”. Again, by involving the user the moral onus moves: if I can make the AI say something inappropriate it is a “gotcha” I can post on social media (Gemini suffered from this). Conversely if it is My AI that I helped train (and visually branded as such), posting a screenshot of something inappropriate says something about me and my choices; in this case induced shame is an attractive product quality.ConclusionAttempting to set a particular boundary of safety is impossible given the heterogeneous nature of nation states, societies and political groups within those nation states and the fact that morality is dynamic (it changes every decade and the “past is a foreign country”). Moreover, the risk is not one-sided: people will be upset no matter what rules you place on the model; the only question is what set of people.This is a significant business risk.The correct framework is to move culpability onto the user via product design decisions that means the user is not “entrapped” by the AI but can only move to more controversial results via the application of choice and effort.My thanks to my anonymous friend! The correct framework is to move culpability onto the user via product design decisions that means the user is not “entrapped” by the AI but can only move to more controversial results via the application of choice and effort. My thanks to my anonymous friend! I think this is good advice, and not just because no one wants bad PR. Overly constricting AI is like any other form of censorship: sure, you may stop a lot of the bad stuff you don’t want, but you will also stop good stuff, particularly new and innovative things that are outside the norm, and which represent some of the biggest potential upsides of AI. It makes sense to me that the solution is not to treat users like children, but let them make choices in a way that makes it clear to everyone that AI is a tool, not an oracle.
