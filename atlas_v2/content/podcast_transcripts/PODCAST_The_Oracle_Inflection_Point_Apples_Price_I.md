More generally, now that I am back in America, expect Interview days to move around a bit, with the default being Thursday, but not nearly as rigid of a schedule as I had in Taiwan.On to the Update:The Oracle Inflection PointI wrote about Oracle’s big stock pop yesterdayin the context of Oracle’s genuinely compelling tech stack and business positioning;Doug O’Laughlin wrote a bigger picture piece on Fabricated Knowledgethat I think is worth highlighting as well. O’Laughlin makes two big points:First, and in-line with with my notes about Oracle’s technology stack, O’Laughlin notes that Oracle’s success suggests that there is less of a moat in terms of legacy cloud incumbency than you might think:This is what disruption looks like. I have argued before that the hyperscalers’ old moats, balance sheets, and CPU-centric distribution were less impregnable than they appeared. SemiAnalysis’sClusterMAX benchmarksconfirmed this, and it is now a genuine contender from Oracle. Capital as a moat is not a lasting one.History suggests that incumbents rarely dominate tangential markets. A focused challenger emerges, captures share, and reshapes the landscape. AWS played that role a decade ago; Oracle may be playing a similar role now. Amazon’s in-house Trainium effort increasingly resembles an “Amazon Basics” strategy, viable but not leading, while Oracle doubles down on the largest GPU-first cloud in the world.The AWS part in particular very much echoes the concerns I expressed inParadigm Shifts and the Winner’s Curse; while AWS (and Apple’s) AI strategies are understandable, both run the risk of falling behind in a world where performance matters most:Go back to that illustration fromThe End of the Beginning: Apple and Amazon are betting that AI is just another primitive in continuous computing that happens everywhere.The most optimistic AI scenarios, however, point to something new:A better word for “Anywhere” is probably autonomous, but I wanted to stick with the “Where” theme; what I’m talking about, however, is agents: AI doing work without any human involvement at all. The potential productivity gains for companies are obvious: there is a massive price umbrella for inference costs if the end result is that you don’t need to employ a human to do the same work. In this world what matters most is performance, not cost, which means that Amazon’s obsession with costs is missing the point; it’s also a world where the company’s lack of a competitive leading edge model makes it harder for them to compete, particularly when there is another company in the ecosystem — Google — that not only has its own custom chip strategy (TPUs), but also is integrating those chips with its competitive leading edge large language model (Gemini).The reason I want to highlight O’Laughlin’s article, however, is the second point: while I noted that Oracle is going to have to use debt to finance the infrastructure necessary to serve these purchase obligations, it was only in passing; O’Laughlin argues that is the most important takeaway:There is no way for Oracle to pay for this with cash flow. They must raise equity or debt to fund their ambitions. Until now, the AI infrastructure boom has been almost entirely self-funded by the cash flows of a select few hyperscalers. Oracle has broken the pattern. It is willing to leverage up to hundreds of billions to seize a share. The stable oligopoly is cracking.I wrote about this earlier, and I think that we are starting to see the markers of a classical bubble.The implications are profound. Amazon, Microsoft, and Google can no longer treat AI infrastructure as a discretionary investment. They must defend their turf. What had been a disciplined, cash-flow-funded race may now turn into a debt-fuelled arms race. That’s a vibe-shift if I’ve ever seen one.Welcome to the next and newest phase of the capital cycle. I believe the Oracle quarter achieved something more elusive than just revenue. I believe Oracle has just sparked the elusive animal spirit to life. I believe that for me, Oracle is the quarter that we will remember in history. I am starting to think that the tariff tantrum will be primarily felt in the lagging-edge parts of the market, while the explicit plan is to spend more on AI capex to drive our economy forward.O’Laughlin makes a critical point: until now the single best riposte to those worried about an AI bubble is that all of this capital expenditure is being funded out of positive free cash flow from the Aggregators; now, however, a debt-fueled player has entered the market:Let’s sketch out the state of the future from here. I believe that the 2000 comparison is now 100% complete. What we have lacked in this cycle so far is almost no debt-led funding. The Oracle RPO shows what’s possible if you’re willing to flex your balance sheet, and this will kick off an uneasy race.Compare the spending of the big three with that of Oracle. Oracle is going to go negative free cash flow to win, and it’s likely that incumbents will respond. I believe it’s time for technology incumbents to start spending a lot more, and most have already indicated they will…I think the simplest way to think about this is through the framework laid out in the book“Boom” by Byrne Hobart. Occasionally, capitalism disregards return on capital once every 20-40 years, to single-mindedly invest in a new technology. What really makes the bubble big is that all the parties are aligned in the same direction for the outcome. I think the stars are aligning, and debt is the real accelerant.I interviewed Hobart about his book last fall; these are his words:Bubbles are this weird financial phenomenon where asset prices move in a way that does not seem justified by economic fundamentals. A lot of money pours into some industry, a lot of stuff gets built, and usually too much of it gets built and a bunch of people lose their shirts and a lot of very smart, sophisticated people are involved with the beginning, a lot of those people are selling at the peak, and a lot of people who are buying at the peak are less smart, less sophisticated, but they’ve been kind of taken in by the vibe and they’re buying at the wrong time and they lose their shirts, and that’s really bad.As we discussed, this thesis isn’t new; Carlota Perez made a similar argument inTechnological Revolutions and Financial Capital. Hobart’s focus, however, isn’t just on the infrastructure that is built, but on the otherwise impossible innovations that occur when everyone buys in all at once:What I took away is your book was much more of a sociological exposé, like spiritual almost, and I shouldn’t say almost because you were actually quite explicit about it. It’s like you were seeking — the goal of this book, it feels like — is to call forth the spirit of the bubble as opposed to have some sort of technocratic overview. You give us useful history, but there’s not really charts or microeconomics, it’s an exhortation. Is that what you were going for?BH:Yes, it is an exhortation. We do want people to pick up a copy and quit their job halfway through reading it or drop out of school and start something crazy. I don’t want to be legally liable if you do something sufficiently crazy, and I think that the spiritual element is something that we did want to talk about in the book, because I think if you — you can apply this totally secular framework to it, and it’s perfectly valid. Of course, if it is a mostly materialist framing of things, then it has a lot more real world data because it’s all reliant on that real world data, but if you have the belief or at least suspicion that all of us are unique and special, and there is something that we are, if not put on this Earth to do, at least there are things that we are able to do that other people wouldn’t do as well, that part of our job is to find those things and do them really well. Bubbles play into that in an interesting way because they tell you it’s time, it’s like you wanted to do this kind of thing…Well, and to your point, a lot of these things have so many interlocking parts, all of which need to be built at the same time. FOMO is this coordination function, and I’m just parroting the points in your book, but it’s this coordination function that gets lots of people solving lots of different problems, all of which need to be solved in parallel and that’s, to your point, you can’t build it out serially, it has to be done that way.BH:Yes, it has to be done in parallel. One way that we talk about it is that industry clusters, they’re really interesting phenomenon where, if you want to work in finance, you do what I did, you get to New York by all means necessary. If you want to be in movies, you go to LA. If you want to be in tech, you go to the Bay Area.What bubbles do is they create one of those clusters in time rather than in space where this is the time when everyone is doing this thing, and so this is the time to do it. If you know that people are building other elements of the — they’re building parts of the infrastructure that your thing needs to be built on, then it’s actually a less risky thing to build. So Amazon was a less risky business because AOL was mass mailing their disks and telling everyone, “Get on the Internet”, and then AOL became a less risky business because Yahoo is giving you stuff to find on the Internet. eBay and Amazon are giving you stuff to buy on the Internet. Everyone’s creating websites so you have these feedback loops in both directions.This is what I meant when I talked about Oracle’s investment being “thrilling”; and, for the record (and in response to some feedback I received), I wasn’t being critical of Larry Ellison while noting his age: if you’re not investing to change the world, then ultimately, what is the point?What remains to be seen is what — beyond the technologies that are created in parallel — is the durable good from the bubble, if it is forming;I discussed this question with Daniel Gross and Nat Friedman in Januaryin the context of Stargate and the imminent arrival of debt financing:DG:By the way, the other lens on the news today is an economic one, which is, put all this stuff aside, it is just the nature of bubbles that after spending a few quarters spending balance sheet money, debt comes into play. That happened at the telco bubble, that happened at the railroad bubble…I think if you look at telecom as a fraction of institutional grade debt, I think in 1996 it’s like 5%, and then in 1999 it suddenly spikes to 10%, and then just before the bubble bursts, it spikes again I think to like 15%. And so, it’s just like the nature of the way these things work. You spend your balance sheet, you want more capital, you start issuing debt.And then the main question to think about is basically, does everything go as planned or not? Because if it does, it’s a wonderful trade, because you get to basically dilate time, you pull things forward, and paying a little bit of interest is fine, but if it doesn’t, and there’s a hiccup along the way, the pain is much greater. I think it’s just a natural act of things…Well, I think the big concern has to be that, what is the debt going towards? The telco bubble, at the end of the day, it was hilariously stupid economics. The bits were going to go to zero, but at the same time, there is still Internet traffic basically running over the infrastructure that was laid down back then.NF:That’s right. Yeah, bubbles are good for the country, they’re not good for sophisticated investor.Well, they’re good for the country depending on what they’re spent on, right?NF:Well, of course, yeah.So, you have a data center, which is I think a 30-year depreciation, and then the GPUs are I think accounted for in a five-year depreciation, but actually are unusable after about 36 months. So, you already have a problem there in terms of your accounting for the GPUs go and by definition, taking debt out on a 36-month sort of item is probably not ideal, that is rapidly diminishing in value. But if say we had a power build out, well, guess what? We can be using electricity forever.NF:That’s right.I think the critical question and moment right now is, can we direct this bubble spending? And this is almost like the depressing thing about this announcement. I don’t want news about a $500 billion investment in data centers, I want news about a $500 billion investment in power generation, which will retain its value, even if the people who build it go bankrupt in the meantime.That discussion was in reference to OpenAI’s Stargate announcement; the company says that its purchase agreement with Oracle is a part of that. What I am watching for is where the power for these Oracle investments comes from; more generally, the best thing the U.S. can do for its future would be to build more power. If AI makes that politically viable it will be an incredible contribution.Apple’s Price IncreaseBefore the iPhone event completely fades from memory —if it ever entered it— I did want to close the loop on a long-running Stratechery hobby-horse: Apple finally raised iPhone prices.The way the company did so is quite clever. Here is a comparison of last year’s lineup and this year’s lineup:20252024Top-of-the-line Big iPhoneiPhone 17 Pro Max$1,199iPhone 16 Pro Max$1,199Top-of-the-line iPhoneiPhone 17 Pro$1,099iPhone 16 Pro$999Big iPhoneiPhone Air$999iPhone 16 Plus$899Regular iPhoneiPhone 17$799iPhone 16$799Apple increased the allure of the big iPhone, making it both incredibly thin and also with the same chip as the Pro, and then slid it into the $999 price point previously occupied by the iPhone Pro; the iPhone Pro, meanwhile, went up by $100. Apple will argue that isn’t a price increase, because $1,099 was the price of the 256GB model last year, and 256GB is now the base level. At the end of the day, however, there no longer is an option for a $999 iPhone Pro, so I’m counting this as a price increase.What is notable is that iPhones are still historically cheap in real terms; here is the 2025 version of a table I have been posting for the last several years, showing iPhone prices in 2020 dollars:2020202120222023202420252 year old iPhone$499$477$441$422$411N/A1 year old iPhone$599$572$530$507$493$568New iPhone$799$763$707$676$657$649iPhone Pro$999$954$883$846$822$892iPhone Pro Max$1,099$1,050$972$1,015$904$973Two additional notes on this table:There is no two-year-old iPhone for sale in the U.S.; rather, there is the iPhone 16e for $599The one-year-old iPhone is $699, which is less of a price decrease than previous old iPhones; this is also the first base iPhone that had increased RAM (to support Apple Intelligence)Overall this supports my assumptionwhen the iPhone 16e was announcedthat Apple was resetting its lineup for AI, and covering its bases for tariffs and inflation as well.
