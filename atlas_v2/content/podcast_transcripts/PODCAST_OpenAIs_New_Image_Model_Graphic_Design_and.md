Ohio, “I know it when I see it.”I gave my definition of AGI inAI’s Uneven Arrival:What o3 and inference-time scaling point to is something different: AI’s that can actually be given tasks and trusted to complete them. This, by extension, looks a lot more like an independent worker than an assistant — ammunition, rather than a rifle sight. That may seem an odd analogy, but it comes froma talk Keith Rabois gave at Stanford…My definition of AGI is that it can be ammunition, i.e. it can be given a task and trusted to complete it at a good-enough rate (my definition of Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI) is the ability to come up with the tasks in the first place).The “feel” part of that question is a more recent discovery: DeepResearch from OpenAIfeelslike AGI; I just got a new employee for the shockingly low price of $200/month.Well, good news for me: I just got another new employee — I already gave it a job for the section below. This was OpenAI’s depiction of me reading all of your (mostly) thoughtful emails toyesterday’s Update:Specifically, I received alotof emails noting that secure communications is aboutmore than end-to-end encryption. I guess I sort of assumed that that was obvious, particularly since I’ve written about overall device security a lot. For example,one month ago, Iquoted a previous Updatethat discussed how the device itself is at the root of encryption; ergo, if the device is compromised the encryption doesn’t matter. I’ve also written about things likeiMessage exploits that don’t require physical access to an iPhone; I would also point to an Article I wrote in 2018 entitledMeltdown, Spectre, and the State of Technologythat went into extensive detail about virtual machines, memory hierarchies, and how the Meltdown and Spectre exploits took advantage of branch prediction to gain unauthorized access to data. This is how I concluded that Article:I ended 2017 without my customary “State of Technology” post, and just as well: Spectre is a far better representation than anything I might have written. Faced with a fundamental imbalance (data fetch slowness versus execution speed), processor engineers devised an ingenious system optimized for performance, but havingfailed to envision the possibility of bad actors abusing the system, everyone was left vulnerable.That general takeaway, both about how the search for speed and convenience can compromise security, and also how it is easy to forget about bad actors, are certainly relevant. In fact, I should point this exact same critique at myself: I didn’t fully consider how my Update, particularly the Signal section, was coming across to people who don’t walk around with encyclopedic knowledge of my writing (which really shouldn’t be any of you). So, to summarize, Signal is a secure messaging app, but that does not mean using Signal makes communications secure.I think that my failure to make that distinction was the main point of objection, but I did want to have another go at the transparency section, because this is actually the part I find the most interesting. This entire episode was, as I said twice in yesterday’s Update, very stupid; so how do smart people end up doing stupid things?Well, go back to theThe Atlantic article that prompted this discussion:If officials want to discuss military activity, they should go into a specially designed space known as a sensitive compartmented information facility, or SCIF — most Cabinet-level national-security officials have one installed in their home — or communicate only on approved government equipment, the lawyers said. Normally, cellphones are not permitted inside a SCIF, which suggests that as these officials were sharing information about an active military operation, they could have been moving around in public. Had they lost their phones, or had they been stolen, the potential risk to national security would have been severe.There is ongoing debate as to whether or not classified information was in fact in the group chat — The Atlantic released the full message threadhere— but my point was not to litigate an ongoing political dispute, but rather to make broader observations about technology’s impact. For example, using a SCIF was a relatively small sacrifice when all communications happened over land lines or in person; just go to another room. It’s a massively greater sacrifice when compared to a phone that is always with you. That’s a big change, and an entirely new set of trade-offs: how much is speed of communications worth relative to security, particularly if the delta is very large (on both sides)?Second, everything being digital means that even deliberations — which today happen mostly over messaging or email — is by default kept forever; I’m not sure that’s good for honest debate, and probably isn’t what the original lawmakers had in mind, given that the first Freedom of Information Act was passed at a time when records meant paper, and deliberations happened orally. One way that government officials get around this requirement is to classify anything and everything; however, when everything is classified, do we really think that SCIF’s are used consistently? The fact that both of the last two administrations ended up with classified material at their homes certainly suggests no.Third, while it is true thatSignal was officially recommended for government officials, I’m quite certain that the disappearing message feature is being leveraged as a way to avoid keeping records. And, I would add, I know with a very high degree of confidence that disappearing messages have been used across administrations.This is why I raised the transparency point in the Update: balancing convenient communications with security is an almost impossible task, particularly with the rise of smartphones. Layering on record-keeping for every piece of digital communications — including deliberations — makes the problem truly impossible, and creates bad incentives as well.Again, none of this is really about this specific episode, which I wasn’t defending; what is interesting to me is how making things digital, and the behavioral changes that result from zero marginal costs, completely upends norms and laws created for the analog era. I will continue to find it fascinating when this upending manifests itself in surprising — and stupid — ways.Tomorrow there will be a Stratechery Interview, and then the next Update will be April 7.This Update will be available as a podcast later today.
