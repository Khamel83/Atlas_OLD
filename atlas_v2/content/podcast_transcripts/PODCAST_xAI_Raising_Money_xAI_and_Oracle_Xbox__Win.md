The rate at which the company is bleeding cash provides a stark illustration of the unprecedented financial demands of the artificial intelligence industry, particularly at xAI, where revenues have been slow to materialize. To cover the gap, Musk’s startup is currently trying to raise $9.3 billion in debt and equity, according to people briefed on the deal terms, who asked not to be identified because the information is private. But even before the money is in the bank, the company has plans to spend more than half of it in just the next three months, the people said.Over the course of 2025, xAI, which is responsible for the AI-powered chatbot Grok, expects to burn through about $13 billion, as reflected in the company’s levered cash flow, according to details shared with investors. As a result, its prolific fundraising efforts are just barely keeping pace with expenses, the people added. A spokesperson for the company declined to comment. However, Musk said “Bloomberg is talking nonsense” in a response to a post on his social media platform X that referenced Bloomberg’s article…Musk’s entrant in the AI race has struggled to develop revenue streams at the same rate as some of its direct competitors, like OpenAI and Anthropic. While almost none of these companies offer public figures on their finances, Bloomberg has previously reported that OpenAI, the creator of ChatGPT, is expecting to bring in revenues of $12.7 billion this year. At xAI, revenues are expected to be just $500 million this year, rising to north of $2 billion next year, investors were recently told…Musk’s team at xAI, which is racing to develop AI that can compete with humans, believes that they have advantages that will eventually allow them to catch up with peers. While some competitors rent chips and server space, xAI is paying for much of the infrastructure itself and getting direct access through X, which previously bought a significant stockpile of the most coveted and high powered computer chips. Musk has said that he expects xAI will continue buying more chips.I don’t, on one hand, see any reason to take Musk’s word that “Bloomberg is talking nonsense”; it would certainly be helpful from my perspective if xAI were to share evidence of strong revenue growth or high demand.Grok 3was state-of-the-art when it launched, but Anthropic and OpenAI seem to have quickly caught up in model quality, while my personal opinion is that Grok’s quality has decreased as it has been optimized for cost and scale. I personally — along with the vast majority of consumers — am back to using ChatGPT most of the time, which is a much better product above-and-beyond model quality, while Anthropic continues to dominate most coding workflows. Obviously I’m not the representative customer — although Stratechery’s spending-per-employee on AI is extremely high! — but absent evidence I find the $500 million revenue figure very plausible.One problem facing xAI is that they don’t have a hyperscaler partner to both share in the costs of training and hosting models, and also to serve as a go-to-market channel for enterprise workloads; OpenAI has Microsoft, Anthropic has Amazon, and Google obviously has itself. xAI frames their ownership of their own infrastructure as a differentiator, but I’m not sure I see why that would be the case, particularly given the fact that GPUs are not long-lived assets relatively speaking (although data centers are); indeed, GPU ownership exacerbates xAI’s revenue challenges, given that the way to make money on those GPU purchases is to make sure they are utilized 100% of the time, which is easier to do the larger the customer base desiring access to them.xAI and OracleCould that mean, however, that the more important news from yesterday is this item fromReuters?Oracle said on Tuesday that its cloud computing division is working with Elon Musk’s artificial intelligence firm xAI to offer the startup’s latest AI model to business customers. Grok 3, as the model is called, first came out February and aimed to rival the latest offerings from DeepSeek and OpenAI. Musk made the model available to paying subscribers of his X social media platform, as well as through xAI itself.Oracle will now offer Grok 3 from its data centers to corporate users who want to use the model but need their data to remain under their existing security protections with Oracle, Karan Batta, senior vice president at Oracle Cloud Infrastructure, told Reuters on Monday. Oracle also offers models from Meta, French startup Mistral and Cohere on the service, part of its overall strategy of providing versions of popular models in a way that businesses can integrate into corporate software. “Our goal here is to make sure that we can provide a portfolio of models – we don’t have our own,” Batta said. “That’s the current strategy. We are going to be the one that offers all of them.”xAI and Oracle actually have had quite the roller-coaster of a relationship. Grok 1 and 2 were both trained on Oracle’s cloud, but xAI did Grok 3 on its own; fromThe Information last July:Musk’s xAI and Oracle have ended talks to expand an existing arrangement under which xAI rents specialized Nvidia AI chips from the tech giant. Instead, xAI is buying chips to build a data center on its own, according to several people involved in the talks. XAI had been discussing a multiyear agreement to rent Nvidia chips from Oracle for a planned supercomputer, The Information reported in May, in what could have been a $10 billion deal. But the talks stalled over issues including Musk’s demand that the supercomputer be built faster than Oracle thought possible, and Oracle’s concern that xAI’s preferred location did not have access to an adequate power supply, according to someone involved in the talks.Musk famously built his new Memphis data center in record time and with off-grid power sources (which haverecently been taken downafter a new electrical substation came online), and hasacquired land for a second Memphis data center; that, however, is why yesterday’s announcement — the coverage of which focused on making xAI available to Oracle’s corporate customers — was so curious. Note this line fromOracle’s press release(emphasis mine):xAI has selected Oracle to offer xAI’s Grok models via Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI) Generative AI service for a wide range of use cases, including content creation, research, and business process automation.xAI will also leverage the scalability, performance, and cost efficiency of OCI’s leading AI infrastructure to train and run inferencing for its next-generation Grok models.Now “train” could have a whole host of meanings; the most likely explanation for that line is that Oracle will do post-training for enterprise specific use cases, which lines up with them taking on enterprise workloads. Still, while Musk ended up being right that he could build a data center faster, economics may end up mattering more than speed in the long run, and Oracle is really the only partner left to dance with.Then again, Oracle isn’t the only entity that might want xAI to survive and thrive; companies like Cursor or Perplexity, for example, should absolutely want there to be more competition at the model layer. Apple might have been a prospective partner, although it does appear that the company is doubling down on OpenAI. Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella, meanwhile, conspicuously showed apre-recorded video with Muskat the Build developer conference last month; Azure offers Grok as an option in a similar manner to this latest Oracle deal. Perhaps the company that could most use xAI, however, is Meta; the fact that xAI now owns X (née Twitter), however, almost certainly makes any sort of tie-up a non-starter. Indeed, the reports that Meta is throwing around all kinds of money to hire AI talent is probably a risk factor for xAI.Regardless, everyone in the ecosystem should want xAI to exist as a check on the other model providers; the challenge for xAI is getting everyone — or at least more people — to want to actually use it and pay for it.Xbox = WindowsFromThe Verge:Microsoft is confirming today that it’s working on a next-gen Xbox console in partnership with AMD. Xbox president Sarah Bond made the announcement in a minute-long videoposted to YouTube, promising to deliver “an Xbox experience not locked to a single store or tied to one device.”The headline is the AMD partnership, but the real news — at least in my interpretation — is that Xbox as we once understood it is well-and-truly dead. Here’s the video and transcript:At Xbox, our vision is for you to play the games you want people you want, anywhere you want. That’s why we’re investing in our next generation hardware lineup across console, handheld, PC, cloud, and accessories.I am thrilled to share we’ve established a strategic multi-year partnership with AMD to co-engineer silicon across a portfolio of devices, including our next generation Xbox consoles, in your living room, and in your hands. Together with AMD, we’re advancing the state of art in gaming silicon to deliver the next generation of graphics innovation to unlock a deeper level of visual quality, an immersive gameplay and player experiences, and hands with the power of AI, all maintaining compatibility with your existing library of Xbox games.This is all about building you a gaming platform that’s always with you, so you can play the games you want across devices, anywhere you want, delivering you an Xbox experience, not locked to a single store or tied to one device. That’s why we’re working closely with the Windows team to ensure that Windows is the number one platform for gaming. The next generation of Xbox is coming to light and this is just the beginning. We can’t wait to show you and see what’s next.“Xbox as we once understood it” was basically a direct PlayStation competitor: sure, it had components that were basically the same as a PC, but it was a single defined bill of goods, with a locked down operating system and a single game store; Microsoft sold consoles at a loss, at least at the beginning, and made money by selling both first party games and from licensing revenue on third party games. It’s a good model, as Sony can attest, but that’s the problem: Sony won by recognizing the changing economics of gaming and pivoting to an exclusives model with the PS4 generation.I wrote about these changing economics at length in 2022’sConsoles and Competition, but briefly: as the costs of asset development dwarfed the cost of the core technology (particularly with the rise of licensable game engines), developers were heavily incentivized to support all platforms, rendering Sony’s investments in hardware differentiation moot; the company responded by acquiring a host of game studios and becoming the default choice for gamers only buying one console, thereby winning the third-party licensing game as well.In short, Sony is the integrated winner, which perfectly explains this Microsoft announcement: they are going fully modular, supporting a host of hardware, from cloud tohandheldsto PCs to a (relatively open) future console; the operating system across those devices will be Windows, and anyone can run on Windows, including competitors like Steam. That right there is the big win: by opening up, future “Xbox’s” will have access to the most games by far, and while Microsoft might make less money on every individual hardware or software sale, they might be able to make it up in volume from Windows licensing; at a minimum this strategy is at least broadly aligned with Windows and Azure and Microsoft’s orientation as a services business, and its nature as a platform.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. our take on Bill Simmons’ and Chuck Klosterman’s normie takes on AI.As a reminder, tomorrow is a federal holiday; there will be no Update.On to the Update:xAI Raising MoneyFromBloomberg:Elon Musk’s artificial intelligence startup xAI is burning through $1 billion a month as the cost of building its advanced AI models races ahead of the limited revenues, according to people briefed on the company’s financials. The rate at which the company is bleeding cash provides a stark illustration of the unprecedented financial demands of the artificial intelligence industry, particularly at xAI, where revenues have been slow to materialize. To cover the gap, Musk’s startup is currently trying to raise $9.3 billion in debt and equity, according to people briefed on the deal terms, who asked not to be identified because the information is private. But even before the money is in the bank, the company has plans to spend more than half of it in just the next three months, the people said.Over the course of 2025, xAI, which is responsible for the AI-powered chatbot Grok, expects to burn through about $13 billion, as reflected in the company’s levered cash flow, according to details shared with investors. As a result, its prolific fundraising efforts are just barely keeping pace with expenses, the people added. A spokesperson for the company declined to comment. However, Musk said “Bloomberg is talking nonsense” in a response to a post on his social media platform X that referenced Bloomberg’s article…Musk’s entrant in the AI race has struggled to develop revenue streams at the same rate as some of its direct competitors, like OpenAI and Anthropic. While almost none of these companies offer public figures on their finances, Bloomberg has previously reported that OpenAI, the creator of ChatGPT, is expecting to bring in revenues of $12.7 billion this year. At xAI, revenues are expected to be just $500 million this year, rising to north of $2 billion next year, investors were recently told…Musk’s team at xAI, which is racing to develop AI that can compete with humans, believes that they have advantages that will eventually allow them to catch up with peers. While some competitors rent chips and server space, xAI is paying for much of the infrastructure itself and getting direct access through X, which previously bought a significant stockpile of the most coveted and high powered computer chips. Musk has said that he expects xAI will continue buying more chips.I don’t, on one hand, see any reason to take Musk’s word that “Bloomberg is talking nonsense”; it would certainly be helpful from my perspective if xAI were to share evidence of strong revenue growth or high demand.Grok 3was state-of-the-art when it launched, but Anthropic and OpenAI seem to have quickly caught up in model quality, while my personal opinion is that Grok’s quality has decreased as it has been optimized for cost and scale. I personally — along with the vast majority of consumers — am back to using ChatGPT most of the time, which is a much better product above-and-beyond model quality, while Anthropic continues to dominate most coding workflows. Obviously I’m not the representative customer — although Stratechery’s spending-per-employee on AI is extremely high! — but absent evidence I find the $500 million revenue figure very plausible.One problem facing xAI is that they don’t have a hyperscaler partner to both share in the costs of training and hosting models, and also to serve as a go-to-market channel for enterprise workloads; OpenAI has Microsoft, Anthropic has Amazon, and Google obviously has itself. xAI frames their ownership of their own infrastructure as a differentiator, but I’m not sure I see why that would be the case, particularly given the fact that GPUs are not long-lived assets relatively speaking (although data centers are); indeed, GPU ownership exacerbates xAI’s revenue challenges, given that the way to make money on those GPU purchases is to make sure they are utilized 100% of the time, which is easier to do the larger the customer base desiring access to them.xAI and OracleCould that mean, however, that the more important news from yesterday is this item fromReuters?Oracle said on Tuesday that its cloud computing division is working with Elon Musk’s artificial intelligence firm xAI to offer the startup’s latest AI model to business customers. Grok 3, as the model is called, first came out February and aimed to rival the latest offerings from DeepSeek and OpenAI. Musk made the model available to paying subscribers of his X social media platform, as well as through xAI itself.Oracle will now offer Grok 3 from its data centers to corporate users who want to use the model but need their data to remain under their existing security protections with Oracle, Karan Batta, senior vice president at Oracle Cloud Infrastructure, told Reuters on Monday. Oracle also offers models from Meta, French startup Mistral and Cohere on the service, part of its overall strategy of providing versions of popular models in a way that businesses can integrate into corporate software. “Our goal here is to make sure that we can provide a portfolio of models – we don’t have our own,” Batta said. “That’s the current strategy. We are going to be the one that offers all of them.”xAI and Oracle actually have had quite the roller-coaster of a relationship. Grok 1 and 2 were both trained on Oracle’s cloud, but xAI did Grok 3 on its own; fromThe Information last July:Musk’s xAI and Oracle have ended talks to expand an existing arrangement under which xAI rents specialized Nvidia AI chips from the tech giant. Instead, xAI is buying chips to build a data center on its own, according to several people involved in the talks. XAI had been discussing a multiyear agreement to rent Nvidia chips from Oracle for a planned supercomputer, The Information reported in May, in what could have been a $10 billion deal. But the talks stalled over issues including Musk’s demand that the supercomputer be built faster than Oracle thought possible, and Oracle’s concern that xAI’s preferred location did not have access to an adequate power supply, according to someone involved in the talks.Musk famously built his new Memphis data center in record time and with off-grid power sources (which haverecently been taken downafter a new electrical substation came online), and hasacquired land for a second Memphis data center; that, however, is why yesterday’s announcement — the coverage of which focused on making xAI available to Oracle’s corporate customers — was so curious. Note this line fromOracle’s press release(emphasis mine):xAI has selected Oracle to offer xAI’s Grok models via Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI) Generative AI service for a wide range of use cases, including content creation, research, and business process automation.xAI will also leverage the scalability, performance, and cost efficiency of OCI’s leading AI infrastructure to train and run inferencing for its next-generation Grok models.Now “train” could have a whole host of meanings; the most likely explanation for that line is that Oracle will do post-training for enterprise specific use cases, which lines up with them taking on enterprise workloads. Still, while Musk ended up being right that he could build a data center faster, economics may end up mattering more than speed in the long run, and Oracle is really the only partner left to dance with.Then again, Oracle isn’t the only entity that might want xAI to survive and thrive; companies like Cursor or Perplexity, for example, should absolutely want there to be more competition at the model layer. Apple might have been a prospective partner, although it does appear that the company is doubling down on OpenAI. Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella, meanwhile, conspicuously showed apre-recorded video with Muskat the Build developer conference last month; Azure offers Grok as an option in a similar manner to this latest Oracle deal. Perhaps the company that could most use xAI, however, is Meta; the fact that xAI now owns X (née Twitter), however, almost certainly makes any sort of tie-up a non-starter. Indeed, the reports that Meta is throwing around all kinds of money to hire AI talent is probably a risk factor for xAI.Regardless, everyone in the ecosystem should want xAI to exist as a check on the other model providers; the challenge for xAI is getting everyone — or at least more people — to want to actually use it and pay for it.Xbox = WindowsFromThe Verge:Microsoft is confirming today that it’s working on a next-gen Xbox console in partnership with AMD. Xbox president Sarah Bond made the announcement in a minute-long videoposted to YouTube, promising to deliver “an Xbox experience not locked to a single store or tied to one device.”The headline is the AMD partnership, but the real news — at least in my interpretation — is that Xbox as we once understood it is well-and-truly dead. Here’s the video and transcript:At Xbox, our vision is for you to play the games you want people you want, anywhere you want. That’s why we’re investing in our next generation hardware lineup across console, handheld, PC, cloud, and accessories.I am thrilled to share we’ve established a strategic multi-year partnership with AMD to co-engineer silicon across a portfolio of devices, including our next generation Xbox consoles, in your living room, and in your hands. Together with AMD, we’re advancing the state of art in gaming silicon to deliver the next generation of graphics innovation to unlock a deeper level of visual quality, an immersive gameplay and player experiences, and hands with the power of AI, all maintaining compatibility with your existing library of Xbox games.This is all about building you a gaming platform that’s always with you, so you can play the games you want across devices, anywhere you want, delivering you an Xbox experience, not locked to a single store or tied to one device. That’s why we’re working closely with the Windows team to ensure that Windows is the number one platform for gaming. The next generation of Xbox is coming to light and this is just the beginning. We can’t wait to show you and see what’s next.“Xbox as we once understood it” was basically a direct PlayStation competitor: sure, it had components that were basically the same as a PC, but it was a single defined bill of goods, with a locked down operating system and a single game store; Microsoft sold consoles at a loss, at least at the beginning, and made money by selling both first party games and from licensing revenue on third party games. It’s a good model, as Sony can attest, but that’s the problem: Sony won by recognizing the changing economics of gaming and pivoting to an exclusives model with the PS4 generation.I wrote about these changing economics at length in 2022’sConsoles and Competition, but briefly: as the costs of asset development dwarfed the cost of the core technology (particularly with the rise of licensable game engines), developers were heavily incentivized to support all platforms, rendering Sony’s investments in hardware differentiation moot; the company responded by acquiring a host of game studios and becoming the default choice for gamers only buying one console, thereby winning the third-party licensing game as well.In short, Sony is the integrated winner, which perfectly explains this Microsoft announcement: they are going fully modular, supporting a host of hardware, from cloud tohandheldsto PCs to a (relatively open) future console; the operating system across those devices will be Windows, and anyone can run on Windows, including competitors like Steam. That right there is the big win: by opening up, future “Xbox’s” will have access to the most games by far, and while Microsoft might make less money on every individual hardware or software sale, they might be able to make it up in volume from Windows licensing; at a minimum this strategy is at least broadly aligned with Windows and Azure and Microsoft’s orientation as a services business, and its nature as a platform.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. I wrote about these changing economics at length in 2022’sConsoles and Competition, but briefly: as the costs of asset development dwarfed the cost of the core technology (particularly with the rise of licensable game engines), developers were heavily incentivized to support all platforms, rendering Sony’s investments in hardware differentiation moot; the company responded by acquiring a host of game studios and becoming the default choice for gamers only buying one console, thereby winning the third-party licensing game as well. In short, Sony is the integrated winner, which perfectly explains this Microsoft announcement: they are going fully modular, supporting a host of hardware, from cloud tohandheldsto PCs to a (relatively open) future console; the operating system across those devices will be Windows, and anyone can run on Windows, including competitors like Steam. That right there is the big win: by opening up, future “Xbox’s” will have access to the most games by far, and while Microsoft might make less money on every individual hardware or software sale, they might be able to make it up in volume from Windows licensing; at a minimum this strategy is at least broadly aligned with Windows and Azure and Microsoft’s orientation as a services business, and its nature as a platform.
