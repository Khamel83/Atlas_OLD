For months or longer, the hackers might have held access to network infrastructure used to cooperate with lawful U.S. requests for communications data, according to people familiar with the matter, which amounts to a major national security risk. The attackers also had access to other tranches of more generic internet traffic, they said.Verizon Communications, AT&T and Lumen Technologies are among the companies whose networks were breached by the recently discovered intrusion, the people said. The widespread compromise is considered a potentially catastrophic security breach and was carried out by a sophisticated Chinese hacking group dubbed Salt Typhoon. It appeared to be geared toward intelligence collection, the people said.The surveillance systems believed to be at issue are used to cooperate with requests for domestic information related to criminal and national security investigations. Under federal law, telecommunications and broadband companies must allow authorities to intercept electronic information pursuant to a court order. It couldn’t be determined if systems that support foreign intelligence surveillance were also vulnerable in the breach…A person familiar with the attack said the U.S. government considered the intrusions to be historically significant and worrisome.You might have noticed that the title of this section of the Update uses the passive voice; the reason is because the subject of an active voice isn’t as clear as you might think. In a narrow sense, the subject should be China: “China Hacks U.S. Communications”. That is certainly what has happened here.The History of CALEAIn truth, though, this hack happened a long time time ago. The first possible date is 1994, and the entity at fault is the U.S. Congress. That is when the “Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (CALEA)” was passed.It used to be that a wiretap involved, well, tapping a wire. Law enforcement would gain physical access to a phone line and attach a device to capture the audio of phone conversations. This was possible because the way telephones used to work is that they would convert audio into an analog electrical signal, which would be converted back into audio by the recipient telephone. The intervening analog signal, though, wasn’t encrypted; any device could reconvert it back into audio.The telephone system doesn’t work like that anymore, as you might expect; your device does convert audio into an analog electrical signal, but it then further converts that analog electrical signal into a digital signal — 1s and 0s, basically, which can be encrypted.Encryption, however, can mean multiple things; I discussed the distinction between end-to-end encryption and transport layer encryption this summer inthe context of the Telegram case, but in short:End-to-end encryption means the digital signal is encrypted on the originating device using a key known only to the device owner, and can only be decrypted by the recipient on their device using the corresponding public key for the originator’s private key.Transport layer encryption means the digital signal is encrypted on the originating device using a key granted (or at least known) by the service receiving the communications; that signal can then be decrypted by the service once it arrives. It is, however, encrypted in transit.Transport layer encryption is the standard for most digital communications; your communication between your device and the server you are accessing, whether that be an e-commerce site, a site like Stratechery, or a communications service, is generally safe from so-called “Man-in-the-middle (MITM)” attacks, where a third-party captures your communication and interferes with it in some way.Notice, though, that old-school wiretapping is a canonical man-in-the-middle attack: law enforcement is — again, literally — tapping the wire you are using to communicate and listening in. Law enforcement rather likes this ability, and was concerned about losing this capability in the transition to digital signals.Thus was born CALEA: Congress mandated that communications providers — who remember, are providing the keys for transport layer encryption — create the capability for law enforcement to listen in on conversations, i.e. leverage their control of encryption keys to give access to the underlying data. This is the capability that was leveraged in these attacks, so it’s not wrong to say that “Congress Hacked U.S. Communications”, by creating this capability in the first place.It’s also not entirely right; CALEA actually expressly excluded data. The goal was to recreate the ability to wiretap voice calls, not to expand surveillance. Moreover, phone calls, even as they became digital, still worked differently than the Internet.That last part would soon change. In the decade after CALEA was passed it increasingly made more sense to simply treat voice digital signals the same as other digital signals, using Voice-over-IP (VoIP). Now everything was packets of 1s and 0s, and restricting CALEA to only voice both became more technically challenging and somewhat nonsensical; meanwhile, law enforcement agencies were making the case that more and more criminal communication was happening through means other than voice.To that end, in 2004, the DOJ, ATF, FBI, and DEA filed a petition with the FCC to expand CALEA to cover all IP traffic, including broadband Internet communications; the FCC did so, and when challenged in court,wonthanks to the Chevron deference doctrine, which granted substantial law-making ability to agencies in lieu of explicit Congressional directions. In this case specifically the court held that Congress’s explicit exclusion of “information services” did not necessarily apply to broadband, which frankly, still confuses me; indeed, this case is one of the stronger arguments against Chevron, which was overturned earlier this year.Regardless, the pertinent takeaway for this Article is that you can argue that the “FCC Hacked U.S. Communications”, and the reason is this: yes, the Chinese hacked into U.S. communications, but they did so via a backdoor mandated by CALEA, which was expanded to include internet communications by the FCC. Without CALEA and without that 2004 expansion there would not have been a vulnerability for the Chinese to hack.Encryption and BackdoorsThis case is another manifestation of the age-old debate about encryption and backdoors that has more recently flared up in the context of end-to-end encryption. Many in tech, including myself, resolutely oppose any attempt to create backdoors in end-to-end encryption, even while admitting the trade-offs that entails.This case explains why: backdoors can and will, in the fullness of time, be hacked. The only way to guarantee true privacy in communications is end-to-end encryption; compromising that may make it easier to catch criminals, but it also puts everyone at risk from other — often worse — bad actors.That’s exactly what happened here. There is obviously an argument for wire-tapping, and it is definitely the case that wire-tapping became much more difficult and complicated with the shift to digital. The arguments for CALEA are clear, and even for the FCC’s expansion to broadband (even if the legal justification is less clear). The insertion of those backdoors into every U.S. telecommunications provider, though, meant the creation of a vulnerability in communications for everyone, the exploitation of which was only a question of time. That time has arrived, via Chinese hackers; they’re to blame for this mess, but not the only ones.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. It couldn’t be determined if systems that support foreign intelligence surveillance were also vulnerable in the breach…A person familiar with the attack said the U.S. government considered the intrusions to be historically significant and worrisome.You might have noticed that the title of this section of the Update uses the passive voice; the reason is because the subject of an active voice isn’t as clear as you might think. In a narrow sense, the subject should be China: “China Hacks U.S. Communications”. That is certainly what has happened here.The History of CALEAIn truth, though, this hack happened a long time time ago. The first possible date is 1994, and the entity at fault is the U.S. Congress. That is when the “Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (CALEA)” was passed.It used to be that a wiretap involved, well, tapping a wire. Law enforcement would gain physical access to a phone line and attach a device to capture the audio of phone conversations. This was possible because the way telephones used to work is that they would convert audio into an analog electrical signal, which would be converted back into audio by the recipient telephone. The intervening analog signal, though, wasn’t encrypted; any device could reconvert it back into audio.The telephone system doesn’t work like that anymore, as you might expect; your device does convert audio into an analog electrical signal, but it then further converts that analog electrical signal into a digital signal — 1s and 0s, basically, which can be encrypted.Encryption, however, can mean multiple things; I discussed the distinction between end-to-end encryption and transport layer encryption this summer inthe context of the Telegram case, but in short:End-to-end encryption means the digital signal is encrypted on the originating device using a key known only to the device owner, and can only be decrypted by the recipient on their device using the corresponding public key for the originator’s private key.Transport layer encryption means the digital signal is encrypted on the originating device using a key granted (or at least known) by the service receiving the communications; that signal can then be decrypted by the service once it arrives. It is, however, encrypted in transit.Transport layer encryption is the standard for most digital communications; your communication between your device and the server you are accessing, whether that be an e-commerce site, a site like Stratechery, or a communications service, is generally safe from so-called “Man-in-the-middle (MITM)” attacks, where a third-party captures your communication and interferes with it in some way.Notice, though, that old-school wiretapping is a canonical man-in-the-middle attack: law enforcement is — again, literally — tapping the wire you are using to communicate and listening in. Law enforcement rather likes this ability, and was concerned about losing this capability in the transition to digital signals.Thus was born CALEA: Congress mandated that communications providers — who remember, are providing the keys for transport layer encryption — create the capability for law enforcement to listen in on conversations, i.e. leverage their control of encryption keys to give access to the underlying data. This is the capability that was leveraged in these attacks, so it’s not wrong to say that “Congress Hacked U.S. Communications”, by creating this capability in the first place.It’s also not entirely right; CALEA actually expressly excluded data. The goal was to recreate the ability to wiretap voice calls, not to expand surveillance. Moreover, phone calls, even as they became digital, still worked differently than the Internet.That last part would soon change. In the decade after CALEA was passed it increasingly made more sense to simply treat voice digital signals the same as other digital signals, using Voice-over-IP (VoIP). Now everything was packets of 1s and 0s, and restricting CALEA to only voice both became more technically challenging and somewhat nonsensical; meanwhile, law enforcement agencies were making the case that more and more criminal communication was happening through means other than voice.To that end, in 2004, the DOJ, ATF, FBI, and DEA filed a petition with the FCC to expand CALEA to cover all IP traffic, including broadband Internet communications; the FCC did so, and when challenged in court,wonthanks to the Chevron deference doctrine, which granted substantial law-making ability to agencies in lieu of explicit Congressional directions. In this case specifically the court held that Congress’s explicit exclusion of “information services” did not necessarily apply to broadband, which frankly, still confuses me; indeed, this case is one of the stronger arguments against Chevron, which was overturned earlier this year.Regardless, the pertinent takeaway for this Article is that you can argue that the “FCC Hacked U.S. Communications”, and the reason is this: yes, the Chinese hacked into U.S. communications, but they did so via a backdoor mandated by CALEA, which was expanded to include internet communications by the FCC. Without CALEA and without that 2004 expansion there would not have been a vulnerability for the Chinese to hack.Encryption and BackdoorsThis case is another manifestation of the age-old debate about encryption and backdoors that has more recently flared up in the context of end-to-end encryption. Many in tech, including myself, resolutely oppose any attempt to create backdoors in end-to-end encryption, even while admitting the trade-offs that entails.This case explains why: backdoors can and will, in the fullness of time, be hacked. The only way to guarantee true privacy in communications is end-to-end encryption; compromising that may make it easier to catch criminals, but it also puts everyone at risk from other — often worse — bad actors.That’s exactly what happened here. There is obviously an argument for wire-tapping, and it is definitely the case that wire-tapping became much more difficult and complicated with the shift to digital. The arguments for CALEA are clear, and even for the FCC’s expansion to broadband (even if the legal justification is less clear). The insertion of those backdoors into every U.S. telecommunications provider, though, meant the creation of a vulnerability in communications for everyone, the exploitation of which was only a question of time. That time has arrived, via Chinese hackers; they’re to blame for this mess, but not the only ones.This Update will be available as a podcast later today.
