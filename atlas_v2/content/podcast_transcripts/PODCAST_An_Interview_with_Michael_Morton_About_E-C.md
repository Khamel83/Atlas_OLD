I can have my Amazon business here, and if I have my standalone DTC business powered by Shopify and I use a third-party logistics company right now that’s providing my fulfillment, I would like it to be separate. That’s what’s been shared with me when I talked to merchants or agency heads. What is going to probably occur over time and what is already occurring today is that no one will be able to compete with Amazon’s speed, scale, and cost. They’re incredibly cost competitive for Fulfillment by Amazon, and they have multichannel fulfillment that shows up in unlabeled boxes and stuff. So as much as they don’t like it now, this is going to be this thing. I think that you check the web traffic and just every month and year, it just ticks higher and higher. Amazon’s in it for the next 20 years, so maybe it’s not a big deal now. All the reporting and scuttlebutt around this is Amazon was the one really pushing for this, Shopify was dragged into it a bit kicking and screaming, but is there still a real hang up as far as sharing data and things along those lines which make it hard? Because I think the dream from a merchant perspective is, “Look, we could just have all our stuff in one place in multiple storefronts”, but if Shopify is not sharing that data between the Shopify site and the warehouse, that gets tricky to manage. Is there any interest in solving that or is Shopify just sort of throwing up as many hurdles as possible while still nominally supporting it? MM:I think the initial friction was because it’s a very sensitive subject on handling the payments and who gets the payments revenue because that’s the bread and butter for Shopify’s business over the long term. It’s where they ring the register. There is some aspect of the data sharing, I obviously have not seen the agreement that was reached by Shopify and Amazon, but from everything that I’ve seen, the friction has been removed and now also it got a lot easier after they divested their logistics business because you had this payments business at risk and then you just bought this logistics business. It was just like headbutting and now you’ve got terms on the payments. And Amazon basically gave them payments. It was like, “You can use your payments”, it really shows that they’re at different goals here. To your point, Shopify wants to own the payments, wants to participate in the upside and Amazon is like, “Look, we’re building the biggest logistics network the world has ever seen and we just want volume and we’ll take it whatever way it gets and it’ll pay off in 25 years”. MM:And Amazon’s fulfillment network is currently underutilized. During COVID, they doubled the square footage and at some point in 2020 and 2021, every single e-commerce company we cover overshot where they made an investment, they hired, they built infrastructure. That was when you clicked and dragged right in your model at one point and it just showed these mind-boggling GMV numbers that everybody had to build to overnight. So getting merchant adoption of Buy with Prime is simple math, you’re pushing more volume through your network, ammortizing the costs over more units and it’s a win-win for everybody. It’s a win for the merchants on Shopify because they increase delivery speed. I’m sure you’ve seen it on the checkout versus the other checkouts. It’s 90% of the business. So as exciting as all the other stuff is, and you’ll see a lot of hand waving around all the other stuff that they’re doing, at the end of the day, this advertising business is going to be driven by unit sales because it’s a seek-and-destroy platform. So if units grow 10% on top of that, you’re going to get some incremental ad load. I think Amazon still thinks there’s room to go there and you’ll get some pricing as advertisers view Amazon as not just an online channel. When you sit and listen to these brands and merchants talk, there is complete consensus that you can’t look at them separately. You can’t just look at them in some myopic ROAS calculation for this one unit we’re selling. Online is impacting offline market share so there’s room to run for Amazon’s advertising product, but we always just like to clarify that 90% of it is the core retail media network. This gets back to your worry for a company that commits to Amazon’s long-term, which is, “Okay, fine. You’re running away from the customer acquisition costs that are imposed on you by Meta and Google”, but what is happening is basically, “Oh, you go to Amazon, you like all this traffic. Well, you’re going to have to pay for that traffic over time”. That’s just the pattern that happens and you see a lot of complaints about this from small, independent, totally commoditized products. But even if you have a good brand, if you’re North Face jacket, how soon till Patagonia is buying North Face AdWords? MM:It’s a ruthlessly competitive market and you hear from a lot of frustrated merchants. This business, the growth of Amazon’s advertising business is one of the most impressive things I think in all of consumer Internet in the last decade from effectively nothing to over a $40 billion run rate business, and there’s a lot of merchants who were in the marketplace five or more years ago when it was simply less efficient. There was this first mover advantage where the ad buyers weren’t all as sophisticated running as like off-site measurement tools and there was an arbitrage. Now, the floodgates have also opened to China-based merchants who are really sophisticated in the ad bidding and the margins have just compressed. This is one of my biggest misses, I would say in my times of Stratechery, is not predicting this, because you talk about it being super impressive, but at the same time it’s the exact thing we saw on Google, the exact same thing we saw on Facebook. If you have the audience and you have, particularly if that audience is search driven, it’s just a matter of executing. Suddenly you’re going to have to pay for what you used to get for free and all the complaints you hear from third-party merchants are all the complaints we heard previously from publishers, it’s all the complaints previously we heard from game developers on Facebook, it’s the same pattern playing over. Once you have the audience, you have the power. MM:Amazon is getting better as well. When you talk to the company, “Hey, how are you still putting up these numbers? This is mind-blowing”. It’s so compelling. This is why the Vizio thing like this is amazing because they just have this burgeoning network and it’s basically just in-store advertising right now. At what point are they giving away Vizio TVs for free? “Look, we need inventory, we need places to put ads” — it’s like the pre-ATT world on steroids, “We can track to the cent this ad resulted in this lift and that’s really compelling”. MM:It’s a game changer. Whoever can really prove incrementality for these low volume growth industries like CPG wins an enormous advertising pool of dollars. The challenge is the CPG companies are sophisticated and they don’t want to pay for transactions that were already going to happen. I always use the example, I consume an unhealthy amount of peanut butter every year and no peanut butter company wants to make themselves pay for Mike’s peanut butter purchase because they know I was going to buy that brand anyways. Walmart is the one that has the ability to show that. I think it’s important to split, let’s just call it the grocery store, what’s sold under that roof into two umbrellas and it’s perishables and what we refer to as non-discretionary. I think Walmart refers to in conversations with people as consumables. So in the perishables, that’s groceries. That’s like your eggs and beef and meat and then in the other side it’s baby wipes and shampoo. And on the perishable aspect, Walmart has 4,000 distribution locations and they’re the stores that cover 90% of America. MM:It’s fresh and no one can rival the purchasing power of Walmart. So we’ve done price comparisons in America’s largest suburban markets for food delivery and Walmart is roughly 25% cheaper than the kind of average — Amazon is middle of the pack, I think Target is second place — and when you look at the non-discretionary stuff, the shampoos of the world, this is intuitive and I think makes sense that Walmart and Amazon are at parity, so that market is going to be split up. I think Walmart just their focus is to win grocery due to the purchasing frequency and then they’re going to try to convert people and make their money back on the general merchandise category. Amazon realizes they can’t muscle their way into this. The legacy grocery industry has like a hundred year head start of them in stores in every town. Well, it’s totally counter to what Amazon does. Amazon is the most scale-focused company there is and plan everything they do around that. It’s predicated on goods being non-perishable, they just have to be, it doesn’t work otherwise. It’s implied to be a big portion of the growth in e-commerce going forward. Amazon could sink a lot of money in this if they really wanted to, that’s why everybody talks about it and it’s so scary. But I’m not sure if it’s going to work, which is why Walmart, to the extent Walmart, they can dominate that, then they can move into your point with a price advantage or distribution advantage or more importantly, a customer acquisition advantage in the stuff they do compete with Amazon with. They’re pretty open, they don’t have the solution today for this problem.
