This is what I wrote in 2022’sThe Services iPhone(note: the chart in the excerpt was updated to be current as of this Update):The most surprising announcement of all, though, were the prices. Everything stayed the same! This was not what I, or close followers of Applelike John Gruber, expected at all. After all, Apple’s strategy the past several years seemed to be focused on wringing more revenue out of existing customers. More importantly, the last year has seen a big increase in inflation:What this means is that in real terms Apple’s products actually gotcheaper. Apple did, to be sure, raise prices around the world, but this is better explained by the fact the company runs on the dollar, which is the strongest in years; to put it another way, those foreign prices are derived from the U.S. price, and that price stayed the same, which means the price is lower.This doesn’t make much sense for the product company Apple has always been thought to be, and doesn’t fully align with the approach I laid out inApple’s Middle Age. It does, though, make all kinds of sense for a services company, which is focused first-and-foremost on increasing its install base. Indeed, this is the missing piece from that Update I wrote about Apple’s changing metrics. To measure its business based on users, not products, was to measure like a services company; to lower the prices of the products that lead to services revenue is to price like one.Apple mostly kept the same prices last year, which I illustrated with a chart showing the relative decline in real terms; here is the chart updated with 2024 prices:202020212022202320242 year old iPhone$499$477$441$422$4111 year old iPhone$599$572$530$507$493New iPhone$799$763$707$676$657iPhone Pro$999$954$883$846$822iPhone Pro Max$1,099$1,050$972$1,015$904Last year’s lack of price raises was gratifying for the way in which it seemed to confirm my 2022 thesis, but it’s possible I was right for the wrong reasons — and that the right reason is why I do expect a price increase later today, which is another way of saying I guess I am interested in Apple Intelligence after all!Specifically, recall that Apple Intelligence is only available on the iPhone 15 Pro, not because Apple is arbitrarily trying to drive upgrades, but because the iPhone 15 Pro is the only device that has 8GB of RAM — and language models, even the scaled-down ones that is running on iOS, need a lot of RAM.To that end, the expectation is that all new iPhone models announced today will have at least 8GB of RAM, and, well, RAM costs money. Apple may have been willing to eat the inflation discount for the last few years, but I don’t expect the company to give away extra margin on top of that. And, to that end, while the company may not have had time (or capacity at TSMC) to get 8GB of RAM into last year’s iPhone 15, they probably already knew that a RAM increase was in the cards, and decided to save the increase for this year.To that end, my guess is that Apple increases the price of every model by $100, probably in conjunction with an increase in the base level of storage for the Pro model. I might be wrong on this point though — more storage does make the higher price land more gently, since Apple can point out that’s how much a 256GB phone cost last year (this is how they raised the price on the Max in 2023).I also, for the record, don’t think this would undo my take inThe Services iPhone; inflation over the last few years has been so high that all of these models would still be priced at a significant discount in real terms to 2020. And, if I’m wrong about a price increase this year, that all but cements the thesis.DirecTV and DisneyFrom theWall Street Journalon September 1:More than 11 million DirecTV subscribers lost access Sunday afternoon to channels owned by Disney, including ESPN and ABC, as the satellite broadcaster and entertainment giant were unable to come to terms on a new distribution deal. The two companies are divided over an increase in fees Disney is seeking to carry some of its more popular channels as well as DirecTV’s desire to have more flexibility in how it sells those channels to its customers, people involved in the negotiations said. “Disney is seeking too much money for what they are granting us,” said DirecTV Chief Content Officer Rob Thun in an interview.Disney said DirecTV is seeking rates that aren’t in line with what other major distributors pay to carry its networks, according to Justin Connolly, president of Disney platform distribution. For DirecTV subscribers, the blackout comes as the college football season has started and days before the NFL season kicks off. The Disney networks went dark Sunday just before the start of the USC-LSU football game on ABC and during coverage of the U.S. Open tennis tournament on ESPN2.There’s a good chance this dispute is settled some time today, before the first NFL football game of the season on ESPN is impacted; to that end it is interesting to compare this dispute to the showdown between Charter and Disney last year. There are two important structural differences from what I wrote about inThe Rise and Fall of ESPN’s Leverage.First, there was a time when DirecTV was a massive asset for Disney relative to the cable companies, because they were a service consumers could switch to if the cable company didn’t have the channels they wanted; now, though, the cable companies mostly make their money off of broadband, which is why Charter was willing to stand up to Disney far more aggressively than they did in the past.On the flipside, though, this demonstrates DirecTV’s weakness in this negotiation: DirecTV doesn’t sell broadband, which means that they don’t really have a reason to exist without channels like ESPN; this in theory gives them much less leverage than Charter.What makes DirecTV unique, however, is their dominance of public spaces like bars; this is much more important to Disney than it might have been five years ago, thanks to the addition of out-of-home viewing to ratings calculations in 2020. Out-of-home viewing makes upan average of 13% of the audiencefor sports; that means that if DirecTV stays dark ESPN’s ratings will take a big hit, which could cost Disney real money in terms of make-goods for advertisers.Bars and SportsAs an aside, I thought this tweet from Frank Madden raised an interesting point:One major downside of streaming sporting events: I’m at a sports bar and every TV with the Packer game on is lagging the Peacock feed at different rates…and somehow they were piping in audio from a source that was ahead of all of them so they had to turn the audio off entirely— Frank Madden (@fmaddenNBA)September 7, 2024There are a few interesting points here that relate to bars and other public spaces.Public gathering spaces cannot use a normal cable feed or, in the case of Peacock, a normal subscription; rather they have to pay for a special package that is priced based on the maximum total occupancy of the space (you may recall that every sports broadcast contains a message about how it is illegal to show the broadcast in a public space without express authorization). As you might expect, these packages can be quite expensive.Peacock has been an especially big headache for bars because, unlike almost every other sports package, it is not sold through DirecTV (DirecTV, for example, retains the right to NFL Sunday Ticket for commercial spaces; YouTubeTV has the residential rights). Rather, you have to sign a deal with a company calledEverPass; EverPass is a joint venture between the NFL and RedBird Capital formed last year selling commercial rights to the NFL.The cheapest equipment solution for bars was to just have a DirecTV receiver at every TV; as Madden noted, though, that doesn’t really work for streaming. Rather, to provide a good bar-wide streaming experience, you would need to invest in something like a video matrix — which are not cheap! — that pulls in one video feed and distributes it (and the audio) to all of the devices on the network.I don’t have any grand takeaways here, other than to note that the abandonment of the cable bundle and the shift to streaming isn’t just a headache for consumers; it’s a big problem for public gathering points as well, which is kind of a bummer.Anyhow, back to the dispute with Disney; fromVariety:The acrimony between DirecTV and Disney in their contract fight is getting more heated, as the blackout of Disney-owned networks on DirecTV is all but certain to extend into a second week. On Saturday, DirecTV filed a complaint with the Federal Communications Commission, alleging Disney has failed to negotiate in good faith. DirecTV asserted that Disney has violated the FCC’s good-faith mandates by predicating any licensing agreement on DirecTV’s waiving any legal claims on Disney’s “anticompetitive actions,” including its ongoing packaging and minimum penetration demands.The key point in DirecTV’scomplaintis the proposed Venu sports streaming service thatFubo successfully blocked. DirecTV wants to offer its own skinny bundles, including packages that are primarily sports, and some that don’t have any sports at all, and Disney wants penetration guarantees and rates that DirecTV complains make those offerings uneconomical. That’s all normal, to be clear, but DirecTV’s specific complaint is about tying those bundles to a covenant not to sue. From the complaint:The negotiations have stalled because Disney insists on bundling and penetration requirements that a federal district court judge in New York recently found in the context of the “Venu” joint venture to be unlawful, anticompetitive, and “bad for consumers.” Disney wants to force DIRECTV to carry a “fat bundle” including less desirable Disney programming — while itself offering cheaper, “skinnier” bundles of programming that consumers want. The Commission has never considered a good faith complaint in these circumstances, and DIRECTV may well wish to bring one in the future concerning Disney’s conduct.Along with these anticompetitive demands, Disney has also insisted that DIRECTV agree to a “clean slate” provision and a covenant not to sue, both of which are intended to prevent DIRECTV from taking legal action regarding Disney’s anticompetitive demands, which would include filing good faith complaints at the Commission. Not three months ago, however, the Media Bureau made clear that such a demand itself constitutes bad faith. DIRECTV accordingly brings this Complaint asking the Commission to declare Disney’s clean slate and covenant not to sue demands to be in bad faith, and to take whatever other action it deems appropriateIt’s hard to read this as anything other than Disney looking to clear the decks for Venu — or perhaps the upcoming ESPN standalone service — by foreclosing another antitrust lawsuit like the one Fubo looks poised to win; I suspect the idea is to let DirecTV have a sports-centric skinny bundle, leave room to undercut it in price with Venu, and avoid a lawsuit via this covenant (and use the existence of this deal to demonstrate that Fubo doesn’t have a case).I don’t have any view into how the FCC might rule on this complaint; my guess is they never do, and that this carriage dispute is settled with something close to the status quo (i.e. no real skinny bundles and higher rates, but no protection for Venu in court). Both companies are in pretty poor negotiating positions here, although I think DirecTV has the weaker hand (because any lost subscribers are probably gone forever); I would expect the final deal to reflect that.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. Everything stayed the same! This was not what I, or close followers of Applelike John Gruber, expected at all. After all, Apple’s strategy the past several years seemed to be focused on wringing more revenue out of existing customers. More importantly, the last year has seen a big increase in inflation:What this means is that in real terms Apple’s products actually gotcheaper. Apple did, to be sure, raise prices around the world, but this is better explained by the fact the company runs on the dollar, which is the strongest in years; to put it another way, those foreign prices are derived from the U.S. price, and that price stayed the same, which means the price is lower.This doesn’t make much sense for the product company Apple has always been thought to be, and doesn’t fully align with the approach I laid out inApple’s Middle Age. It does, though, make all kinds of sense for a services company, which is focused first-and-foremost on increasing its install base. Indeed, this is the missing piece from that Update I wrote about Apple’s changing metrics. Apple may have been willing to eat the inflation discount for the last few years, but I don’t expect the company to give away extra margin on top of that. And, to that end, while the company may not have had time (or capacity at TSMC) to get 8GB of RAM into last year’s iPhone 15, they probably already knew that a RAM increase was in the cards, and decided to save the increase for this year.To that end, my guess is that Apple increases the price of every model by $100, probably in conjunction with an increase in the base level of storage for the Pro model. And, if I’m wrong about a price increase this year, that all but cements the thesis.DirecTV and DisneyFrom theWall Street Journalon September 1:More than 11 million DirecTV subscribers lost access Sunday afternoon to channels owned by Disney, including ESPN and ABC, as the satellite broadcaster and entertainment giant were unable to come to terms on a new distribution deal. The two companies are divided over an increase in fees Disney is seeking to carry some of its more popular channels as well as DirecTV’s desire to have more flexibility in how it sells those channels to its customers, people involved in the negotiations said. “Disney is seeking too much money for what they are granting us,” said DirecTV Chief Content Officer Rob Thun in an interview.Disney said DirecTV is seeking rates that aren’t in line with what other major distributors pay to carry its networks, according to Justin Connolly, president of Disney platform distribution. For DirecTV subscribers, the blackout comes as the college football season has started and days before the NFL season kicks off. The Disney networks went dark Sunday just before the start of the USC-LSU football game on ABC and during coverage of the U.S. Open tennis tournament on ESPN2.There’s a good chance this dispute is settled some time today, before the first NFL football game of the season on ESPN is impacted; to that end it is interesting to compare this dispute to the showdown between Charter and Disney last year. There are two important structural differences from what I wrote about inThe Rise and Fall of ESPN’s Leverage.First, there was a time when DirecTV was a massive asset for Disney relative to the cable companies, because they were a service consumers could switch to if the cable company didn’t have the channels they wanted; now, though, the cable companies mostly make their money off of broadband, which is why Charter was willing to stand up to Disney far more aggressively than they did in the past.On the flipside, though, this demonstrates DirecTV’s weakness in this negotiation: DirecTV doesn’t sell broadband, which means that they don’t really have a reason to exist without channels like ESPN; this in theory gives them much less leverage than Charter.What makes DirecTV unique, however, is their dominance of public spaces like bars; this is much more important to Disney than it might have been five years ago, thanks to the addition of out-of-home viewing to ratings calculations in 2020. Out-of-home viewing makes upan average of 13% of the audiencefor sports; that means that if DirecTV stays dark ESPN’s ratings will take a big hit, which could cost Disney real money in terms of make-goods for advertisers.Bars and SportsAs an aside, I thought this tweet from Frank Madden raised an interesting point:One major downside of streaming sporting events: I’m at a sports bar and every TV with the Packer game on is lagging the Peacock feed at different rates…and somehow they were piping in audio from a source that was ahead of all of them so they had to turn the audio off entirely— Frank Madden (@fmaddenNBA)September 7, 2024There are a few interesting points here that relate to bars and other public spaces.Public gathering spaces cannot use a normal cable feed or, in the case of Peacock, a normal subscription; rather they have to pay for a special package that is priced based on the maximum total occupancy of the space (you may recall that every sports broadcast contains a message about how it is illegal to show the broadcast in a public space without express authorization). As you might expect, these packages can be quite expensive.Peacock has been an especially big headache for bars because, unlike almost every other sports package, it is not sold through DirecTV (DirecTV, for example, retains the right to NFL Sunday Ticket for commercial spaces; YouTubeTV has the residential rights). Rather, you have to sign a deal with a company calledEverPass; EverPass is a joint venture between the NFL and RedBird Capital formed last year selling commercial rights to the NFL.The cheapest equipment solution for bars was to just have a DirecTV receiver at every TV; as Madden noted, though, that doesn’t really work for streaming. Rather, to provide a good bar-wide streaming experience, you would need to invest in something like a video matrix — which are not cheap! — that pulls in one video feed and distributes it (and the audio) to all of the devices on the network.I don’t have any grand takeaways here, other than to note that the abandonment of the cable bundle and the shift to streaming isn’t just a headache for consumers; it’s a big problem for public gathering points as well, which is kind of a bummer.Anyhow, back to the dispute with Disney; fromVariety:The acrimony between DirecTV and Disney in their contract fight is getting more heated, as the blackout of Disney-owned networks on DirecTV is all but certain to extend into a second week. On Saturday, DirecTV filed a complaint with the Federal Communications Commission, alleging Disney has failed to negotiate in good faith. DirecTV asserted that Disney has violated the FCC’s good-faith mandates by predicating any licensing agreement on DirecTV’s waiving any legal claims on Disney’s “anticompetitive actions,” including its ongoing packaging and minimum penetration demands.The key point in DirecTV’scomplaintis the proposed Venu sports streaming service thatFubo successfully blocked. DirecTV wants to offer its own skinny bundles, including packages that are primarily sports, and some that don’t have any sports at all, and Disney wants penetration guarantees and rates that DirecTV complains make those offerings uneconomical. That’s all normal, to be clear, but DirecTV’s specific complaint is about tying those bundles to a covenant not to sue. From the complaint:The negotiations have stalled because Disney insists on bundling and penetration requirements that a federal district court judge in New York recently found in the context of the “Venu” joint venture to be unlawful, anticompetitive, and “bad for consumers.” Disney wants to force DIRECTV to carry a “fat bundle” including less desirable Disney programming — while itself offering cheaper, “skinnier” bundles of programming that consumers want. The Commission has never considered a good faith complaint in these circumstances, and DIRECTV may well wish to bring one in the future concerning Disney’s conduct.Along with these anticompetitive demands, Disney has also insisted that DIRECTV agree to a “clean slate” provision and a covenant not to sue, both of which are intended to prevent DIRECTV from taking legal action regarding Disney’s anticompetitive demands, which would include filing good faith complaints at the Commission. Not three months ago, however, the Media Bureau made clear that such a demand itself constitutes bad faith. DIRECTV accordingly brings this Complaint asking the Commission to declare Disney’s clean slate and covenant not to sue demands to be in bad faith, and to take whatever other action it deems appropriateIt’s hard to read this as anything other than Disney looking to clear the decks for Venu — or perhaps the upcoming ESPN standalone service — by foreclosing another antitrust lawsuit like the one Fubo looks poised to win; I suspect the idea is to let DirecTV have a sports-centric skinny bundle, leave room to undercut it in price with Venu, and avoid a lawsuit via this covenant (and use the existence of this deal to demonstrate that Fubo doesn’t have a case).I don’t have any view into how the FCC might rule on this complaint; my guess is they never do, and that this carriage dispute is settled with something close to the status quo (i.e. no real skinny bundles and higher rates, but no protection for Venu in court). Both companies are in pretty poor negotiating positions here, although I think DirecTV has the weaker hand (because any lost subscribers are probably gone forever); I would expect the final deal to reflect that.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. DirecTV asserted that Disney has violated the FCC’s good-faith mandates by predicating any licensing agreement on DirecTV’s waiving any legal claims on Disney’s “anticompetitive actions,” including its ongoing packaging and minimum penetration demands. The key point in DirecTV’scomplaintis the proposed Venu sports streaming service thatFubo successfully blocked. DirecTV wants to offer its own skinny bundles, including packages that are primarily sports, and some that don’t have any sports at all, and Disney wants penetration guarantees and rates that DirecTV complains make those offerings uneconomical. That’s all normal, to be clear, but DirecTV’s specific complaint is about tying those bundles to a covenant not to sue. From the complaint: The negotiations have stalled because Disney insists on bundling and penetration requirements that a federal district court judge in New York recently found in the context of the “Venu” joint venture to be unlawful, anticompetitive, and “bad for consumers.” Disney wants to force DIRECTV to carry a “fat bundle” including less desirable Disney programming — while itself offering cheaper, “skinnier” bundles of programming that consumers want. The Commission has never considered a good faith complaint in these circumstances, and DIRECTV may well wish to bring one in the future concerning Disney’s conduct. Along with these anticompetitive demands, Disney has also insisted that DIRECTV agree to a “clean slate” provision and a covenant not to sue, both of which are intended to prevent DIRECTV from taking legal action regarding Disney’s anticompetitive demands, which would include filing good faith complaints at the Commission. Not three months ago, however, the Media Bureau made clear that such a demand itself constitutes bad faith. DIRECTV accordingly brings this Complaint asking the Commission to declare Disney’s clean slate and covenant not to sue demands to be in bad faith, and to take whatever other action it deems appropriate It’s hard to read this as anything other than Disney looking to clear the decks for Venu — or perhaps the upcoming ESPN standalone service — by foreclosing another antitrust lawsuit like the one Fubo looks poised to win; I suspect the idea is to let DirecTV have a sports-centric skinny bundle, leave room to undercut it in price with Venu, and avoid a lawsuit via this covenant (and use the existence of this deal to demonstrate that Fubo doesn’t have a case). I don’t have any view into how the FCC might rule on this complaint; my guess is they never do, and that this carriage dispute is settled with something close to the status quo (i.e. no real skinny bundles and higher rates, but no protection for Venu in court). Both companies are in pretty poor negotiating positions here, although I think DirecTV has the weaker hand (because any lost subscribers are probably gone forever); I would expect the final deal to reflect that.
