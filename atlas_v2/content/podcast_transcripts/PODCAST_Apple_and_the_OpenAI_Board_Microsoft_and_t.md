There will be a new episode ofSharp Techtomorrow: instead of two shorter episodes we will be doing one longer episode a week, and I expect we’ll be spending a lot of time on reader questions (for those who are not regular listeners, Sharp Tech is the best venue to bring up questions and topics you are curious about: you can send your questions toemail@sharptech.fmand we may cover them on the show).On to the Update:Apple and the OpenAI BoardFrom theFinancial Times:Microsoft has given up its seat as an observer on the board of OpenAI while Apple will not take up a similar position, amid growing scrutiny by global regulators of Big Tech’s investments in AI start-ups. Microsoft, which has invested $13bn in the maker of the generative AI chatbot ChatGPT, said in a letter to OpenAI that its withdrawal from its board role would be “effective immediately”. Apple had also been expected to take an observer role on OpenAI’s board as part of a deal to integrate ChatGPT into the iPhone maker’s devices, but would not do so, according to a person with direct knowledge of the matter. Apple declined to comment.Let’s start with Apple: I can’t decide if I’m lucky or unlucky that I didn’t yet write aboutlast week’s newsthat Phil Schiller would become an OpenAI board observer on behalf of Apple. On one hand, there is the obvious take: wow, it sure is weird that Apple gets a board observer seat for free while Microsoft only got one after investing $15 billion and nearly seeing OpenAI implode because of conflict between the board and CEO Sam Altman! Well, that is a take that is now up in smoke.In fact, though, I thought that Apple was making a mistake in taking the board observer seat, for a number of reasons. Go back towhat I wrote after the WWDC announcement of the OpenAI partnership:Federighi makes the same point I did above: there are lots of things that broad-based models like ChatGPT are good at that Apple isn’t even attempting to take on, and why would they? These models cost an astronomical amount of money to train and inference, and whatever differentiation exists is predicated on factors that Apple doesn’t have a competitive advantage in. What Apple does have is a massive userbase that the model that wants to win will desire access to, so why not trade that access for the ability to leverage whichever model agrees to Apple’s terms?…To put it another way, and in Stratechery terms, Apple is positioning itself as an AI Aggregator: the company owns users and, by extension, generative AI demand by virtue of owning its platforms, and it is deepening its moat through Apple Intelligence, which only Apple can do; that demand is then being brought to bear on suppliers who probably have to eat the costs of getting privileged access to Apple’s userbase.The implication of being an Aggregator is that you don’t need to explicitly control your suppliers; you can simply set the terms of access to your userbase and thus exert far more implicit control without the messiness of direct intervention or regulatory concerns (more on this below). Indeed, that is exactly what has played out in the App Store: Apple controls developers without owning or observing any of them, up to and including behemoths like Meta. Think about something like App Tracking Transparency (ATT):Apple obliterated billions of dollars of revenueand completely remade the digital ad industry without raising a regulatory hackle just by changing some App Store rules (another example of whyApple deserves extra scrutiny).An important factor in making this strategy work, though, is diversification in supply: a disgruntled developer who wants to go around Apple to, say, support a new platform, needs to recruit a massive number of developers to go with them; this is such a challenging problem that Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta all failed in various efforts to do just that. Aggregating AI is a bit different, at least for now, because that privileged position in Apple Intelligence does depend on making a deal with Apple, but (1) that is why Apple is correctlytalking to Anthropic and Googleabout offering alternatives (and thus competition and, by extension, more control for Apple), (2) why Apple really ought towarm up to Meta, and (3) would be well-served (and can skip over the messy optics of point 2 while getting the same benefits) in making that privileged position completely modular and a market in its own right.Becoming more tightly tied to OpenAI in any way beyond the current agreement worked against all of these advantages. In the near term, having a board observer position with OpenAI is a signal to Anthropic and Google that they are at an inherent disadvantage when it comes to that privileged position with OpenAI, reducing the likelihood or requiring more concessions to get additional partnerships. In the longer term, it is important that Apple does not integrate too tightly with OpenAI from a product perspective, because to be an Aggregator is to offer a modular interface which any supplier can plug into, the better to maximize competition for access to your users; however, given that AI is new, there will be a strong temptation within product teams to do the opposite, and having board-level access might encourage product teams to integrate too deeply with one provider.Finally, the optics were totally wrong for Apple. Sure, it might have felt good to embarrass Microsoft, but joining the board of OpenAI emphasizes the narrative that Apple needs OpenAI, suggests that OpenAI is far more integral to Apple Intelligence than it actually is, and puts Apple in the firing line for any future OpenAI controversies. This was a bad idea, and the company is right to back out.Microsoft and the OpenAI BoardThe Microsoft situation is much more complicated, and speaks to the differences in their own value chain. Namely, Microsoft is by-and-large not an Aggregator when it comes to AI:The company has that $15 billion investment in OpenAI, which gives them a right to future profits; this by extension means that Microsoft is invested in OpenAI specifically succeeding.Microsoft is incorporating OpenAI’s technologies into its products. This means that the company is right to seek more control rather than less, because it wants to make its products better, but this also means that OpenAI has more direct impact on Microsoft’s business outcomes.Microsoft does offer a marketplace of sorts when it comes to Azure, but it is pursuing customers based on the AI offerings it can bring to bear; this is in contrast to Apple which has users for reasons independent of AI, which gives them more power over AI companies (Microsoft does have more power in terms of companies that are already on Azure and which want to add AI, but if that is the relevant level of competition than they are back in the same 2nd-place position relative to AWS that they are for the rest of cloud computing).Given all of this, Microsoft should want to be a board observer, particularly givenwhat happened last November; the complication in this case is likely the theme of the week: regulators. FromBloomberglate last month:Microsoft Corp.’s $13 billion investment into OpenAI Inc. is set to come under added scrutiny from European Union’s antitrust watchdogs, who are poised to quiz rivals about the AI firm’s exclusive use of Microsoft’s cloud technology. Margrethe Vestager, the bloc’s antitrust chief, announced Friday that the EU has ruled out an investigation under the EU’s merger rules into the deal. Instead, she announced that regulators are asking Microsoft’s rivals about the US company’s exclusivity clauses with OpenAI, and whether they might have a negative effect on competition.And, from theNew York Timesin early June:Federal regulators have reached a deal that allows them to proceed with antitrust investigations into the dominant roles that Microsoft, OpenAI and Nvidia play in the artificial intelligence industry, in the strongest sign of how regulatory scrutiny into the powerful technology has escalated…Under the arrangement, the Justice Department will take the lead in investigating whether the behavior of Nvidia, the biggest maker of A.I. chips, has violated antitrust laws, the people said. The F.T.C. will play the lead role in examining the conduct of OpenAI, which makes the ChatGPT chatbot, and Microsoft, which has invested $13 billion in OpenAI and made deals with other A.I. companies, the people said.The irony of last fall’s OpenAI near-meltdown is that (1) it suggested that Microsoft doesn’t have very much explicit control over OpenAI (its control is about having GPUs), (2) completely justified Microsoft taking on a board observer seat, which (3) suggests to regulators that Microsoft has control of OpenAI. It’s really a bit of a conundrum for the company that is ultimately downstream from taking a dependency on technology it doesn’t control. At the end of the day, though, access to that technology is more important than whatever influence or information a board observer seat might garner, which means point three is the one that matters: I see Microsoft’s exit as an indication that the company does see a real regulatory threat to its OpenAI relationship and is seeking to minimize that threat as much as possible.This, for the record, was Microsoft’s explanation in a letter to OpenAI, asreported by Axios:“We appreciate the support shown by OpenAI leadership and the OpenAI board as we made this decision,” Microsoft said. “As you know, we accepted the non-voting board observer role at a time when OpenAI was in the process of rebuilding its board,” Microsoft said in the letter. “This position provided insights into the board’s activities without compromising its independence, and we appreciated the opportunity to serve as an observer during this period of change. Over the past eight months we have witnessed significant progress from the newly formed board and are confident in the company’s direction. Given all of this we no longer believe our limited role as an observer is necessary,” Microsoft added.This doesn’t really hold water. Microsoft has probably attended what, two or three board meetings since November? I guess you could make the case that Microsoft is confident in its relationship with Altman and believes he has consolidated control over OpenAI since November, which mostly seems correct. There is also the fact that Microsoft appears to be makingsignificant investments in diversifying away from OpenAI, which themselves are undergoingregulatory scrutiny from the FTC; both points suggest that Microsoft is wise to untangle its enmeshment with OpenAI, at least publicly, but it is a decision that entails much more risk than Apple’s.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. Think about something like App Tracking Transparency (ATT):Apple obliterated billions of dollars of revenueand completely remade the digital ad industry without raising a regulatory hackle just by changing some App Store rules (another example of whyApple deserves extra scrutiny).An important factor in making this strategy work, though, is diversification in supply: a disgruntled developer who wants to go around Apple to, say, support a new platform, needs to recruit a massive number of developers to go with them; this is such a challenging problem that Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta all failed in various efforts to do just that. Aggregating AI is a bit different, at least for now, because that privileged position in Apple Intelligence does depend on making a deal with Apple, but (1) that is why Apple is correctlytalking to Anthropic and Googleabout offering alternatives (and thus competition and, by extension, more control for Apple), (2) why Apple really ought towarm up to Meta, and (3) would be well-served (and can skip over the messy optics of point 2 while getting the same benefits) in making that privileged position completely modular and a market in its own right.Becoming more tightly tied to OpenAI in any way beyond the current agreement worked against all of these advantages. In the near term, having a board observer position with OpenAI is a signal to Anthropic and Google that they are at an inherent disadvantage when it comes to that privileged position with OpenAI, reducing the likelihood or requiring more concessions to get additional partnerships. In the longer term, it is important that Apple does not integrate too tightly with OpenAI from a product perspective, because to be an Aggregator is to offer a modular interface which any supplier can plug into, the better to maximize competition for access to your users; however, given that AI is new, there will be a strong temptation within product teams to do the opposite, and having board-level access might encourage product teams to integrate too deeply with one provider.Finally, the optics were totally wrong for Apple. Sure, it might have felt good to embarrass Microsoft, but joining the board of OpenAI emphasizes the narrative that Apple needs OpenAI, suggests that OpenAI is far more integral to Apple Intelligence than it actually is, and puts Apple in the firing line for any future OpenAI controversies. This was a bad idea, and the company is right to back out.Microsoft and the OpenAI BoardThe Microsoft situation is much more complicated, and speaks to the differences in their own value chain.
