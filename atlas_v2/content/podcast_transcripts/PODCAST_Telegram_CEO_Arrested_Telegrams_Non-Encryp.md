The arrest of the 39-year-old technology billionaire prompted on Sunday a warning from Moscow to Paris that he should be accorded his rights and criticism from X owner Elon Musk who said that free speech in Europe was under attack.There was no official confirmation from France of the arrest, but two French police sources and one Russian source who spoke on condition of anonymity said that Durov was arrested shortly after arriving at Le Bourget airport on a private jet from Azerbaijan…Durov, who has dual French and United Arab Emirates citizenship, was arrested as part of a preliminary police investigation into allegedly allowing a wide range of crimes due to a lack of moderators on Telegram and a lack of cooperation with police, a third French police source said…The encrypted application, with close to 1 billion users, is particularly influential in Russia, Ukraine and the republics of the former Soviet Union. It is ranked as one of the major social media platforms after Facebook, YouTube, WhatsApp, Instagram, TikTok and WeChat.There is a lot going on with this story, but let’s start with that last paragraph: it’s wrong, at least in the way you ought to think about encryption. The most basic level of encryption is between your device and a server; this is and has been the standard for basically every Internet application for years (even most webpages are encrypted in transit at this point). Telegram messages are encrypted between your device and your server. However, messages on the server are not encrypted, which means they can be accessed by Telegram.That is why “encryption” in the context of messaging means end-to-end encryption; this means that your messages are encrypted on your device and can only ever be decrypted and thus read by your intended recipient. Telegram does support this with “Secret Chats”, but these are not the default. Moreover, Telegram’s implementation has a lot of oddities, including some non-standard encryption techniques, the fact that secret chats can only be between two devices (not two accounts, so you can’t access a secret chat started on your phone from your computer), and that both users have to be online at the same time to initiate a secret chat (I’ll come back to these oddities in a moment).Group chats, meanwhile, are not encrypted at all: everyone and everything posted in a Telegram group chat (and one-on-one chats by default) are stored on Telegram’s servers in unencrypted form (or encrypted with a key Telegram owns). This is in contrast to Signal and WhatsApp, which encrypt all conversations by default, both one-on-one chats and also group chats.Telegram’s Non-Encrypted AdvantageOne thing that is important to note here is that there are real advantages that come fromnotbeing encrypted. Telegram chats, for example, have their full history easily accessible from any device you sign in with; sync — both in terms of ongoing messages, and history — is much easier (or even viable) when you don’t have to deal with encryption. It is also far more scalable: WhatsApp and Signal only recently increased their group sizes to 1,024 and 1,000 maximum users recently, while iMessage is capped at 32.This does, as an aside, speak to some minute but important differences in security between the three apps:iMessage’s maximum group size is so small because every message in a group chat is actually sent directly between the sender and every person in the group chat; in other words, when you send a message to a 32 person group chat, you are actually sending 32 separate messages (and potentially more if some of those users are using iMessage on multiple devices). This is also the most secure implementation: there is both forward secrecy (breaking one message does not give you access to past messages) and self-healing (breaking one message gives you access to future messages, until you are off-line for a single message, at which point you have lost the chain).Signal uses what is called client-side fan-out: after an initial back-and-forth exchange with everyone in the group, including the exchange of “send keys” for everyone in the group, you send one message that is individually-encrypted for everyone in the group. This provides forward secrecy, but sacrifices self-healing; however, because the “send keys” are themselves encrypted, there is plausible deniability, in that you don’t know for certain who sent a particular message (Signal does maintain the structure of the group in encrypted form on its servers to maintain a consistent state for all users over time).WhatsApp is the most reliant on a central server, using what is called “server-side fan-out”: (encrypted) messages are sent to a central server (with “send keys” for everyone in the group) and are then distributed by the server to everyone. Part of this process entails maintaining the structure of the group centrally, and while there is a degree of plausible deniability, it does not go as far as Signal does.The actual specifics of how these various systems work are incredibly complex (and my apologies in anticipation if these simplifications got any details wrong); if you want to understand more I highly recommend these YouTube videos fromComputerphile:How Signal Instant Messaging Protocol Works (& WhatsApp etc)Double Ratchet Messaging EncryptionWhat’s Up With Group Messaging(which includes a quick note at the end that one way to compromise these groups is by automatically adding an additional “silent” member of the group)Here is the important takeaway with regards to Telegram, though: Telegram didn’t do any of that, which has arguably been a feature, not a bug. Two of the biggest Telegram features that have helped fuel its popularity are Channels and Groups:Channels are one-way broadcasts, that may also allow for reactions and comments. This is more akin to a blog or a Facebook page. Channels can have an unlimited number of subscribers.Groups are, well, group chats, but they can scale to 200,000 members. Note that that is a two orders of magnitude increase in capacity compared to WhatsApp and Signal; this is directly related to the fact that Telegram groups are not end-to-end encrypted.I think that this point is crucial to understanding Telegram and its place in the market. Yes, the company has played extremely fast-and-easy with how it characterizes its product, to the extent that most users and media (i.e. the excerpt above) mistakenly believe the product has more inherent security than it does. What is also true, though, is that Telegram’s specific feature set are integral to the product-market fit it obtained: there is a place in the market for large scalable group discussion, and filling that hole in the market does depend on an absence of end-to-end encryption.Telegram ComplexitiesThere is one more important fact to know about Telegram, and some important observations and allegations; from theFinancial Timesin March:Pavel Durov told the Financial Times that Dubai-based Telegram had grown to become one of the world’s most popular social media apps while making “hundreds of millions of dollars” in revenues after introducing advertising and premium subscription services two years ago. “We are hoping to become profitable next year, if not this year,” said the Russia-born founder in his first public interview since 2017. He added that the platform has 900mn monthly active users, up from 500mn at the beginning of 2021…Once largely home to the freewheeling cryptocurrency community, the company, which only has about 50 full-time employees, has exploded in popularity over the past few years to become a vital communication tool for governments and officials globally, as well as a lifeline to citizens in conflict zones. Researchers warn that the lightly moderated platform remains a hotbed for criminal activity, as well as extremist or terrorist content and misinformation. Critics have suggested that the Kremlin may have links to or leverage over Telegram, a claim that Durov dismissed as “inaccurate”.Start with the fact: Telegram the company is very small. It’s so small, in fact, that I do think the most plausible explanation for why the company doesn’t have a more fully-featured end-to-end encryption offering is that it doesn’t really have the resources for something that its users haven’t seemed to care about. Go back to the secret chats I described above: yes, the implementation is weird, but it’s also “easy”, at least relative to the complicated server-mediated key exchange processes that WhatsApp and Signal employ (the latter only has about 50 employees as well, but is serving a much smaller user base, and was built to be end-to-end encrypted from the beginning).The observation is that there is no question that Telegram has been far too reticent to correct the record about its level of security, or lack thereof; indeed,there is a good casethat the company has been outright deceptive about this fact. For my part, I have been genuinely surprised at the number of tech-savvy folks who have been Telegram users who were unaware that the service is not end-to-end encrypted.The allegation, meanwhile, is the most serious of all: there are many who believe that Telegram did, at some point, cut some sort of deal with the Russian government (see, for example,this Wired story). There is not any conclusive evidence on this point; a more benign argument is that Telegram is not just insecure, but purposely so, given the access its API gives to its data, and that said API has been fully utilized by the Russian government, which is why Telegram is allowed to operate in Russia while other western alternative messaging services are not.This does, needless to say, make it hard to definitively interpret what exactly is happening in France. There is one argument that what is happening is on the level of espionage and spycraft; there is, on the other hand, the reported reason: that Telegram insufficiently moderates its service. That, needless to say, slots right in with the recent European-wide demands on other social media networks to moderate — some would say censor — their content more aggressively.It’s possible that the answer is a little bit of column A and a little bit of column B. What is notable about the moderation point, though, is that Telegram is vulnerable on this point for the same reason it found product-market fit with large-scale channels and groups: its content is not encrypted on Telegram’s servers. That means that Telegram could, if it so chose, access the content of any group (and most chats, since most chats aren’t “secret”); that, by extension, means that any refusal to hand over data by Telegram is ultimately a choice made by its executives. The refusal to do just that under government order is, for better or worse, a crime in nearly every country.This, by extension, explains why the only possible path to delivering a privacy-preserving product is to build in end-to-end encryption from the beginning. Apple and Meta and Signal simply cannot hand over message contents, because it is mathematically impossible; the former two can — and must — deliver relevant metadata about who is in a chat and when they communicated, while Signal is the only entity in a position to legitimately respond to a government request with nothing but a shrug.There is, by the way, an allegory here to the First Amendment and its relationship to freedom of speech. Everyone has a commitment to the latter, at least in theory; what does seem increasingly clear is that the only place that truly has it in practice is the United States, precisely because the First Amendment is codified. Similarly, all messaging services — including Telegram — want to say that they are private; the only ones that are private in practice are the ones where it is codified by true end-to-end encryption.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. practice, and the Fubo and Venu sports streaming lawsuit.On to the Update:Telegram CEO ArrestedFromReuters:Pavel Durov, the Russian-born billionaire founder and owner of the Telegram messaging app, was arrested at Le Bourget airport outside Paris shortly after landing on a private jet late on Saturday and placed in custody, three sources told Reuters. The arrest of the 39-year-old technology billionaire prompted on Sunday a warning from Moscow to Paris that he should be accorded his rights and criticism from X owner Elon Musk who said that free speech in Europe was under attack.There was no official confirmation from France of the arrest, but two French police sources and one Russian source who spoke on condition of anonymity said that Durov was arrested shortly after arriving at Le Bourget airport on a private jet from Azerbaijan…Durov, who has dual French and United Arab Emirates citizenship, was arrested as part of a preliminary police investigation into allegedly allowing a wide range of crimes due to a lack of moderators on Telegram and a lack of cooperation with police, a third French police source said…The encrypted application, with close to 1 billion users, is particularly influential in Russia, Ukraine and the republics of the former Soviet Union. It is ranked as one of the major social media platforms after Facebook, YouTube, WhatsApp, Instagram, TikTok and WeChat.There is a lot going on with this story, but let’s start with that last paragraph: it’s wrong, at least in the way you ought to think about encryption. The most basic level of encryption is between your device and a server; this is and has been the standard for basically every Internet application for years (even most webpages are encrypted in transit at this point). Telegram messages are encrypted between your device and your server. However, messages on the server are not encrypted, which means they can be accessed by Telegram.That is why “encryption” in the context of messaging means end-to-end encryption; this means that your messages are encrypted on your device and can only ever be decrypted and thus read by your intended recipient. Telegram does support this with “Secret Chats”, but these are not the default. Moreover, Telegram’s implementation has a lot of oddities, including some non-standard encryption techniques, the fact that secret chats can only be between two devices (not two accounts, so you can’t access a secret chat started on your phone from your computer), and that both users have to be online at the same time to initiate a secret chat (I’ll come back to these oddities in a moment).Group chats, meanwhile, are not encrypted at all: everyone and everything posted in a Telegram group chat (and one-on-one chats by default) are stored on Telegram’s servers in unencrypted form (or encrypted with a key Telegram owns). This is in contrast to Signal and WhatsApp, which encrypt all conversations by default, both one-on-one chats and also group chats.Telegram’s Non-Encrypted AdvantageOne thing that is important to note here is that there are real advantages that come fromnotbeing encrypted. Telegram chats, for example, have their full history easily accessible from any device you sign in with; sync — both in terms of ongoing messages, and history — is much easier (or even viable) when you don’t have to deal with encryption. It is also far more scalable: WhatsApp and Signal only recently increased their group sizes to 1,024 and 1,000 maximum users recently, while iMessage is capped at 32.This does, as an aside, speak to some minute but important differences in security between the three apps:iMessage’s maximum group size is so small because every message in a group chat is actually sent directly between the sender and every person in the group chat; in other words, when you send a message to a 32 person group chat, you are actually sending 32 separate messages (and potentially more if some of those users are using iMessage on multiple devices). This is also the most secure implementation: there is both forward secrecy (breaking one message does not give you access to past messages) and self-healing (breaking one message gives you access to future messages, until you are off-line for a single message, at which point you have lost the chain).Signal uses what is called client-side fan-out: after an initial back-and-forth exchange with everyone in the group, including the exchange of “send keys” for everyone in the group, you send one message that is individually-encrypted for everyone in the group. This provides forward secrecy, but sacrifices self-healing; however, because the “send keys” are themselves encrypted, there is plausible deniability, in that you don’t know for certain who sent a particular message (Signal does maintain the structure of the group in encrypted form on its servers to maintain a consistent state for all users over time).WhatsApp is the most reliant on a central server, using what is called “server-side fan-out”: (encrypted) messages are sent to a central server (with “send keys” for everyone in the group) and are then distributed by the server to everyone. Part of this process entails maintaining the structure of the group centrally, and while there is a degree of plausible deniability, it does not go as far as Signal does.The actual specifics of how these various systems work are incredibly complex (and my apologies in anticipation if these simplifications got any details wrong); if you want to understand more I highly recommend these YouTube videos fromComputerphile:How Signal Instant Messaging Protocol Works (& WhatsApp etc)Double Ratchet Messaging EncryptionWhat’s Up With Group Messaging(which includes a quick note at the end that one way to compromise these groups is by automatically adding an additional “silent” member of the group)Here is the important takeaway with regards to Telegram, though: Telegram didn’t do any of that, which has arguably been a feature, not a bug. Two of the biggest Telegram features that have helped fuel its popularity are Channels and Groups:Channels are one-way broadcasts, that may also allow for reactions and comments. This is more akin to a blog or a Facebook page. Channels can have an unlimited number of subscribers.Groups are, well, group chats, but they can scale to 200,000 members. Note that that is a two orders of magnitude increase in capacity compared to WhatsApp and Signal; this is directly related to the fact that Telegram groups are not end-to-end encrypted.I think that this point is crucial to understanding Telegram and its place in the market. Yes, the company has played extremely fast-and-easy with how it characterizes its product, to the extent that most users and media (i.e. the excerpt above) mistakenly believe the product has more inherent security than it does. What is also true, though, is that Telegram’s specific feature set are integral to the product-market fit it obtained: there is a place in the market for large scalable group discussion, and filling that hole in the market does depend on an absence of end-to-end encryption.Telegram ComplexitiesThere is one more important fact to know about Telegram, and some important observations and allegations; from theFinancial Timesin March:Pavel Durov told the Financial Times that Dubai-based Telegram had grown to become one of the world’s most popular social media apps while making “hundreds of millions of dollars” in revenues after introducing advertising and premium subscription services two years ago. “We are hoping to become profitable next year, if not this year,” said the Russia-born founder in his first public interview since 2017. He added that the platform has 900mn monthly active users, up from 500mn at the beginning of 2021…Once largely home to the freewheeling cryptocurrency community, the company, which only has about 50 full-time employees, has exploded in popularity over the past few years to become a vital communication tool for governments and officials globally, as well as a lifeline to citizens in conflict zones. Researchers warn that the lightly moderated platform remains a hotbed for criminal activity, as well as extremist or terrorist content and misinformation. Critics have suggested that the Kremlin may have links to or leverage over Telegram, a claim that Durov dismissed as “inaccurate”.Start with the fact: Telegram the company is very small. It’s so small, in fact, that I do think the most plausible explanation for why the company doesn’t have a more fully-featured end-to-end encryption offering is that it doesn’t really have the resources for something that its users haven’t seemed to care about. Go back to the secret chats I described above: yes, the implementation is weird, but it’s also “easy”, at least relative to the complicated server-mediated key exchange processes that WhatsApp and Signal employ (the latter only has about 50 employees as well, but is serving a much smaller user base, and was built to be end-to-end encrypted from the beginning).The observation is that there is no question that Telegram has been far too reticent to correct the record about its level of security, or lack thereof; indeed,there is a good casethat the company has been outright deceptive about this fact. For my part, I have been genuinely surprised at the number of tech-savvy folks who have been Telegram users who were unaware that the service is not end-to-end encrypted.The allegation, meanwhile, is the most serious of all: there are many who believe that Telegram did, at some point, cut some sort of deal with the Russian government (see, for example,this Wired story). There is not any conclusive evidence on this point; a more benign argument is that Telegram is not just insecure, but purposely so, given the access its API gives to its data, and that said API has been fully utilized by the Russian government, which is why Telegram is allowed to operate in Russia while other western alternative messaging services are not.This does, needless to say, make it hard to definitively interpret what exactly is happening in France. There is one argument that what is happening is on the level of espionage and spycraft; there is, on the other hand, the reported reason: that Telegram insufficiently moderates its service. That, needless to say, slots right in with the recent European-wide demands on other social media networks to moderate — some would say censor — their content more aggressively.It’s possible that the answer is a little bit of column A and a little bit of column B. What is notable about the moderation point, though, is that Telegram is vulnerable on this point for the same reason it found product-market fit with large-scale channels and groups: its content is not encrypted on Telegram’s servers. That means that Telegram could, if it so chose, access the content of any group (and most chats, since most chats aren’t “secret”); that, by extension, means that any refusal to hand over data by Telegram is ultimately a choice made by its executives. The refusal to do just that under government order is, for better or worse, a crime in nearly every country.This, by extension, explains why the only possible path to delivering a privacy-preserving product is to build in end-to-end encryption from the beginning. Apple and Meta and Signal simply cannot hand over message contents, because it is mathematically impossible; the former two can — and must — deliver relevant metadata about who is in a chat and when they communicated, while Signal is the only entity in a position to legitimately respond to a government request with nothing but a shrug.There is, by the way, an allegory here to the First Amendment and its relationship to freedom of speech. Everyone has a commitment to the latter, at least in theory; what does seem increasingly clear is that the only place that truly has it in practice is the United States, precisely because the First Amendment is codified. Similarly, all messaging services — including Telegram — want to say that they are private; the only ones that are private in practice are the ones where it is codified by true end-to-end encryption.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. Apple and Meta and Signal simply cannot hand over message contents, because it is mathematically impossible; the former two can — and must — deliver relevant metadata about who is in a chat and when they communicated, while Signal is the only entity in a position to legitimately respond to a government request with nothing but a shrug. There is, by the way, an allegory here to the First Amendment and its relationship to freedom of speech. Everyone has a commitment to the latter, at least in theory; what does seem increasingly clear is that the only place that truly has it in practice is the United States, precisely because the First Amendment is codified. Similarly, all messaging services — including Telegram — want to say that they are private; the only ones that are private in practice are the ones where it is codified by true end-to-end encryption.
