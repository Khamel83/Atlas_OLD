The unusual deal caps off what Wang himself described as “a really volatile time” for the startup and its employees. Windsurf, which makes a popular AI tool for programmers, had previously agreed to be purchased by OpenAI for $3 billion, but the deal unraveled in part because of tension with Microsoft Corp., a major OpenAI investor, Bloomberg News has reported…The Cognition deal includes Windsurf’s intellectual property as well as its remaining employees, cash, assets and brand. Windsurf has $82 million in annual recurring revenue and its enterprise business has been growing fast, Cognition co-founder and CEO Scott Wu wrote in a note to staff. All of Windsurf’s employees will participate financially in the deal and see their vesting schedules accelerate, Wu wrote. “Jeff and I worked together to ensure that every single employee is treated with respect and well taken care of in this transaction,” Wu said in the note.Cognition last raised money in the spring, bringing in hundreds of millions at a valuation of $4 billion, part of a deal led by 8VC, the Joe Lonsdale-backed venture capital firm. The company has raised more than $300 million to date, according to PitchBook data, from a list of investors that includes Founders Fund, Khosla Ventures and Conviction Partners. The company has billed its generative AI coding tool Devin as “the world’s first AI software engineer.”I predicted yesterday that this story would have a somewhat happy ending, for PR reasons if nothing else; this particular outcome, however, is actually pretty interesting, and may point to a future model for these sorts of transactions. For context,here is the announcement postfrom Cognition:We’re excited to share that Cognition has signed a definitive agreement to acquire Windsurf, the agentic IDE. The acquisition includes Windsurf’s IP, product, trademark and brand, and strong business. Above all, it includes Windsurf’s world-class people, some of the best talent in our industry, whom we’re privileged to welcome to our team…So, what exactly is the deal? With this acquisition, Cognition will own Windsurf’s beloved product and strong business:The Windsurf IDE, now with full access to the latest Claude modelsWindsurf’s IP, including their trademark and the strong brand they have built$82M of ARR and a fast-growing business, with enterprise ARR doubling quarter-over-quarterA user base that includes 350+ enterprise customers and hundreds of thousands of daily active usersAbove all, we are welcoming some of the most impressive people in our industry, including world-class GTM, engineering, and product teams.What is noticeably absent is a purchase price; in fact, my strong suspicion is that the purchase price nets out to $0. Remember, it was reported that Windsurf was left with around $100 million in cash, which was approximately equal to the unvested stock it owed remaining employees; my guess is that Cognition got the Windsurf business, and then will use the cash to fulfill the promises made to Windsurf employees to make them whole.The interesting part of this deal, however, is that it actually makes a lot of sense. Cognition is also in the AI-coding space, although Devin, their primary product, is much more focused on being an end-to-end agent that does everything for you; what is important is that Cognition’s team actually has a chance to keep a bit of Windsurf’s business, most of which was probably prepared to walk out the door. And, going forward, Windsurf’s data is likely to be very useful in developing Devin; the best way to develop a coding agent is to continually get data and feedback from actual coders. Of course this may all be for naught: Windsurf might lose whatever business they still have, and Cognition is taking on the overhead of this deal because they feel they need the help; maybe it won’t amount to anything, but at least there is a chance it will.The big question in my mind is whether, assuming the old acquihire model is dead, there might be other deals similar to this one going forward: Big Tech gets the talent they want, while startups in adjacent spaces have the opportunity to consolidate far sooner than they would be able to normally if they had to actually pay money. It’s possible that this entire situation is mostly unique to AI, given the talent wars, and of course the startups themselves need talent! Still, I’m glad this situation worked out, and it would be kind of nifty to see it succeed and become some sort of model going forward.Nvidia Can Sell to ChinaSpeaking of closing the loop on stories, from theWall Street Journal:Nvidia said it has received assurances from the Trump administration that it can sell its H20 artificial-intelligence chip in China, days after Chief Executive Jensen Huang met President Trump. The turnabout comes after the Commerce Department restricted sales of the chip in April, costing Nvidia billions of dollars. It is welcome news for the $4 trillion chip company and Huang, who is visiting Beijing this week. The administration said Nvidia would be allowed to sell the H20 chip after licenses are granted by the Commerce Department, according to the company. Nvidia said it would begin deliveries soon. In addition, it said it has developed a new chip with lower performance that meets all U.S. export criteria so it can be shipped to China.The approval could be seen as a gesture of good faith by the U.S. in its trade talks with China. Access to chips and advanced technology has been a main priority for Chinese negotiators. The license approvals will be some of the only licenses being granted by the Trump administration, which is restricting exports of AI chips and other products to protect national security. Huang plans to meet top Chinese officials during his stay in Beijing, people familiar with the matter said. Huang met Trump last week, attempting to persuade the president that his company should be allowed to continue to do business with China and tap AI talent there, according to people familiar with the meeting. Huang told Trump that Nvidia should be allowed to sell its technology freely to most parts of the world so American companies can dominate AI instead of Chinese companies, the people said. The CEO also discussed similar topics with Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick, they said.This is good news, for all of the reasons I wrote aboutwhen I decried the initial H20 ban in April:China — which actually builds power generation — is not iso power; they are iso compute: Huawei’s goal with the CloudMatrix 384 is to get as much compute as possible, power efficiency be damned.Again, this comes with more tradeoffs than just power usage, given the challenges of scaling up inferior chips; I mentioned the brittleness and cost above, but there is also going to be a big software challenge. That, though, is the other piece of bad news for Nvidia: banning the company’s chips from China will weaken the CUDA moat.As I notedwhen the Biden Administration first placed limits on Nvidia in China, there are two problems this presents:There is reason to create generalizable software that does what CUDA does, but that isn’t locked to Nvidia chips.There is a motivation for the ecosystem to tear out any CUDA-specific code so that various frameworks can more easily run on Chinese infrastructure, which will also benefit non-Nvidia alternatives outside of China.And, of course, in the long run there is the potential of Huawei selling outside of China.More broadly, I stand by my overall position inAI Promise and Chip Precariousness, that a policy predicated on reserving AI capabilities exclusively for the U.S. is a mistake: not only is it likely to fail, but also success carries with it extraordinary danger if you play out the game theory around the U.S. having a potentially transformative military capability that is ultimately dependent on chips made in Taiwan. Given this reality, the absolute most logical thing for the U.S. to do is to maintain control of the chokepoints that it both controls today and, structurally, is likely to maintain in the long run. Nvidia dominance is one of them, which is exactly why it is good to allow the company to sell into China.The other, of course, is chip production: it’s good for the U.S. if China is dependent on TSMC as much as we are; perhaps the Trump administration is starting to see the light and will follow through with the rest of my prescription, which is cutting off equipment sales to China while also allowing Chinese companies to source from TSMC, even beyond being able to buy these relatively limited Nvidia chips.Grok 4 and KimiFromArtificial Analysis on X:xAI gave us early access to Grok 4 – and the results are in. We have run our full suite of benchmarks and Grok 4 achieves an Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index of 73, ahead of OpenAI o3 at 70, Google Gemini 2.5 Pro at 70, Anthropic Claude 4 Opus at 64 and DeepSeek R1 0528 at 68. Full results breakdown below. This is the first time that @elonmusk’s @xai has the lead the AI frontier. Grok 3 scored competitively with the latest models from OpenAI, Anthropic and Google – but Grok 4 is the first time that our Intelligence Index has shown xAI in first place…Grok 4 is a reasoning model, meaning it ‘thinks’ before answering. The xAI API does not share reasoning tokens generated by the model.Then, fromReuters:Chinese artificial intelligence startup Moonshot AI released a new open-source AI model on Friday, joining a wave of similar releases from local rivals, as it seeks to reclaim its position in the competitive domestic market. The model, called Kimi K2, features enhanced coding capabilities and excels at general agent tasks and tool integration, allowing it to break down complex tasks more effectively, the company said in a statement. Moonshot claimed the model outperforms mainstream open-source models in some areas, including DeepSeek’s V3, and rival capabilities of leading U.S. models such as those from Anthropic in certain functions such as coding.The release follows a trend among Chinese companies toward open-sourcing AI models, contrasting with many U.S. tech giants like OpenAI and Google that keep their most advanced AI models proprietary. Some American firms, including Meta Platforms have also released open-source models.Kimi is not a reasoning model, but is receiving pretty rave reviews from online testers; it is,like DeepSeek R1, a very large parameter model with a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture, which is clearly the best approach for companies that are bandwidth constrained, as Moonshot is; available evidence suggests that K2 was trained on Nvidia A100s and H100s.That both models are good speaks to two important points: first, compute scaling still pays dividends (Grok), and second, architecture innovation still pays dividends (K2). In other words, there are multiple avenues of improvement for better models.And, per the point above, China already has very good models! What are we trying to constrain exactly? Sure, I suppose you could make the case that, were chip controls stringently enforced going forward, China would eventually fall behind, but I’m skeptical and again, that is arguably a worse outcome in terms of risk.Moreover, as long as Chinese companies keep open-sourcing these models, the greater pressure that will put on prices in the long run, and the larger window there will be for innovation. rumors about leadership are so pervasive and hard to parse.On to the Update:Cognition Buys WindsurfJust to close the loop onyesterday’s Update, fromBloomberg:Artificial intelligence coding startup Cognition AI Inc. has agreed to buy what remains of Windsurf for an undisclosed sum days after Alphabet Inc.’s Google struck a $2.4 billion deal for Windsurf’s top talent and licensing rights. “Among all the teams in the AI space, Cognition was literally the one we have respected the most, and they are a perfect fit to bring Windsurf to the next phase,” Jeff Wang, Windsurf’s interim chief executive officer, said in an email to employees on Monday. The unusual deal caps off what Wang himself described as “a really volatile time” for the startup and its employees. Windsurf, which makes a popular AI tool for programmers, had previously agreed to be purchased by OpenAI for $3 billion, but the deal unraveled in part because of tension with Microsoft Corp., a major OpenAI investor, Bloomberg News has reported…The Cognition deal includes Windsurf’s intellectual property as well as its remaining employees, cash, assets and brand. Windsurf has $82 million in annual recurring revenue and its enterprise business has been growing fast, Cognition co-founder and CEO Scott Wu wrote in a note to staff. All of Windsurf’s employees will participate financially in the deal and see their vesting schedules accelerate, Wu wrote. “Jeff and I worked together to ensure that every single employee is treated with respect and well taken care of in this transaction,” Wu said in the note.Cognition last raised money in the spring, bringing in hundreds of millions at a valuation of $4 billion, part of a deal led by 8VC, the Joe Lonsdale-backed venture capital firm. The company has raised more than $300 million to date, according to PitchBook data, from a list of investors that includes Founders Fund, Khosla Ventures and Conviction Partners. The company has billed its generative AI coding tool Devin as “the world’s first AI software engineer.”I predicted yesterday that this story would have a somewhat happy ending, for PR reasons if nothing else; this particular outcome, however, is actually pretty interesting, and may point to a future model for these sorts of transactions. For context,here is the announcement postfrom Cognition:We’re excited to share that Cognition has signed a definitive agreement to acquire Windsurf, the agentic IDE. The acquisition includes Windsurf’s IP, product, trademark and brand, and strong business. Above all, it includes Windsurf’s world-class people, some of the best talent in our industry, whom we’re privileged to welcome to our team…So, what exactly is the deal? With this acquisition, Cognition will own Windsurf’s beloved product and strong business:The Windsurf IDE, now with full access to the latest Claude modelsWindsurf’s IP, including their trademark and the strong brand they have built$82M of ARR and a fast-growing business, with enterprise ARR doubling quarter-over-quarterA user base that includes 350+ enterprise customers and hundreds of thousands of daily active usersAbove all, we are welcoming some of the most impressive people in our industry, including world-class GTM, engineering, and product teams.What is noticeably absent is a purchase price; in fact, my strong suspicion is that the purchase price nets out to $0. Remember, it was reported that Windsurf was left with around $100 million in cash, which was approximately equal to the unvested stock it owed remaining employees; my guess is that Cognition got the Windsurf business, and then will use the cash to fulfill the promises made to Windsurf employees to make them whole.The interesting part of this deal, however, is that it actually makes a lot of sense. Cognition is also in the AI-coding space, although Devin, their primary product, is much more focused on being an end-to-end agent that does everything for you; what is important is that Cognition’s team actually has a chance to keep a bit of Windsurf’s business, most of which was probably prepared to walk out the door. And, going forward, Windsurf’s data is likely to be very useful in developing Devin; the best way to develop a coding agent is to continually get data and feedback from actual coders. Of course this may all be for naught: Windsurf might lose whatever business they still have, and Cognition is taking on the overhead of this deal because they feel they need the help; maybe it won’t amount to anything, but at least there is a chance it will.The big question in my mind is whether, assuming the old acquihire model is dead, there might be other deals similar to this one going forward: Big Tech gets the talent they want, while startups in adjacent spaces have the opportunity to consolidate far sooner than they would be able to normally if they had to actually pay money. It’s possible that this entire situation is mostly unique to AI, given the talent wars, and of course the startups themselves need talent! Still, I’m glad this situation worked out, and it would be kind of nifty to see it succeed and become some sort of model going forward.Nvidia Can Sell to ChinaSpeaking of closing the loop on stories, from theWall Street Journal:Nvidia said it has received assurances from the Trump administration that it can sell its H20 artificial-intelligence chip in China, days after Chief Executive Jensen Huang met President Trump. The turnabout comes after the Commerce Department restricted sales of the chip in April, costing Nvidia billions of dollars. It is welcome news for the $4 trillion chip company and Huang, who is visiting Beijing this week. The administration said Nvidia would be allowed to sell the H20 chip after licenses are granted by the Commerce Department, according to the company. Nvidia said it would begin deliveries soon. In addition, it said it has developed a new chip with lower performance that meets all U.S. export criteria so it can be shipped to China.The approval could be seen as a gesture of good faith by the U.S. in its trade talks with China. Access to chips and advanced technology has been a main priority for Chinese negotiators. The license approvals will be some of the only licenses being granted by the Trump administration, which is restricting exports of AI chips and other products to protect national security. Huang plans to meet top Chinese officials during his stay in Beijing, people familiar with the matter said. Huang met Trump last week, attempting to persuade the president that his company should be allowed to continue to do business with China and tap AI talent there, according to people familiar with the meeting. Huang told Trump that Nvidia should be allowed to sell its technology freely to most parts of the world so American companies can dominate AI instead of Chinese companies, the people said. The CEO also discussed similar topics with Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick, they said.This is good news, for all of the reasons I wrote aboutwhen I decried the initial H20 ban in April:China — which actually builds power generation — is not iso power; they are iso compute: Huawei’s goal with the CloudMatrix 384 is to get as much compute as possible, power efficiency be damned.Again, this comes with more tradeoffs than just power usage, given the challenges of scaling up inferior chips; I mentioned the brittleness and cost above, but there is also going to be a big software challenge. That, though, is the other piece of bad news for Nvidia: banning the company’s chips from China will weaken the CUDA moat.As I notedwhen the Biden Administration first placed limits on Nvidia in China, there are two problems this presents:There is reason to create generalizable software that does what CUDA does, but that isn’t locked to Nvidia chips.There is a motivation for the ecosystem to tear out any CUDA-specific code so that various frameworks can more easily run on Chinese infrastructure, which will also benefit non-Nvidia alternatives outside of China.And, of course, in the long run there is the potential of Huawei selling outside of China.More broadly, I stand by my overall position inAI Promise and Chip Precariousness, that a policy predicated on reserving AI capabilities exclusively for the U.S. is a mistake: not only is it likely to fail, but also success carries with it extraordinary danger if you play out the game theory around the U.S. having a potentially transformative military capability that is ultimately dependent on chips made in Taiwan. Given this reality, the absolute most logical thing for the U.S. to do is to maintain control of the chokepoints that it both controls today and, structurally, is likely to maintain in the long run. Nvidia dominance is one of them, which is exactly why it is good to allow the company to sell into China.The other, of course, is chip production: it’s good for the U.S. if China is dependent on TSMC as much as we are; perhaps the Trump administration is starting to see the light and will follow through with the rest of my prescription, which is cutting off equipment sales to China while also allowing Chinese companies to source from TSMC, even beyond being able to buy these relatively limited Nvidia chips.Grok 4 and KimiFromArtificial Analysis on X:xAI gave us early access to Grok 4 – and the results are in. Grok 4 is now the leading AI model. We have run our full suite of benchmarks and Grok 4 achieves an Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index of 73, ahead of OpenAI o3 at 70, Google Gemini 2.5 Pro at 70, Anthropic Claude 4 Opus at 64 and DeepSeek R1 0528 at 68. Full results breakdown below. This is the first time that @elonmusk’s @xai has the lead the AI frontier. Grok 3 scored competitively with the latest models from OpenAI, Anthropic and Google – but Grok 4 is the first time that our Intelligence Index has shown xAI in first place…Grok 4 is a reasoning model, meaning it ‘thinks’ before answering. The xAI API does not share reasoning tokens generated by the model.Then, fromReuters:Chinese artificial intelligence startup Moonshot AI released a new open-source AI model on Friday, joining a wave of similar releases from local rivals, as it seeks to reclaim its position in the competitive domestic market. The model, called Kimi K2, features enhanced coding capabilities and excels at general agent tasks and tool integration, allowing it to break down complex tasks more effectively, the company said in a statement. Moonshot claimed the model outperforms mainstream open-source models in some areas, including DeepSeek’s V3, and rival capabilities of leading U.S. models such as those from Anthropic in certain functions such as coding.The release follows a trend among Chinese companies toward open-sourcing AI models, contrasting with many U.S. tech giants like OpenAI and Google that keep their most advanced AI models proprietary. Some American firms, including Meta Platforms have also released open-source models.Kimi is not a reasoning model, but is receiving pretty rave reviews from online testers; it is,like DeepSeek R1, a very large parameter model with a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture, which is clearly the best approach for companies that are bandwidth constrained, as Moonshot is; available evidence suggests that K2 was trained on Nvidia A100s and H100s.That both models are good speaks to two important points: first, compute scaling still pays dividends (Grok), and second, architecture innovation still pays dividends (K2). In other words, there are multiple avenues of improvement for better models.And, per the point above, China already has very good models! What are we trying to constrain exactly? Sure, I suppose you could make the case that, were chip controls stringently enforced going forward, China would eventually fall behind, but I’m skeptical and again, that is arguably a worse outcome in terms of risk.Moreover, as long as Chinese companies keep open-sourcing these models, the greater pressure that will put on prices in the long run, and the larger window there will be for innovation. Granted, it’s dangerous for obvious reasons to become dependent on China, but right now it’s hard to argue that the country’s AI efforts aren’t a net positive for everyone but the leading foundational AI companies who would very much like to preserve their margins.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. The release follows a trend among Chinese companies toward open-sourcing AI models, contrasting with many U.S. tech giants like OpenAI and Google that keep their most advanced AI models proprietary.
