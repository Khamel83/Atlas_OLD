I highly recommend this one for anyone who hasn’t been reading Stratechery from the beginning.OnMonday’s episode, we discussed the analogies between Apple, Microsoft, and IBM, and why I was frustrated with the Metallica video on the Apple Vision Pro.On to the Update:Intel’s New CEOFromReuters:Intel named former board member and chip industry veteran Lip-Bu Tan as its CEO and signaled the struggling but storied chipmaker was unlikely to split up its chip-design and manufacturing operations. The appointment, effective March 18, comes three months after Intel ousted CEO and company veteran Pat Gelsinger, whose costly and ambitious plan to turn the company around was faltering and sapping investor confidence.Tan, a former Intel board member, had been seen as a CEO contender thanks to his deep experience in the chip industry as well as a longtime technology investor in promising startups. He was approached by Intel’s board in December to gauge his interest in taking up the job, Reuters had reported. “Together, we will work hard to restore Intel’s position as a world-class products company, establish ourselves as a world-class foundry and delight our customers like never before,” Tan said in a letter to Intel employees on Wednesday.Intel shares surged 12% in extended trading on Wednesday, and analysts welcomed the move that they said was likely to bring some stability to the chipmaker. The company’s stock had declined 60% in 2024.I have to admit that, while Tan was rumored to be a candidate, I was pretty surprised by this appointment, and need to change a few of my assumptions about what has happened at Intel over the last few months and, frankly, the entire Gelsinger tenure.Reevaluating GelsingerGelsinger was named Intel CEO in January 2021 and announced hisIDM 2.0 strategyin March 2021; Tan joined the board in September 2022, i.e. well after the strategy — that Gelsinger put forward to the board before he was even hired — was underway, and had his role expanded to oversee Intel’s foundry operations in 2023. Then, last August, he resigned; fromReuters at the time:Over time, Tan grew frustrated by the company’s large workforce, its approach to contract manufacturing and Intel’s risk-averse and bureaucratic culture, according to the sources, who were not authorized to speak publicly. The circumstances around Tan’s exit have not previously been reported. The departure of the industry veteran, who is well-regarded by investors, over Intel’s strategy illustrates the uncertainty of its turnaround efforts.I wrote inan Update:It seems clear that Tan has a point: I was too gentle last week when I admonished Gelsinger for being slow to cut the dividend and slow to do layoffs. With regards to the latter, Intel ended last year with 124,800 people; to put that in context, TSMC had 76,478 employees and AMD 26,000, which is to say that the two companies combined had fewer employees than Intel while making better x86 chips, an actually competitive GPU, and oh yeah, making chips for everyone else on earth, including Apple and Nvidia. A 15,000 employee cut is both too small and too late.A week later, however,I gave up on Intel’s turnaround, arguing the company couldn’t pull it off without major government intervention, the price of which should be splitting the company; yes, Intel needed itself to be its own first best customer, but I despaired that, as long as the company was one, the culture would ever sufficiently change to make the foundry business viable.A few months later, and it seemed the board was moving in a similar direction whenthey fired Gelsinger. This also seemed to cast the Tan resignation in a new light: he had oversight over foundry, and quit, and then a few months later the CEO whose turnaround plan was centered on foundry was out the door. Perhaps they were connected.Now, though, Tan is back, and still committed to foundry; fromBloomberg:Incoming Intel Corp. Chief Executive Officer Lip-Bu Tan is signaling that he’ll stick with his predecessor’s plan to make chips for other companies, even as he vows to learn from past mistakes. Tan, who was named to the CEO job on Wednesday,sent a letterto employees before meeting with staff in the afternoon, offering a rough sketch of his plans for the embattled chipmaker. Part of the message: He will keep working to make Intel a top foundry — a business that produces chips for outside clients. That endeavor has been costly for Intel so far, and contributed to the ouster of prior CEO Pat Gelsinger.In other words, the problem isn’t that Gelsinger had a bad plan; it’s that he had terrible execution — that’s why he got fired. And, well, it’s a reasonable argument — it’s the one Tan actually made when he stepped down. I added inmy Update at the time:I do still stand by my take that Gelsinger deserves a chance to deliver on 18A, and that the broader strategy is correct. There is, however, a concerning trend that is emerging: Gelsinger is relentlessly positive and optimistic, but, to invokehis mentor Andy Grove, he’s not nearly paranoid enough:Intel failed to anticipate the post-COVID crash.Intel failed to appreciate how AI would curtail CPU spending.Intel didn’t seem to see the performance capabilities Qualcomm delivered for ARM PCs coming; another thing I should have noted last week is that Intel is taking a much larger dependency on TSMC — and thus lower margins — because the TSMC-manufactured Lunar Lake chip needs to be manufactured in much higher quantities than anticipated because Intel doesn’t have a better answer for Copilot+ PCs.These mistakes are on top of the too-late cut in dividends and too-little too-late layoffs I already mentioned; the common thread with all of them is planning for a rosy future and then getting surprised when everything is worse than anticipated. What Intel needs is not optimism or, at this point, even paranoia: the building is on fire and this is a company that needs to get much more desperate much more quickly.This is basically Tan’s thinking, according tothis report from Reuters:Intel’s incoming CEO Lip-Bu Tan has considered significant changes to its chip manufacturing methods and artificial intelligence strategies ahead of his return to the company on Tuesday, two people familiar with Tan’s thinking told Reuters, in a sweeping bid to revive the ailing technology giant.The new trajectory includes restructuring the company’s approach to AI and staff cuts to address what Tan views as a slow-moving and bloated middle management layer. Revamping the company’s manufacturing operations, which at one time only made chips for Intel but have been repurposed to make semiconductors for outside clients such as Nvidia, is one of Tan’s core priorities, these sources said…Tan has been a vocal internal critic of Gelsinger’s execution, according to the two sources familiar with Tan’s plans…Tan’s views were shaped by months of reviewing Intel’s manufacturing process after the board in late 2023 appointed him to a special role overseeing it, according to a regulatory filing. In his assessment, he expressed frustration with the company’s culture, sources told Reuters, saying it had lost the “only the paranoid survive” ethos enshrined by former CEO Andy Grove. He also came to believe that decision-making was slowed down by a bloated workforce, Reuters reported.Tan presented some of his ideas to Intel’s board last year, but they declined to put them into place, according to two people familiar with the matter. By August, Tan abruptly resigned over differences with the board, Reuters reported. When he returns as CEO this week, he will lay fresh eyes on Intel’s workforce, which was slashed by roughly 15,000 to almost 109,000 at the end of last year, the sources said.I’ve felt a vague sense of unease about my Intel position ever since I wroteIntel Honesty, and I think I finally understand why: I was correct that Intel’s path was unsustainable, and something needed to be done. What made me uneasy, however, is that I believed the strategy was the right one. It follows, then, that I should have taken what I myself wrote in the wake of Tan’s resignation more seriously: the problem was Gelsinger’s execution!And maybe that’s the ultimate tragedy, at least from Gelsinger’s perspective. When Gelsinger resigned I wrote thathis plan was 10 years too late; what is maybe more correct is Gelsinger himself was 15 years too late. He left Intel in 2009 after being passed over for the CEO job in 2005; that was an era where Gelsinger’s optimism might have been more suitable (or maybe not: Intel needed to be paranoid about mobile!). What Intel needs now, however, is not optimism, but the sort of desperation that may finally drive a culture change. Things can get worse!Lip-Bu Tan and CadenceSo is Tan the right person to make those changes? Cadence’s stock price under his leadership is certainly one mark in his favor:Tan took over a company in crisis: Cadence had a traditional up-front licensing model that was very susceptible to industry downturns, one of which hit hard in 2008, right on the heels of a failed attempt to buy the number three player in the Electronic Design Automation (EDA) space. The revenue slump was compounded by accounting issues that led to restated earnings and the resignation of the CEO; that’s when Tan became first interim CEO in October 2008, and then permanent CEO in January 2009.Tan made some critical changes immediately. First, he shifted Cadence’s business model to subscriptions away from licensing. Secondly, he leaned into the shift towards systems design and away from standalone chips; this meant investments and acquisitions around reusable IP. Third, Cadence allied itself extremely closely with TSMC, becoming the EDA of choice for TSMC’s leading nodes; this went part-and-parcel with being the EDA software of choice for Apple, which was the customer for those leading nodes. All of this was, by all accounts, downstream froma change in culture, including an embrace of total honesty and uncompromising feedback, both internally and from customers — that’s certainly what Intel needs!At the same time, look again at where Tan — brilliantly, to be sure! — positioned Cadence: directly between TSMC and Apple during the smartphone boom. That stock price increase is impressive, to be sure, but less impressive when you look at this chart:Cadence still has the best returns here, having increased 391% during Tan’s tenure, just surpassing its direct competitor Synopsis’ 358% increase; then again, Synopsis wasn’t a company in crisis either, so they were starting from a higher base. What is clear, however, is that this was a good decade to be selling EDA software! Apple, meanwhile, was up 315% over the same time period; TSMC and Qualcomm tracked for a while, but fell off towards the end of Tan’s tenure.The point of this chart is not to diminish Tan’s track record; rather, it’s to point out that his success at Cadence was, first and foremost, predicated on surfing one of the biggest waves in technology history. It’s possible that a combination of AI and national security concerns could be that wave for Intel, but for now they are the one being overwhelmed. The massive challenge for Tan will be figuring out how to stand up on the surfboard — if in fact the surfboard even exists.All that noted, I’m pleased about this hire, because I would actually prefer if I were wrong inIntel Honesty: I want Intel to succeed, and to do so on their own terms. I thought Gelsinger’s strategy was plausible, and I’m open to the idea that he just did a bad job executing it. Tan thought he did, and now he gets to prove it.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. The appointment, effective March 18, comes three months after Intel ousted CEO and company veteran Pat Gelsinger, whose costly and ambitious plan to turn the company around was faltering and sapping investor confidence.Tan, a former Intel board member, had been seen as a CEO contender thanks to his deep experience in the chip industry as well as a longtime technology investor in promising startups. He was approached by Intel’s board in December to gauge his interest in taking up the job, Reuters had reported. “Together, we will work hard to restore Intel’s position as a world-class products company, establish ourselves as a world-class foundry and delight our customers like never before,” Tan said in a letter to Intel employees on Wednesday.Intel shares surged 12% in extended trading on Wednesday, and analysts welcomed the move that they said was likely to bring some stability to the chipmaker. The company’s stock had declined 60% in 2024.I have to admit that, while Tan was rumored to be a candidate, I was pretty surprised by this appointment, and need to change a few of my assumptions about what has happened at Intel over the last few months and, frankly, the entire Gelsinger tenure.Reevaluating GelsingerGelsinger was named Intel CEO in January 2021 and announced hisIDM 2.0 strategyin March 2021; Tan joined the board in September 2022, i.e. well after the strategy — that Gelsinger put forward to the board before he was even hired — was underway, and had his role expanded to oversee Intel’s foundry operations in 2023. Then, last August, he resigned; fromReuters at the time:Over time, Tan grew frustrated by the company’s large workforce, its approach to contract manufacturing and Intel’s risk-averse and bureaucratic culture, according to the sources, who were not authorized to speak publicly. The circumstances around Tan’s exit have not previously been reported. The departure of the industry veteran, who is well-regarded by investors, over Intel’s strategy illustrates the uncertainty of its turnaround efforts.I wrote inan Update:It seems clear that Tan has a point: I was too gentle last week when I admonished Gelsinger for being slow to cut the dividend and slow to do layoffs. With regards to the latter, Intel ended last year with 124,800 people; to put that in context, TSMC had 76,478 employees and AMD 26,000, which is to say that the two companies combined had fewer employees than Intel while making better x86 chips, an actually competitive GPU, and oh yeah, making chips for everyone else on earth, including Apple and Nvidia. A 15,000 employee cut is both too small and too late.A week later, however,I gave up on Intel’s turnaround, arguing the company couldn’t pull it off without major government intervention, the price of which should be splitting the company; yes, Intel needed itself to be its own first best customer, but I despaired that, as long as the company was one, the culture would ever sufficiently change to make the foundry business viable.A few months later, and it seemed the board was moving in a similar direction whenthey fired Gelsinger. This also seemed to cast the Tan resignation in a new light: he had oversight over foundry, and quit, and then a few months later the CEO whose turnaround plan was centered on foundry was out the door. Perhaps they were connected.Now, though, Tan is back, and still committed to foundry; fromBloomberg:Incoming Intel Corp. Chief Executive Officer Lip-Bu Tan is signaling that he’ll stick with his predecessor’s plan to make chips for other companies, even as he vows to learn from past mistakes. Tan, who was named to the CEO job on Wednesday,sent a letterto employees before meeting with staff in the afternoon, offering a rough sketch of his plans for the embattled chipmaker. Part of the message: He will keep working to make Intel a top foundry — a business that produces chips for outside clients. That endeavor has been costly for Intel so far, and contributed to the ouster of prior CEO Pat Gelsinger.In other words, the problem isn’t that Gelsinger had a bad plan; it’s that he had terrible execution — that’s why he got fired. And, well, it’s a reasonable argument — it’s the one Tan actually made when he stepped down. I added inmy Update at the time:I do still stand by my take that Gelsinger deserves a chance to deliver on 18A, and that the broader strategy is correct. There is, however, a concerning trend that is emerging: Gelsinger is relentlessly positive and optimistic, but, to invokehis mentor Andy Grove, he’s not nearly paranoid enough:Intel failed to anticipate the post-COVID crash.Intel failed to appreciate how AI would curtail CPU spending.Intel didn’t seem to see the performance capabilities Qualcomm delivered for ARM PCs coming; another thing I should have noted last week is that Intel is taking a much larger dependency on TSMC — and thus lower margins — because the TSMC-manufactured Lunar Lake chip needs to be manufactured in much higher quantities than anticipated because Intel doesn’t have a better answer for Copilot+ PCs.These mistakes are on top of the too-late cut in dividends and too-little too-late layoffs I already mentioned; the common thread with all of them is planning for a rosy future and then getting surprised when everything is worse than anticipated. What Intel needs is not optimism or, at this point, even paranoia: the building is on fire and this is a company that needs to get much more desperate much more quickly.This is basically Tan’s thinking, according tothis report from Reuters:Intel’s incoming CEO Lip-Bu Tan has considered significant changes to its chip manufacturing methods and artificial intelligence strategies ahead of his return to the company on Tuesday, two people familiar with Tan’s thinking told Reuters, in a sweeping bid to revive the ailing technology giant.The new trajectory includes restructuring the company’s approach to AI and staff cuts to address what Tan views as a slow-moving and bloated middle management layer. Revamping the company’s manufacturing operations, which at one time only made chips for Intel but have been repurposed to make semiconductors for outside clients such as Nvidia, is one of Tan’s core priorities, these sources said…Tan has been a vocal internal critic of Gelsinger’s execution, according to the two sources familiar with Tan’s plans…Tan’s views were shaped by months of reviewing Intel’s manufacturing process after the board in late 2023 appointed him to a special role overseeing it, according to a regulatory filing. In his assessment, he expressed frustration with the company’s culture, sources told Reuters, saying it had lost the “only the paranoid survive” ethos enshrined by former CEO Andy Grove. He also came to believe that decision-making was slowed down by a bloated workforce, Reuters reported.Tan presented some of his ideas to Intel’s board last year, but they declined to put them into place, according to two people familiar with the matter. By August, Tan abruptly resigned over differences with the board, Reuters reported. When he returns as CEO this week, he will lay fresh eyes on Intel’s workforce, which was slashed by roughly 15,000 to almost 109,000 at the end of last year, the sources said.I’ve felt a vague sense of unease about my Intel position ever since I wroteIntel Honesty, and I think I finally understand why: I was correct that Intel’s path was unsustainable, and something needed to be done. What made me uneasy, however, is that I believed the strategy was the right one. It follows, then, that I should have taken what I myself wrote in the wake of Tan’s resignation more seriously: the problem was Gelsinger’s execution!And maybe that’s the ultimate tragedy, at least from Gelsinger’s perspective. When Gelsinger resigned I wrote thathis plan was 10 years too late; what is maybe more correct is Gelsinger himself was 15 years too late. He left Intel in 2009 after being passed over for the CEO job in 2005; that was an era where Gelsinger’s optimism might have been more suitable (or maybe not: Intel needed to be paranoid about mobile!). What Intel needs now, however, is not optimism, but the sort of desperation that may finally drive a culture change. Things can get worse!Lip-Bu Tan and CadenceSo is Tan the right person to make those changes? Cadence’s stock price under his leadership is certainly one mark in his favor:Tan took over a company in crisis: Cadence had a traditional up-front licensing model that was very susceptible to industry downturns, one of which hit hard in 2008, right on the heels of a failed attempt to buy the number three player in the Electronic Design Automation (EDA) space. The revenue slump was compounded by accounting issues that led to restated earnings and the resignation of the CEO; that’s when Tan became first interim CEO in October 2008, and then permanent CEO in January 2009.Tan made some critical changes immediately. First, he shifted Cadence’s business model to subscriptions away from licensing. Secondly, he leaned into the shift towards systems design and away from standalone chips; this meant investments and acquisitions around reusable IP. Third, Cadence allied itself extremely closely with TSMC, becoming the EDA of choice for TSMC’s leading nodes; this went part-and-parcel with being the EDA software of choice for Apple, which was the customer for those leading nodes. All of this was, by all accounts, downstream froma change in culture, including an embrace of total honesty and uncompromising feedback, both internally and from customers — that’s certainly what Intel needs!At the same time, look again at where Tan — brilliantly, to be sure! — positioned Cadence: directly between TSMC and Apple during the smartphone boom. That stock price increase is impressive, to be sure, but less impressive when you look at this chart:Cadence still has the best returns here, having increased 391% during Tan’s tenure, just surpassing its direct competitor Synopsis’ 358% increase; then again, Synopsis wasn’t a company in crisis either, so they were starting from a higher base. What is clear, however, is that this was a good decade to be selling EDA software! Apple, meanwhile, was up 315% over the same time period; TSMC and Qualcomm tracked for a while, but fell off towards the end of Tan’s tenure.The point of this chart is not to diminish Tan’s track record; rather, it’s to point out that his success at Cadence was, first and foremost, predicated on surfing one of the biggest waves in technology history. It’s possible that a combination of AI and national security concerns could be that wave for Intel, but for now they are the one being overwhelmed. The massive challenge for Tan will be figuring out how to stand up on the surfboard — if in fact the surfboard even exists.All that noted, I’m pleased about this hire, because I would actually prefer if I were wrong inIntel Honesty: I want Intel to succeed, and to do so on their own terms. I thought Gelsinger’s strategy was plausible, and I’m open to the idea that he just did a bad job executing it. Tan thought he did, and now he gets to prove it.This Update will be available as a podcast later today. The point of this chart is not to diminish Tan’s track record; rather, it’s to point out that his success at Cadence was, first and foremost, predicated on surfing one of the biggest waves in technology history. It’s possible that a combination of AI and national security concerns could be that wave for Intel, but for now they are the one being overwhelmed. The massive challenge for Tan will be figuring out how to stand up on the surfboard — if in fact the surfboard even exists. All that noted, I’m pleased about this hire, because I would actually prefer if I were wrong inIntel Honesty: I want Intel to succeed, and to do so on their own terms. I thought Gelsinger’s strategy was plausible, and I’m open to the idea that he just did a bad job executing it. Tan thought he did, and now he gets to prove it.
