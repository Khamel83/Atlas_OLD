TYLER COWEN: Today I'm here with Nate Silver, who is a statistician, writer, and poker player. His latest book is "On the Edge: The Art of Risking Everything." Nate, welcome.

NATE SILVER: Thanks for having me, Tyler.

TYLER COWEN: Now, when people ask you about your career, what do you tell them you do?

NATE SILVER: I'm not sure I have a good answer for that. I think I'm kind of a writer slash analyst slash media person slash professional poker player.

TYLER COWEN: How do you think about expected utility theory? Do you think it's descriptive of behavior or normative?

NATE SILVER: I think it's more normative than descriptive, but I think that's what makes it useful. I think people have a lot of cognitive biases and the idea is that expected utility theory provides a framework for thinking through decisions more rationally.

TYLER COWEN: How has your thinking about risk evolved?

NATE SILVER: I think one thing that's changed is I've become more comfortable with uncertainty. When I first started doing polling and prediction, I thought the goal was to be as confident as possible. Now I think the goal is to be appropriately uncertain.

TYLER COWEN: Do you think most people are too risk averse or too risk seeking?

NATE SILVER: I think it depends on the domain. In some domains, people are too risk averse. In other domains, they're too risk seeking. The classic example is people buy lottery tickets but also buy insurance.

TYLER COWEN: Let's talk about poker. What has poker taught you about life?

NATE SILVER: Poker has taught me a lot about variance and sample size. In poker, you can make the right decision and still lose, and you can make the wrong decision and still win. The key is to focus on the process, not the outcomes.

TYLER COWEN: How do you handle the emotional side of variance in poker?

NATE SILVER: It's hard. I think having a bankroll helps because it gives you a buffer against bad runs. But ultimately, I think you have to accept that variance is part of the game.

TYLER COWEN: What's your view on artificial intelligence and its development?

NATE SILVER: I think AI is progressing faster than most people expected, but I also think there are still fundamental limitations. I'm optimistic about AI, but I don't think we're on the verge of artificial general intelligence.

TYLER COWEN: How do you think about prediction markets?

NATE SILVER: I think prediction markets are useful, but they're not magic. They aggregate information well, but they can also have biases and limitations just like any other forecasting method.
