Tuesday Thoughts…
  • Sean Penn’s ‘Dick’-ish employee review : Call me Ishmael, here to update you on the saga of Sean Penn ’s labor practices. When we last checked in , the National Labor Relations Board was scrutinizing the two-time Oscar winner following a letter he wrote to workers at CORE, his Covid vaccine charity. In his letter, Penn chastised disgruntled employees “who might find themselves predisposed to a culture of complaint” and suggested that, instead of “cyber whining” about feeling overworked, they could simply quit—a very Sean Penn thing to say, of course.

    N.L.R.B. Administrative Law Judge Lisa Ross didn’t think Penn’s letter rose to the level of threatening employees for trying to improve working conditions, like long hours and a lack of breaks. But the agency’s general counsel disagreed. In September, the N.L.R.B. overturned Ross. “I do not know what the Board wants me to do in this situation, other than rule in the General Counsel’s favor,” a frustrated Ross wrote, before stepping down from the case.

    Mathew Rosengart , Penn’s lawyer, told me that the labor board is “chasing Sean Penn like Ahab chasing Moby Dick.” Rosengart is appealing to the inspector general “to get the N.L.R.B., at long last, to see the light, stop wasting time and money, and drop a case that they simply cannot fairly win.” The N.L.R.B. has been very aggressive these past few Biden years, and has faced all sorts of legal fire accordingly, but involving the I.G. is an especially provocative countermeasure. —Eriq Gardner

  • Playing the Oscar odds : Ridley Scott ’s Gladiator II got a thumbs-up from Matt Belloni , but is it a best picture contender? If you’re skeptical, there’s an opportunity to profit via Kalshi, which claims to be the country’s first regulated prediction market. Currently, Kalshi gives the big-budget sequel a 55 percent shot of snagging an Oscar nomination.

    You may have heard about Kalshi’s battles with the Commodity Futures Trading Commission. The regulatory body tried to stop the platform from allowing bets on U.S. congressional elections, and in response, Kalshi asked for, and received, an injunction from a federal court to hold off the regulators. An appeal is ongoing, and while a stock market related to the Academy Awards isn’t directly part of this case, references to the C.F.T.C.’s efforts in that regard are hinting at shaky legal standing for Oscar betting. It remains legal for now, allowing Kalshi to place The Brutalist as the favorite to win best picture, with Anora and Emilia Pérez close behind. Meanwhile, Kalshi traders have money on Mikey Madison for best actress and Adrien Brody for best actor. They also think Donald Trump is going to win the election. —E.G.

  • Is Netflix liable for a teen’s suicide? : This past week, the tragic suicide of Sewell Setzer III garnered significant attention when his family filed a lawsuit against Character.AI, attributing the 14-year-old’s death to his interactions with a chatbot modeled on Game of Thrones character Daenerys Targaryen. This isn’t the first time the entertainment industry has faced legal action over a fan’s suicide. Perhaps the most famous case occurred in the 1980s, when CBS Records successfully defended against claims that Ozzy Osbourne ’s music had driven a teen to kill himself.

    Yesterday, the parents of Bella Herndon , who was 15 when she took her own life in 2017 after watching the main character in Netflix’s 13 Reasons Why do the same, received some welcome news from the U.S. Supreme Court. The justices ordered Netflix to respond to the parents’ petition that the court hear the case. The streaming giant had initially declined to respond, probably hoping the Supremes would treat it as trivial.

    Bella’s family previously filed a class action alleging that Netflix had acted negligently by recommending 13 Reasons Why to impressionable young viewers without cautioning them about risks. The case failed for a multitude of reasons, from a lack of standing to the First Amendment, but the question now before the court is a wonky one, focused on the technicalities of diversity jurisdiction—whether this case belongs in federal or state court when both Herndon’s family and Netflix are based in California while other, unidentified teenagers are residents of other states. Here’s the petition . Netflix will now have to come up with a comprehensive justification for why the justices should forgo a review. —E.G.

A Menendez Hollywood Ending
Thanks to Ryan Murphy and a Netflix docuseries, everyone is reconsidering the fate of the infamous brothers—including L.A. District Attorney George Gascón, who is in danger of being deposed in next Tuesday’s election.
ERIQ GARDNER
During my recent trip to Los Angeles, Lyle and Erik Menendez were all anyone wanted to talk about. The brothers, convicted in 1999 for the brutal murder of their parents in their Beverly Hills mansion, are now seen in a more sympathetic light, thanks largely to Ryan Murphy ’s Netflix series Monsters: The Lyle and Erik Menendez Story , and Alejandro Hartmann ’s Netflix documentary, The Menendez Brothers , which explores the severe abuse they allegedly suffered at the hands of their father, José , a former RCA Records executive. Indeed, the question on everyone’s mind—from folks at CAA to Uber drivers to the brothers’ outspoken celebrity advocate , Kim Kardashian —was whether the Menendezes would be released. Last Thursday, over the objections of some L.A. prosecutors, L.A. District Attorney George Gascón announced plans to seek the brothers’ resentencing, a move that could—pending a judge’s approval—free the duo after decades in prison.

The timing of Gascón’s decision, coming just days before an election in which he’s significantly behind in the polls, has drawn criticism from his opponent, Nathan Hochman . “He’s 30 points down in the polls, and he’s doing this now because he wants to distract voters from a record of public safety failure with a made-for-TV media event,” Hochman told me in a phone interview. Nevertheless, the D.A. race has cast a light on Hollywood’s quiet shift away from the more progressive policies that got Gascón elected four years ago—ending cash bail, ordering prosecutors not to seek enhanced sentences for prior convictions or gang affiliations, etcetera.

Hochman remained noncommittal about whether he would have advocated for the Menendez brothers’ release. “I have no idea,” he told me. “No one should weigh in on that decision unless they’ve thoroughly reviewed the case.” But he pointed out that the habeas petition was filed back in May 2023 and suggested that Gascón, after delaying a response for 18 months, could have waited until November 26, long after the election, when a Los Angeles judge was already scheduled to discuss the brothers’ fate at a hearing.

It’s unlikely that Gascón’s decision to revisit the Menendez case, however popular it may be, will do much for his electoral chances. Four years after coming to office with a progressive agenda, he faces immense backlash over the perception that his policies have made Los Angeles less safe. The traditionally liberal circles of entertainment and fashion—people like Jimmy Iovine , Peter Guber , and Alex von Fürstenburg —have moved their support to Hochman. “While I absolutely have a history of being a hard-core Democrat, I had to take a real look at Nathan’s platform and the state of our city at present,” Laura Wasser , the prominent divorce lawyer, told me. “I feel certain we need a change.”

Guests at a recent Hochman fundraiser at Il Moro in West L.A. included Netflix co-C.E.O. Ted Sarandos , Universal Music Group chair Sherry Lansing , and Lionsgate vice chair Michael Burns . Sarandos’s participation is particularly noteworthy, given the December 2021 murder of his mother-in-law, philanthropist Jackie Avant , by a repeat offender—an incident that brought considerable scrutiny to Gascón’s policies. (A few weeks after the murder, Matt Belloni reported that Gascón faced hostile questioning from Hollywood activists on a Zoom call.) Netflix co-founder Reed Hastings , who contributed $525,000 to Gascón’s 2020 campaign, has kept his powder dry this time. (A Netflix spokesperson declined to comment.)
Law & Order: Hollywood
Hochman calls himself a “hard moderate,” language that seems tailored to appeal to liberal-leaning voters who want their D.A. to crack down on crime, but not go too far. A federal prosecutor in the 1990s who eventually oversaw the Department of Justice’s tax division during the twilight of George W. Bush ’s presidency, the 60-year-old Stanford Law alum has oscillated between Democratic and Republican affiliations. Now, after surviving California’s jungle primary system, which advances just two candidates regardless of party, Hochman says he’s nonpartisan, although he’s endorsed Kamala Harris for president. When I asked him about the Los Angeles Times ’s non-endorsement of Harris, at owner Patrick Soon-Shiong ’s insistence, Hochman said he was disappointed the paper didn’t back him (Gascón got the nod) and suggested that the editorial page should either align more closely with the paper’s reporting on the city’s crime rate or get out of the endorsement business altogether.

Being a D.A. in L.A. means operating in a media fishbowl. One only has to look back to the original prosecution of the Menendez brothers, which resulted in a hung jury, an embarrassment to then-D.A. Gil Garcetti . The second trial, where the judge basically rejected abuse as a defense, culminated in convictions, a saving grace for Garcetti following his notorious failure to convict O.J. Simpson for the double murder of Nicole Brown Simpson and Ronald Goldman .

Hochman addressed the issue of how media coverage can taint a D.A.’s priorities, and faults Gascón for tailoring his criminal justice philosophies to curry favor with the public, abandoning his stated progressive principles when it suits the moment. He cited instances like the child molestation prosecution of Hannah Tubbs , a 26-year-old transgender woman who was being tried as a juvenile, until the case was featured on Fox News and Gascón announced a change in stance. Hochman also mentioned the recent murder of General Hospital actor Johnny Wactor , in which the accused killers were charged with “special circumstances,” meaning they’d receive longer sentences because of aggravating factors, something Gascón once pledged he wouldn’t do. Gascón’s campaign manager, Elise Moore , responded that Hochman is “cherry-picking to support his false narrative,” adding that Gascón has prosecuted hate crimes, environmental crimes, and police misconduct more aggressively than previous administrations.

Perhaps counterintuitively, given his concern over legal cases being influenced by outside media attention, Hochman told me he’s in favor of reintroducing cameras into L.A. courtrooms, a practice that’s dwindled since the eight-month-long O.J. trial enraptured international audiences. “Being able to hear and see what goes on in a courtroom, I think, is incredibly useful for the public,” he said. “It removes the veil of what otherwise seems like a pretty secret process.” He said he’d hope the cameras would educate and inform the public, without descending into performative legal theater.
