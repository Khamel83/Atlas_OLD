Mayer & Staggs Want to Downsize

Man, the good old days, when Candle Media was stalking Hollywood for production companies to purchase at outrageous valuations. Seems like another era, and now the Blackstone-backed roll-up company run by Disney alums Kevin Mayer and Tom Staggs is looking to offload at least one asset. I’m told Candle has engaged Raine to explore a sale of ATTN. Founded by Matthew Segal and Jarrett Moreno , ATTN is a video-based media publisher and creative agency with a social-issues angle. Candle acquired ATTN in May 2022 for a reported $100 million in cash and stock, and it’s been a bright spot in the challenged portfolio, more profitable and bigger than when it was bought, per a source familiar.

But ATTN isn’t as core to the current Candle thesis as Reese Witherspoon ’s Hello Sunshine and its other production entities—Exile, Faraway Road, and Candle True Stories, all of which have been consolidated under Hello Sunshine C.E.O. Sarah Harden . Candle also owns Cocomelon studio Moonbug Entertainment, which operates separately. Are those two units also for sale? A Candle rep says no, but a separate bank, Moelis & Co., is still advising on potential M&A deals on the studio side. And now Raine is out pitching ATTN in the publishing and creative agency world.

It’s gonna be interesting to see how the merger dance plays out among all these independent, privately backed producers that were conceived (and valued) on pre-correction models. I could see Moonbug selling to Disney or a rival, and the studio group could roll into Legendary or another general-interest player—though probably not for anything near that $900 million valuation that Candle put on Hello Sunshine back in 2021.

The Courtship of Brett & Melania

Amazon’s decision to pay up for a documentary about Mrs. Trump, given the current political environment, may not be such a shocker. But Ratner’s return to moviemaking represents a more significant vibe shift.

Kim Masters

“I thought I was being punked,” a veteran agent told me after hearing the news that Amazon had agreed to pay an eight-figure tribute to license a Melania Trump documentary directed by the canceled filmmaker Brett Ratner . That agent was hardly the only one in shock. First, there was the price tag, as reported by my partner Matt Belloni . “$40 million? That’s absurd. Maybe $5 million,” an executive who has experience in the documentary space told me. The general assumption was that Jeff Bezos , the Amazon founder, had discovered yet another avenue to work his way into Donald Trump ’s deepest (and most crowded) orifice, weeks after pulling The Washington Post ’s endorsement of Kamala Harris and earning a prized seat on oligarch’s row at the inauguration.

The truly baffling question was how Ratner had been welcomed into the project and, literally, the White House. The director, who rose to prominence via the Rush Hour movies and directed sequels to franchises as varied as X-Men and The Silence of the Lambs , had been trying, and failing, to get himself uncanceled since the Los Angeles Times published an exposé, in 2017, in which six women accused him of sexual misconduct. (Through his attorney at the time, Marty Singer , Ratner “categorically” denied the allegations, and he was never charged.)

Ratner has long rolled in eyebrow-raising circles: Among others, he’d been tight with the music mogul Russell Simmons , who has faced multiple rape accusations (Simmons has denied the allegations against him), and oligarch Roman Abramovich . In 2022, Abramovich issued a denial following a report alleging that he’d asked Ratner to loan him money after his assets in the U.K. and the U.S. were seized in response to Russia’s invasion of Ukraine.

Ratner also has deep connections to people who matter in Trumpworld. Starting in 2013, for instance, he was partners in RatPac-Dune Entertainment, a film finance/production company, with Steven Mnuchin , who subsequently became Treasury secretary during Trump I. He then partnered with Len Blavatnik , the owner of Warner Music Group and a political donor whose contributions to G.O.P. candidates suddenly accelerated in 2015. (Small world: In the 1990s, Blavatnik, a Ukraine-born American citizen, had been in business with oligarch Oleg Deripaska . In 2019, Mnuchin faced hell from congressional Dems for his plan to lift sanctions on Deripaska.)

Ratner appears to have been the first and only choice to direct the Melania film—though, in fairness, how many respected directors would want to make a documentary controlled by this particular subject? By mid-December, a source involved in the bidding for the project told me that Ratner had been installed in an eight-bedroom house at Mar-a-Lago as Melania’s guest. He hadn’t even met her yet, this source said. (Ratner did not respond to a request for comment.)

Melania told Fox News that the doc offers a look at her life “from transition team to moving to the White House, packing, establishing my team, the first-lady office, moving into the White House…” Her early life and career apparently will not be covered extensively or at all. A source with knowledge of the situation told me that Ratner shot for more than 30 days and had such unfettered access to the White House that Secret Service agents were said to be uneasy. Unsurprisingly, I’m told Elon Musk is a presence in the film, too. (The White House declined to comment.)

At this point, filming on the Melania doc has wrapped. Her reps insisted on a theatrical release, which insiders say will be brief and limited. The film will stream on Amazon Prime Video and then will be expanded into a separate three- or four-part docuseries. There are rumors that Ratner may also be making an additional film about Trump. (Amazon declined to comment.)

The Usual Suspects

After the downfall of Harvey Weinstein in late 2017, studios and agencies set about ridding themselves of individuals accused of sexual misconduct, even if the allegations fell short of actual assault. Now, we have a president who was found liable by a jury for sexual abuse being filmed by an alleged harasser at the first lady’s behest. It remains unclear who initiated the idea of bringing Ratner into the Melania project, but a source with a first-hand view of the goings-on told me that Melania herself said she was a fan of Ratner’s work ( Tower Heist ? Red Dragon ?) and liked the idea that he was an outcast. I suppose that’s plausible, but there were others who could have made the connection, such as Marc Beckman , Melania’s representative of many years, and the founder of Designers Management Agency.

A source told me that Beckman, who had set up the recent Melania book, was acquainted with Ratner through some earlier business dealings involving a client of his marketing firm. (The company’s website lists past or present clients including the NBA, Warner Bros., Procter & Gamble, Gucci, and the New York City Ballet.) Beckman confirmed to me that he handled the negotiations with bidders, but he did not respond to other questions.

There was also the producer of the doc, Fernando Sulichin , who pulled together financing before Amazon was in the picture. Born in Argentina and now living in London, Sulichin keeps a low profile and is not widely known in Hollywood. But he has a certain Zelig-like way of turning up in unusual places. In 2015, Sean Penn and the Mexican American actress Kate del Castillo travelled to meet and interview drug lord Joaquin “El Chapo” Guzmán at his hideout in Sinaloa and nearly got caught up in a planned government raid. Sulichin was there.

Sulichin also arranged for Oliver Stone to conduct 20 hours of interviews with Vladimir Putin , which became the four-part Showtime series The Putin Interviews . (Several critics took issue with Stone for being, in the words of the New York Times review, “solicitous, even obsequious” toward his subject.) “Fernando has remarkable access to powerful people, and I don’t know how and why,” one longtime associate told me. “He likes being discreet. He doesn’t like headlines.” Having known him for years, this person told me, “I don’t know what’s behind the curtain.” (For those interested in a potential meet-up, a source says the producer will be in L.A. this week.)

There is some conflict among sources about whether Sulichin was involved with the Melania doc and then brought in Ratner, or whether it was the other way around. One person who was involved in the bidding told me that he was “highly confident” that Sulichin came first; another source close to Sulichin insisted that Ratner brought him into the project. But a Sulichin associate remembered that the producer had once questioned whether the “coast was clear” for Ratner to make a comeback. (Sulichin did not respond to a request for comment.)

Ratner may have had another important player in his corner, as well. Born in Miami, the 55-year-old filmmaker has said that he didn’t know his biological father, but that Al Malnik , a lawyer and businessman once alleged to have ties to organized crime (which he has denied), had played a paternal role in his life. Back in the day, Malnik represented Rat Pack stars Dean Martin and Sammy Davis Jr. as well as a number of Las Vegas hotels. On his website —yes, the now-91-year-old Malnik has one—he refers to Ratner as his “eleventh son” and mentions that the two were at work on some unspecified documentaries. Several sources told me Malnik has very much wanted to help Ratner get back to making movies.

It should surprise no one that Malnik happens to be a Mar-a-Lago guy. Last June, Modern Luxury Palm Beach ran a photo-heavy piece entitled The Ultimate Palm Beach Wedding at Mar-a-Lago , which featured the nuptials of Malnik’s son Jarod and his bride, Alexa . Trump put in an appearance beside the happy couple with his familiar thumbs-up pose. It’s not clear whether Malnik had any role in helping Ratner get the Melania gig, but perhaps the relationship helped ease the director into Trumpworld.

One source involved with the project wonders whether Beckman, the agent who handled the licensing of the film to Amazon, fully grasped how rancid Ratner’s reputation had become in Hollywood, even apart from the allegations against him. “I don’t know whether Marc really knows the breadth of this guy,” this person said. “He strikes me as someone who really doesn’t know much about that old-school, sordid male world” that Ratner inhabited.

The Tsujihara Affair

Ratner, after all, was closely associated with one of the biggest scandals in recent Hollywood history. Charlotte Kirk , the British aspiring actress who brought down then-Warner Bros. chairman and C.E.O. Kevin Tsujihara and NBCUniversal vice chair Ron Meyer , was able to pursue her Hollywood dreams in the U.S. because, in 2012, Ratner went to bat for her with the Department of Homeland Security. The director wrote that Kirk, then 20, had “remarkable talent” with a “unique ability to deliver each of her lines seamlessly.” Kirk got a special visa, but the problem for both Tsujihara and Meyer was that she was very much not in demand. Despite their powerful jobs, their efforts to help her land roles failed.

In 2013, Ratner and Australian billionaire James Packer , then Ratner’s partner (the “Pac” in RatPac Entertainment), introduced Kirk to Tsujihara to celebrate their new $450 million film financing deal with Warner Bros. Having apparently tired of his own affair with Kirk, according to text messages that I previously reported at The Hollywood Reporter , Packer was ready to pass her along. “I have the opportunity of a lifetime for u,” he texted Tsujihara one night in September 2013. “Come to [the Hotel] Bel air now. U will never be able to pay me.” Meanwhile, he texted Kirk, saying he was going to introduce her to “the most important man u can meet.”

That led to what Kirk later described as an unpleasant sexual encounter in the hotel that night. But she saw the married Tsujihara again, all the while pressing him to help her find acting jobs. Tsujihara resigned in 2019 after a former colleague and I reported on his relationship with Kirk. The following year, NBCUniversal fired Meyer because of his own entanglement with the actress.

As she continued to press Tsujihara to help her launch a career, Kirk was repeatedly texting Ratner to demand help finding acting roles. The situation led to a legal confrontation over a confidential settlement Kirk had signed, but an arbitrator took a skeptical view of Kirk’s claim that she was the victim of “sex trafficking.” Lawyers for the actress drafted a complaint alleging that Ratner and associates “passed Kirk around like an inanimate sex toy,” but neither that document nor any lawsuit was ever filed. Ratner, via Marty Singer, his lawyer at the time, denied everything.

“Out of Whack”

The fact that Amazon ended up acquiring Ratner’s film is not without irony. Roy Price , the previous head of Amazon Studios, resigned in 2017 due to misconduct allegations that I had started reporting even before Weinstein’s downfall. Amazon made a point of replacing Price with a woman, Jen Salke . And when the order came down to license the Melania doc, she was tasked with the negotiation that would put the studio in business with Ratner.

Of course, Salke got it done, albeit at a price that was many millions higher than what the other two bidders, Disney and Paramount, offered. “I don’t think Jen Salke saw this as a piece of content she had to have,” said an executive at another studio that made a far more limited offer for the doc. “All of it is out of whack.”

Though Prime Video is run by Mike Hopkins , Amazon has several women in top jobs in its entertainment operations, in addition to Salke. Marketing chief Sue Kroll will have to sell the doc, and Courtenay Valenti , who oversees theatrical and streaming films, will have to handle its release. Though they would hardly say so publicly, it’s a very safe bet that none of these women has a scintilla of interest in dealing with this project.

Yes, everything seems to be out of whack—not just in Hollywood, which is still reeling from the streaming revolution, the pandemic, and two strikes—but in Washington and in the world. We have an administration that has barred government employees from forming affinity groups for women and people of color, a president who blamed a deadly plane crash on D.E.I. initiatives even before the bodies had been pulled out of the Potomac, federal agencies that have banned the word “feminism.” Meanwhile, sexual misconduct allegations don’t even seem to register. So the Ratner movie might also seem to represent a return to the bad old days for what used to be called the “weaker sex.” He’s pushed for years to make another Rush Hour movie without success, but we all know how much the studios love their recognizable I.P. Will progressive Hollywood allow him back? It’s pretty clear at this point that almost anything can happen. It’s enough to make one wonder whether Melania still has that jacket—the one reading, “I really don’t care. Do u?”
