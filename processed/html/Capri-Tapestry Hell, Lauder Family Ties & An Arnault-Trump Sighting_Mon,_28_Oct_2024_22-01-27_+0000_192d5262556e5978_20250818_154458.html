Three Things You Should Know…
  • Rachel on the Jane Lauder news : After Fabrizio Freda announced in August that he would be retiring as the C.E.O. of The Estée Lauder Companies at the end of this fiscal year, Jane Lauder was on a shortlist of candidates who could succeed him. After all, she was the executive vice president and chief data officer and erstwhile heiress apparent—the niece of chairman emeritus Leonard and granddaughter of Estée , herself. Jane was also the last family member with an operational role at the beauty giant. In the last month, however, it became clear that Stéphane de La Faverie would prevail. Last night, in advance of Thursday’s earnings and the imminent announcement of The Estée Lauder Companies’ next C.E.O., Jane announced that she was stepping down but would remain on the board. A few hours later, the Journal confirmed that de La Faverie would get the job instead.

    That domino effectively paved the way for Stéphane’s ascension. “Jane felt like the board wasn’t really doing what it was supposed to, and she’s going to––with all of her shares, name, experience, and board seat––do what she can to stop everyone from wearing blinders,” said a person close to the family, insinuating that Lauder will now be able to offer a more lucid perspective on the company. “The family thing is always a big deal. Is there friction sometimes? Absolutely,” this person continued. “But I can’t imagine that they would have wanted [Jane] to make this decision, and the fact that she did is incredible.” More to come on Wednesday. — Rachel Strugatz

  • Remember that time Alexandre went to Mar-a-Lago? : I got a text this weekend from someone squarely in the High Fashion for Harris camp who said they had it on good authority that LVMH scion Alexandre Arnault was getting a group together to go to the Trump rally at Madison Square Garden on Sunday. I promptly registered to attend and have since been inundated with SMS spam. After all that, I didn’t make it to the rally—my kid and a source call took priority—but Puck’s senior Washington correspondent, Tara Palmeri , was on the lookout, and confirmed with her people that Arnault was indeed there. (I later heard from a source that he was spotted around 3 p.m., likely in one of the donor booths, but left before the speeches began.) I reached out to a rep for Arnault at Tiffany, where he works, for comment. They did not respond.

    The Arnaults have made a point of maintaining a relationship with Trump, stretching back to the now-infamous opening of the Louis Vuitton factory in Texas in October 2019, which the president attended. (There was a photo of Alexandre with then-Louis Vuitton C.E.O. Michael Burke ; his father, LVMH C.E.O. Bernard Arnault ; Trump; and Ivanka Trump that made the rounds, with Alexandre occasionally misidentified in captions as Jared Kushner .) Ever since, the 32-year-old Alexandre and his wife, Géraldine Guyot-Arnault , whose 2021 wedding in Venice was attended by Beyoncé , Jay-Z , and Roger Federer , have spent plenty of time with the Kushners. Just this past September, Ivanka attended a store opening for Guyot-Arnault’s fashion line, Destree, on the Upper East Side.

    Of course, high-altitude political patronage is one thing. But a trip to the MSG rally, where Elon Musk joined a horde of MAGA-world C-listers, goes beyond any sort of social obligation, diminishing not only Alexandre’s personal brand, but possibly even besmirching the company’s. (Even the Kushners have distanced themselves from Trump during his four years out of office, undoubtedly hoping to be invited to more events like that Destree store opening. They did not attend the rally.)

    Alexandre has paid at least one visit to the former president in recent years—a dinner at Mar-a-Lago in early 2023, according to Trump —and with good reason. Like Musk and Jeff Bezos , the Arnaults have a lot to lose if they don’t make nice with Trump, who once threatened to slap a 100 percent tariff on French wine in retaliation for a tax on U.S.-based businesses operating in France. (A trade war between Airbus and Boeing that began in 2004 was at the root of the jousting.)

    In the end, most wine, handbags, and cheese were protected, but Trump did institute a 25 percent tariff on certain goods—including cognac imported from France—in January 2021. While the tariff was only enforced for a few months, the Biden administration only temporarily suspended it, which means it could be reinstated pretty easily. Incidentally, cognac sales—a meaningful contributor to LVMH’s wine and spirits division—have been down significantly in recent years, both in the U.S. and China.

    Anyway, like a lot of these rich guys, the Arnaults believe that being nice to a vindictive Trump is more important than being nice to Harris. Forget the cognac tax. If he is reelected and they don’t show their loyalty, he may retaliate and enforce that handbag tax after all.

Life After the Tapestry-Capri Annulment
There are rarely any redos when a proposed corporate marriage of this magnitude fails to make it to the altar. Alas, Plan B doesn’t look good for Capri, and even worse for Michael Kors.
LAUREN SHERMAN
On Thursday, a federal judge sided with the Federal Trade Commission, blocking the $8.5 billion merger between Tapestry and Capri. The two companies said they would appeal, but their plea is almost unequivocally doomed. Judge Jennifer Rochon ’s ruling accepts the F.T.C. logic of essentially inventing a new market category—“accessible luxury” handbags—and then agrees that the merged company would dominate it. By the agency’s own self-imposed narrow definition, I suppose that’s true.

Shortly after it all went down, a friend forwarded a Substack article on the subject from anti-monopolist Matt Stoller . The judge, Stoller wrote, was entirely in line with the law. Moreover, he argued, it was bizarre that Tapestry and Capri had argued that “these companies would still compete even if they were all part of the same conglomerate, instead of cooperating to raise prices. That’s a weird statement, to assert a corporation wouldn’t profit maximize.”

Sure, he’s right, but fashion is weird. You see this kind of internal competition all the time within the groups in Europe. Kering, LVMH, and Richemont all price their different brands differently—some lower, some higher—to appeal to different segments of the market. That said, it’s embarrassing to everyone involved to keep calling a combined Tapestry and Capri the “LVMH of the U.S.” (It was never going to portend that sort of dominance…) And while I still believe that this was far too small a merger to invoke the ire of the F.T.C., I suspect that their definition of “accessible luxury” really means outlet-store luxury , which Coach (Tapestry) and Michael Kors (Capri) do dominate—and would really dominate in partnership.

Capri’s stock nosedived 47 percent on Friday, while Tapestry’s shares shot up 14 percent, perhaps reflecting the fact that many executives are still in shock, while some claim they saw this coming. Now, the companies will appeal because the deal they struck requires them to do so. But it won’t do anything, or course, and both companies will need to figure out what’s next fairly quickly. The Biggest Loser
Let’s start with Capri: First off, condolences to group C.E.O. John Idol , he of Kors-I.P.O. and Tommy Hilfiger-exit fame, who was supposed to retire years ago but hung on to pull off one last big deal. Now, there’s no way he stays and, ergo, no way Capri looks the same in 18 months. Jimmy Choo and Versace, the two smaller brands in the portfolio, are likely to sell quickly—for less than the cost that Capri acquired them but at a reasonable price nonetheless.

The biggest loser here is Michael Kors, which has fewer options. One close observer predicted it would go to a licensor, such as ABG or G-III, which owns Donna Karan. Kors would be a large business for them to absorb, but also a ubiquitous and valuable one. So the real condolences go out to Michael Kors , himself, who did everything right to maintain the value of the ready-to-wear while his counterparts on the operations side missed out on several opportunities to take the business further upmarket.

Tapestry is in a stronger position due to its successful repositioning of Coach, which began a decade ago. Sure, the company still leans on off-price, but it also sells handbags to young people in a convincing way—from the Tabby bag, originally an outlet-only style that eventually became a full-price hit, up to this Brooklyn hobo that’s been trending on TikTok. That said, I don’t think Tapestry exists in its current form in a year and a half, either. The other two brands in the portfolio—Kate Spade New York and Stuart Weitzman—have languished, proving that the company doesn’t have the executive talent to make things work across a portfolio of three , let alone six.
Those two brands will almost certainly be sold. There’s an argument that Tapestry C.E.O. Joanne Crevoiserat should still try to create a larger group by acquiring other companies that are not competing in the accessible-luxury handbag space. LVMH-owned Marc Jacobs, which Tapestry has looked at in the past, even though LVMH says it’s not for sale, wouldn’t work. Nor would independently owned Tory Burch—also not for sale—which is obliterating the competition both at full-price but also at outlet, where it has supplanted Kate Spade New York.

Crevoiserat could go after a ready-to-wear brand like Khaite, which is small but pure luxury and currently backed by a private equity owner. In the past, people have suggested to me that Alo Yoga, which was looking to raise at a $10 billion valuation at the end of last year, would be an interesting addition. After all, Coach has succeeded because it’s offering something young people can relate to, and that’s exactly what has made Alo successful.

The problem with all those scenarios is that it’s very unlikely the Tapestry board would be willing to give Crevoiserat, a C.F.O. by trade, another cycle to get a deal like that done when this one failed so miserably. Indeed, at this magnitude, failed deals usually lead to fired C.E.O.s.
