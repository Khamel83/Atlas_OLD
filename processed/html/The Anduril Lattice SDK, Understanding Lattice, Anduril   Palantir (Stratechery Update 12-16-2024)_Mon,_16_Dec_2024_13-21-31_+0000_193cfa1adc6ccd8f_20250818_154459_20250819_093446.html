The Anduril Lattice SDK

On Tuesday, Palmer Luckey, the founder of Anduril, posted the following on X :

I, unfortunately, didn’t see this tweet until Wednesday morning, after I had already posted that day’s Update; fortunately — for me, anyways, if not Luckey! — the only story I was able to find about the big announcement was this press release reprint in the Orange County Business Journal :

Defense company Anduril Industries said today it is releasing Lattice SDK to enable partners to build decentralized applications that operate directly on Lattice’s infrastructure. The SDK provides developers with data model definitions, API bindings, code samples, and reference implementations, making it easier to create solutions that seamlessly integrate into the Lattice ecosystem. Costa Mesa-based Anduril also said it has launched the Lattice Partner Program, an effort designed to enable companies to integrate diverse capabilities into Lattice.

“At launch, the program features a diverse cohort of over 10 partners, demonstrating the expansive range of capabilities that can be integrated through Lattice. Among these are Apex, Forterra, Impulse Space, Numerica, Oracle, Saronic, Scale AI, Spire Global, Striveworks, Textron Systems and Valinor; each contributing unique technologies that enhance operational efficiency and situational awareness,” Anduril said.

Anduril was co-founded by OC entrepreneur Palmer Luckey.

God bless the local angle!

In fact, though, I understand the lack of coverage; SDKs aren’t sexy like drone defense or autonomous air vehicles or autonomous underwater vehicles or the other cool hardware products that Anduril is known for, and which make for great headline photos or B-roll video. What this SDK represents, however, is the most audacious statement of purpose yet from Anduril, and this publication for one is extremely interested.

Understanding Lattice

Last September I interviewed Anduril Co-Founder and CEO Brian Schimpf and I introduced Lattice this way (I’m going to selectively copy-and-paste from the interview extensively, but I recommend the whole thing):

When you think about a defense contractor, you think about hardware, weapons of war. We sort of joked about that at the very beginning, and the prompt to have you on Stratechery now was the announcement of this new drone fighter Fury , which we’ll get to in a little bit. But what you’re saying, and I think rings true to me, is that hardware is probably the wrong frame of reference for thinking about Anduril and why you are fundamentally different than other defense contractors.

BS: Yeah, I think that’s right. I mean, look, at the end of the day, the DOD, the Department of Defense, they know how to buy hardware. They can count the number of planes, they know why it costs money, they can count the number of missiles. There’s a very clear understanding of what a price for that should be, what you’re paying for and what you’re getting, it’s very obvious. The government does not believe it will run a factory to produce these things on the whole. There’s some things they produce, which is very random, but the software side is not that today.

So the software side, it is constantly a discussion and debate around how much of the government own the IP, what should the structures be, how do you get the incentives you want out of industry? It’s a really tricky problem. Where you look at it from the government’s perspective, they’re sort of like, “Well, it doesn’t cost you to make marginal copies of this, so why am I paying you?” And you look at it from the software companies perspective-

Software is a pure R&D cost. It’s not a marginal cost, which doesn’t match up with the way government is paid for stuff traditionally.

BS: Exactly. So you just have this massive mismatch, and that is a real challenge. So by doing the hardware side, we’re in a lot of ways able to wrap it in metal. The software is often the, “Why us?”. It’s like we’re able to solve problems in an interesting and novel way because we can make them smarter, but it’s very hard to price that, understand that, know how that fits, but the hardware side enables us to solve this.

The second piece of this is the hardware of what has been built to date is not designed around how do I have the right software APIs, control, be able to deploy modern machine learning models into it, it’s just not how it works. It’s very hard-coded, it’s very baked, it’s very designed for manual control of these things, and so when you want to apply all these advanced software techniques, it falls apart. So designing hardware that’s open and API-driven from the beginning, that has enabled us to be extremely effective of how we tie these together as an interesting family of systems.

So let me see if I have what you just articulated right from a big picture perspective, I want to tie it all together. So you are a Silicon Valley startup, and you are able then to get investors that understand the idea of investing huge upfront amounts in R&D to build a software stack knowing that you can monetize in the long run, so that lets you build this differentiated operating system, basically Lattice, that then in the long run will let you produce very highly capable weapons that are much more capable relative to their marginal cost, which is highly differentiated. You could sell it to the military, “Look, you can get this weapon for relatively cheap, that’s incredibly capable because we have this ability.” It’s because you did the R&D because you’re a software company, you have this massive go-to-market advantage in the long run for any weapons platform you want to build. Is that the good one-minute summary there?

BS: That’s basically spot on and that’s exactly how we think about the business.

If you grok this excerpt, you get why this announcement is a big deal. Anduril isn’t in the hardware business, they’re in the software business. Or, more specifically, Anduril isn’t a drone company, they’re an operating system company — a true tech platform.

That “platform” word is funny; “platform” is a word that the Department of Defense is familiar with, but with a very different meaning: in military terminology the F-35 is a platform, a submarine is a platform, a tank is a platform, etc. These are self-contained products that deliver weapons, operate sensors, transport humans, etc. They of course have software — a massive cost and major driver of delays — but that software is custom-built for the hardware that it runs on. Note the word that I used above; in tech we call these “products”.

A platform in tech, on the other hand, is the foundation for products, and is usually an operating system of some type. Windows is the preeminent example: Windows provided defined APIs for hardware makers on one hand, creating a market for computers, peripherals, etc., and a set of software APIs on the other that let developers build applications that ran on Windows.

Platforms are extraordinarily valuable, both to the world and to the company that builds them. Again, look at Microsoft: an untold number of companies owe their existence to the fact that the Windows platform existed, and the productivity gains to the world from having a common platform for applications were enormous. Microsoft, meanwhile, became the most valuable company in the world, and its founder the world’s richest man.

It’s hard to imagine a world without Windows — or iOS or Android or Linux or the Internet itself (not all platforms are technically operating systems, to be clear) — because the benefits of platforms are so enormous, but give it a try: we might, for example, still use a word processor for typing, calculators for calculating, stereos for playing music. Those are easy examples, because they existed before platforms, but then you realize how much more we do on computers or smartphones that simply doesn’t make sense in world of dedicated hardware; moreover, actually trying to operate across individual products would be unfeasible given the impossibility of sharing and coordinating information.

That non-platform world, however, is military reality with its products-called-platforms; the DoD has ships and planes and submarines and tanks and artillery and radar and sonar, but the coordination layer is all humans, with all of the lack of scalability and potential for error that entails. Moreover, there are a host of other potential weapons systems — like the myriad of apps on your smartphone — that simply don’t exist because they are impractical to build. Worse, this is a problem that is only becoming more acute, particularly with the explosion of data and the importance of communication, which is exactly what Lattice is focused on.

Back to the interview:

So I want to dive a little bit more into Lattice. You mentioned the three different platform or three areas that — we’re using platform too much, I have to avoid it — the three different areas that you’re building products in, and that Lattice started in the sensor area. Was Lattice extended to all three, or do you have distinct operating system platforms between those three?

BS: We try to take it as one kind of holistic platform, and we really think about it as more of a la carte umbrella where everything works together, very similar to AWS, it’s like everything is going to work. You’re going to have the same security and networking across everything, and the same sort of model, but certain times you need different components.

At the crux of it, we think of it as networking layer at the base, so how do I move data around between all these different assets, all these different systems? In light of the challenges that DOD has, which is everything is over a radio link, everything is getting jammed, interrupted, and the network is constantly changing. There’s no high-speed pipes that you can really rely on.

Then we think of it as three different pieces of what you’re really trying to do. One is sense-making — how do I take all the sensors that are available in my environment and understand what is going on? In a self-driving car sense, this is like, “Where are lane lines? Am I staying in the road?”. In the defense world, this is typically, “Where is the adversary aircraft? Are those friendly aircraft or adversary aircraft? What is their intent? What are they trying to do?” And you’re pulling this from lots of different systems. So, that sense-making piece is absolutely huge. So, we do that both on a single platform and we do this across platforms.

And this really ties into the networking layer where if you get cut off from the network, can you still accomplish that in isolation? Does this scale up from an individual weapon to a pod of weapons to the overall platform and it has to go up and down seamlessly?

BS: That’s right. Yeah, it’ll go all the way from if I get fully cut off, I still complete my mission and do what I need to do locally, to I talk to the team I have, to I start pulling in information and sharing information with different granularities across the whole theater of operations as I need to, and as I have availability. So, you can kind of think about it operating at lots of different levels with lots of different information sharing at every stage.

But that sense-making is really key. In a lot of ways, that is what DOD does. They want to find out, “Where’s the adversary and how do I engage them most effectively?” either because I’m maneuvering and I don’t want to get found, or I want to stop them or defeat them or knock out some capability. So, that targeting is a huge part of this and we think about that as one of the pillars of this. We do this across computer vision, radar systems, radio signals, you name it, we’re kind of working across a very wide array of sensors on that.

Then there’s the decision support side. So, “How do I have a user interface that actually enables people to make informed decisions with the right information at the right level?” This is a really hard challenge, which is when I’m talking about tasking and operating these systems, coming up with even the vocabulary of, “How I tell it what to do and how do I interface with that and keep track of a whole battle-space while an adversary’s trying to confuse you?”, that’s very hard. We spend a lot of time on that user interface and, “How does this actually work?”.

The third part is that, “How do I actually task these effects or make these things happen in the real world?”. This is where we add a lot of the autonomy functionality into what we do. This could be as simple as, “Tell a camera to go look somewhere”. In the higher-end cases, this is, “Hey, I have a team of aircraft and they’re going to go look for radar systems and they’re going to fan out, get the right geometry to figure out exactly where they are, then as they identify them, get imagery of it and then you can decide, ‘Okay, now I want to strike this one. Okay, what weapons do I have available?’ Go out and do it.”

This is a platform, and it’s clearly necessary, but again, the DoD buys hardware, not software. That meant that Anduril had to sell weapons powered by Lattice, not Lattice itself, which further meant integrating into hardware makers; I asked Schimpf about this:

One thing that’s interesting is as part as the Fury announcement, you announced that you were acquiring Blue Force Technologies , which worked on Fury, and that was just like a week after you’d announced you were acquiring Adranos , which makes solid rocket motors. You’ve acquired Area-I , Copious Imaging , Dive Technologies .

I’m curious about this approach because I could see two explanations here and maybe it’s both. One is Lattice is an operating system and there are network effects and there’s a bit where bringing that into your network has compounding effects and for any startup going forward, this is the obvious exit path. Number two is Anduril has spent six years figuring out the DOD and the sales process and how to get into it and that’s such a difficult task that it’s just easier to go via you if you’re someone new. Is it both those, is it a little bit A, little bit of B?

BS: Yeah, I think it’s both those. I think absolutely on the Lattice network effects piece, I think it’s true. I think as we add more platforms, more sensors, more capabilities into this, everything gets more valuable, I think that’s spot on.

Can a startup build for Lattice without being a part of Anduril or do they have to be a part of Anduril?

BS: We’ve definitely had third parties integrate. We’re looking at doing more of a partner program on this, its just such a weird space in the DOD where the value story is pretty straightforward and if you’re an Apple or any of these things around, “How do you capture value on this, how do you create value?” — it’s such a simple win-win scenario, but in the DOD case, it’s quite hard. It’s not obvious how you do this in a win-win scenario and so we’ve been playing with some different models on it.

You can’t put up a developer site and let anyone download the SDK or whatever it is.

BS: Exactly, right. But say we even did that, you’re like, “Okay, did I enable you to get access to a new market?”, kind of, but not really. You still had to go find these contracts and go through this brutal sales cycle and then say I do this, “Can I capture any sort of revenue share or value from your integration with it?” It’s like, “No, that’s not really how the contracting works”. So, it’s like figuring out this win-win story is quite challenging, even if it’s exactly what the DOD wants, they ask for it all the time. Just figuring out how to get it there is not obvious from just the basic economic incentives.

It feels like this is the workaround, right? Ideally the DOD would actually fix their process and understand that in a software world, you have to allow for R&D ahead of the purchase, but there’s a bit where Anduril’s becoming the API for the Pentagon, where if you want to actually build something new, the way to get in is going to have to be via Anduril because the DOD is locked into this five defense contractor view of the world that they themselves pushed and inspired the Last Dinner a bit. It’s almost like you have a network effect on multiple levels, not just a software network effect, but like a channel network effect in some respects.

BS: That’s right. And the channel side, I think, is to your second point actually huge. The ability to — because when you sell these DOD programs, it’s not just, “Do you have the right tool?”, it’s do you have the security infrastructure and the program management support and the ability to do all the Hill and lobbying pieces and work with the Pentagon? Do they believe you can scale? Do you have a production system that can scale your hardware? All of these are very, very hard and these are areas where I think when we’ve done these acquisitions, we’ve been able to accelerate these companies on all of those fronts. How do we actually make these even more successful and enable them to scale much bigger?

This is where this week’s big update comes in. First, Anduril has in fact put up a developer site and released an SDK. Secondly, there is encouraging news that the Pentagon is starting to understand what true platforms are all about. Two weeks ago the Pentagon announced a new $100 million contract with Anduril for software , specifically Lattice; from Breaking Defense :

As the US military embraces artificial intelligence and big data, the Pentagon is trying to wean itself from reliance on centralized mega-systems and instead empower frontline leaders at the “tactical edge.” That’s why, last week, the Pentagon’s Chief Digital & AI Office (CDAO) awarded startup defense firm Anduril a $100 million, three-year contract to expand its Lattice Mesh. Lattice is a system that lets frontline forces rapidly access data from over a hundred different types of sensors — for example, to track incoming hostile drones — over existing tactical networks, without having to first relay that data through central processing hubs.

Then, this week, Anduril released a Lattice Software Development Kit (SDK) that will allow other companies to build their own applications to run on Lattice Mesh, without having to get permission from Anduril. The company announced an initial list of 10 partners, ranging from startups like Apex (space systems) and Saronic (unmanned boats) to giants like Oracle and Textron. But in the future, an Anduril executive told Breaking Defense, any company can access the kit and write software for Lattice…

It’s a decentralized business model for a decentralized military system. It’s also a departure from traditional practice for both the defense industry and the Defense Department, which historically favor centralized mega-projects where a single lead contractor can achieve “vendor lock” and keep competitors out for decades. But the recent Lattice Mesh announcements suggest the rise of a more open, flexible and adaptable approach…

What’s in it for Anduril? While Keane didn’t say so outright, the firm is clearly betting that an open door on Lattice Mesh is good for business, much as Apple benefits from letting third parties develop applications for their iPhones: More third-party options make the underlying product more useful, so it sells better.

It goes both ways: yes, 3rd-party applications, sensors, and even weapons will make Lattice more valuable; what is critical, though, is that Lattice will make those applications, sensors, and weapons more viable, less expensive, and more effective. That is the power of platforms.

Anduril + Palantir

One of the questions that I had about Anduril previously was how they fit alongside Palantir, which has a similar operating system-type platform approach to data, but which operates more at the battlefield commander level. That Breaking Defense article actually gets at an important distinction:

This open-door, decentralized approach is not unique to Lattice Mesh. Indeed, Lattice is just one of several pieces of the military’s evolving capability to share intelligence and transmit orders across US and allied forces on land, sea, air, space, and cyberspace — known as Combined Joint All-Domain Command & Control (CJADC2). Over time, the military has backed off from one-size-fits-all approaches to CJADC2. Instead of imposing a single universal data standard and a single mega-network, it’s moving towards a “federated” approach that allows different systems for different niches but tries to make them all compatible.

That wasn’t obvious from the start, Palmieri emphasized. “When we started off in January of 2023, we thought … we could find one capability that would do all those things,” she told Breaking Defense. But through a series of quarterly Global Information Dominance Experiments, “what we realized was that there wasn’t a single data integration layer that you could select as best of breed across all those organizations and missions … What you needed at the strategic and operational level for data integration was different than what we needed at a tactical level.”

When CDAO released the initial operational toolkit for CJADC2 — officially called the “Minimum Viable Capability” and publicly announced in February — it focused on strategic coordination at the four-star Combatant Commands. But data-sharing tools for headquarters staff aren’t suitable for combat units, Palmieri said. Headquarters need huge amounts of information to produce detailed plans in hours or days; frontline commanders need much smaller volumes of data, but they need it right now. That second niche is where Lattice Mesh came in. “The data mesh capability is not the answer for everything,” Palmieri said. “What we found with the data mesh that Anduril provided was it was really good at that low-latency data exchange.”

It’s the first niche that Palantir is aiming to fill, and as further evidence that these two approaches are compatible is this news from Bloomberg :

Software company Palantir Technologies Inc. and weapons maker Anduril Industries Inc. plan to accelerate the use of artificial intelligence in the US military and are inviting other companies to join the effort. Under an agreement announced Friday, battlefield information created by sensors, vehicles, robots and weapons will be collected by Anduril’s Lattice software and then pulled into a secure Palantir platform that will prepare the data for use in AI training and development. The data gathered would including information classified at the highest levels of secrecy.

This is the exact structure laid out above: Lattice is the operating system for the battlefield, meant for in-the-moment decision-making, while Palantir is the ultimate source of truth, meant for analysis and battle-planning. The needs and corresponding priorities are completely different, so it makes sense that different companies are doing the respective work; what is critical, however, is that they work together, and this agreement is very good news in that regard.

Stepping back, there are three broader points to make.

First, I expect the “operating system of the U.S. military” category to be exceptionally lucrative, and frankly, it’s hard to think of two companies and founding teams that deserve it more than Anduril and Palantir. Both companies were founded at the height of tech anti-American and anti-military sentiment, and both sets of founders were derided in many quarters for their choices; they’ve earned this opportunity.

Second, it’s not a surprise that this advancement is coming from tech. One, there is the platform concept I wrote about above. Two, the idea of co-opetition — because all of tech needs to work together at some level — lends itself well to building out appropriate systems at different levels of the stack that work together. Three, the venture capital funding model is the only way these companies could fund the R&D to even get in the Pentagon’s door in the first place.

Third, Palantir is in particularly good shape, because it’s approach is aligned with Enterprise Philosophy and the First Wave of AI ; Anduril is much more of a pure play military operation. At the same time, the platform aspects of Lattice have the potential to have a broader impact in terms of startup opportunities, particularly when it comes to manufacturing. I’m happy to help Luckey get the word out in that regard.
