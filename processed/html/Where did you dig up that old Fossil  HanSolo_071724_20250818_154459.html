💥"Where did you dig up that old Fossil?" - Han Solo💥

It’s been a while since we last discussed Fossil Group Inc. ($FOSL):

💥Watch Out!💥

Feb 11
Read full story

Since 3Q’23, the company has continued to struggle. Net sales were down 15.6% YoY and 21.6% YoY for 4Q’23 and 1Q’24, respectively. Adjusted EBITDA continues to be in the red even for the holiday season of 4Q’23. And liquidity is now down to $122.8mm as of 1Q’24 compared to $217.2mm in 1Q’23.*

The company has also retained Evercore Inc ($EVR) as financial advisor to try and tackle the present financial pressures.

S&P Global took note of all this and downgraded the company from CCC+ to CCC citing an “unsustainable” capital structure, “very high leverage”, and “uneven cash flow prospects.” The 7% unsecured notes due ‘26 ($150mm principal) are, as of July 17, 2024, pricing at around 12c. Yeesh! The stock hasn’t done so great either:

And given these poor results (and some activist pressure that we’ll delve into more below), there’s been a significant changing of the guard at Fossil.

The company’s CEO, Kosta Kartsotis, stepped down upon 4Q’23 results and COO, Jeffrey Boyer, stepped up as interim CEO. Three months later, CFO, Sunil Doshi, resigned and Ankura Consulting Group LLC affiliated Andrew Skobe subsequently became interim CFO. Mr. Skobe’s LinkedIn page highlights his “[p]roficiency with corporate restructurings, financings.”

Things have been shaking at the board level too. Two new independent directors joined the ten member board: Eugene Davis and Pamela Corrie.

Let’s take a look at these two new directors

According to the press release, in the past five years, Mr. Davis has been on the board of Aeromexico, Babylon Holdings Limited, Bluestem Group Inc, GTT Communications, MediaMath Holdings Inc, Loyalty Ventures Inc, PGX Holdings Inc, and Skillsoft Corp.

What do all those names have in common?

Yep, every single one has been in bankruptcy proceedings. But hey, just a coincidence right? Mr. Davis is notoriously active on distressed boards — basically the O.G. who kicked off the whole independent director industry in bankruptcy. Indeed, the press release omits about 5,234,114 distressed boards over the (many) years.

Wait. Eugene Davis. We saw that name and <sniffing around> we wondered whether Akin Gump Strauss Hauer & Feld LLP (“Akin”) might be somewhere near this thing; Mr. Davis and Akin have a long-standing working relationship. And, lo and behold, guess who is listed as a notice party on behalf of the company:

What about Ms. Corrie? She joined the board as part of a cooperation agreement with shareholder, Buxton Helmsley Capital Partners LLC (“Buxton”).** Buxton previously ran an activist campaign against Fossil earlier in the year. It featured some choice words for management. Here’s Buxton pushing for new board member appointments:

“Fossil is a beloved brand that has delighted millions of customers for years. However, it has become clear to us how much the Company’s future is in danger. For far too long, Fossil’s Board and management have destroyed massive shareholder value due to a lack of proactivity, made numerous, inexcusable strategic blunders, and – now made very clear to us – represent a grave threat to the Company’s investors and long-term prospects.”

“The facts under the current regime are disturbing: Fossil’s stock price has dropped approximately 97% since CEO Kosta Kartsotis was named Board Chair in May 2010, not a single incumbent director or executive officer has purchased even one share of the Company’s stock in the open market in three years, and five of eight directors have never purchased a single share since joining Board. Further, the Company continues to employ a CFO who either inappropriately answered or entirely refused to answer basic questions related to financial statements he previously certified. From his silence, in the face of what should be easy to answer questions, we believe the CFO is leaving open the possibility of later abandoning his previous certifications of the Company’s financial statements.”

In this letter to the board, Buxton even made mention of its previous engagements in Endo International Plc and Mallinckrodt Plc:

“In both instances, the companies filed for bankruptcy protection just over a quarter after [Buxton] had gone public with specifically identified apparent violations of applicable accounting standards and securities laws by these companies.”

“Thus, [Buxton] has a well-established track record of uncovering, identifying and successfully arguing apparent accounting standard and securities law violations.”

Talk about a veiled threat LOL.

“While at previous companies that [Buxton] and certain of its investors held a financial interest in (including Endo and Mallinckrodt), we were forced to initiate public campaigns and discuss serious alleged violations of applicable and securities regulations, we do not believe that will be required in the case of this Company and sincerely hope that belief will remain intact.” (emphasis added)

Buxton be like:

But, as we’ve mentioned, the CEO and CFO that Buxton lambasted in the letter are both now gone and in exchange for the appointment of Ms. Corrie to the board, Buxton has entered into a standstill period.

And Ms. Corrie isn’t unfamiliar with distressed companies either. She was literally a restructuring attorney at Weil Gotshal & Manges LLP and has been on the board of names like Bed Bath & Beyond, Pier 1 Imports Inc, Boxed Inc., Lord & Taylor, and recently, JOANN Inc.

At least the board is in experienced hands when/if Fossil finds itself in bankruptcy.

💥New Chapter 11 Bankruptcy - Tubular Synergy Group LP💥

On July 9, 2024, Dallas-based Tubular Synergy Group LP (“Tubular”) and affiliate OCTG Connections LLC (“OCTG,” together, the “debtors”) filed chapter 11 bankruptcy cases in the Northern District of Texas (Judge Everett). The debtors are a sales, marketing and supply chain services distributor of casings, tubing and line pipe for the onshore oil and gas industry. They’re looking for a “good, old-fashioned restructuring,” as Holland (“Holly”) O’Neill, an attorney at debtor counsel Foley & Lardner LLP, phrased it at the first day hearing. They expect to file a plan in the coming months and seek to emerge as a “leaner, stronger” company, according to the first-day declaration of CEO W. Byron Dunn.

Dunn, a native Texan, veteran of fifty years in the oil patch and former CEO of Lone Star Steel, founded the debtors in ‘08 after U.S. Steel ($X) bought Lone Star. Tubular affiliate OCTG (an acronym meaning “oil country tubular goods”) holds various patents, specifically, a proprietary pipe connection for use in horizontal drilling.

The debtors’ capital structure is straightforward: a senior secured revolver via CIT Northbridge Credit LLC (“CITNB”). CIT supported a ‘23 refinancing, which saw the facility upsized to $45mm. Dunn’s narrative begins on March 4, ‘24, which happens to be the day CIT “temporarily increased” the revolver commitment to $55mm and established a “Special Advance Loan” not to exceed $5.5mm in aggregate. Why? Dunn’s declaration does not say. But on April 15, the debtors received a notice of default, the first of three(!). CIT claimed non-compliance with the loan agreement and declared loans “in excess of the Borrowing Base immediately due and payable.” That works out to about $39.32mm. CIT commenced a cash-flow sweep. Dunn says this “severely constrained” liquidity and resulted in “severe disruptions” to the debtors’ operations, and we do not think he’s bullsh*tting us.

Another notice of default landed on June 18. This one alleged non-compliance with six different sections of the loan agreement, including “...alleged misrepresentation pursuant to Section 4(c) of the First Amendment.” The third and final arrived ten days later: non-compliance with two more sections. Basically the debtors, according to CIT, tripped every section of the loan agreement other than the preamble, 😝.

What’s the cause of all the trouble? Dunn is silent. We’re left to wonder. Have exogenous circumstances pushed some ratio to a level shrieking disaster? The debtors successfully navigated periods of worse stress in the oil and gas market, Dunn says. They serve customers not just in Texas, but all the oil-producing states: North Dakota, New Mexico, Oklahoma, Colorado, Mississippi, Arkansas, Alabama, Pennsylvania, and Louisiana. It’s not like they’re trapped in a non-producing basin. Of course the entire industry is going the way of the dinosaur, thanks to the vaunted energy transition! But that’s . . . a bit in the future, perhaps? In the meantime, crude is up 15% this year. Does the one-rig drop in the Baker-Hughes Onshore U.S. Rig Count augur a looming collapse? No; increased per-well productivity has rendered rig count increasingly moot. So what is it? Covid? Supply chain? The impact of AI? Last year, Dunn said this in the press release announcing the refi/upsize: "Demand for our tubular products has been steadfast while inflationary pressure has altered our costs, which compelled us to upsize our credit facility." Hmm, that’s interesting: the upsize was to address runaway costs? Have we read that right? But that was last year. Very 15 minutes ago. People laughed at Paul Krugman when he said the U.S. economy had attained the “goldilocks” state of declining price pressures without job losses. Well, haters, core CPI rose a measly 0.1% in June. A beautiful inflation report! Who’s laughing now?

Here’s the Dallas Fed’s Texas Manufacturing Survey from June, which doesn’t sound very “goldilocks” to us:

So . . . maybe two cuts before year-end is back on the menu? The market seems to think one is on the way and is increasingly pricing in more.

Speculation, of course: it may be something else entirely. CITNB, in its reservation of rights related to various debtor motions, mentioned that in the weeks prior to the filing, “...the parties were discussing CITNB’s willingness to take ownership of its collateral in light of these chronic defaults, likely through a foreclosure, which CITNB was preparing to effectuate.” The debtors knew of CITNB’s plans. CITNB, for its part, “...was unaware that the Debtors were preparing to file these Chapter 11 Cases until the Petition Date.” You’ve gotta love that automatic stay: stopped CITNB’s machinations in their tracks.

The debtors obtained all requested relief on an interim basis at a two-and-a-half hour first-day hearing on July 11, 2024. It was not without drama. The cash collateral motion filed July 9, 2024 by the debtors (docket 9) was — despite the bargeloads of “adequate assurance” promised CITNB — of a non-consensual nature. Something of a shocker to CITNB, don’t you think? Winston & Strawn LLP, counsel for CITNB, and Foley for the debtors hashed out a deal that was largely complete by the hearing. The interim order (docket 49) grants the debtors the use of cash collateral until a second interim hearing scheduled for July 25, 2024. The order specifies the following (emphasis added):

The Debtors are authorized to use the Lender’s Cash Collateral to fund payroll expenses and critical ordinary course post-petition operating expenses to the extent they are required to be paid during the Cash Collateral Period; provided that during the Cash Collateral Period, the Debtors shall provide the Lender with a report by 5:00 p.m. CT each day of all payments they intend to make on the next business day and such payments will be deemed to be in compliance with this Interim Order and can be made by the Debtors unless the Lender provides a written objection (by email from the Lenders to the Debtors, copying counsel) to such payments by 10:00 a.m. CT the following day. (emphasis added)

A negative notice approval! Cool, huh? The parties also discussed the debtors’ need to pay critical vendors. A certain yard refused to release pipe to a customer of the debtors unless the debtors paid its bill. Dunn, under oath, testified that failure to deliver would result in “big demerits.” Critical indeed! The Court ultimately agreed to authorize the debtors to make the payment, but to first use “best reasonable efforts” to find an alternate vendor. It’s as if someone finally remembered the original “critical vendor” standard, go figure, 🤔.

The debtors are represented by Foley and Lardner LLP (Holland N. O’Neil, Stephen A. Jones). CITNB is represented by Winston & Strawn LLP (Jordan Klein, LeElle Slifer, Greg Gartland, Amila Golic). We reckon they’ll both be working awfully hard over the next several weeks to determine what, exactly, this filing might ultimately achieve that would result in something different than what CITNB originally contemplated. 🤷‍♀️

🔗What We’re Reading (8 Reads)🔗

The company email inbox is drowning in interesting stories. Here are some links from the past six weeks that caught our eye:

1. Barnes & Noble Education Inc ($BNED)(Long Lifelines). BNED completed a series of transactions the week of June 11, 2024 that included a $95mm equity raise through a $50mm new equity investment and a $45mm equity rights offering, a $34mm conversion of existing 2L debt into common stock, and an extension of its $325mm ABL facility to '28. What seemed to drive the stock down over 50% that week, however, was the 1-for-100 reverse stock split. It's pretty clear that the reverse split was a desperate move for the company to remain in compliance with NYSE listing standards. The company has once again kicked the can down the road, though it is only a matter of time before it comes back around. The stock is up 2% over the past month.

2. Distressed Hospitality (Long Williamsburg Hipsters). The William Vale hotel in Brooklyn finally sold as part of the All Year Holdings chapter 11 bankruptcy case with EOS Hospitality getting the property for $177mm, a nice increase off of a prior $157mm deal fell through in ‘22. Proceeds will be used to pay off bondholders in Israel (a subject we previously covered here).

3. Food Delivery (Short Customer Orders). Service fees. Delivery fees. App fees. Tips. Customers are beginning to revolt against food delivery apps in the face of city-imposed wage increase requirements.

4. Media (Short Game of Thrones). This is a tough read from The Verge. Choice bit:

“It feels like this bananas, bananas cultural event that I don’t think a lot of people will ever experience the same way again,” Renfro said. She was still talking about the fantasy TV show, with an equal measure of relief and nostalgia, and as I listened, I hoped it would not one day be how people spoke about journalism, too.

Meanwhile, instead of pivoting to video or podcasts or whatever the f*ck like every media company has done over the last 15 years, it appears all they needed to do was film Hollywood stars choking on their own hot sauce covered tongues to make money. Here is Bloomberg writing how, on one hand, BuzzFeed Inc. ($BZFD) can’t seem to find a buyer for First We Feast, the company that owns Hot Ones, the wildly successful YouTube program (for the $70mm asking price) but, on the other hand, Hot Ones scores $30mm in annual revenue “…largely from brand and licensing deals….” 🤯

5. Multi-Level Marketing (Short Cringe ): We read this in the not too distant past, which foretold the end of the age of the cringe marketer, aka “influencer.” Maybe they’re all just flocking to TikTok? Here is Business of Finance on the latest and greatest threat to the MLM model:

Direct selling or multi-level marketing companies were already being weakened by changing consumer preferences, and the growing power of indie brands. Now, TikTok Shop is coming for their valuable base of salespeople.

6. Rental Cars (Short EV Fleets). On June 21, 2024, Hertz Global Holdings Inc. ($HTZ) announced the issuance of $750mm of 1L senior secured notes and $250mm of exchangeable senior 2L secured PIK notes. The $750mm 1L notes represent an upsize from the previously announced $500mm, have an interest rate of 12.625%, and are due in '29. The exchangeable notes have an interest rate of 8% and are also due in '29. The exchange rate is initially equal to 150.9388 shares of common stock per $1,000 in principal value, which, at close, represented an 89% premium to the $3.51 closing price. Following the announcement, the company's 4.625% and 5% notes jumped nearly 10% to ~74c and ~64c, respectively.

This report comes a few weeks after the company disclosed heavy losses from its failed attempt to enter the EV market. The company is still in the process of offloading 11k EVs.

7. Retail (Long Forever21 Being Forever Financially Challenged). Per CNBC, “Forever 21 is asking landlords for a break on rent as the legacy fast-fashion player’s sales decline and it struggles to keep up with savvier competitors….” Here we go again? “The retailer, which has more than 380 stores in the U.S., has asked some landlords to cut its rent by as much as 50% … [w]hile the company is facing financial difficulties, it has yet to hire advisors and isn’t considering a second bankruptcy protection filing….” Is that ‘yet’ doing some heavy lifting? Time will tell.

8. Sam Bankman-Fried (Long a VC Legend). FTX was an epic failure and SBF is now working on his ping pong game while advising convicted criminals on crypto in prison. Out in the free world, however, the FTX bankruptcy plods on and some news dropped early in June that left us aghast. Here is The Block:

In the latest update from the FTX estate, helmed by CEO John Ray III, the company has offloaded its remaining shares in Anthropic, the AI startup behind the chatbot Claude, according to the firm's latest bankruptcy filings.

FTX sold the remaining 15 million shares for about $30 each, netting over $450 million in proceeds. That brings the total haul from FTX's original $500 million investment in the company to about $1.3 billion, for a profit of around $800 million.

Wait, what?!? SBF invested in Anthropic in October ‘21, y’all. That’s a $800mm haul in less than three years. Say what you will about SBF and how he managed FTX but he may be one of the best VC investors of all time purely on the basis of that one investment. Share 💥New Chapter 11 Bankruptcy Tubular Synergy Group LP💥 On July 9, 2024, Dallas- based Tubular Synergy Group LP (“Tubular”) and affiliate OCTG Connections LLC (“OCTG,” together, the “debtors”) filed chapter 11 bankruptcy cases in the Northern District of Texas ( Judge Everett ). The debtors are a sales, marketing and supply chain services distributor of casings, tubing and line pipe for the onshore oil and gas industry. They’re looking for a “good, old- fashioned restructuring,” as Holland (“Holly”) O’Neill , an attorney at debtor counsel Foley & Lardner LLP , phrased it at the first day hearing. They expect to file a plan in the coming months and seek to emerge as a “leaner, stronger” company, according to the first- day declaration of CEO W. Byron Dunn . Dunn, a native Texan, veteran of fifty years in the oil patch and former CEO of Lone Star Steel , founded the debtors in ‘08 after U.S. Steel ( $X ) bought Lone Star. Tubular affiliate OCTG (an acronym meaning “oil country tubular goods”) holds various patents, specifically, a proprietary pipe connection for use in horizontal drilling. The debtors’ capital structure is straightforward: a senior secured revolver via CIT Northbridge Credit LLC (“CITNB”). CIT supported a ‘23 refinancing , which saw the facility upsized to $45mm. Dunn’s narrative begins on March 4, ‘24, which happens to be the day CIT “temporarily increased” the revolver commitment to $55mm and established a “Special Advance Loan” not to exceed $5.5mm in aggregate. Why? Dunn’s declaration does not say. But on April 15, the debtors received a notice of default, the first of three(!). CIT claimed non- compliance with the loan agreement and declared loans “in excess of the Borrowing Base immediately due and payable.” That works out to about $39.32mm. CIT commenced a cash- flow sweep. Dunn says this “severely constrained” liquidity and resulted in “severe disruptions” to the debtors’ operations, and we do not think he’s bullsh*tting us. Another notice of default landed on June 18. This one alleged non- compliance with six different sections of the loan agreement, including “ ... alleged misrepresentation pursuant to Section 4(c) of the First Amendment. ” The third and final arrived ten days later: non- compliance with two more sections. Basically the debtors, according to CIT, tripped every section of the loan agreement other than the preamble, 😝. What’s the cause of all the trouble? Dunn is silent. We’re left to wonder. Have exogenous circumstances pushed some ratio to a level shrieking disaster? The debtors successfully navigated periods of worse stress in the oil and gas market, Dunn says. They serve customers not just in Texas, but all the oil- producing states: North Dakota, New Mexico, Oklahoma, Colorado, Mississippi, Arkansas, Alabama, Pennsylvania, and Louisiana. It’s not like they’re trapped in a non- producing basin. Of course the entire industry is going the way of the dinosaur, thanks to the vaunted energy transition! But that’s . . . a bit in the future , perhaps? In the meantime, crude is up 15% this year. Does the one- rig drop in the Baker- Hughes Onshore U.S. Rig Count augur a looming collapse? No; increased per- well productivity has rendered rig count increasingly moot. So what is it? Covid? Supply chain? The impact of AI? Last year, Dunn said this in the press release announcing the refi upsize: "Demand for our tubular products has been steadfast while inflationary pressure has altered our costs, which compelled us to upsize our credit facility." Hmm, that’s interesting: the upsize was to address runaway costs? Have we read that right? But that was last year. Very 15 minutes ago. People laughed at Paul Krugman when he said the U.S. economy had attained the “goldilocks” state of declining price pressures without job losses. Well, haters, core CPI rose a measly 0.1% in June. A beautiful inflation report! Who’s laughing now? Here’s the Dallas Fed’s Texas Manufacturing Survey from June, which doesn’t sound very “goldilocks” to us: Source: Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas So . . . maybe two cuts before year- end is back on the menu? The market seems to think one is on the way and is increasingly pricing in more. Speculation, of course: it may be something else entirely. CITNB, in its reservation of rights related to various debtor motions, mentioned that in the weeks prior to the filing, “... the parties were discussing CITNB’s willingness to take ownership of its collateral in light of these chronic defaults, likely through a foreclosure, which CITNB was preparing to effectuate.” The debtors knew of CITNB’s plans. CITNB, for its part, “ ... was unaware that the Debtors were preparing to file these Chapter 11 Cases until the Petition Date. ” You’ve gotta love that automatic stay: stopped CITNB’s machinations in their tracks. The debtors obtained all requested relief on an interim basis at a two- and- a- half hour first- day hearing on July 11, 2024. It was not without drama. The cash collateral motion filed July 9, 2024 by the debtors (docket 9) was despite the bargeloads of “adequate assurance” promised CITNB of a non- consensual nature. Something of a shocker to CITNB, don’t you think? Winston & Strawn LLP , counsel for CITNB, and Foley for the debtors hashed out a deal that was largely complete by the hearing. The interim order (docket 49) grants the debtors the use of cash collateral until a second interim hearing scheduled for July 25, 2024. The order specifies the following (emphasis added): The Debtors are authorized to use the Lender’s Cash Collateral to fund payroll expenses and critical ordinary course post- petition operating expenses to the extent they are required to be paid during the Cash Collateral Period; provided that during the Cash Collateral Period, the Debtors shall provide the Lender with a report by 5:00 p.m. CT each day of all payments they intend to make on the next business day and such payments will be deemed to be in compliance with this Interim Order and can be made by the Debtors unless the Lender provides a written objection (by email from the Lenders to the Debtors, copying counsel) to such payments by 10:00 a.m. CT the following day. (emphasis added) A negative notice approval! Cool, huh? The parties also discussed the debtors’ need to pay critical vendors.
