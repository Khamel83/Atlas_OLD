About Those CAA Layoffs…
The long-awaited CAA layoffs hit on Friday, and they were mostly as predicted. The total at Hollywood’s biggest and most influential talent agency, for now, is about 30—largely in the motion picture and television departments. That’s a small blip on the surface of the Death Star, which employs about 3,500 people worldwide—though more agents have been given the boot and also the courtesy of some time to go elsewhere, if they can. ( Josh Pearl , a veteran comedy agent, landed at Mosaic as a manager today. There are a few more I won’t name because they’re still scrambling.)

Of the group that’s public, I think senior TV literary agents Joel Begleiter and Adam Biren are the most surprising, both because they were given zero warning and were generally productive and liked. More reductions are almost certainly coming in the new year, per sources, and reviews and bonus conversations will be brutal. (CAA declined to comment.)

Honestly, I’m a little surprised there weren’t more cuts. CAA, and all of the major talent agencies, were built for an entertainment business that no longer exists: Cable TV is dead, pilot season is dead, the rest of broadcast is teetering, and the streaming model doesn’t produce nearly the kind of long-term hits that pay for all those agents, with their assistants and set visits and lunches at Nerano. The Writers Guild killed packaging, which means the studios now keep the fees that used to go to the agencies, and that loss is only going to worsen as the spigot of past packages slowly runs dry. Last year’s strikes exacerbated the decline in series and movies being made, and, given the growth in audience and monetization of user-generated content, that decline may be forever. Fewer shows and movies, less talent needed for those shows and movies, fewer traditional film and TV agents needed to service that talent. Easy math.

CAA has specific issues, too. It operates on an October-to-October fiscal calendar, meaning that the period just completed was particularly decimated by the strikes, which ended in September but didn’t lead to an uptick in production until well into 2024. In its 2022 acquisition of rival ICM Partners, CAA took on all those agents and employees to get the publishing and sports divisions it wanted, as well as the three ICM showrunners who actually matter. At the time, the bulk wasn’t a big concern, and with that extra EBITDA, C.E.O. Bryan Lourd was able to generate the $7 billion valuation he coveted in last year’s sale of owner TPG’s stake, which allowed him and partners Richard Lovett and Kevin Huvane to extract hundreds of millions of dollars. But in the changed landscape, the bulk has become more like deadweight, and CAA’s new parent company, the family office of Kering fashion mogul François-Henri Pinault , is far from immune to the general slowdown in the luxury market. It periodically squeezes CAA on costs, per sources.

CAA certainly isn’t alone here. Rival UTA has undergone its own recent layoffs, and WME is now caught up in parent Endeavor’s ongoing and somewhat tragicomic effort to take the whole company private after spending years trying to go public. Endeavor C.E.O. Ari Emanuel announced a complex deal today to buy out Endeavor assets OpenBets and IMG Arena and put them in a separate, management-owned entity. That kind of asset-building is what Ari cares about most, not the stagnating Hollywood representation business. To these Big Three diversified agencies, with sports and branding and books and chefs and influencers, so few individual clients actually matter now, and because of that, so few agents actually matter, too.

And this is a more uncomfortable topic, but the decades-old agency apprenticeship model—toil on someone’s desk, get promoted, join the team for a few big clients and then, if you’re lucky, develop your own—has produced a ton of junior and mid-level reps that, in a challenged market, just aren’t good enough. Or at least they don’t sign or take over enough of their own clients. They’re servicers, “on the team,” which is a CAA hallmark and is fine when there are tons of high-commission clients to service. But less fine when the revenue—and the entertainment business in general—no longer justifies the big team.

Quote of the Week
“We feel good about our position and our ability to remain a stand-alone.”
— Chris McCarthy , the Paramount Global co-C.E.O., talking about its streaming strategy on Thursday’s earnings call—which, of course, is an amusing contradiction of McCarthy’s own comments a few months ago, when he was not only feeling not good about the company’s position in streaming but was specifically outlining his dual-track plan to get Paramount out of its position as a stand-alone streamer. The goal, he declared at an employee town hall in June, was “to enter a deep, long-term relationship with a leading technology platform, which already has the full scale that we are trying to obtain.”

This, he said, “would allow us to focus a greater percent of our budget on the things we do best—that’s making hit content.” The other option, he announced, “involves us joining forces with one or more other S.V.O.D. players.” So, a merger or joint venture.

Hmmm. What could possibly have changed McCarthy’s mind so drastically? After all, he knew this summer that Paramount’s streaming business would soon be announced as profitable. It might be that Skydance, which in the intervening months has become Paramount’s incoming owner, wants to make a go of streaming, and McCarthy, more so than his fellow co-C.E.O.s, George Cheeks and Brian Robbins , is hoping to remain part of the company post-acquisition.

Thanksgiving’s Refreshingly Old-School Box Office Battle
For the first time since ‘Fantastic Beasts: The Crimes of Grindelwald’ opened days before ‘Ralph Breaks the Internet,’ in 2018, this year’s mid-November box office slate will mimic the pattern from the prior two decades. Everyone should win.
SCOTT MENDELSON
The pre-Thanksgiving trifecta of Gladiator II, Wicked: Part One , and Moana 2 provides a veritable pre-Covid-esque theatrical cornucopia that should produce pre-pandemic box office returns. As we’ve seen semi-regularly since early 2021, theatrical tentpoles are crucial to bringing the overall marketplace back. In short, if you release it, they will come.

By moving Wicked to the weekend before the holiday break, Universal is able to position Jon M. Chu ’s $145 million musical fantasy, based on the popular stage show seen by more than 65 million people, as this year’s proverbial Hunger Games- or Twilight -sized YA blockbuster. Moana , for its part, is the most watched film of all time on Disney+. If the theatrical industry has returned to normalcy, both movies should do serious box office.

Official tracking has Wicked opening around $90 million and Moana 2 earning as much as $135 million over its Wednesday-Sunday Thanksgiving weekend debut frame. Buzz for Wicked is also growing, with presales among its female-centric fan base likely to increase as word spreads on social media and the review embargo drops. Overseas tracking is less certain, but Wicked has earned $5 billion worldwide as a stage musical, and Moana is an A-level Disney princess property worldwide. Even many of Disney’s underwhelming 2020s animated films pulled strong overseas-skewing splits. Wish wasn’t a “hit,” but it did generate 75 percent of its $255 million worldwide cume outside of North America.

Meanwhile, in a typical year, Ridley Scott ’s well-reviewed Gladiator II might have opened in early November—like Scott’s American Gangster, in 2007, which starred Gladiator II ’s Denzel Washington alongside former Gladiator headliner Russell Crowe . However, Hollywood mostly avoided the weeks before and after Election Day, so it, too, is playing in the holiday sandbox. Thanks to its larger budget, Gladiator II now has to fill the role of both adult-skewing counterprogramming option—see also: Knives Out in 2019 or Denzel’s Deja Vu in 2006—and a tentpole unto itself. As of this writing, the $250 million, R-rated actioner is tracking for an estimated $65 million domestic debut. I’ve heard that the overseas tracking is strong, suggesting that the movie might be an example of nostalgia-skewing I.P. that succeeds in other territories, unlike Twisters and (relatively speaking) Beetlejuice Beetlejuice .
The Wednesday-Sunday Window
For the first time since 2018, this year’s mid-November slate might mimic the box office pattern from the prior two decades, at least in terms of overall grosses for the pre-Thanksgiving YA fantasy flick and the holiday weekend Disney toon. And like most such Turkey Day showdowns in the 2000s and especially the 2010s, it could be an everybody-wins weekend.

Traditionally, Thanksgiving was topped not by the holiday tentpole, but by the young adult sensation flick opening the week prior . In most years, when a YA fantasy franchise movie (such as one of the Twilight sequels) opened the weekend before the holiday, that film’s second weekend would beat the opening weekend of the holiday’s big opener, be it a Disney toon or otherwise. In 2019, Disney slotted Frozen II the week before Thanksgiving, positioning it as the YA fantasy flick and the family-friendly animated feature of the season. It worked.

However, then and presumably now, there’s room for multiple winners during what might look like a contested weekend. In 2013, The Hunger Games: Catching Fire earned $100 million in its Wednesday-Sunday “second weekend” (after opening to $158 million), while the first Frozen went wide for a $93 million Wednesday-Sunday debut. Catching Fire would become that year’s biggest domestic earner ($425 million), while Frozen topped the worldwide list with $1.3 billion. Neither Lionsgate nor Disney had any reason to complain.

In 2010, Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows: Part I beat Tangled over the long Thanksgiving weekend ($75 million versus $67 million) and in overall grosses ($960 million versus $600 million). Both Warner Bros. and Disney were happy with their respective results.
Rising Tides Lifting All Boats?
Heading into the holiday season, Moana 2 is tracking to crush the $125 million Wednesday-Sunday haul for Frozen II (earned in its second weekend, following its $130 million debut in 2019) to net a new Thanksgiving record. Even if the animated sequel ends up looking and feeling like a Disney+ series pretzeled into a theatrical sequel, strong 2010s nostalgia for Disney titles—think Avatar: The Way of Water , Inside Out 2 , and Deadpool & Wolverine —has been kicking butt the past two years.

Meanwhile, enthusiasm for Wicked should continue to build among fans of the Broadway show between now and opening day. Those interested in movie-specific hooks, like pop star Ariana Grande in a scene-stealing comic turn, will also tune in. Interestingly, Gladiator II was essentially tied with Wicked for overall interest ( as of this writing ) among women.

Could the frequently shirtless Paul Mescal and co-star Pedro Pascal draw otherwise interested female audiences (and their male companions) away from Wicked on opening weekend and over the holiday? Sure, but the fact that its audience skewed 80 percent female didn’t hurt the $142 million opening of The Twilight Saga: New Moon in November 2009. History has shown that a demographic ceiling matters less if the ceiling is high enough.

It would be encouraging if Disney’s first big Thanksgiving hit of the 2020s weren’t a sequel, but alas… Hopefully, C.E.O. Bob Iger remembers that you can’t (theoretically, not a prediction) make $1 billion off Moana 2 without rolling the dice on (and being satisfied with a $643 million global total for) an original movie—like, say, Moana , in 2016. Similarly, whether Wicked: Part One or Moana 2 comes out on top, Universal might get more of a benefit from a “new” franchise in Wicked . After all, Moana 2 ’s success won’t change the narrative over Disney’s reliance on previously successful properties. If that’s the conversation we’re having on December 2, it will be good news for all invested parties.
