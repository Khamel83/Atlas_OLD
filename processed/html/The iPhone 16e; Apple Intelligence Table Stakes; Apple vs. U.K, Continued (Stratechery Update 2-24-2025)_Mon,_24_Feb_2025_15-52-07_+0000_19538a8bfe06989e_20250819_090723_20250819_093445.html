The iPhone 16e

From Bloomberg :

Apple Inc. introduced a new low-end smartphone called the iPhone 16e priced at $599, aiming to revive growth after a sluggish holiday season. The rebranded device goes on sale Feb. 28 and will cost $170 more than the $429 iPhone SE that it replaces. Preorders for the phone begin Feb. 21.

Apple modernized the device by removing the now-antiquated home button and adding a larger, 6.1-inch screen with Face ID. The model has a 48-megapixel camera, the same A18 chip as the iPhone 16 and a USB-C charging port. It also runs the company’s AI software, Apple Intelligence. In a first, the product also includes an in-house cellular modem chip. That replaces a key component from Qualcomm Inc. in a long-awaited shift.

The new installment represents the biggest change in the history of Apple’s low-end iPhone, which was introduced as the SE in 2016 and last updated nearly three years ago. As part of the shift, the current iPhone SE and iPhone 14 have been discontinued…The new model should give Apple a more compelling way to reach the lower end of the smartphone market. The company spent years working on the device, aiming to close the gap between its low-end phone and the main iPhones with newer technologies like facial recognition.

The dominant framing of the iPhone 16e is that Apple is abandoning the low-end phone market; Mark Gurman wrote in his weekly Bloomberg column :

Apple Inc.’s revamped low-end iPhone has finally arrived and the most significant development isn’t a new feature or app, but rather the price. It’s $599, which is $170 more than the iPhone SE it replaces. That change puts the new iPhone 16e model in a whole different category than its predecessor. And it means Apple has effectively abandoned the budget smartphone market, ceding those sales to Samsung Electronics Co., Alphabet Inc.’s Google and the China-based players.

It's interesting to look at the history of the low-end iPhone:

SE 3rd Gen 2022 $429 64 GB
16e 2025 $599 128 GB

That is indeed a pretty big price increase! The first factor to consider, however, is inflation; here are historical prices normalized to January 2025:

Model Released Price Storage 2025 Price
SE 1st Gen 2016 $399 16 GB $532
SE 1st Gen v2 2017 $399 32 GB $520
SE 2nd Gen 2020 $399 64 GB $491
SE 3rd Gen 2022 $429 64 GB $474
16e 2025 $599 128 GB $599

That's still a price increase, but a much lower one, particularly relative to the original iPhone SE. Still, even keeping pace with inflation is notable, because the most striking thing about recent | iPhone | announcements is the fact that Apple has not increased prices with inflation, i.e. iPhones have gotten cheaper:

The goal of this strategy has, presumably, been to sell more iPhones, thus maximizing the addressable market for Apple services, but that doesn't seem to be working. Apple doesn't disclose iPhone unit sales, but given that nominal pricing has remained the same it seems safe to assume that iPhone unit sales are basically holding steady:

It's impossible to prove a counterfactual of course; it may be the case that the iPhone's decreasing real price has stopped a significant decline in unit sales. I think it's more likely, however, that iPhone demand is pretty inelastic: if you want a new phone you're going to get one, and Apple's marketshare is what it is. And, to the extent that is the case, Apple is arguably leaving money on the table by not increasing iPhone prices to at least keep pace with inflation.

Still, even with inflation accounted for, the 16e is still more expensive than its predecessors at the low end. There's another factor to consider, though: tariffs. The recent 10% tariffs President Trump levied on all Chinese goods does not — in contrast to the tariffs in his first term — have an exception for iPhones.

Tariffs are levied on the landed price of products, which is typically the invoice from the manufacturer plus freight and insurance costs; if we assume that Apple wants a 45% margin on every iPhone it sells, that necessitates a $21 price increase over the original iPhone SE:

  • 1st Gen SE price: $532
  • Target margin: $239
  • Landed price: $207
  • Tariffs: $21
  • Total: $553

Now we're within $46, or a bit more than half that amount to cover significantly upgraded components — including Face ID, much more storage, and an A18 chip — if Apple wants to keep their margins.

Apple Intelligence Table Stakes

There's one more factor — which may ultimately invalidate the entire last section! — and I think it has to do with the name change from SE to 16e: the latter suggests that this iPhone will be upgraded somewhat regularly. To that end, note Apple's current lineup:

Price Chip
iPhone 16 Pro $999 A18 Pro
iPhone 16 $799 A18
iPhone 15 $699 A16
iPhone 16e $599 A18

If Apple followed their normal pattern than the iPhone 15 would drop by $100 next year, matching the current 16e price. In fact, I think the iPhone 15 will be discontinued, for one obvious reason: the A16 chip doesn't support Apple Intelligence. Indeed, notice that Apple's marketing pages place the iPhone 16e above the iPhone 15 even though it is less expensive and lacks features like MagSafe or dual-cameras:

Honestly, I'm kind of surprised Apple didn't just discontinue the iPhone 15 along with the iPhone 14; I expect that to happen this fall when the iPhone 16 will drop to $699, while the iPhone 16e will probably stick at $599.

The key factor undergirding this analysis is the recognition that Apple sees Apple Intelligence support as table stakes for its entire lineup going forward; the problem is that Apple just couldn't figure out a way to have a powerful enough phone that hit the traditional SE price point, so the company is resetting the lineup for the next couple of years, and eventually cheaper iPhones may be back.

Honestly, I think this is the more compelling explanation for the 16e's price; Apple wants to sell more devices to drive more services, and cheaper iPhones help with that. For now, however, Apple Intelligence across the lineup is a bigger priority.

Apple vs. U.K, Continued

From the Washington Post :

Apple disabled its most secure data storage offering for new customers in Britain on Friday rather than comply with a secret government order that would have allowed police and intelligence agencies to access the encrypted content. The order under the country’s Investigatory Powers Act, reported by The Washington Post two weeks ago, requires the California maker of iPhones and Mac computers to create a backdoor capability allowing authorities to snoop on iCloud storage anywhere in the world.

That would nullify the technology in Apple’s Advanced Data Protection service, which provides such strong encryption that the company itself is unable to retrieve users’ information. Apple rolled out that optional end-to-end encryption globally starting in late 2022…British customers who already have Advanced Data Protection will be warned later to disable it or lose access to iCloud.

This response by Apple is exactly what I predicted when the Washington Post first broke the story: Apple would rather not offer Advanced Data Protection than insert a back door. Note, however, that this does not fully address the U.K.'s demands: they wanted a backdoor for all users, not just U.K. ones; we will see if the country pushes on this point, which would leave Apple no choice but to either comply or leave the market.

I think I've explained this issue pretty comprehensively at this point, but just to be crystal clear, let me address these complaints by Epic CEO Tim Sweeney head-on:

I think my bonafides in terms of criticizing Apple — including its at-times self-serving privacy policies — are pretty well-established at this point, so I'm happy to take this head-on. The fact of the matter is that Apple has to follow the laws of the countries in which it operates; that's why, to take a pertinent example, iCloud keys for Chinese users are stored in China. In this case, the U.K. directive was specifically about Advanced Data Protection; Apple needed to either comply or withdraw the service (and, as noted above, that may not be enough).

Moreover, as I noted last week , this is simply a return to the status quo and the uneasy compromise that Apple had previously struck with law enforcement: iPhones are encrypted, but the default and easiest-path cloud backup service were not, and thus accessible with a warrant.

Therefore, to Sweeney's point, if this is opening the front door, well, the front door has been open for years, and even now is only closed if the user opts in to end-to-end iCloud encryption. In other words, there is no change for most U.K. users; saying Apple compromised the security of all of them is simply wrong, and suggesting that the company shouldn't follow the laws in the countries in which it operates is a very weird stance for someone who spends most of their time lobbying for laws that compel Apple to change its behavior.
