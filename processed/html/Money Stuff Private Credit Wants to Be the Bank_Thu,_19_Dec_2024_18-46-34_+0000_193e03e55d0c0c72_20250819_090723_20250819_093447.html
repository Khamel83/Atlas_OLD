Narrow banking

One of the big stories of modern finance is the rise of narrow banking in the form of private credit. “Narrow banking” is, in banking theory, roughly the idea that banks shouldn’t make loans. Instead:

  1. Banks should take money from depositors and keep it somewhere very safe. After all, the depositors might ask for their money back at any time, and it would be a crisis if the banks didn’t have it. So if you deposit money in a bank, the bank should just keep your cash in an envelope with your name on it. Or, more practically, the bank should keep the money in its account at the Federal Reserve, or perhaps in short-term government securities. The bank should keep the money somewhere with basically no credit or interest-rate risk.
  2. Meanwhile, loan funds should make loans. Some ambitious entrepreneur can raise a pool of money from investors, saying “I will use your money to make loans, and you’ll get the interest on the loans, minus my fees.” The investors in these loan funds would lock up their money for a long time, to match the duration of the loans: A fund making 30-year mortgage loans wouldn’t pay back its investors for 30 years. [1] And the investors would bear the credit risk: If the loans didn’t get paid back, the investors would lose money.

This is different from normal modern banking, in which banks take deposits and use them to make loans. Mostly normal banking works fine: Although depositors can ask for their money back at any time, they mostly don’t, so bank deposits are a pretty predictable and stable source of funding. And although borrowers sometimes default on their loans, the bank has a diversified portfolio of loans and does good credit analysis, so the loans mostly get paid back and there’s enough money for the depositors. And there are prudential supervision and capital and liquidity requirements to make extra sure that the money will be there. But every so often it doesn’t work out. Every so often, the depositors get nervous and ask for their money back at once, and it is a mess.

Normal banking is a bit of magic, a sleight of hand that transforms risky assets (loans) into risk-free ones (bank deposits), “ a superposition of fraud and genius that interposes itself between investors and entrepreneurs ” so the entrepreneurs can take risks with the investors’ money but the investors feel safe. But this magic is fragile , the equilibrium is unstable , and every so often it blows up .

Narrow banking is an idea that is proposed from time to time — often after a financial crisis — to solve this problem. Narrow banking gets rid of most of the magic: Your safe deposits are invested only in safe assets (Fed reserves, Treasury bills), and risky loans are made only by investors who knowingly bear the risks of those loans. This is a safer, less fragile system, one that is not so vulnerable to bank runs or financial crises.

But the downside is: no magic. [2] In particular, I have been reading about the idea of narrow banking for years, and I used to think “that’s all well and good but how realistic is this loan-fund idea?” The point of banking, it seemed to me, was that you can fund a lot of loans with bank deposits, surely more (and more cheaply) than you could fund by going out and raising loan funds. “Put money in the bank and you can get it back whenever” is a simpler and more traditional and more appealing pitch than “give me money for seven years to fund a portfolio of consumer loans, and you’ll get most of the risks and rewards of those loans.” Sure some people would want to invest in loan funds, but surely those funds couldn’t grow to anything like the scale of the modern banking system. Narrow banking, it seemed to me, would involve a big reduction in credit.

But I think the obvious modern answer is something like: No, Blackstone and Apollo and BlackRock really can raise loan funds that … aren’t there yet, but it is easier than it used to be to imagine them rivaling the banking system. Five years ago, “private credit” meant something like “making loans to fund leveraged buyouts without going through a syndication process.” Now it means something closer to “substituting for every possible form of bank lending.” Bloomberg’s Paula Seligson reports :

Private credit firms want more than corporate lending. The largest are laying the groundwork to finance everything from auto loans and residential mortgages to chip manufacturing and data centers in an effort to swell the size of the market by the trillions.

It’s part of a race to grab a bigger share of a universe of potential investments that Apollo Global Management Inc. has said could be as large as $40 trillion. The staggering estimate is tied to a boom in private investment-grade debt related to infrastructure and asset-backed finance. Those areas will be top priority for firms next year, according to 10 managers interviewed by Bloomberg News. …

Data provider Preqin currently pegs the size of private debt assets under management at around $1.6 trillion, a number focused on corporate debt strategies.

The total assets of US commercial banks are about $23.5 trillion , for context. So private credit right now is much smaller, but it has ambitions to be bigger. And it is getting into all sorts of consumer lending:

Asset-backed finance refers to packaging together pools of loans or assets that generate cash, and then lending against the recurring cash flow of that portfolio. The scope is broad: everything from auto loans or mortgages to music royalties or trade receivables. Small business loans and income streams from mobile phone towers or data centers can count, too. …

Sixth Street Partners expects to grow its ABF assets under management over the next five years to make up a significant portion of its investments, said Co-Chief Investment Officer Joshua Easterly. The firm is currently focused on consumer products like mortgages and car loans and looking to expand in commercial lending.

Residential housing is another frontier for private credit funds, especially loans to consumers with good credit. One area of focus for Carlyle Group Inc. is targeting pools of capital with exposure to people who have significant home equity, such as through home equity loans, solar loans and auto loans, as they are protected from inflation, said Mark Jenkins, its head of global credit. Blackstone is looking to expand further in products such as home efficiency loans and residential solar financing, according to Zawadzki.

And the banks, meanwhile, are letting it happen:

Private debt has increasing opportunities to take market share in these spaces as banks continue on a quest to become more asset-light as a result of stricter post-Global Financial Crisis regulations. A second Trump administration might soften some requirements but isn’t expected to change this long-term shift.

“The fundamental model of a bank is changing from lending to earn a return to lending to make fees and attract deposits, and I don’t think that paradigm shift is changing anytime soon,” said Loris Nazarian, an assistant portfolio manager at Oaktree Capital Management’s private assets business.

Banks are slowly getting out of the business of “lending to earn a return,” which will mean that loans have to come from somewhere else.

Who else? Well, from first principles, if some people want to borrow money now and pay it back later, they should borrow it from people who have money now and won’t need it until later. This isn’t quite true of bank depositors, or it is true only in some risky aggregate statistical sense: The money in your checking account is money that you don’t need right this minute , but it’s probably not money that you won’t need for seven years.

On the other hand, there are pretty obvious sources of money who really don’t need it for a long time. Life insurance companies take money from policyholders today and mostly pay it out many years later, when the policyholders die. Endowments hold a lot of money in perpetuity and spend only a fraction of it each year. And, most notably, normal people save money for retirement, and don’t plan to use that money for many years.

And so private credit managers manage a lot of money for insurance companies and endowments and pensions and other traditional long-term investors. But they are also very interested in managing your retirement money . Apollo, these days, increasingly positions itself as a retirement-services company . And :

KKR & Co. Co-Chief Executive Officer Scott Nuttall said the long-term nature of 401(k)s makes them a sensible vehicle for private-market investments.

“People are saving for retirement in this target-date format, ” Nuttall said Tuesday at a Goldman Sachs Group Inc. conference. “They’re thinking about their asset allocation decades out — not having private markets as part of that solution just isn’t really sensible.”

If you were building a financial system from scratch, and you had to decide “where should the money for 30-year mortgages come from,” and your choices were “people’s checking accounts” or “people’s retirement funds,” isn’t the retirement fund the obvious answer ?

Meanwhile, at the Wall Street Journal, Telis Demos writes :

It may be no surprise that people are eager to put off paying in full for their holiday shopping. But who would want to fund that? Increasingly, there is an answer: private credit.

Buy now, pay later provider Affirm last week announced a $4 billion commitment for lending from investment firm Sixth Street. ...

Via what is termed asset-based finance, private-credit managers are increasingly in the mainstream of the kinds of lending that banks or the securitization markets have long dominated. ...

Meanwhile, for buy now, pay later and consumer lenders, it shows how the challenge of high interest rates can be met. A key step is to secure longer-term commitments—a hallmark of private credit—as part of a diversified mix of incoming money.

“Who would want to fund” people who don’t want to pay in full for their holiday shopping? I mean, that’s a credit card! That’s the point of a credit card: You buy stuff and pay for it later! For a long time, that was a core business of banking. Now it is private credit.

We talk about these ideas from time to time. One argument that I have often made is that, if banks are no longer the best source of funds for loans — if “the fundamental model of a bank is changing from lending to earn a return to lending to make fees” — then the natural evolution is for the banks, which have the customer relationships and the loan officers, to make the loans, and for private credit funds to give them the money for those loans. And so we have talked about banks shifting the risks of their loans to investors, and about how regional banks are partnering with private credit firms to make loans, and how even giant banks like Barclays Plc and JPMorgan Chase & Co. want private-credit capital to fund their loans.

The idea here is that, since banks have historically been the big source of loans, they have a lot of the tools needed to make loans. They have branches and loan officers and customer relationships and people who can make credit decisions and other people who can send out monthly bills. And they used to use their deposits to make loans, but now they do less of that and banking is narrower. But since they still have the customer-service side of things, they make the loans, and private credit firms fund them.

But that is perhaps a temporary state of affairs on the road to narrow banking. If banks are not where the money is, why should they make the loans? If banks don’t hold the credit risk, why should they make the credit decisions? Banks have branches, yes, but who goes to a bank branch to borrow money? You go to an app. Why would a bank have the best app?

And so consumer credit might increasingly look like the Sixth Street/Affirm deal : A financial technology startup with a good app but no balance sheet does the customer-facing part of the loans, and a private credit firm with a big balance sheet puts up the money. And you don’t need a bank.

M&A repricing

A story that you sometimes hear these days is that venture capital and private equity firms have a lot of money locked up in private companies, but they are having trouble cashing out, because the public markets don’t want their companies. The market for initial public offerings, the story goes, is weak, so private-company investors have to hold onto companies for longer than they wanted to and resort to creative measures ( continuation funds , net asset value loans ) to return money to their limited partners.

This story would have some intuitive appeal if it was like “these funds bought all these companies at high prices in 2019, when the stock market was doing great, but now it’s 2024 and the stock market is down so they can’t sell them at attractive prices.” It is a bit of a weirder story in the real world, though, in that the US stock market is near record highs and is up more than 80% over the past five years. Why can’t you sell your private companies to the public market? The public market seems fine?

Anyway I thought of this puzzle when I read this Bloomberg story about companies abandoning bad acquisitions:

Companies that spent billions on poorly timed acquisitions in recent years are now offloading those assets at knockdown prices.

Alibaba Group Holding Ltd. announced Tuesday it’s going to sell Chinese department-store chain Intime to a local apparel group for $1 billion. The price is around 30% of the company’s valuation when Alibaba bought it during the heady days of 2017. The internet giant, which has largely abandoned its acquisitive ways amid government pressure, said it will book a $1.3 billion loss on the transaction.

The deal came a day after BlackBerry Ltd. said it would divest its Cylance endpoint security unit to software startup Arctic Wolf for $160 million plus a small amount of stock. That’s a far cry from the $1.4 billion BlackBerry paid when it agreed to buy the business in 2018. Under BlackBerry’s ownership, Cylance reported substantial losses and its revenue fell over 50%, according to Royal Bank of Canada analysts.

The moves show how companies that were major acquirers during the boom times may sober up and regret those purchases only a few years later.

Some of this probably is a shift in market conditions, but some of it is more “we made bad deals, oops.”

OpenAI

I have always thought of the OpenAI for-profit conversion problem as conceptually pretty simple:

  1. OpenAI is a big company that is worth a lot of money.
  2. Some of it is owned by shareholders (venture capitalists and employees, mostly). These shareholders’ shares are a bit odd — many of them have profit caps — but roughly speaking it seems reasonable to say “if you invested $100 million at a $20 billion valuation then you own 0.5% of the company” or whatever, possibly with some adjustments for particular details.
  3. A lot of it is owned by Microsoft Corp. Microsoft’s shares are even odder: They are not shares at all, but a series of claims on OpenAI’s profits, roughly speaking 75% of the first $17 billion of profits, 49% of the next tranche and 0% of profits above some cap. Converting this into percentage ownership is harder than in the case above, but not conceptually impossible. You could build some discounted cash flow or option-valuation model to say “Microsoft’s profit share converts to an expected 45% ownership,” or whatever (I don’t know the actual number). And presumably OpenAI’s advisers would have a model that gets some low number, and Microsoft’s advisers would have a model that gets some higher number, and they negotiate, and ultimately Microsoft and OpenAI could agree to cancel the weird profit interest and convert it into some mutually agreeable number of shares.
  4. And the rest of the company — the portion not owned by VCs, employees and Microsoft [3] — is owned by a nonprofit organization that controls OpenAI. The residual profits of OpenAI, after paying out Microsoft and everyone else, go to the nonprofit.
  5. Now OpenAI wants to organize itself as a more normal company, not controlled by the nonprofit: Instead of the shareholders and Microsoft all owning weird quasi-shares of a capped-profit subsidiary of a nonprofit, they’d just own regular shares of a regular company. (Well, a public benefit company.)
  6. But nonprofit law requires that, to do that, OpenAI has to give the nonprofit fair compensation for its stake: You can’t just convert a nonprofit to a for-profit company and keep all of its money. You have to, effectively, buy out the nonprofit’s current interest.
  7. But that’s fine: If the employees and VCs own 35% of OpenAI, and Microsoft’s stake is economically equivalent to 45% of OpenAI (made-up numbers, to be clear), then just by subtracting you know that the nonprofit’s stake is 20% of the company. And then you just give the nonprofit 20% of the new for-profit company’s shares, and let the nonprofit keep them or sell them or whatever. The nonprofit is fully compensated for its ownership of OpenAI, because it gets exactly the same economic ownership before and after the conversion. Its compensation for its ownership stake of OpenAI is its ownership stake of OpenAI. You can debate the details of valuation, but the basic idea is straightforward.

But here is a New York Times article about the conversion that frames it in a way I hadn’t thought of:

Under the watchful eyes of government regulators, the press and the public, [OpenAI Chief Executive Officer Sam] Altman and his colleagues are working to sever the nonprofit’s control while ensuring that the existing board is properly compensated for the changes, according to four people familiar with the negotiations who spoke on the condition of anonymity.

Mr. Altman and his colleagues need to answer a question: What is a fair price for ceding control over a technology that might change the world? Proper compensation to the nonprofit is still being debated, but it could easily be in the billions of dollars, one person said. …

“Any potential restructuring would ensure the nonprofit continues to exist and thrive, and receives full value for its current stake in the OpenAI for-profit with an enhanced ability to pursue its mission,” Bret Taylor, chairman of the nonprofit board, said in a statement to The Times.

Fine, right, “receives full value for its current stake,” seems simple. But:

If the nonprofit is removed from OpenAI’s chain of command, it could spin off into funding research on topics like ethics in artificial intelligence, one person said. But Mr. Altman and his colleagues have not yet assigned a dollar value to the nonprofit’s potential loss of control.

“Here, the asset in question is so unique and potentially so earth-shattering,” said Alexander L. Reid, a lawyer advising nonprofits at the law firm BakerHostetler. “How much is it worth to control the power to bring the genie out of the bottle?”

See, the loss to the nonprofit is not just “you own the economic equivalent of 20% of this company, so here’s 20% of the stock.” It’s “your whole purpose as a nonprofit is to make sure that our AI does not destroy humanity, by overseeing how we build it; but now we can build it however we want.” The nonprofit’s upside from controlling OpenAI is uncertain — it might flop, it might become a trillion-dollar company — but can be reasonably estimated using normal valuation principles, and thus reduced to a dollar amount or a number of shares. The nonprofit’s downside from not controlling OpenAI — “without our supervision, Sam Altman might destroy humanity” — is not really susceptible to conventional valuation techniques. “It may be difficult to know what role money will play in a post-[artificial general intelligence] world,” OpenAI used to tell its investors , and I suppose that’s a plausible objection to any compensation the nonprofit could be offered. “You are offering us money, but money is not what we care about.”

The Bitcoin Ponzi model

I feel like the basic story of Bitcoin over the last five years or so has been “more people — normal people, exchange-traded funds, corporate treasuries, institutional investors, etc. — are going to become comfortable with Bitcoin, so they will buy it, so its price will go up.” That story has played out, more types of investors have come around on Bitcoin, and its price has gone up.

How does that story end? What happens when everyone is comfortable with Bitcoin and owns some? The other day, I mentioned a Bloomberg article with the headline “ Crypto Surge Lures Final Holdouts ,” and while I’m not sure I would take “final” literally, it’s plausible that we’re more than halfway from Bitcoin’s foundation to maximal adoption and respectability. What happens if we get there? Possible answers include:

  1. Widespread adoption of Bitcoin will make it increasingly valuable, people who allocate 1% or 2% of their portfolio to Bitcoin will say “you know what really this should be 10%,” they’ll keep buying Bitcoin and it will keep going up. Bitcoin will be something like the stock market , an index of global economic growth that keeps going up as the economy grows.
  2. Widespread adoption of Bitcoin will make it stable : People who buy Bitcoin thinking “this is a stable store of value in an uncertain world” will be correct, and if they ever need to sell some Bitcoin to pay expenses, they’ll find a ready buyer who wants to save money in the form of Bitcoin. And the price of Bitcoin will stay stable at, you know, pick a number, $100,000 or more, say. Bitcoin will be something like cash , or gold , a reliable store of value and a small portion of most people’s financial assets.
  3. Widespread adoption of Bitcoin will cause a collapse: The only reason to buy Bitcoin now at $100,000 is because you think that, in a month, some institutional-investor holdout will capitulate and decide to buy Bitcoin, and you’ll be able to sell it to them at $110,000. But once there’s no one left to capitulate, there’ll be no demand for Bitcoin, and its price will collapse.

I have to say, in a heavily caveated way — I know nothing, this is not investment advice, I’ll probably be wrong, etc. — that I would bet on Answer 2? It seems to me that Bitcoin has more or less made the transition from “hey here’s a weird thing” to “this is a store of value that has a place in institutional portfolios,” and that feels like a plausible long-term equilibrium. That makes me nervous — it’s an equilibrium based solely on social acceptance, and that could change — but it’s my best guess.

Here, however, is J.P. Konig with an extremely fun articulation of Answer 3 :

If you want to buy some bitcoins, go right ahead. We can even help by regulating the trading venues to make it safe. But don't force others to play.

Alas, that seems to be where we are headed. There is a growing effort to arm-twist the rest of society into joining in by having governments acquire bitcoins, in the U.S.'s case a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve. The U.S. government has never entered the World Series of Poker. Nor has it gone to Vegas to bet billions to tax payer funds on roulette or built a strategic Powerball ticket reserve, but it appears to be genuinely entertaining the idea of rolling the dice on Bitcoin.

Bitcoin is an incredibly infectious early-bird game, one that after sixteen years continues to find a constant stream of new recruits. How contagious? I originally estimated in a 2022 post, Three potential paths for the price of bitcoin, that adoption wouldn't rise above 10%-15% of the global population, but I may have been underestimating its transmissibility. My worry is that calls for government support will only accelerate as more voters, government officials, and bureaucrats catch the orange coin mind virus and act on it. It begins with a small strategic reserve of a few billion dollars. It ends with the Department of Bitcoin Price Appreciation being allocated 50% of yearly tax revenues to make the number go up, to the detriment of infrastructure like roads, hospitals, and law enforcement. At that point we've entered a dystopia in which society rapidly deteriorates because we've all become obsessed on a bet.

A portion of the bull case for Bitcoin is something like “once every big bank and asset manager and also the US Treasury has a chunk of their assets in Bitcoin, surely they won’t let the price of Bitcoin go down too much,” but I guess there is something a little dystopian about that. [4]
