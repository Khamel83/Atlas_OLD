Good morning,

This week’s Stratechery Interview is with Hugo Barra . Barra is the CEO of Detect , a startup in the healthcare diagnostics space; we do discuss Detect at the end of this Interview, but this is not really a traditional founder interview.

Barra has a long and fascinating career in tech: his first startup was acquired by Nuance, the voice recognition company that powered the initial version of Siri, and has since been acquired by Microsoft; from there he went to Google, where he became the Director of Product Management for Android. In 2013 Barra left to become Vice President of Global Operations for Xiaomi, where I interviewed | him previously. In 2017 Barra came back to the U.S. and joined Meta as the Vice President of Virtual Reality, before leaving in 2021 to work at Detect.

In the first half of this interview we work our way through each of those stops, and, in the Android section, discuss the Apple antitrust case. The second half was prompted by an excellent post Barra wrote on his personal blog about the Vision Pro : we get into the Vision Pro, what Apple got right and got wrong, how the competition with Meta will shake out, and how virtual reality can actually become something people want to use on a regular basis.

Nuance

Hugo Barra, welcome to Stratechery.

Hugo Barra: Thank you, Ben. Thanks for having me.

I was inspired to reach out to you for a Stratechery Interview because of a really fantastic blog post you wrote about the Apple Vision Pro , and I thought it’d be interesting to do a two-month on retrospective of the device generally. But before we get to that, you’ve had a very interesting and long career in tech and I’d love to learn more about you. Where are you from originally? How did you get started in tech? What was the path from childhood on?

HB: I was born and raised in Brazil and was gifted the book Being Digital by Nicholas Negroponte when I was starting to think about college. I became pretty obsessed with the idea of going to school at MIT. My mother is an architect, my father is an engineer, the Media Lab at MIT is part of the architecture department in a very funky way or connected to it I should say, and it was just the right thing. So I got into tech through the history of the Media Lab and then finally making my way to MIT in 1996. I spent my entire time, both in college and after college, for twenty years working in mobile, back in the Palm Pilot days through the Pocket PC era and beyond, and crossed paths with you about halfway through I think that mobile career.

So tell me about Lobby7, this was a voice oriented startup you started after college, right? I know it was acquired by Nuance Communication, which was well-known for their Dragon NaturallySpeaking dictation technology. I think Apple licensed that for Siri, then Microsoft acquired Nuance outright, but it feels like one of those technologies in the long run where, of course, the big companies own it, but what drew you to voice as a medium even way back in the early days?

HB: I was pretty heavily influenced by my thesis advisor at MIT, Dr. Pattie Maes from the Media Lab. Lobby7, which by the way was a temporary name we gave the company. It’s the lobby to building seven, which is the main entrance to MIT, and it was totally one of those temporary names that just stayed.

It’s a good name, I like it.

HB: We had a choice between mobile gaming and mobile speech recognition. I still don’t quite recall why the heck we picked mobile speech recognition instead of a very profitable mobile gaming trajectory, but we did, and the company didn’t last very long. We ran out of money after about three years, got acquired by Nuance, but were given a budget in several years of time to develop what we thought was the next or a big part of the next major UI paradigm as mobile came into focus, which was speech recognition. So what we built at Nuance eventually became, I was long gone by the time it launched, but eventually became the cloud backend to Apple’s first version of Siri, their voice assistant. But I was long gone, I joined Google in 2008.

We’ll get to Google, but I think this is going to be a pattern we’re going to throughout your career, where you’ve latched onto paradigms that make sense to be the thing but maybe a few years early. And I’m just curious, what has drawn you to that as far as you’re starting with voice? We’ll get to the VR stuff in a little bit, but it does seem to be a bit of a pattern.

HB: Yeah, it’s interesting, and I probably don’t think about it nearly as much as I should, because I could probably learn a few lessons from my own story there. Mobile to me was an obvious thing, it was obvious that the world was going to go through this computing paradigm shift. It was a matter of when, not a matter of if, and I had access to a lot of people who were early thinkers in the space, a lot of interesting research that came out of MIT and I just felt like the UI part of the problem was one that no one had really started to attack yet. When we’re looking for an idea to focus an entire company in your career for many years, you look for those kinds of problems. So I don’t think we were deeply thoughtful about why speech mattered, it just felt like some of the early research was pointing in an interesting direction and you definitely needed to rethink the way speech was done.

We ended up contributing to an IEEE standard for distributed speech recognition very early on in the life of the company but it was really accidental, I think, that we ended up where we did in choosing speech. And you’re right, it took quite a bit longer than I think any of us expected, I think it wouldn’t be unfair to say that speech is still fighting its way into the mobile computing paradigm.

Is it one of those things where — you mentioned that Nuance was the cloud backend for Siri — but eventually it just has to be local and almost the biggest constraint was having local devices that were power enough and capable enough to have the immediacy and the interaction and experience that’s going to be key for taking off?

HB: Early on, you knew that you couldn’t run it locally because you needed an extremely powerful compute-hungry system to handle the large dictionaries and grammars that we needed for those types of tasks like searching a large database or speaking using a somewhat natural UI. So we knew that we couldn’t put those things on the device’s client side at all, and we had very, very little bandwidth available. We’re talking back in the CDPD days, pre-GPRS where you had 12 kilobits per second of bandwidth available to you with relatively high latency.

So the first challenge that we tackled was, “How can we do some amount of the operations of speech recognition client side that was not compute-intensive, but that would strip down the signal enough that you could stream it in relatively real-time fashion to the server and then recognized?”. The first DSR standard that we helped create at IEE had us down to about five or six kilobits per second without any loss in the quality of the signal. We were doing feature extraction, essentially creating vectors out of the signal client site and then shipping those to a server that knew how to put it back together and a lot of how speech is done is still today, but the blocks have moved back to the client side, thankfully, so we can do things faster.

It’s amazing how much of tech innovation and development is really about fancier and fancier compression capabilities.

HB: Completely, from the very beginning.

Android

So from there, from Nuance, you go to Google. I think you were working on some voice stuff there early, then you switch over to Android. I think this is where people in our audience, who know who you are, probably comes from that era. You were very much the face of Android. It was a very fun era, Google and Apple going back and forth, some very spicy Google I/Os that you were associated with. As you look back a decade on, what stands out to you about that era?

HB: It was probably the most fascinating thing that I’ve ever been part of, because the arrival of iOS and the iPhone created existential risk for an entire ecosystem of OEMs, Samsung and HTC, and at the time, we still had LG and Sony and so many other names in the space, and Android was the right antidote at the right time with the backing and the desire to re-arm that ecosystem with the operating system that it needed to stand up and compete against the giant that clearly was coming our way.

It was equal parts developer excitement, user excitement. We had such a huge base of hardcore tech fans who loved Android and wanted to be part of it and the other third of it was obviously the OEM ecosystem and just how excited they were to be the next phone selected to be the first vessel for the next version of Android and so on. So it was really 360-degree excitement, and we just had to keep our heads down and make sure that we were moving as fast as possible.

Yeah, that’s definitely the one example — you weren’t ahead of the wave, you were directly on top of the wave and you’re going to go where it might take you.

HB: Yeah, and it was just really fun to have such a formidable competitor, whether they were slightly ahead or slight behind us is a technicality, and also depends on which part of the world you’re talking about, but it was just phenomenal to live in an era of such intense competition. iOS borrowed just as much from Android as Android borrowed from iOS, and I think just seeing all of that play out and the impact that it had in the world, giving so many billions of people access to compute and information that they couldn’t possibly have, couldn’t afford a computer at home and so on. So it was a truly special magical moment.

What was your reaction when you first used an iPhone? Because you had been interested in mobile, you were focused on UI paradigms, and suddenly there was this device, no keyboard, all touch, the multi-touch thing. Was it just like, “Oh yeah, this is it!”? That’s the reaction?

HB: Experiencing multi-touch for the first time with the capacity of display of course was magic. I felt it again when I experienced gaze and pinch with the Vision Pro.

I was foreshadowing a future question, you made it explicit, yes.

HB: We’ll come back to that, but it was a magical moment. It’s truly one of those things that defines a whole era, a whole path in the history of compute, and it was absolutely magical. It’s one of those things where you see it and you’re like, “Of course, this is the way. It’s not a matter of who invented it or why or what patents they have, this is the way. Let’s just all get on board and work on the next innovation that builds on top of it.”

I’ve been calling back to that era this week in the context of the DOJ filing a case against Apple , and the point I’ve been making is back then, it was pretty conventional wisdom that, “Oh, it’s great Apple invented multi-touch, it’s also great they invented the GUI, but this is going to go the way of Mac versus Windows and Apple is going to be so focused on being integrated that they’re going to miss the boat, Android is this broader operating system that works across OEMs, the modular approach is going to win”.

It’s meaningful to me personally because I got my start at Stratechery arguing the opposite that no, this is a fundamental misunderstanding of this market. It’s not like the PC market, Apple is going to be fine, it’s going to go from strength to strength and it’s funny, because I tell that story to young people today, and it just doesn’t compute. They can’t fathom a world in which Apple is not the obvious winner.

I’m curious, when you look back to that era, and you see this case that in many respects is saying, “No, it’s unfair that you’re integrated”, maybe I’m editorializing too much of this question, but it just seems like you can’t have it be the wrong strategy a decade ago and now it’s anti-competitive today. I don’t know. What’s your perspective on that?

HB: I’m with you, and I think that when you look at the DOJ’s case against Apple in the US where the company has, I don’t know, anywhere from 55% to 75% market share depending on who you want to quote or the period of time, and you compare it to what’s happening in the rest of the world where Apple barely has 20% market share.

Like 20, yeah.

HB: Right? And I think up until 2023, they were never number one, and they just slightly passed Samsung, essentially tied for the number one spot in 2023. I think that if you squint a little bit, Apple is not in a dramatically different place when you think about this in global terms.

I do think that they overplayed their hand in the App Store. You and I may disagree that I think that they also overplayed their hand with iMessage, but for the most part, all the other stuff, as sad as it sometimes makes me to hear because I feel like Android is not being given a fair chance in places where it could, a lot of the other stuff is basically Apple out-executing the others. And the rest of the ecosystem, the Android ecosystem to be clear, failing to coordinate in ways that I think could have led to much more high-quality products that are really capable of competing. We can talk about Samsung as without a doubt being the focal point here.

The cost of modularization is you have all these coordination costs, but the coordination costs can also apply to the user experience. If you don’t have the same entity controlling it top to bottom, you’re going to have rough edges. And to your point, it’s a lot easier to pull together a bunch of feuding managers or teams in a company than it is to pull together lots of feuding competitive independent companies all over the world. Arguably, that Android has overcome that to be as good as it is, you buy a Samsung phone today, it’s pretty good, is a testament to the fact that they’ve made it work.

HB: Yeah, Android in the early days had a very, very tough job of building a platform that could enable all different types of smartphones to be good enough for so many people, for apps to work across all of them. It’s crazy that Android at one point was running anywhere from what you might call VGA, sub-VGA resolution phones all the way to super high resolution devices, and the same apps were running and they were running relatively smoothly. Just the amount of work to keep all of that working well was pretty insane, and that’s where the energy went. I think where the Android ecosystem didn’t pivot when it should have was when it was time to compete for well-integrated experiences, super high-quality smooth things that made sense, and it was the lack of collaboration I think between Google and Samsung that landed us here.

They needed to be best friends, and something happened there and it just didn’t happen for too long.

HB: I am very biased in how I see this, I think, because I’m much closer to one side of that partnership than I am to the other. But I do largely attribute it to Samsung having too much of an Apple-wannabe trauma and not accepting the fact that they’re not a software developer, they’re not a software player, they’re not innovators of the same ilk as Apple or even as Google, so I think had they decided to collaborate, cooperate with Google earlier — and to be fair, they still aren’t — but had they decided to do that, I don’t know, I think as recently as six or seven years ago, we could be in a different place today. I don’t think we would be seeing an Apple with 20% global market share.

I think the other big miss here is Nokia. They had it all, except for the software, and Stephen Elop going there and going “No, we’re going to be differentiated, we have Windows”. Had they fully embraced Android with their capabilities in phone manufacturing, with their distribution channels, they could actually still be as dominant as they were. It was one of the great screw-ups, it was a frustrating screw-up because you could see the screw-up happening as it occurred.

HB: I think that one would have been so much harder to turn around, because just the mindset at headquarters, at Nokia headquarters in Finland of, “We can’t make a mistake, nothing bad will happen to us, we are on top of our game, we always will be”. They didn’t panic for a second. I think Samsung panics from time to time, Nokia didn’t panic for a second.

No, but that’s the thing. The Samsung panic helped them, I wrote an article ages ago about how Samsung’s panic saved them.

HB: Precisely. Nokia should have panicked.

That’s right.

HB: And they would have helped themselves by panicking.

That’s right, because everyone at the time was dumping on Samsung because they were so blatantly ripping off Apple in just really embarrassing ways.

HB: They do.

The shape of their phone chargers was copying Apple. I wrote an article saying, “Look, they’re the only pre-mobile OEM that is actually thriving in this era and there’s something to be said for the shamelessness and the sheer panic with which they responded to Apple because it means they’re in a much better spot than Nokia being the obvious counterpoint.”

HB: Totally. And let’s not forget about BlackBerry, who obviously was a much smaller company from a unit shipment’s perspective than Nokia, but obviously they played a hugely important role in the US, in particular in other developed markets and they too failed to pivot at a critical time when they should have.

There’s a funny story, which I don’t think I’ve ever talked about publicly, but I think it was around 2012 or 2013 after I knew that BlackBerry had officially decided to not get on board with Android despite the many attempts that we’d made, also with Nokia. A small group of us, a friend of mine, Robin Chan , a well-known investor here in Silicon Valley, myself and a couple of others, we actually made a funded attempt to convince the BlackBerry board to allow for a takeover by a new management team with a very clear vision of going all in on Android, maintaining the BES/BIS business model that they had, but migrating the whole stack as quickly as possible over to Android. We would allow BlackBerry users to still have phones with hard keys if they wanted, with real physical keyboards, if they wanted, even though we knew that a multi-touch keyboard was the future. And the board was so hard-headed, they couldn’t possibly understand where we were coming from, that takeover attempt completely failed.

That’s the actual canonical story of the integrated company that is ahead because of being integrated and then misses the boat when the structure of the market changed.

Real quick aside, because I’m curious, how did Apple overplay their hand with iMessage, and what should they have done differently?

HB: I think that they should have seen regulators coming after them and attempting to make a technical case that perhaps was not exactly about iMessage not being open or not following a standard, or not allowing interoperability. They were going to come after them in some way, shape, or form with some technical case that they could pursue given that sort of the iMessage thing, as you explained in a recent Stratechery essay, just like that probably doesn’t hold water legally.

But it moves people politically.

HB: It moves people politically. It’s obviously a very emotionally charged issue because of what happens in high schools in the US. By the way, that’s not a thing in any other country, let’s be clear about that, people just use WhatsApp and they just get on with their lives, but in the US it’s a thing. It’s very charged emotionally, tons of stakeholders involved and so on, and I think they could have seen this coming and just gone to RCS a year earlier than they started to go. We’ll see, obviously what happens, that’s still playing out. But I think had they said, “Fine, we will support RCS. We’re going to find a way to allow for interoperability between Android and iOS for messaging, for rich messaging, and let’s just get on with our lives.” I really think that a change in strategy earlier there could have saved them quite a bit of headache. And by the way, it would’ve been good for their brand too.

I think that’s the story of this in general, is just a myopic focus on details and missing the larger picture, which is, you do have an exposed hide, and that exposed hide is your integration, and you can’t sacrifice that, you have to just be constantly building a moat around that. All these little differentiations on the edge, even if legally okay, are exposing the part of your business that is actually really unique and also easier to make an antitrust case about.

HB: I hope WhatsApp wins.

I’m a big WhatsApp fan.

Xiaomi

We first met when you were at Xiaomi, I wrote about Xiaomi a bit back in the day . I did kind of pull back in part because I wasn’t always entirely sure what the company was doing, it seemed to be this broader ecosystem play where we’re going to get phones into people’s hands at a relatively low price, and then we’re going to have this broader Xiaomi ecosystem, but now they seem to be just mostly a phone company. Looking back, I understand obviously you haven’t been there for a long time, but what’s your recollection of your time there? What did you learn? What would you change? What’s the high level overview of Hugo at Xiaomi?

HB: The strategy was always to focus on building super high quality smartphones for lower prices. We would build high-end smartphones and sell those for mid-end prices, build mid-end phones and sell those for low-end prices, and never build low-end phones, and the strategy was to fill that gap between the low margin of the hardware business and where we wanted to be as a company with software margins, advertising dollars and all sorts of other things, including subscription services and things that the company did get to launch and did so quite successfully in China, but never really came around to launching the rest of the world.

We realized relatively early on, before the 2018 IPO, that doing business globally, if we wanted to scale fast and quickly, was going to require playing a bit more by the rules of channel distribution and retail and so on, so we migrated the business model to being a bit higher margin, still preserving the essence of the company, which was quality products that are sold for a great value, but the company was making a bit more money in a lot of the models working with distributors and so on and so forth, and a little bit less focused in rolling out a software strategy, a software revenue strategy outside of mainland China.

There was also an element of the strategy which you will recall, which was around smart devices, IoT products, all of these ecosystems of consumer electronics from home appliances, to mobile accessories, to toys and other things that actually got us pretty far in a number of markets. We were selling air purifiers in India, and we couldn’t make enough of them for quite a while. But the company did narrow its focus on the smartphone business because that was where Xiaomi was so successful, and Xiaomi is the number three smartphone brand in the world today. They lost that spot for a few years, but they’re back up there.

But the future of the company, in addition to being a smartphone player, is EVs. Xiaomi just recently announced its first EV , I think it’s actually launching in the next couple of days, in late March of 2024. It’s a spectacular execution of an EV. I haven’t been in one, but I know a lot about them. Funny enough, I think there’s a lot about it that I think we would’ve seen in an Apple Car if that project were to ever launch, the perspective of a smartphone designer who cares equally about the hardware and the software when applied to a car. We haven’t seen that in any car that has ever been made, so I have some high hopes there, but obviously time will tell.

Oculus

What a tour, to go from Google and then to be with Xiaomi, emerging it, and then you come full circle, you come back to Facebook, and there you’re working on the Oculus team. What was motivating that shift? Was it a sense that the mobile thing, “It’s kind of set, now it’s on to the next thing,” or was desire just to come back to the US that worked out? What brought you to VR and made that attractive to you in the first place?

HB: My transition from living in Beijing, China, and working for Xiaomi, to being back in Silicon Valley working for Meta, then-Facebook, was relatively equal parts personal, just wanting a little bit of my life back, and opportunity and excitement. I think that magic of experiencing multi-touch for the first time back in 2007 is very much what I felt when I experienced Oculus Rift with controllers, which I got to see behind closed doors before the world had seen it, except it was like all of your sensors are recruited into that experience, not just your hand and your eyes. You’re immersed in what’s obviously something of great promise.

The question obviously was always going to be, “What is this going to be useful for?” at first. But to me, experiencing virtual reality with that degree of fidelity and immersiveness with Oculus Rift in probably around 2015 was seeing the future. It was like, “Of course, we are going to head into this spatial computing paradigm, whatever we’d like to call it, at some point. Is it a matter of if or a matter of when? It’s definitely a matter of when.” I think it still is a matter of when. So I was incredibly seduced by the potential of that new medium and said to myself, “I’ve just been given a chance to run this team, of course, I’m going to take that.”

Yep. That was 2017, that was still seven years ago, and one concern I’ve always had about Meta in this space is I think there’s an aspect — one way to know if something is too early is the number of things you need to invent . I think Mark Zuckerberg has said something along these lines that, “Oh, we have to invent eight core technologies”, or five, whatever it is, it’s some number that’s higher than one. The concern is this kind of feels like smartphones in the 1990s, which is, “Oh, there’s all these things, this is clearly the future.” And yes, it was clearly the future, but there’s a lot of companies that burned out along the way because there was just too much that had to be invented to get there.

I think the key thing about the iPhone is the iPhone was mostly off-the-shelf technology, but it added on the multi-touch, and it added on this new paradigm and obviously the technology that then accelerated the underlying technology. So you even could think about VR because of the chip progression and miniaturization and all these sorts of things. Is that a fair critique today? Was it a fair critique in 2017? Or was Meta right on time, and this is just what needed to be done for this to happen?

HB: I think Meta was relatively on time, because to me the thing that made it possible for you to really see this future as a mass market consumer future was when we were able to demonstrate that and well, this was not quite the case when I joined in 2017, but we were very close when I did join to demonstrating that magic of Oculus Rift, which was a PC virtual reality headset, this headset connected to a computer with external positioning stations that triangulated the position of the headset. It was a whole set up to demonstrate that you could take all of that and squeeze it into a standalone headset that still gave you hand presence and six degree of freedom positioning in the space around you was the thing. To me, that was the thing, and that was Oculus Quest and we didn’t quite get there in one generation.

Yeah, and I think Oculus Quest is underrated in retrospect. It was a big deal because it was all-in-one, and it shipped, it got out the door, and that’s a meaningful change in trajectory for VR.

HB: And it had one job to do, which was, it had to make you feel like you were in another physical reality and you had to feel comfortable doing so, your hands felt like they were your hands. If you moved around in that virtual space, you wouldn’t for even a split second feel strange or feel like the interaction wasn’t quite physically accurate, and that bar it passed.

I think this bit about presence, I completely agree with you, I think it’s the single most important thing to grok, and it changes your views about VR in the long run. My favorite example, it’s the most boring cliché example, but I talk about Horizon Workrooms where having meetings in VR, and it sounds like, “Wow, that’s the lamest possible use case I could imagine,” but it’s actually amazing because your memories, your recollection is about being in this room with other people, even though you’re all over the world. How does this vision get sold? I sound like a crazy person talking about this to normal people because it’s hard to imagine without experiencing it. It remains a fundamental obstacle.

HB: It does remain a fundamental obstacle, and I like to talk about two major frontiers that we are yet to crack. So we’ve cracked entertainment, gaming and VR is fantastic if you’re into that sort of thing. I think Apple Immersive and the value of video in immersive video in VR, it’s another thing that is also going to be cracked, and it’s phenomenal, and it’s just a matter of execution from this point out.

But the things where there’s still a whole bunch of net new innovation and inventions that the world has to see are the frontier of productivity, working with others in virtual reality, not just being with others, but actually doing work very productively in this new medium, and feeling like you’re better off there than not.

Then the other frontier, which is I think a lot more fundamental, is what I’ve always called teleportation. It’s this idea of being able to go to bring somebody to your home and be able to hang out like if you were actually at your house, or go to their house, or going somewhere else with them, and really feeling like when you look at them in the eye, there’s a human element to it. If you’ve spent time with them in real life, you will feel like it’s them and you can’t quite put your finger on it. Of course, we’re well into uncanny valley here, but you feel like you’re there with them.

When we crack that frontier, to me, it’s going to be like having a fixed-line phone all over again. It’s like, “Yeah, in your living room you have that thing that you put on, and then yeah, you can go visit grandma, or grandma can come visit you, but you have to put on the thing,” so it’s like having a fixed-line phone again, that it could even be a single-purpose device for a lot of people. So those are the two frontiers that I think we will need to crack in the next ten years to really turn this spatial computing thing into a paradigm that everybody believes is the future.

The one part that does very much work, and I think was the most compelling part of that Workrooms demo, and you get an aspect of this in the Vision Pro, is the audio part, and it makes sense that audio is easier to get right. All of our listeners, I’m sure, have experienced spatial audio with AirPods or whatever it might be, but the spatial audio in the context of wearing a headset, that’s the part that’s really compelling, where the person on your right, you hear them to your right and the person on your left, you hear them to your left and it’s louder or quieter based on where they are, that’s the visual part for me that really lands that sense of presence.

HB: I have this image of people at Apple chuckling after they launched AirPods spatial audio, this was well before Vision Pro, and trying to explain that there was a reason for spatial audio to exist and people are sort of pretending like they get it. Of course the reason why they’re experimenting with this is the fact that Vision Pro is coming and it’ll finally make sense, but they had to make this technology sort of bulletproof and well-developed. But it’s crazy what Apple can get away with years before the justification exists with products that come before that.

Vision Pro

So Vision Pro, I’m going to link to this article , I think it’s the best post about the Vision Pro, I think it’s even-handed, goes through pluses and minuses, trade-offs. Yes, it’s from a former competitor, a competitor in every respect, but I think it’s well worth the read.

HB: Thank you, Ben.

Let’s assume nobody has read it for this interview right now. So what’s your high-level takeaway of the Vision Pro as someone who’s been so invested in this space for so long?

HB: The highest level, I think, consequence to the world of Vision Pro launching is simply the fact that Apple cares, because when Apple cares about something to the point of putting a product in the world with their perspective, it means everybody cares. When Apple cares, everybody cares, and whether anyone who’s working in VR for the past decade-plus cares to admit or not, it is the North Star that the world needed to take this thing seriously, to take virtual reality seriously. To me that is a reason for everyone who cares even the slightest bit about virtual reality to celebrate is this Apple VR debut is amazing for everyone involved. It creates curiosity, desire, it creates this wave of consumer interest that I think is good for everybody.

It, of course, seeds the world with some just ridiculously amazing ideas. There’s very few of them, I think the only one that I say is a P0 insight is Gaze + Pinch, which is the multi-touch equivalent of virtual reality. HB: I think it absolutely can play out that way, and I love the thought behind some of the work that Microsoft is doing with Teams and with Microsoft Mesh , which is precisely this idea of you’re in a meeting with a bunch of people over video, now you’re going to work on a thing, and it just makes more sense for you to do that in an immersive environment because you can have more surfaces to collaborate, or maybe you’re actually working on a 3D model, or there are 3D authoring tools that you can benefit. So you’re sitting at your desk in a video call, just like you and I are right now, “Oh, let’s jump in.” You take your headset, put it on, and jump in, continuing that interaction that you were having with people in that meeting, but now you’re going into an immersive environment. That jumping in and out of VR on an as-needed basis for hyper-collaboration value is a huge deal, I think it’s the work version of teleportation, in a way.

I don’t know if you’ve ever used a tool called Gather Town , which is this cartoonish tool for people to feel like they’re working in the same physical space, even when they’re remote, and it’s more like a map where you can move around and go into a room or go to your office and so on. There’s a beauty to that tool in that I think people really could be feeling like they are in the same physical space with others. They don’t have to spend all day in that physical space, but what it will take is seamless integration between the tools that we use for 2D collaboration today, getting on a Zoom call, getting on a Teams call, and actually jumping in to work on a thing together and not feeling like that transition is super jarring. Otherwise, people won’t want to do it.

It’s going to take awhile to nail that, and I frankly trust Apple to nail that before anybody else, but when we do get there, I think enterprise could become a huge wedge into the consumer VR market.

Very good. Well, we should touch base again as this continues to develop. I think it’s one of those things where, if you look out 100 years, yeah, of course VR is a thing, but it’s the question with all tech, how long is it actually going to take? And in some respects, it feels very near, and then sometimes you get the details and it feels so far away. So I guess we’ll have to wait and see how it plays out.

HB: I hope the industry gives you a lot more reasons to talk and write about this in the next few years, and I think it will.

Sounds good. Hugo Barra, it’s great to have you on Stratechery and look forward to talking again soon.

HB: Thank you, Ben. It’s great to be here. Appreciate it.
