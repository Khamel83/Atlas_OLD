D.C. State of Mind
On Thursday, I made an impromptu trip down to Washington, D.C., to tend to some business, see a few people, and catch the flavor of the capital amid this administrative transition. Owing to some last-minute flight delays, I ended up opting for the Acela—always the smarter choice, honestly—and subsequently descended through the metaphorical journey of this larger presidential campaign.

The train started in New York, the former stomping grounds of the next president, through Philadelphia, where Kamala Harris concluded her campaign, and then Wilmington, its erstwhile base of operation, down to Union Station, a quick walk from the White House. Barreling past the fall foliage, I spent much of the ride contemplating the impact that the forthcoming Trump administration will have on our industry.

Trump’s original ascent, back in 2016, caught much of the media by surprise, and some of the sector’s largest companies spent the subsequent four years engaged in daily journalistic combat with the White House. This so-called #Resistance era, which was defined by Trump’s many scandals (and dual impeachments) in addition to the Times and Post ’s rocketing subscriber numbers and CNN’s record revenues, was a boon for the media business and democratic activism, alike.

Now, however, the era seems like a relic. The Trump phenomenon is no longer novel or even a phenomenon—the guy has effectively placed himself at the nucleus of the Republican establishment. Meanwhile, the media industry has balkanized considerably in the intervening years. A decade ago, Trump’s rise represented a broad shock that was processed through what remained of the monoculture. These days, media companies are more efficient and specialized and personality-driven and, well, smaller .

And many of them, like the Post and CNN, have been unequivocally humbled. Paramount Global, the parentco of CBS, is in the process of a sale to the Ellison family and RedBird Capital. Last week, Comcast president Mike Cavanagh intimated that he’d be open to spinning off his company’s cable networks, including MSNBC and CNBC. Six years after buying Time for $190 million, Marc Benioff is unloading it at a loss.

Naturally, my partners were examining the same question with far more expertise and aplomb. Dylan Byers takes on this consequential question in his characteristically brilliant piece The #Resistance Is Futile . Trump, after all, came along when the media industry was beginning to lose its nerve. Now, he’s reappeared at an even more vexing, more critical moment—all of which leads us to believe that the future will not replicate the past.

Matt Belloni , meanwhile, offers some typically peerless analysis on how the entertainment industry is bracing for the Trump revival. In Why Hollywood Executives Are So Giddy About Trump 2.0 , Matt recognizes that the transactional president may become the surprising accelerant for a legacy business in need of consolidation. Meanwhile, the great John Ourand captures the methodical way in which the folks in Bristol prepared for this moment. Republicans Buy ESPN Subscriptions, Too is a thought-provoking work sure to make the rounds in executive suites this week.

But if you’re looking to curl up by the fire this weekend with a decidedly apolitical piece, I’d turn your attention to Lauren Sherman ’s most recent masterstroke, Vuitton’s Retro Trick & Gucci’s New Story , which examines the challenges plaguing two of the world’s largest fashion brands. It’s delicious and insider-y and full of insights on how brands are marketing themselves in the modern global economy, whether or not Trump is ultimately able to impose his preferred tariff policies. Indeed, it is one of the great stories of our time, amid these changing times, and precisely what you should always expect from Puck.
