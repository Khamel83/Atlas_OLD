Sotheby’s must have been pleased by the crowds that turned up on Sunday for the auction preview of artworks belonging to the late Palm Beach grande dame Sydell Miller, expected to make $200 million. Yes, there was a big eye-catching Monet to chase, not to mention a Matisse and three Lalanne tables. But the self-made beauty mogul’s taste was idiosyncratic—or, at least, not on theme with what most collectors are pursuing these days.

Nevertheless, the crowds showed up, and returned the next night, as the industry assembled in a way we haven’t seen for a few seasons. Impressionist and modern evening sales have lost their verve in the art market: These days, most of the action takes place across the phone banks while the webcast cameras pivot back and forth between whispering specialists. But on Monday
night at Sotheby’s, the crowd of dealers and art advisors arrived early to mingle and schmooze in the lobby. Some were ready to bid, others were there just to catch the vibe. A few, one private dealer suggested, may have been secretly hoping for the drama of a tough night, similar to the heart-in-throat Gerald Fineberg sale of contemporary art at Christie’s a year and a half ago.

Whatever their motivation, the onlookers stayed late enough to witness solid demand, proving that the art market has not been squelched by a lack of dry powder. Instead, the problem has been a lack of material exciting enough to tempt buyers. And yet, the Miller sale, and some isolated works from the various-owners sales, demonstrated that buyers are willing to chase specific lots that have been priced to sell and, in some cases, validated by third-party guarantors.

White Gloves

All 25 Miller lots sold—a feat known as a “white glove sale” in the trade. When these sales happen, they look easy, belying the fact that they require a lot of work behind the scenes. The Miller family had help from a large team of advisors, including former Sotheby’s specialist David Norman. Miller had been a client of Norman’s 25 years ago, and now Norman was helping to price the works in a way that would draw bidders—the family declined to take a guarantee—and selectively field irrevocable bids. In the end, the Miller heirs took seven irrevocable bids—as Sotheby’s calls them, or “third-party guarantees” to the rest of the art world.

That was a smart move. In the end, four of the top six prices in the Miller sale came with irrevocable bids. Over the weekend, yet another art advisor mentioned to me that their
clients have begun to view the third-party guarantees as a vote of confidence in a work and an encouragement to bid. That view seemed to have borne out on the big Monet, Kandinsky, Yves Klein, and Henry Moore works that the Miller family sold. Those seven irrevocable bids accounted for over half of the sale’s total value.

In the case of the Monet waterlilies ( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/a-legacy-of-beauty-the-collection-of-sydell-miller-evening-auction/nympheas?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ), the $60 million whisper number had been a bone of contention. Sotheby’s folks are rightly unhappy with some of my opinions about the auction house, so let’s give them
the credit they deserve here. The work is a late example of one of the artist’s most famous subjects, but the painter never signed it. It’s from the large trove of late waterlily paintings that he never let out of the studio. (His heirs stamped it with his signature, and so the art market considers these works to be less valuable.) There were also some stretcher marks on the canvas that were made more prominent by the way the work was hung and lit. Despite all of that, Sotheby’s was able to hit the price they pitched to the estate, which had been 50 percent higher than what was predicted across town. The final selling price was $65.5 million.

The Kandinsky ( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/a-legacy-of-beauty-the-collection-of-sydell-miller-evening-auction/weisses-oval-white-oval?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ), too, might have been expected to be fully priced at $15 million, and the irrevocable bid may not have been the tipping point, here. Still, the work saw bidding that pushed the hammer price more than 25 percent above the estimate, to $19 million. That’s a selling price of $21.6 million.

The first irrevocable bid the Miller family took was on the Yves Klein sponge painting ( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/a-legacy-of-beauty-the-collection-of-sydell-miller-evening-auction/relief-eponge-bleu-sans-titre-re-28?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) estimated at $8 million, well above what recent sales would have suggested for this work. Imagine everyone’s surprise when the bidding turned into a dogged match of wills between Upper East Side advisor Baird Ryan and veteran dealer Andrew Fabricant. Bidding from opposite sides of the room to a $14.2 million selling price with fees, Fabricant outlasted his rival and walked away with the esoteric object.

Another third-party-backed work that achieved a surprise success was the washed-out Rothko work on paper ( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/a-legacy-of-beauty-the-collection-of-sydell-miller-evening-auction/untitled?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) that had been estimated at $2 million and sold for $5.1 million. The ocher and pale yellow work had a long and distinguished provenance, including the fashion designer Hubert de Givenchy, but has yet to be included in Rothko’s catalogue raisonné for works on paper. You can see how the irrevocable bid might have been helpful here, and it was. (There were two minor Rothkos in the various-owners sale that followed, but neither came close to reaching the estimate.)

Lalannes Lead the Way

One place where Miller’s taste matches the current market momentum is in her love of Les Lalannes. The evening sale included three tables, one by François-Xavier Lalanne, the surrealist sculptor/furniture designer now dominating the auction market, and two by his wife, Claude. The first to sell ( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/a-legacy-of-beauty-the-collection-of-sydell-miller-evening-auction/troupeau-delephants-dans-les-arbres-table?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) was an example of the “troop of elephants
among trees” table. Miller had owned two of these. One was sold three years ago for $6.6 million. Initially, Monday night’s bidding was quite slow, and it seemed the table would get knocked down for a price below the previous sale.

But slow bidding isn’t necessarily feckless bidding, and the price reached an even $10 million—$6 million above the estimate. The final selling price of $11.6 million wasn’t double the previous price but still close enough to impress.

Miller had commissioned Claude Lalanne to make two tables with a waterlily theme to accompany her Monet. The first sold ( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/a-legacy-of-beauty-the-collection-of-sydell-miller-evening-auction/a-unique-nenuphars-console-table?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) for $3.84 million above a $1.5 million estimate. But the second table ( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/a-legacy-of-beauty-the-collection-of-sydell-miller-evening-auction/a-unique-nenuphars-console-table-2?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) in the set went for only $2.16 million to a different buyer. To most of us, that result seems
nonsensical: How could two such similar works reach such different prices? That’s what we
mean by a connoisseur’s market: The buyers here have persnickety wants, and what may seem identical to the uninitiated can be glaringly different to the connoisseur. Also, once the first table was sold and the buyer showed they weren’t going to pursue the second table, there was one bidder fewer driving the price forward.

All of this isn’t to say the Miller sale was without miscalculations. The large Dubuffet of mixed oil and acrylic paints was estimated at $2.5 million, which proved far too ambitious. The work was offered and failed to sell when bidding reached $1.6 million. It passed. Just before the end of the sale, auctioneer Oliver Barker brought the work back to the block. But this time, he could only eke out bids up to $1.25 million—which was accepted. Barker is a superbly talented auctioneer—but even he can’t make up for a bidder’s disappointment and, perhaps, spite. In the end, it cost the family around half a million to
secure the white glove bragging rights (or simply to not have to deal with selling the work privately). Of course, the $215.9 million in the bank from the rest of the sale is likely consolation enough.

Various Owners, Various Stories

Sydell Miller was not the only selling point at the auctions last night. The various-owners sale of modern material had several transactions that were telling, each in its own way. Buyers may have money, but the overall market mood is still picky.

The run of female surrealists at the beginning of the sale showed solid results for Remedios Varo ( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/modern-evening-auction-2/los-caminos-tortuosos?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ), Leonor Fini ( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/modern-evening-auction-2/les-stylites-les-stylists?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ), and Leonora Carrington. Another Carrington was sold at the end 
( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/modern-evening-auction-2/temple-of-the-word?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ), also for a solid number. The most eye-catching of the works was the 6-foot-tall sculpture ( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/modern-evening-auction-2/la-grande-dame-the-cat-woman?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) by Carrington, La Grande Dame, which was bought by Eduardo Costantini, the Argentine collector, for $11.3 million.

The Tiffany Studios window was sold ( https://www.sothebys.com/en/buy/auction/2024/modern-evening-auction-2/the-danner-memorial-window?locale=en&utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F19%2F24%29&utm_content=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F17%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) at a huge price of nearly $11.4 million, with fees, above a $5 million estimate. That price doesn’t include the installation necessary to get the same effect Sotheby’s was able to create in the preview. (Though I’m sure the auction house
would be willing to throw in the recording of atmospheric morning soundscapes to the successful bidder.)

Paul Signac’s Antibes. La Pointe de Bacon, from 1917, which had been in the same family for 70 years, attracted a lot of bidding and sold for $9.95 million with fees over a $6 million estimate. Henri-Edmond Cross’s Eucalyptus et oliviers, from 1907, held by the Farkas family for more than 50 years, also did well, at $1.38 million with fees.

Franz Marc’s Das Lange Gelbe Pferd (The Long Yellow Horse), from 1913, failed to get bids to even half the $8 million estimate. It went unsold. An Ernst Ludwig Kirchner train scene made a price below the estimate, but a Max Beckmann sold well at $2.4 million with fees. That was over a $1.5 million estimate.

It was a rough night for Pablo Picasso. The titan of modern art has seen his auction sales dwindle this year. According
to ARTDAI’s numbers, $580 million in Picasso’s art was auctioned in 2023. So far in 2024, that number is $191 million. Miller’s large painting from 1925 depicting a sculptress isn’t quite what current collectors are looking for. The bidding stopped far short of the $30 million that Sotheby’s and the Miller family were hoping for. It sold for $24.8 million with fees. Considering that performance, the Neumann family’s Buste de femme from 1949 did quite well by getting bids to $8.5 million, just below the $9 million estimate. Another Picasso priced at $7 million was withdrawn despite, or maybe because, it was guaranteed by Sotheby’s. A work on paper and a late painting were bought in. And two other works that were backed by irrevocable bids sold at or just above the estimate.

Henri Matisse had a mixed night, too. Miller’s 1942 portrait, Jeune fille en robe rose, was estimated at $3 million, but saw strong bidding to $8.3
million, or nearly three times the estimate. The final price was $9.73 million. Later, in the various-owners sale, a Matisse odalisque from a Japanese collection, and backed by a direct guarantee from Sotheby’s, was offered with a $12 million estimate. A similar image from around the same period in the early 1920s sold for $14.3 million in 2018. But the appetite for this work was no longer there. It was “bought in,” as they say in the business, without a single bid.
