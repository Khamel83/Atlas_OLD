Today’s Daily Update interview is another installment of the Stratechery Founder series; as a reminder, one of the challenges in covering startups is the lack of available data. My solution is to go in the opposite direction and interview founders directly, letting them give their subjective overview of their companies, while pressing them on their business model, background, and long-term potential.

Today’s interview is with Deel Co-Founder and CEO Alex Bouaziz ( https://twitter.com/Bouazizalex ). Deel is a human resources platform with a focus on globally distributed workers: companies using Deel can hire contractors abroad, leverage Deel to hire employees in countries where they don’t have a presence, or to manage payroll and other HR functions in countries where they do. Deel raised $50 million at a $12 billion valuation last May ( https://www.axios.com/2022/05/11/deel-venture-capital-funding ) , after growing from $4 million in annual recurring revenue at the beginning of 2021 to $295 million at the beginning of this year ( https://www.forbes.com/sites/kenrickcai/2023/01/23/deel-emerson-collective-funding-12-billion-valuation-300-million-revenue/?sh=f33d40d77a00 ).

Deel’s startling growth was reason enough to talk to Bouaziz, and we get into their business model, the impact of COVID, having to get good at terminations, and the power of founder-market fit; what was also very interesting to me are larger trends in terms of remote work and its long-term implications that Deel’s model takes advantage of.

Background

*Alex Bouaziz ( https://twitter.com/Bouazizalex ) , CEO and co-founder of Deel ( https://www.deel.com/ ) , welcome to Stratechery.*

*Alex Bouaziz:* Thank you so much for having me. I’ve been looking forward chatting with you for a while.

*I want to first learn a bit more about you and your background. It’s quite international in nature. I think the countries that I see listed include France, Israel, the US, the UK. Am I missing any or does that cover the gamut?*

*AB:* It depends how long do I need to live in a country for you to count it as a country, but yeah, I was born and raised in Paris, moved to Israel for my studies when I was 16, then moved to the US for my masters. That’s actually where I met Shuo [Wang] ( https://www.linkedin.com/in/shuooo ) , my co-founder, who you should talk to at some point. She’s the cool one in this band, let’s say. Then I did my PhD in the UK where eventually I dropped out to start building companies. These are like the key countries, and then I spent a little bit of time in Spain and China and a few others.

*I do want to circle back to that background, I think you could probably see a natural link to Deel, but why don’t you start out by — what were the companies that you started that you dropped out to get started?*

*AB:* Yeah, I’m a good entrepreneur, in that I had to fail with many companies before I was able to finally be successful! First, like every single founder, I was very excited by consumer. Every founder I meet wants to build a consumer business when they start out, and then they realize consumer is really freaking hard. So I built a video company at the time that didn’t really work out, and then toyed around that idea, generally working around user-generated content and enabling people — I mean in some ways kind of ties back to Deel — but people from all over world to use their talents to create videos together, but that didn’t really work out. Then I got sucked into the crypto scene and built one of the first crypto NFT projects at the time actually where I was very excited about it. What I love about crypto is it’s very decentralized, it enables a lot of people to get paid globally, and cuts out on some of the terrible ways to get paid like SWIFT. Really crypto for me was the embodiment of leveraging a lot of amazing talents everywhere to build great products and that kind of led up to Deel. Some of them were a bit more successful than others, but definitely nowhere near I guess what we’ve been able to accomplish at Deel today.

*I don’t know if this is an articulated-by-you founding myth, but one of the things I read in researching for this interview is that as part of some of these companies you were hiring contractors in different places and realized you were probably fragrantly breaking the law. Is that true? Was that happening or is that an after-the-fact story?*

*AB:* Breaking the law, never! But basically when we started those companies, I was out of school, I moved back to Israel, I’m not Israeli, I wasn’t at the time, I didn’t have a lot of connections, didn’t have any money to build, so I couldn’t afford Israeli developers, and it was just too expensive, so I had to find ways to be able to build. I eventually worked with a couple people around the world based in the Ukraine or in Serbia or in the UK, to build my first products. As a founder worrying about compliance and payments is the last thing you want to do, you just want to start working and get the best people you can very quickly, and when I was engaging with those people around the world, I definitely realized I was literally Googling “template contracts Ukraine, Israel” and I was like, “Okay, that’s definitely not the way to do it.”

I also remember I was paying those guys from my bank and I don’t know if you know, bank UIs are terrible, doing it manually, not giving them a good experience. So the whole concept wasn’t done properly and wasn’t thought out, not just for me but also for the other guys on their side and their experience. I definitely wasn’t doing it the right way. At the very beginning of your company it doesn’t truly matter, but as you scale the company, it’s important. But the fun fact is all the guys I worked with at the time actually were part of the Deel adventure so they’ve been with me all this time.

Deel

*Tell me more about Deel. I’ve been hinting at it along and what you do for companies, but I’ll let you go first. You’ve had a lot of opportunities to pitch Deel. Give me what is the elevator pitch about what Deel is and why it’s compelling.*

*AB:* For sure. Deel is the first true global HR platform to hire people globally. As a company, if you want to hire someone in a country that you don’t have infrastructure in, usually you struggle to do that. We’re the first company that bridges the full HR aspect of running a company, so HR and having all of your employees in one database, combined with the fact that we can run payroll, we can help you hire anyone anywhere as a contractor, as a full-time employee, where we have our own entities and that’s in over a hundred countries.

So let’s say tomorrow you decide you want to join Apple, and Apple doesn’t have an entity in Taiwan, they could literally employ you through us where we have our own entity located in Taiwan. We can put you on payroll, give you all of the best benefits, all of the best practices, and Apple will be able to work with you directly. So we’ve evolved over time. We started with the ability to hire contractors, then deployed our entities over the world and realized we can do more and do payroll for people’s entities and eventually came to the point where we think we can be the first true global HR platform in general. It’s really about helping hundreds of millions of people get to work for the best companies in the world, wherever they are. So yeah, very excited about what we’re doing.

*You talked about a bunch of things there. You started out with contractors, but then you’re doing payroll. So step me through this exactly. If I’m, say an employer, and I want to hire someone, what are the ways that I might interface with Deel to get it? Let’s start with contractors. If I want to hire a contractor, I’m in Taiwan say I want to hire a contractor in France where you are, so I don’t employ the contractor directly, you employ them? Walk me through that, how does that work?*

*AB:* Actually that’s how we evolved as a company, we evolved as a company with our customers, the cycle of our customers and their HR relationship with their teammates evolved. So when we started the company, we built a solution for you to hire someone as a contractor in another country compliantly.

*You provided the proper contract and the rules and regulations and stuff like that.*

*AB:* Exactly. So in France for example, there are fifteen different ways to be set up as a contractor. We help you understand, is your contractor set up the right way, do they have the right documents? We tailor the contracts to Taiwan’s legal infrastructure and France’s legal infrastructure so that your risk of misclassifying that person is greatly diminished because you’ve done all the right steps to engage this person in the right way. So that was our first product.

*Got it. And do you still offer that product?*

*AB:* Yeah, it’s our bread and butter. It’s the product that until mid-2020 it was our most popular product and then the other one took over because there’s more demand in general.

*Got it, and how would I pay Deel for helping me set that up?*

*AB:* Basically we’d be doing all of the payments for your teammates and that’s actually something very special on the other side. Like I was telling you when we started Deel, I was doing those payments manually to all my teammates around the world and it was not very convenient for them. In this product you just pay us and pay our fee on top of it, $49 a month per contractor, and then on the other side, your contractors gets their fund in one platform and they can actually choose how they want to get paid. Crypto, TransferWise, Payoneer. They have full control of their funds irrespective of how you paid us in order to get them paid, which gives them a lot of control and a great experience too.

*What’s the normal way someone gets paid in France? Is it direct deposit or what is it?*

*AB:* Yeah, in that case it’d be a direct deposit. But let’s say, you know, want get paid in Payoneer, you want to get paid in PayPal, you want to take part of your paycheck in crypto and then rest in Filipino pesos and rest in USD, you have full control on your money rather than one SWIFT payment that’s made by your account payable teams or directly for your bank manually. Those routes are not optimized and for timing and they’re very — a SWIFT payment takes five plus days, the local payments or a crypto payment will take a couple of minutes.

*Right. Okay, I’m confused though why you keep coming back to crypto because you’re not a crypto company. If I’m just a bog-standard contractor in France and I just want to get paid like everybody else gets paid, that’s what you will provide.*

*AB:* Yeah, I just like crypto, but if you want, you can take part of your paycheck in crypto. I don’t know why I keep coming back to it, I just like it quite a bit, it’s very powerful in countries where the payment network actually is not great so that’s why I keep coming back to it.

*Got it. But it can be just a normal direct deposit or whatever you want it to be.*

*AB:* Exactly.

*But it’s totally up to the contractor?*

*AB:* Exactly. Flexibility is very big for them.

*Okay, so I want to hire a contractor, I pay money to Deel or I send the money to Deel plus $49, you pay out the contractor, that makes sense. That’s number one, and just to be clear, the legal relationship is between me and the contractor, Deel is not in the middle.*

*AB:* Correct.

*So that’s number one. So number two, as I understand it is, you actually employ the person on my behalf, is that right?*

*AB:* Correct. So we’ve got a hundred plus entities around the world. You tell me “Employ that person for me in that country” and depending on the rules and legislation, I can technically have that person on payroll on my entity but working for you by the end of the day, anywhere.

*As a company, why might I want to hire someone via Deel as opposed to just hire them as a contractor?*

*AB:* It depends. First of all, there are some countries where a contractor relationship after a certain amount of time might get misclassified, so you know want to be able to employ them so that you remove that risk away from you. Sometimes people want to be employees for multiple reasons, getting mortgages or because it’s the best practice in the country, so you want to be able to afford that solution to your teammates. There’s tons of reasons why you’d want to do it, but just the flexibility of being able to go from a contractor model to an employee, even if you don’t have the infrastructure, is something that’s really big for our customers.

*Are the customers that are using this employee relationship, is it usually that they have a lot of employees in that country or only one or two?*

*AB:* Typically they don’t have an entity in that country, so that’s why they usually leverage our entity. Sometimes it happens that they might, and we’re able to do a lot more things nowadays. For example, we can do immigrations in the country, getting a visa and sometimes you need to have the right sponsorship infrastructure to be sponsoring a visa. Sometimes they might have an entity, but our main common use case is you want to go into a country, you don’t want to set up an entity because it’s expensive. You need to be audited, you need to keep up with HR and compliance, there’s quite a burden to it, and you need to report taxes on it at the end of the year if you’re a US company, so the ability to just being able to hire anyone anywhere without having to set up infrastructure is a game changer for our companies.

*How did you come up with this model? You start out with contracting, was it companies coming to you saying, “Look, we actually need to hire an employee”? What was the “aha” moment, or were you always building towards this?*

*AB:* Yeah, that’s exactly what happened. Companies were coming to us and saying, “Look, we love the contracting model. It’s very efficient for us, but truly that person is an employee. So diminishing the risk of misclassification, helping us navigate through it is great, but they’re definitely an employee so I need a solution and I want to hire them in a compliant way,” and this is what they want.

Typically, if you look backwards, what people used to do is they used to go through third-party agencies that used to take a pretty hefty pay cut, 20, 25% of the salary of that person in order to employ them locally for local agencies, and you’ll have all those little local agencies working on Windows XPs in all those countries. We really changed the game by owning all of the infrastructure all the way from entity, but also lawyers in every country, payroll managers in every country, so that you can really hire quickly, but also the full spectrum from hiring all the way to termination is fully handled by us.

*What’s the business model for the employment version then? In this case, the employer is still paying Deel but then in this case the legal relationship is between the employee and Deel, correct?*

*AB:* Correct, you’ll have an MSA with us and you’d come and sign an agreement and say, “Hey, I want to employ this person in Japan, this person in Nigeria, this person in Colombia.” Then, we would employ them locally. We would pay them every month their salary, pay the taxes, and manage all of this for you. Then, you would just settle their salary, the employer tax on top of it, and our management fee on top of it, very simple.

*Got it. Is the management fee a percentage of salary?*

*AB:* Flat fee, $599 regardless of the salary of the person, which is really big when you hire salespeople.

*Right, totally, that makes sense, $599, which probably I would guess salespeople are by far one of the biggest categories for this specific contract.*

*AB:* It’s definitely one of the big ones. Engineers as well and generally white collar is a pretty big one, but salespeople, especially if you’re thinking, “Hey, I want to expand into this new country. I want to check out the market, hire the first people on the ground before I decide do I go all the way and open my own entity,” that’s a very quick way to test out the market.

The AWS of Hiring

*When it comes to markets that have much stricter employment laws, whether that comes from laying people off or where it’s much more difficult to terminate employees, as opposed to the US where it’s much more an at-will market, how does that work? Is that part of your MSA agreement where the employer agrees to keep paying whatever they need to, or how does Deel protect itself because you’re technically on the hook?*

*AB:* We are back to back with the people that are using our product, where technically they need to pay for anything that’s employee-related. If there was anything on the payroll side, all of this is on our side, but as long as you’re engaging with this person, and when you’re hiring in countries like my home country like France or the Netherlands or Germany, you need to understand, and we try to educate you at best of how much will it cost to actually terminate this person, and how does probation period work over time, there’s a lot of caveats into making it happen.

Transparently, during the last six months there was tons of layoffs, we helped I think terminate people employed for us, over 6,000 plus people across 80 plus countries. The exercise of terminating people in that country is something we’re really, really good at in-house. That’s actually a huge lift for our customers being able to do all of this and negotiating with them and going to court if there’s a need to do it with our internal legal counsel. That’s actually one of the great reasons to use us as well, not having to deal with termination in a country, because we have experts that can do it for you. Typically, in these countries you end up settling and that’s the discussion that you have with your employee if it’s past probation period, and we really handle all of this for you, which is something that our customers truly value.

*Were there things you discovered over the last few months as there were more layoffs that you hadn’t fully considered or thought out and you had to adjust?*

*AB:* There’s a difference between what I hadn’t considered versus what my legal team had considered. They’re experts, they know, but when we went to mass layoffs, for example, there’s different regulations on how to do mass layoffs. There’s a certain threshold of employees in a country where if you’re doing a mass layoff there, then there’s regulations on how to do it, which makes things even more complicated.

*Moreover, even if none of your customers are over the limit, Deel by collectively employing all these people might be over the limit.*

*AB:* Correct, which is quite interesting to navigate and that’s something I hadn’t fully thought through at the time, but that’s definitely something that our legal team had fully mapped out. It’s quite interesting when as a company you don’t always think about building a very fully fleshed out infrastructure to offboard your revenue-driving employees and business.

Over time, over the last six months, we had a significant wave of people willing to help on that front. We really built a best-in-class way to offboard people, which is very counterintuitive, but at the same time that also shows, hey, when things are tough this is why we’re here and this is why we want to be helping you.

This is why if you were going to work with your own lawyers and your own entities on doing this, it’s probably going to cost you a ton of money on legal fees, while we have the experts that know how to do it and do it all day. Counterintuitive, but we really think down the line, one, it’s part of our experience. We need to do it really well, but it truly pays off as we saw more customers coming and consolidating because of that legal experience.

*It’s like your pitch is to be the AWS of employing people where the promise is not just flexibility on the way up, but flexibility on the way down as well and you have to absorb all those costs.*

*AB:* Correct, I mean we do it in a way where we’re here for our customers and this is exactly in those moments that we build true trust with them. Opening an entity and putting people on payroll is doable at scale. It’s hard, but it’s not the hardest thing. Managing it at scale and keeping up with compliance in every single country, tax events, changes, or termination, this is where you start really valuing the relationship that you have with us.

*We spent a long time on number two because I think that’s actually really fascinating how you are actually this mass employer over the world on behalf of your customers.*

*AB:* We’ll be the biggest employer.

*Number one contracting, number two, you are the employer. Then, number three, this is where companies do set up entities in other countries, but you are just providing basically HR software or payroll — walk me through the third level.*

*AB:* Yes, Global Payroll ( https://www.deel.com/global-payroll ) , if you think about the cycle I was telling you earlier on, you start with a contractor. Then, you employ a couple of people in the country. Then, you say, “Okay, actually I want to double down in that country. I’m going to open my own entity.” Running payroll in that country, if you have one entity, it’s manageable. When a CFO starts having ten, fifteen of them it starts to be a real mess to manage. They work with local payroll managers, they get Excel files, they try to consolidate. They have no real visibility on what is Social Security in France versus the Netherlands versus Japan, it starts to be really complicated, and what we realize is we have a 100 entities plus that we’re running payroll on of our own, and we have everything in-house. If we can do it for our entities where we have thousands of employees, we can probably do it for our customers entities as well, and start truly consolidating everyone, contractors, employees where you don’t have infrastructure, plus all of your employees, all of your entities in one place where we do the payroll for you. That’s when it starts making even more and more sense.

*Is this a product that potentially rolls back into the domestic market of the companies that you’re in? If they’re using you for payroll internationally, why not just use you for payroll domestically as well?*

*AB:* You’re getting there, exactly. Not in every market — I mean, sure, you could do it in every market, but owning the payroll engine in every market is crazy, the only company that really does that is ADP. You got to be very tactical in terms of where you own the actual payroll engine itself. If you think, for example in France, PayFit is a unicorn that just does payroll for SMBs in France, payroll engines are very hard to build. The only real country today where HR and payroll is so connected together is the US because of all of the different players and how they’ve built it. If you look at any other country today, it’s usually two different systems.

There is room for bringing it together, but the key market that we need to bring it in order to be competitive together is the United States, that’s exactly what you’re saying. The two big products that we launched at the beginning of the year was US payroll where we can do local payroll for US companies as well as our HR infrastructure ( https://www.deel.com/hr ). All of the know-how, how to work with people, how to hire people, how to terminate people, taxes, all of this know-how built into an HR system when you hire someone directly or through us, all of that knowledge is pushed so that you can do it the right way yourself. You basically went full circle into how we did it from a product cycle to get to where we are today.

*For the payroll product where a company does set up a specific entity abroad, what’s the business model there? Do you also charge per employee?*

*AB:* Just per employee, that’s all.

*Got it. And that’s also $599 or what’s the price there?*

*AB:* No, that’s $39.

*That’s the cheapest one, which makes the most sense.*

The COVID Impact

*You referred multiple times to your customers and building trust, and you talked about building trust in the context of doing mass layoffs. I guess that raises the question of employees. Is that just the commodity that you’re dealing with, or do you see your role as being a job board? Do you help source employees? Are you a way for them to find jobs? Or is it just you’re a behind-the-scenes software that they can go to your portal and decide how I’m going to get paid, but that’s kind of the extent of it?*

*AB:* We build trust in general with our customers in that specific case because it is very timely. That’s one of the things that a lot customers came to us for and generally we’re very aligned with both sides. We’re here to help employees or contractors that did not have the opportunity to be able to work for companies globally to be able to do it by streamlining the processes for them, it’s opening up opportunities for them. We’ve helped so many people being able to get hired by companies as they brought the solution saying, “Hey, you don’t hire remotely, but here’s a great solution for you to do so.” I’d say actually people love to ask me, “What changed about the business?” because we started in 2019 pre-pandemic —

*Which I’m going to get to in a moment, but yes.*

*AB:* Everybody loves to talk about this saying, “Oh, maybe that was one of the catalysts of your business.” What I think really happened is because of this everybody went distributed. Then, HR just realized, “Well, my teammate in Ukraine and in the Philippines is on Slack and just as valuable as my teammate in the US. I need to give them a great experience.” What we doubled down on, tripled down on is the employee experience when they get onboarded, when they have support, local support, rather than what used to be 10 years ago. If you tell a VC or someone that you have to meet in Ukraine or in the Philippines, they say, “Oh, you’re outsourcing your tech.” No, it’s just you have a global team. The mindset shift from people leaders to give a great experience to their teammates is truly what helped us flourish. I would say we’re tripling down on the experience of employees and giving them the ability to be hired locally, to have local support, to be there for them.

Now at the end of the day when our customers come and say, “I need to terminate someone,” we don’t have much choice, but we don’t operate at all as a talent management base. We are literally the home for your global team, we don’t get involved with talent and hiring, that’s not our mandate, we’re just here to make it easy for both the employer and the employee to work together.

*Right, makes sense. You have to choose one I would think. You talked about the pandemic a little bit in the context of everyone being remote, but I’m curious. There are a couple ways to approach this, you did start Deel before the pandemic and you saw something in the market then. I think number two is how or if did the pandemic change things? Then, number three, going forward is there a COVID overhang now where there’s a snap back to way the way things might have been before, or has this been a meaningful and longterm fundamental shift in the way companies think about hiring?*

*AB:* On the first point, I think there are amazing people everywhere. The reason we started Deel is me and Shuo both lived around the world, have amazing friends that are as talented as some of the people that you might hire for a lot of money in the Bay Area.

*We go back full circle to your wandering background!*

*AB:* Shuo and I studied back at MIT in the US and I struggled to get my visa to be able to stay in the US after that, so I decided to just go to the UK and start my PhD, but some of my friends went to the Bay Area and got paid loads of money by the Stripes and the Snaps of the world. Some of the others went back to their home country, Croatia, Albania, and they were paid like $500 a month and they were probably the smartest people I knew. There’s definitely a gap in opportunities there. When you live through that and when you live across countries and you meet such amazing people it’s obvious that you want to be able to work with those people. If you combine that with the fact that over time, and God knows I love them, but a lot of my friends in the Bay Area were literally switching jobs every year to get a 20% increase like Google, Facebook, Twitter, one after the other.

It’s very hard to build a company for people where it’s really all about the money and you’re always scared of losing your team. Giving opportunities to people that don’t have opportunities, one, for them to be able to make more income, generally also increasing loyalty to the company, which is super important as when you start was one of the reasons we saw the market. We said, “Okay, this is going to be big,” but it was always happening, it was happening differently. Either people were doing it but not talking about it, or they were opening big offices in some of those regions and hiring thousands of people, it just wasn’t something that was as openly talked about.

When it comes to your second point I think of course from a PR standpoint we got a bit more spotlight because of what happened during that time and that helped us, but I would say the major shift we had was a lot of the larger companies that we thought we would get five years down the line started using us because they had that hair-on-fire problem. “My best engineer is going back to Croatia to be with his family or her family during COVID. What do I do? How do I handle this?” So that triggered the sales cycle that would’ve taken so much longer to shorten the sales cycle. And then when we’re inside of those accounts, because like you just understood, we’re able to offer so many different products, we’re able to land-and-expand and if we make it really easy for you to hire anyone in another country, then you start thinking, “Well, then borders are not my limiting factor anymore, I can just hire people wherever they are.”

*Just one quick question referring to the last point.*

*AB:* Yeah, go for it.

*Did this help shift or drive the shift from focusing on contracts to being an employer? Was it a case that larger companies that were suddenly falling in your lap actually preferred to have employees versus contractors or was that not really connected at all?*

*AB:* Actually, smaller companies prefer to have employees rather than contractors because the bigger companies are used to hiring people as contractors through staffing agencies, so they were much more comfortable with the model if you do it the right way.

*Oh, interesting.*

*AB:* But over time, what we saw is just the talent strategy has shifted. It went from, “We need to be careful on cost,” at the very beginning of COVID, so like contractors, and then, “Oh, but we need to retain talent and we’re willing to pay so much money to have the tools to do that.” So let’s move towards the employee model.

And then eventually, what we saw is depending on your needs, depending on the company, depending on the role, having the flexibility — and that’s one of our strengths to some extent — having the flexibility of starting with a contractor and then moving to an employee or managing the relationship and even enabling it, that’s one of the powerful things that we’ve seen. Enabling the person to say, “What do you want? We could hire you as an employee, we could hire you as a contractor.” Because some people want to travel the world, and if you’re traveling, then you cannot be six months there, six months there and the employee in a country are three months there because you create some form of a permanent establishment risk to the company. So that flexibility is really something that’s very powerful.

*So do you think that going forward, that COVID, just to get to point three, is that a meaningful shift to your business or is there some COVID overhang where companies are snapping back and maybe it’s not as big of a deal as it used to be?*

*AB:* Generally, the business has evolved over time. Now we’re really the home for your team, the HR solution, the global payroll solution, as well as contractor and EOR. Two things: I think generally most companies, much CHROs and head of talent I’m talking to understand that no matter their strategy, whether it’s on office, hybrid, all of them understand that you always need to have the leeway to be able to hire globally now — if there’s an amazing talent somewhere, not giving them an opportunity is not smart, it’s pretty silly.

Even some of the larger companies, if you look at our customers, we’ve got 15,000 plus customers from small companies all the way to Nike, Subway, banks, I can’t name all of them. Even those large banks that have decided everybody needs to be in office, actually a lot of their engineers, designers and white collars are fully distributed. They just don’t brand it as much and don’t talk about it as much. I’m very biased, but I definitely think that the world is a big place with a lot of talent and more and more companies are understanding that it’s not about just hiring within a thirty-miles radius.

White Collar Globalization

*There’s an obvious positive spin at Deel, which you’ve articulated a couple of times, where you’re giving opportunities to people in developing countries in particular, or your classmates from MIT that went back to Croatia and were dramatically underpaid relative to your classmates in the Valley. There is a negative spin though, which is that you’re increasing competition for workers in developed countries. You had this recent hiring report ( https://www.deel.com/resources/state-of-global-hiring-report-2023 ) that you sent me, and of your ten most popular countries for remote workers, only four saw average salary increases across all new job contracts: Philippines, India, Brazil, and Italy. Meanwhile, Nigeria, Mexico, Netherlands, US, and Argentina saw a decline in overall average starting salaries. Do you think this is a trend that developed countries should be worried about?*

*AB:* I think that there are, a couple places in the world where salaries have gotten a little too crazy and it’s very, very hard with the salaries to be a sustainable business and in a market where everybody’s thinking about profitability and getting to the right numbers, if you’re paying your average software engineer $500,000+ a year, it’s going to be very hard to build your business. So definitely think that there’s going to be a rebalance.

*So you think it’s more of a macro issue.*

*AB:* Yeah, exactly.

*Well, let’s look at something like software engineers specifically. So you noted that software engineers in developing countries make 28% more working for national organizations than domestic ones, which fits your thesis. They get a chance to work for Google instead of the local whatever, government or whatever it is. At the same time, software engineers on Deel earned 7% less last year than the previous year. Now again, I guess that could fit in the macroeconomic factor, but is it also the case that if you have a global engineering pool there’s more supply which is also going to reduce prices?*

*AB:* I think when it comes to engineering talent specifically, there’s always going to be a need for more supply, so I’m not too worried about this specific role to be very honest. But across other roles, for sure, to some extent, if someone is very, very talented, they should have a shot at having the position and until now, when I was at school, when I was a little younger, I used to study this idea of the brain drain, the smartest people in our country go to the US because this is the way and this is the place for you to be able to make a career. Challenging that is something I’m very excited about. I want you to be able to work for the best company, stay in your country, stay with your family if that’s what you want to do, and grow your community, and if this is the impact to some extent that happens behind that, then I think this is just to some extent recalibrating the word a little bit and redistributing wealth, which I think I’m very excited about.

*Big picture, just to zoom back — and this is less a commentary on Deel — one of my theses is that in the 1970s there was a double whammy on the US working class, where you had automation at home and globalization abroad. It feels like we’re in a similar period here in the 2020s with white collar work, where the analogy to automation in the factory is AI and the stuff that we’re obviously seeing with these large language models. Then the analogy to globalization is remote work, and I guess the concern that could be articulated in terms of the shift remote work is, well, once you can just hire someone in Kansas, you can just hire someone in the Philippines or maybe you want in South America if you want to keep the same timezone. Is that a trend that you see from your position?*

*AB:* Yeah, for sure, but I also think that hiring someone in Kansas is going to be much more popular than only hiring people in the Bay Area. So even within the United States, enabling people to be hired in states that are a little less popular for tech workers and enabling people to stay in the Midwest because their family is there is doing to grow as well. So to some extent, I agree with what you just said, but I do think that this also has a local impact where the main cities — I’m in Paris right now, a lot of people in France get hired in cities around Paris, not just Paris anymore, and that is something I think is very powerful too.

*What are some of the other jobs on Deel? We’ve talked about sales people, we talked about software engineers. What are some of the other big categories?*

*AB:* Any white collar job typically we’re able to support. So software engineers, designers, product managers, payroll managers. Then we can really go wild. We have pilots getting paid on Deel, we have teachers getting paid on Deel. It’s just what does the company truly need in order to scale and where can they find those resources? So for us, apart from some specific roles where we would need to basically set up another entity and get specific business licenses like financial advisory to be able to hire some of those roles, we technically can hire anything that’s within our collective convention within the country, the ability to just follow the local laws. In France, for example, you only can hire a specific type of people for your type of organization. So as long as we follow the local labor laws for a specific role, then we can hire them.

*How American-centric is Deel? There’s a tidbit in your report that APAC companies hired globally at the fastest rate of all regions and that LATAM was the hottest region to hire from. But that’s sort of a rate question. I presume, is it safe to assume that the largest absolute number is still very US-centric?*

*AB:* Yeah, 50% of our customers are based in the US in general, and LATAM is actually one of the predominant market that US companies hire into. Generally what I think, APAC is a very interesting market, because if you look at countries like Japan, they struggle to have really good engineers because the local talent is very, very hard to compete, and so being able to hire into Singapore, Singapore is also tough, but into the Philippines or into India, into Vietnam, it’s such a power move for them and such a game-changing in terms of the tech that they can develop.

One of the thing I hadn’t realized is when we started working in LATAM and selling it to Latin businesses, so Colombian companies or Brazilian companies, I always thought, “Oh, they’ll hire in Portugal, in Spain, in Europe”. Just the ability to hire in Colombia when you’re a Brazilian company is actually a pretty big game changer too. There’s some locality here, which is cool as well. But, yes, to come back to your point, 50% of our business comes from the United States.

*Right. You mentioned Ukraine a couple of times and Ukraine was very well known for having a very large software development ecosystem. What has happened or is happening with the war? How much of an impact did that have on your business?*

*AB:* Ukrainians are some of the most talented people I’ve ever met. Our key first two developers that are still at the company today are from Ukraine, so they’re the heart and soul of Deel, it’s always been one of the key markets for us. As you said, a lot of companies open centers, that used to be the way. You open a big center and hire a lot of people in the local country, it’s still happening. Most of the people in Ukraine haven’t even been able to leave the country because they’re not allowed to exit the country under specific conditions and rules so it’s still a big country where people are hiring.

Obviously we do our best to help locally during the war. We have a product on the contractor side where we enable people to take an advance on their payments so if they needed the cash right now, we open that up so that they could do it for free and rapidly. I still think it’s going to be a growing population in the Ukraine that’s going to be hired on Deel and we’re going to be there to support it. Obviously they need some headspace when it comes to what’s happening there, but again, some of the smartest people we’ve ever met and some of the best universities when it comes at least to computer science, something I’ve invested a lot in myself. So yeah, it’s going to keep on growing for sure.

Deel’s Future

*So what can go wrong? We’ve talked about the macro factors to an extent, how that might impact how much people are paid. You’ve had to learn to get very good at terminations, which is sort of weird for a growing company to have to think about. So there are macro issues — there’s a macro possible condition if there is a real backlash to, I guess, globalization broadly or whatever, or because this is all white collar work. Do you feel pretty immune from that? That’s I guess part one. Then part two, from a micro perspective, what specific obstacles do you see in front of your business as you’re growing and expanding?*

*AB:* Yeah, I think generally the world has become much more borderless over the last few years, and if you combine that with the fact that knowledge has become much more available and people have been able to leverage that, a lot more of like YouTube and just access to knowledge has become so much more open and so much more available, that we see a rising amount of talent that are just self-learners, it’s not just about universities anymore. So this for me is the trend that we’re truly going to ride on, like the fact that everybody that’s talented is going to have opportunities and more and more talented people are going to come out of those countries over time.

*Do you see yourself getting into some sort of certification so people can show to employers — I guess that’s kind of back to the question of will you service employees at some point? If people are self-taught, for example, how do they demonstrate their knowledge to future employers?*

*AB:* So actually we do have a card product ( https://help.letsdeel.com/hc/en-gb/articles/4407737595409-What-is-the-Deel-Card- ) , a cash advance product, and a ton of perks that we’re giving to the end users. We do our best to support the employees in the contractor sides. It’s always been interesting for us to think about, I don’t know if we’re going to go there eventually, but the idea that if you’ve been hired by a global company before and we kind of have that data, we could help you in your next job, validate that you were hired the right way and the termination process wasn’t crazy, you truly parted ways or resigned for a new opportunity. I think there is interesting data there for sure where we can help companies hire globally and feel more comfortable, because a lot of companies when they want to hire globally, they’re scared. They don’t know if they’re going to be able to work with that person, they don’t know how to run a background check locally, a reference check is very complicated to do. So, this is another layer where if we can help the employees have a strong profile, a strong validation, and help them work backwards into getting and applying for jobs, there’s something there for sure.

*So, what could go wrong for Deel specifically? You look good, you look like you’re sleeping very well, so maybe nothing is keeping you up at night.*

*AB:* (laughing) I actually woke up for an APAC all hands at 6:30 AM this morning, so I definitely didn’t get enough sleep. But we have 600 people in APAC now, so pretty big team. I don’t know if you saw, we acquired a public company in Australia back in November ( https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20221121005278/en/HR-Company-Deel-Completes-PayGroup-Acquisition ) , so it was a pretty interesting move for us.

What could go wrong? I mean right now, I think on the core products we’ve really got something that’s working really well and we’re ahead of the market. We’re the biggest company in this market and helping tons of companies. I think there is tons of improvements we need to do in order to truly educate our customers and give them a better experience, but something we’ll get over time.

I think we have the opportunity to become a generational company. That’s what’s interesting to me is the fact that we’re bridging the global aspect of hiring and the payroll aspect of hiring in one full stack HR platform, and that hasn’t been done before and it’s just branching out into different parts of the HR world, different parts of the finance word. Consolidating all of this in one place, we got to execute on the product side and we got to execute on a lot of fronts in order to truly build a company we’re meant to build. So, it is mainly going to be execution.

*Is your biggest competitor, do you view it as like SAP or something like that then?*

*AB:* No, I like SAP! They’re nice and we’re integrating with them. But I think, look, when I think about HR in general, and this is where I want to get to, and I think we have a really good shot at that — I’ve never seen someone tell me “I love my HR system”. As an employee, as a client it’s just never that feeling where you feel attached to it. But if you feel about your relationship with your HR system, you get paid on it, you sign your contract on it, you get your payslips on it, potentially additional products like advances on your payments and all of this, there is a case for an HR product you truly love.

I think Workday and SAP have built amazing products, but there’s still this “Apple of HR”, this idea of a product that you truly, truly love that enables you globally to be hired and to work for the best companies, that has still yet to be built. Getting there is going to be hard because of all the different components that we need to bring on to satisfy our customers. This idea of consolidating and giving a fully flushed-out product that works globally and that can do everything in one place is a very nice vision and very nice dream, but the execution of it or operational execution of it is very complex.

So, what could go wrong? I do think we’ll get to billion plus in revenue, we’ll be a great company. For me, it would be like if we don’t build that full HR infrastructure that truly makes people happy, then we won’t be able to become the hundred plus billion dollar company, that generational company that we have a chance to be.

Tech and Integration

*How much of Deel is a tech company or a software company? Because it sounds like you have all these employees, 600 employees in APAC, what proportion of them are lawyers? Where do you slot yourself in that in terms of the role that software plays versus just the nature of being a one-stop shop for all these different countries?*

*AB:* Yeah. So, Deel, we are very unique in that aspect because we are very focused on owning everything, we want to own the full stack. We have all our customer support agents, they’re all in house, like four hundred, five hundred plus people, when most companies usually try to outsource this to vendors. The fact that we can globally cover and bring everybody in-house, something big. Same for our back office, for SDRs, for lawyers or payroll managers, because when you’re dealing with people’s paychecks, contracts, owning the infrastructure and not relying on third parties.

So for example, a lot of our competitors, they don’t own the payroll process, they work with third parties, like accounts in local countries in order to run the payroll. When you do that, you’re dependent on the accountant’s SLA in order to answer your employees or your customers, and when the employees are coming to you and saying, “Why did I get taxed this amount of money?”, telling them you’re going to have to wait five days for my accountants locally to potentially answer is not an experience you want to have. I want to be able to answer to you within a couple hours and say, “Hey, this is a full breakdown, this is why it’s there” and if we own the infrastructure, this is the type of experience we can truly give.

We kind of took it a step further than the way things are now. If you look at the market in general, no one even owned the entities, they used to work with partners all over the world. Taking it a step further is having the lawyers in-house, having the payroll managers in-house, and all of this is to focus on the customer experience. We’re very much at the company, I’m an engineer, so as much as a lot of the operational processes are gapped by people because you need to productize it, you need to understand it.

And some people want to have this local HR touch, they want to have someone locally that they can talk to that speaks their language and that can help them. We’re moving billions of dollars a month across 450 different bank accounts. Think about the fact that in Kenya we have to run payroll with our local bank account there, that part is pretty crazy. Plus the automation of all the different processes, like onboarding, hiring, filling the different forms, all the local forms, there’s no way you cannot. Sure it’s not deep, deep, deep tech, but there’s no way you can build a company, even to the stage where we are, we’ve got strong automations level and processes at scale.

*That makes total sense. I like the point about integration, because if you think about your place in the value chain, you have all these different companies, your customers interacting with Deel and then you are interacting with all these however many employees are on your platform, and you want to have the full point-of-integration somewhere in that value chain, that definitely makes sense.*

*One more question: How do you acquire customers? You talked about how COVID dropped a lot of large enterprises in your lap. I could see you starting out like a lot of backend companies — they start with startups because it’s just easier, and then if the startup grows big, then now they have big customers, whereas going into large companies is very hard because they’re already with SAP or they’re already with Workday or whatever it might be, but you ended up where you actually had big companies coming to you out of desperation. How has your customer acquisition approach shifted, if it has at all? Are you more of a big company player now on accident and if startups want to join up they can, how do you think about that broadly?*

*AB:* I think we’re very lucky in a way where most companies in our ecosystem, they usually come with a solution and they’re like, “Well, if you want to use us, you got to rip up everything you have and then put us in place” for HR, payroll, et cetera. With Deel, it’s kind of like that core bundling and unbundling experience. You can come to us just to run payroll in Kenya for you or you can come to us just to employ that person in Singapore, with one specific visa.

*Which by the way I think makes sense because you are integrated organizationally, you can more easily offer modular interfaces to people on the outside.*

*AB:* Exactly. This whole modular interface is something that makes us really special. You come in just to employ that person in that country and then if we do a good job when you’re like, I can land-and-expand there, “Oh, talk to me about that product, talk to me about that product”. That’s how we’re able to come into the accounts and start really growing, and at the same time we’re able to serve companies who have some large enterprise.

We’re learning, because the company, if you think about it, the product is turning four years old on the 19th of March, a very young company. But we’re starting to have bigger companies that are saying, “Okay, I want the full stack”, so that go-to-market is quite interesting. Like you said, we started with startups, because when we started, I couldn’t exactly say that we had compliance figured out in all those countries or have entities in all those countries, and we grew with our customers.

Today, we have a pretty healthy mix of companies and generally I think we’re able to have a product that’s self-onboarding and self-usable enough that most of the startups are able to get going really fast, you just need to educate them on what happens if you terminate that person in that country and how much is that truly going to cost you. Then some of the bigger enterprises, we truly are able to penetrate with one specific module and be true partners to them in terms of how do they think through the next stage of evolution of the company.

Most of the companies we’re talking to right now and the conversation always evolves. CHROs, CPOs, they always go through different phases. And right now, most of the big companies are talking to us thinking, “Okay, I need to cut cost. I’m going to double down. I don’t want to have a super distributed workforce. I want people to be more in one or two offices, help me take those three countries, four countries to open up big offices into and hire aggressively into and help me run payroll there.” Or “Let me try it out with a couple people here and there and see how it goes”. So those conversations are happening at the enterprise level, I still think there’s a huge gap in that knowledge where most people leaders haven’t fully figured out global. And even if they have, it’s usually very disconnected with local HR in every country and the global head of HR and has some form of visibility, but not enough visibility to truly own it.

*Do you have a big sales force or is this mostly a “They find you for one specific thing” and it’s a land-and-expand model?*

*AB:* We have a pretty good go-to-market team to be completely honest. We have a pretty big sales force, I think about five hundred, six hundred plus people. I’ll say, it’s a pretty even split between inbound and outbound as well. But yeah, definitely go-to-market has been one of the strengths on that front for sure. The thing is our ACV [annual contract value] is really high if you think about it.

*Very good. I find your company really fascinating, because number one, it slots into a sort of broad-based model of companies that can be built these days, which is you’re like an API for worldwide employment. You plug in on both sides, so that’s compelling. The second thing though is the broad-based question of what’s happening to the white collar workforce broadly and the real benefits that are going to accrue to your classmate in Croatia and the risks that perhaps will accrue to your classmates in the Valley and there’s lots of interesting angles to think about in that regard. You actually reached out to me and I was immediately hooked and compelled and I’m glad we could get a chance to talk about it.*

*AB:* Yeah, we’re very excited about what the future holds on that front. I think like you said, the world is becoming more borderless, it’s a very timely product and I think global hybrid, how you’re going to manage your workforce is in the mind of every CEO and HR leader. Like you said, there’s a decent product-founder fit, I like to think and as we build this out, we’ll have some very interesting insights on how the word will kind of shape up and with the new product, with the HR infrastructure coming up, we are actually going to have full visibility on the full workforce of our customers. So, this is going to give us an even better angle in terms of understanding how they’re thinking about managing their global workforce. Very excited about this too and thank you for answering. Very happy to speak with you.

*AB:* Thank you so much.
