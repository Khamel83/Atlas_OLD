Good morning,

Today’s Stratechery Interview is with Joanna Stern ( https://twitter.com/JoannaStern ). Stern is the Senior Personal Technology Columnist at the Wall Street Journal ( https://www.wsj.com/news/author/joanna-stern ) , but “columnist” sells her short: Stern not only writes, but also makes what are, in my opinion, the best videos in tech journalism. They are funny yet informative, with a resolutely consumer-centric approach pioneered by Walt Mossberg, Stern’s predecessor.

In this interview we talked about Stern’s background and both how she arrived in this role and found her voice. We also dive into the nuts and bolts of making a video like her review of the Apple Vision Pro ( https://www.wsj.com/video/series/joanna-stern-personal-technology/vision-pro-review-24-hours-in-apples-mixed-reality-headset/05CD2E77-897D-49A9-A87E-9B8A93E3E45F ) (and where writing the accompanying column ( https://www.wsj.com/tech/apple-vision-pro-review-39f2d82e ) fits in her workflow). I do suggest watching this video if you haven’t: we reference it throughout the interview, including where we discuss the Vision Pro generally. We also discuss Stern’s investigation into iPhone passcode theft ( https://www.wsj.com/tech/personal-tech/apple-iphone-security-theft-passcode-data-privacya-basic-iphone-feature-helps-criminals-steal-your-digital-life-cbf14b1a ) , her foray into documentaries ( https://www.wsj.com/video/series/eternal ) , and what it takes to succeed as a content creator on the Internet.

Background

*Joanna Stern, welcome to Stratechery.*

So there’s no Persona ( https://x.com/JoannaStern/status/1752333973823328380?s=20 ) , there’s no goggles ( https://x.com/rjonesy/status/1752443941671121397?s=20 ) , it’s just you here in person.*

*JS:* I mean, I can put it on and wear it the whole time. I was on CNBC ( https://x.com/SquawkStreet/status/1752367415239860699?s=20 ) right after the review dropped today ( https://www.wsj.com/tech/apple-vision-pro-review-39f2d82e ). It was about a six minute, five minute segment, and I wore it the whole time on live TV, and now I appear to be going viral ( https://x.com/JoannaStern/status/1752484664655122880?s=20 ) because everyone’s just dying at me talking to these two great anchors, but I’m wearing this headset. I did think in the middle, “Should I take it off?” But then I realized, “I’m on live TV and my hair is going to be a mess if I do this. I’m just going to stick with it.” And I mean, I obviously could see everything on set really well, so why would I take it off?

*Yeah, I mean that’s the thing. I’m actually curious about that because when you’re wearing it, you can obviously see out, but it does feel like — I’m glad you’re going viral because it feels like a perfect Joanna Stern moment where, look, you’re going to carry the bit to the end and it’s going to be super funny and we are all the beneficiaries of that.*

*JS:* Yeah. People are like, “Oh, I can’t believe you did it.” I’m like, “What else would I have done? I was there to show the headset, why wouldn’t I wear this on live TV the whole time?” But yeah, I did wear it. Look, for 24 hours ( https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8xI10SFgzQ8&t=3s ) , I challenged myself to wear it as long as I could. I went numerous stretches, like six hours long, so wearing it to me is no big deal.

I also had done this in the past, I did it with a Meta Quest 2 ( https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rtLTZUaMSDQ ) , maybe two years ago I did this video “24 Hours in the Metaverse”, it was when everyone was freaking out about the Metaverse, “What’s the Metaverse?”, and so I kind of just do this sometimes to me and we don’t know yet the lasting damage of it to my brain and my eyes and probably my psychology, but I think it’s the best way to test. Just go full in, don’t leave and figure out everything you can while you’ve got this thing on.

*It reminds me of that New Yorker cartoon ( https://www.newyorker.com/cartoon/a16995 ) where they’re sitting around a fire, it’s like back to the Stone Age, and he’s like, “At least we created a lot of shareholder value along the way.” In your case, “At least I went viral along the way”.*

*JS:* (laughing) Yeah. Except the 24 hour video isn’t going viral, just me sitting on CNBC set for seven minutes is.

*(laughing) You never know what’s going to hit!*

*JS:* I know.

*Well, you are famous now, I mean, speaking of TV and not just a columnist but also a video star, all of which I sort of want to get to in a moment. But before we start there, I always like to start out hearing about people’s background and how did you get to where you are? How’d you get interested in technology? What was your path to being one of the preeminent tech columnists in the world?*

*JS:* I guess it started with Windows 95. My dad bought us a Windows 95, you don’t want me to go that far back, I guess.

*I do. No, actually, I find that the most interesting.*

*JS:* I think that is kind of where it started. Look, I won’t say I grew up reading computer magazines because I didn’t really. I definitely grew up reading tech sites probably more in my college years. But yeah, since a young age, I’ve loved technology.

*Was it just the excitement around that or you suddenly wanted to use the computer? What was it about that particular launch?*

*JS:* We had a family desktop PC. This I think always shocks people because I was a Windows user for probably first twenty years of my life. I was a predominantly Windows user through end of college, and just that was the world I knew.

I think maybe just in terms of growing up and being surrounded by tech, I was always very interested about in devices and the ways that I could use them in my life or help people use them. So for instance, I think when it was one of the first GPS units came out and my dad was like, “Well, should we get one of these?” And I was like, “We should definitely get one of these.” We put it in the car and we were downloading maps and things like that.

So I think those sorts of experiences for me were, for certainly early in my life, just thought, “Wow, I just really like tech.” I don’t really have a moment where I was like, “I want to do something in tech.” I just always remember loving tech.

*Well, I think that angle’s pretty interesting. You said you weren’t reading computer magazines. You weren’t — the implication of that — into sort of like a “feeds and speeds” aspect, it was, “How can it actually be used in the real world?”.*

*JS:* Definitely. I was one of the first to have an MP3 player, I had a Rio ( https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rio_Audio ). Then I was one of the first to have — I still have it here — I think it was an Olympus, I had too many disc players, I had a Sony and I believe in an Olympus, and I was obsessed with these mini disc players. Recording music, recording through mics, figuring out how to get the music onto these discs. So yeah, I was always just playing around with stuff and trying to buy stuff and save money to buy stuff.

*So you were writing for, I think, for Computer Laptop Review or something along those lines ( https://www.laptopmag.com/ ). How did you start writing online?*

*JS:* That’s right, that was your question. It wasn’t like, “Where in my childhood, did I start playing with technology?”.

*No, I think that’s the part that’s interesting, I’m going to come back to it. I already have it sort of teed up for a follow-up, so stay tuned.*

*JS:* Yeah, my family business is public relations, PR. My mom had a PR agency, still does, still a thriving business, she’s retired. But I always thought I’m going to go into PR, just kind of figured that. So I was interested in journalism of course and writing articles and writing press releases, but I just sort of thought, “This is the world I’m going to go.” Then in college I got really into the student newspaper. I went to Union College, which is a small school in upstate New York and got really into the school paper, got really into journalism but figured, “Okay, my jobs out of college are going to be in PR.”

And I did, I got hired at a PR agency at a college and I was put on the Skype account. On that account, and I had already read tech sites, I was following tech, so they thought I was just a huge star on this PR team, like, “Oh, she really gets tech.” So I realized very quickly working at this PR agency and trying to get people to write about Skype that I was going to be probably better at writing about this stuff than trying to get other people to write about it. So I went out and tried to get a job in tech journalism and I applied to a bunch of places and ended up getting a job at Laptop Magazine, that was really the start of it.

I reviewed I think hundreds of laptops there over two years, two and a half years just trying to learn as much as I could about — I mean, I knew about how computers worked, processors, RAM, hard drives, stuff like that, but I wasn’t really deep into it. I didn’t really know the differences between different cores and CPU speeds. So I learned so much on that job of just about what are the parts and the components of the computers and how they were getting better and then I just kept going and got hired at Engadget ( https://www.engadget.com/about/editors/joanna-stern/ ) to keep reviewing laptops. Netbooks were the big thing, I was known as this Netbook Queen because I was obsessed with these small mobile laptops because I thought, “This is the coolest thing. You can work anywhere and have a keyboard and do all the things, but you don’t need a big chunky laptop.” And after that, went to go start The Verge ( https://www.theverge.com/authors/joanna-stern ).

*Well I want to get to that, but it is sort of interesting, I think you did this all backwards. I believe that the usual career path is people want to be a journalist and then they work in tech journalism and then decide they can make much more money and have much more secure prospects in PR. So it’s funny, it’s like the videos where you’re rewinding it and going backwards, that was sort of your career path.*

*JS:* Absolutely, totally. I think people were like, “Journalism? You’ve got this family business, you can go take that over at some point. Seems like it’s doing very well.” And I was like, “Yeah, but I could write. It’d be so creative. I could be on the cutting edge of all of this stuff.”

*Here you are.*

*JS:* I guess I got lucky because I know it doesn’t work out for everybody.

The Verge, Video, and the Wall Street Journal

*Well, I’m curious, at that time you were at Engadget and then you were with the crew with Joshua Topolsky ( https://twitter.com/joshuatopolsky ) and Nilay Patel ( https://twitter.com/reckless ) that left. You had the intervening site, This Is My Next ( https://web.archive.org/web/20110506035401/thisismynext.com/about ) , I think that started right about when I started Stratechery, it was certainly in the same timeframe, and then that became The Verge. What was sort of the feeling then amongst that group, like as far as publishing on the web? You mentioned the, “Is Journalism secure?”, was that very much the feeling then? Or were you guys pretty confident that, “Look, no, we have a winning formula here”?*

*JS:* I don’t feel like we were confident. When I think back on that time, we worked so hard. I think one of the best things about starting that site was just being around really smart, fun people who just work their ass off, and that’s me. I love to work because I love what I do and that was just the feeling of all of us when we were trying to build this thing.

It definitely didn’t feel certain because we were leaving Engadget, which was owned by AOL — lord knows who owns all of that now, seriously, I can’t even remember. That was a giant. We had tons of resources there, but yet we couldn’t do certain things, I wasn’t even a full-time employee and that was part of one of the issues we had, was like, “Why can’t we hire people as full-time but we had all this other capital at Engadget to do things?” So I was breaking out and going to do this other thing even though it was, it wasn’t even Vox Media at the time, what didn’t feel certain. We didn’t know if what we were going to build was going to work out.

You have a little bit of confidence just working with really smart people who have done really good things. And look, I was very involved in the design of the reviews team working with Nilay, Josh, Paul [Miller] ( https://twitter.com/futurepaul ) at the time, then we brought on Dieter [Bohn] ( https://twitter.com/backlon ). We were all trying to think, “Well, we don’t want to do the same thing as CNET, we don’t want to do the same thing as Wired, is there room for something else?”, and we were trying to figure that out too. Now looking back on it more than ten years, it’s like we changed a lot about how reviews are done on the Internet.

*Was there a niche that you think you identified that The Verge filled? Or is it just the case that you just listed like a murderer’s row of tech reviewers and journalists and analysts? Is it that you just had the best people?*

*JS:* I think part of it was that we had the best people and we had a lot of knowledge and we were still young in this industry. I mean, tech blogs had been around, Engadget, Gizmodo. Look, everyone I think has their different views of history. It’s funny because I remember actually once coming to Josh and being like, “I had a dream last night that we made a tech magazine. We printed out the magazine and we were shipping the magazine and a monthly magazine.” Because I had worked at Laptop, which was a magazine and he is like, “Well, we’re never going to do that, but this is a version of a magazine for the Internet.”

We had a lot of different thoughts and ideas about that, there were these beautiful features, there was the reviews section, and it really was a melding of just smart people who were just so excited about what are the ways we can tell tech stories in new ways, but also keeping true to some of the history of tech sites. I think that’s one of the things that makes The Verge still work really well, is there’s an enthusiasm about tech and there’s enthusiasm about the history of tech and where it’s been and where it’s going. So yeah, I think we all just came at it that way.

*I think you actually started doing some TV around this time with ABC. How did you know you would be so good on camera? Or was that an accidental discovery as well?*

*JS:* Well, it’s funny, and I tell Nilay this all the time. My biggest regret at that time was like, “Why didn’t I just break off and go do YouTube?” I would’ve been the next big YouTube star. And if I had done that—

*Yeah, MKBHD better watch out!*

*JS:* Right. But if I had done that 15 years ago, where would I be now? I went the route of actually more at that time like, “I want to go to look at traditional broadcast journalism.” I don’t know why I thought that, I just thought, “Well, video is doing really well on the Internet”, but also I had this kind of love still for traditional media and mainstream audiences. So I just decided, “Okay, let me do some more video.”

Now we were doing amazing video and The Verge still does amazing video there, I actually know that The Verge really pioneered just the idea that tech videos and tech review videos could look beautiful. I will remember we shot one of the first videos with — I don’t remember the name of everyone on the team, but we shot and it was an ASUS Eee PC. It was my review and I was like, “This is such a cool ASUS Eee PC”, and I was like, “Okay, Joanna.” But this video team was great and we shot these beautiful shots. It was the Eee PC Transformer and the screen came off. You remember that?

*Yeah, I remember that. I was at Microsoft at this time. I think I might’ve had that, or I had one ASUS that was like this when Windows 8 came out and there was all these wild designs out there. Yeah, I actually totally forgot about that.*

*JS:* Yeah, and we shot this beautiful review of it, it had motion graphics and we hadn’t seen this before. This was not what videos of tech products looked like on the Internet. So, I was very interested in that, but I was also interested in broadcast and just thought, “Okay, I’m going to go try to do some more video on my own and find my video voice.” So yeah, I went to ABC News and learned a lot about video, and voiceovers, and all that kind of stuff.

*Well, so the story of your going to the Wall Street Journal was kind of weird at the time. As I recall, they had hired Farhad Manjoo ( https://twitter.com/fmanjoo ) to work, he was there for two weeks and then he’s like, “My dream has always been to work for the New York Times, so I’m moving across town.” And then, they hired you and I have to say it’s a marvelous fit. You’re very much the heir to Walt Mossberg ( https://stratechery.com/2017/the-walt-mossberg-brand/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDE3L3RoZS13YWx0LW1vc3NiZXJnLWJyYW5kLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzA5Mzc3NzcxLCJpYXQiOjE3MDY3ODU3NzEsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBpLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiUnNldVlFOGpnSGVQcFdKZjFmQWI0QyIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.vp5TfdMkcG-eF72u_JuXIaGLlYfRaRZPdz58yNk6ywlTA4I43Hhl_arNcmp3bCEUvYHuN793JEhZDEjvjaHY6BReo7r0GccMaHHfivCOQbcHcI476UW_BOuYPoLVsvyyfD7wTgMrbCA1XTo1WcE2_Pn4yOO0JaMKnK1B0bkXhzo0bA--4G71TbwLNoPEsDxey5hNaB737GWWi8b2kKeBqOFRTgsRK4vSoGLVYjN-iVDWd-SJWX5jx5zLli4ZQPrWO_nInNcmeAi0kYrjPJhAFDdOs_KsLw6HoiLyT3Y9m5S_2siE_-WMkpDZeVqty5fUt3SvpRexffkIndxxtAi20g ) and his quest to explain technology to normal people. I had written down, “Was this an explicit goal and something you model yourself after, or just the way you’ve always tried to approach things?”, and I think you already answered it. It’s just the way you’ve always wanted to approach tech. Was that something that was in your mind when you took this role, like, “This is a position that does what I’ve always done”?*

*JS:* Yes. I wouldn’t say I grew up reading Walt, I probably started reading some of his columns in college as I started getting more into new technology and looking at the media world. But to me, that was just like, “Wow”. It was the thing. He says what he thinks about this product and it matters, it matters what he says, it influences what people buy, it influences what the companies do, it has an impact. And to me, that was always just like, “Wow”.

I went to some of the first D Conferences too, and it was like, “Wow, these are impartial people talking to these people in the tech industry, asking tough questions.” He was doing something obviously different in his day job when he is writing the columns. But to me, it was just like you get to — and look, there’s the simple thing of too, it’s like you get to play with all this cool tech and then just write about it and people might read it.

*Right, people care.*

*JS:* That was the simplest basic thing, that’s really cool. And that’s a job? That’s a full-time job that will pay? I don’t know. Who wouldn’t want that job?

Web Versus Print

*I mean, one thing I was thinking about while reading your review ( https://www.wsj.com/tech/apple-vision-pro-review-39f2d82e ) , and this ties back to the Mossberg point, is even the article on the web was clearly crafted for the web. In a way, I think you articulated very well why The Verge was important, it was a publication built for the web, and in a way that the first generation always sort of apes print, but it can be something different. There’s all these visuals and cool web effects as you’re scrolling and yet, your review, I presume, is going to be in tomorrow’s Wall Street Journal that is going to be in print, on paper. Do you ever think about paper at all anymore or is that something that, “Oh, yeah, I guess it’s going to end up there at some point”?*

*JS:* Actually, it’s really funny that you asked me that today because, look, the time and effort that goes into making these reviews, I don’t know how much people know behind the scenes, but—

*Oh, we’re going to ask. Don’t worry.*

*JS:* So much. So, so much. And at some point today, what we call a read back, a read back is just like the print version of the story. The print read back came and they murdered it because they’ve got to cut it, they got to fit it into a space. I just said to the editor, “I trust you. Fine, look at it, whatever.” I said, “Make sure the QR code is there for my video review.” And he’s like, “It’ll be there.”

That said, tomorrow morning I will wake up, and this is a really interesting thing, I will wake up to a series of emails from people who read the column in print and those questions and those responses will be very thoughtful. They will be very clearly from a different type of reader, I’m not going to say all older, but many of them older. *JS:* I will say it is not the easiest to do it at the Wall Street Journal. There’s a lot of things that go on behind the scenes like describing that skiing situation, right? I’ve got to get permission, I’ve got to do things above board as everyone would, but there’s a certain level of rigor that goes into that that goes into the Wall Street Journal. But yeah, I think that’s my fear is getting older, just not being afraid to try new things and not being able to, just not getting stuck in doing the same formulaic type of thing.

*Well, you’re definitely succeeding in that regard.*

*JS:* Okay, good.

*The reviews are an obvious thing, but you had an interesting set of stories and videos last year about how people will steal iPhone passcodes ( https://www.wsj.com/tech/personal-tech/apple-iphone-security-theft-passcode-data-privacya-basic-iphone-feature-helps-criminals-steal-your-digital-life-cbf14b1a ) to get access to their accounts. I’m curious how that story came about and how’d you think about approaching a genuinely news breaking or investigative type story as opposed to the review angle that you’re most well known for?*

*JS:* Yeah, I actually had written this down to chat about, because I think when you asked about how I have come to differentiate myself from Walt and where we were 10, 15 years ago. I stuck to a lot of the principles that Walt stuck to, but I added in some things. Humor was definitely one of them, video being another and it’s not like I added them in, it was just natural for me. This is how I felt like I think about technology and storytelling, and maybe some other people will want it too, maybe there’s room for this.

One of the ways I also just started to push myself in new directions in the last couple of years were with some investigations, and there’s still a thread that comes through it all though, which is really like I am here on the side of the consumer. I am here to help people understand technology, help them decide what they should buy, but also here to raise the flags when companies aren’t doing things or may not be aware of things.

As that evolved, I, over the years, have gotten lots of reader questions, so many reader questions and this is a really nice thing. I also inherited from Walt, just a really active Wall Street Journal community and base of readers just send a lot of questions. Really, a lot of is, “I won’t buy.” A lot of it is, “What printer should I buy?”, or, “You recommended this printer and it sucks”, and a lot of that stuff.

*You hear from the haters as well.*

*JS:* Yeah. With these investigations I’ve picked up in the last couple of years, the biggest one stem — some of them are not investigations, they’re just more serious type stuff — they stem typically from reader questions or issues, and people who come to me with just kind of crazy things. I did a documentary a few years ago on death and technology ( https://www.wsj.com/video/series/eternal ).

*I want to ask you about that, so hold that one.*

*JS:* Yeah, but that came from a readers and readers writing in about that question, and this investigative series also came from readers writing in. I started getting emails — well, a friend of mine knew somebody else who called me to tell me about this story, about how his phone had gotten stolen in New York. He wakes up the next morning, phone is stolen, he wakes up with a dummy phone next to him, he believes he’s drugged. He doesn’t know what happened, he goes to the cops. Then the next thing he notices is that he’s got tons of money drained from his accounts and it was like, “Okay, I can’t get back into his Apple account”.

So he started telling me the story, “Okay, that’s definitely crazy. Let me know if you hear more.” Then I got an email a few months later from another person who said, “I was in New York City, New Year’s Eve. A woman comes up to me, starts talking to me, then the next thing I noticed, she’s run off with my phone. I wake up the next morning, I’m locked out of my Apple account, thousands of dollars stolen, I’ve got an Apple Card opened in my name, I don’t know how this person did this.”

It’s like, that’s interesting, I’ve heard two of these stories now happen in New York, and started digging into it and it turns out it’s happening across the country to, I believe thousands and thousands and thousands of people. Probably we’re going to get into hundreds of thousands soon, maybe more. It’s already happening and as we described in these investigations, in these stories, these criminals and thieves have just figured out that if they steal an iPhone and a passcode, well, then they can unlock everything.

*Everything.*

*JS:* And it was fascinating to report because the way I got into this was talking to the victims and so one victim would go to another.

*You get another side of both sort of your audience and you get another side of the companies, like Apple in this case.*

*JS:* And I did not know it was going to lead back to where it led, right? We sort of thought at first — just we didn’t know, we just didn’t know. In fact, we only start when one victim agreed to talk to another victim, and then finally one victim said, “I really think this person stole my passcode.” And I was like, “Oh”, and then we started testing. We were like, well, the passcode does do all of that.

*Yeah.*

*JS:* Then that led to looking at some court documents of people who had been tried for this and some of the criminal records of people and talking to law enforcement, all stuff that I had never really — I had never done some of that kind of reporting, and I loved it. There was the running joke that I was going to leave tech journalism this year and become a crime reporter.

*It’s the whole video running backwards, now you have the investigative journalist bug.*

*JS:* I totally do. I am a crime reporter. I know how to do it all.

Longform and the Web

*Well, you mentioned this death and technology video ( https://www.wsj.com/video/series/eternal ) that you did. There’s a long form video, Emmy Award-winning video about how people might preserve their digital selves. It’s tremendously moving, particularly at the end — I feel emotional right now — when you’re playing the voice of the young woman in the UK that was at the center of it. Is this something you also want to do more of? I guess which bug is stronger, this documentary making versus the crime reporter making?*

*JS:* Well, it’s funny, I actually think that the crime investigating piece could have been a really good documentary if we had spent time and didn’t feel the pressure to keep publishing on it.

*Right. That’s the challenge.*

*JS:* Because over the year, we did a good series of pieces of talking to the victims and then finally at the end of the year, I finally got into one of these prisons to talk to somebody who had been committing these crimes, and so that piece was just like, “Wow, if we had had this six months ago, we would’ve put this together into this crazy package of hearing from the victims and hearing from him”.

*But you probably wouldn’t have gotten that content had you not been publishing all along.*

*JS:* Probably not. Yeah, exactly. So for me, I don’t know, the documentary took a lot out of me. I’m also the kind of person that loves to keep publishing, and this is one of the things about loving what I do, but I try to be working on a bigger project, but also doing these smaller projects, because if I’m not publishing, I just feel like — I always feel like there’s this, I think it’s maybe my quote, somebody else, but, “You’re only as good as your last piece”, and people forget.

*Yeah, I think that’s part of being on the Internet. The Internet is you’re only as good as what you just wrote and your archive is not valuable. It’s content marketing for the promise of what you’ll write in the future.*

*JS:* You just said it so much better, I’m writing that down, can I have that on a quote or something?
