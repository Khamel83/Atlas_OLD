AI at CES
---------

From the Las Vegas Sun ( https://lasvegassun.com/news/2024/jan/09/once-again-ces-will-transform-las-vegas-into-the-e/ ) :

Hundreds of thousands of people will descend on Las Vegas this week for CES, the annual electronics show bringing together industry leaders, government officials, celebrities and a slew of exhibitors to show off what’s new in the world of technology. Running today through Friday, the show which boasts more than 2.5 million square feet of exhibit space, is expected to bring more than 130,000 people to town, said Melissa Harrison, vice president of marketing and communications at the Consumer Technology Association, which owns and produces the annual event. “This is where business gets done,” Harrison said. “You’re going to see the greatest minds, the most powerful brands and most impactful technology.”

I used to write about CES every year, but my last mention of the famous consumer electronics show was in 2020, where I admitted that the event everyone claims to hate but secretly loves had gotten pretty boring:

Bohn chalks the prevalence of foldable screen concepts, and lack of products, up to a lack of confidence, both in foldable technology itself, and also in what use cases it might have; John Gruber argues ( https://daringfireball.net/2020/01/concept_electronics_show ) that companies at CES don’t know how to design products.

Or, perhaps, there isn’t an obvious place for these companies to go. Again, I already cited the risk of confirmation bias, but once you have a screen with you everywhere, slightly different screens, or, in the case of wearables or voice control, no screens, is at best evolutionary, and likely augmentative. Sure, something may materialize out of thin air, but even back in the 80s and 90s it was clear that both the Internet and mobile were going to be something big, if we could figure out how to get there. The figuring out how to get there still exists, but what is the there?

The answer four years later is painfully obvious: AI! From Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-01-08/ai-pc-and-other-ai-gadgets-to-take-over-ces-2024-in-las-vegas ) :

Even before CES began, the theme of the massive, annual electronics festival in Las Vegas had long been decided: artificial intelligence gadgets. With so much hype and money swirling around ChatGPT and its counterparts, it was inevitable that practically every device maker would jump on the AI bandwagon.

As much as CES serves as a hub for the products of tomorrow, it also typically doubles as a preview of how tech giants and startups will market their wares in the coming year. And if early announcements are any indication, AI-branded products will become the new “smart” gadgets of 2024.

In the lead up to the event, LG Electronics Inc. touted AI television sets. Samsung Electronics Co. will show off a vacuum bot that uses AI to detect stains and distinguish between hard floors and carpets. (The company is expected to separately launch an AI phone later this month). One startup is set to debut an AI system for accelerating the ordering process at drive-thru restaurants. Intel Corp. is simply promising to talk about “AI everywhere.”

The reality, of course, is that these are not simply the new “smart” gadgets: they would probably be called “smart” gadgets absent the ChatGPT phenomenon: the “AI” is just things like vision recognition and a machine learning-derived algorithm to determine floor type; there are no large language models in a vacuum cleaner. To put it another way, perhaps the biggest impact of ChatGPT on CES was that it stopped the goalpost-moving in terms of the word “AI”: for years and years AI was the future, machine learning was the present, and mere algorithms were the past, irrespective of the actual capabilities being described. Now, though, AI is here — in fact, it’s everywhere, no matter the underlying technology.

The Rabbit R1
-------------

That noted, the eye-catching gadget of the day was very much about AI, in the ChatGPT vein. From TechCrunch ( https://techcrunch.com/2024/01/09/can-a-striking-design-set-rabbits-r1-pocket-ai-apart-from-a-gaggle-of-virtual-assistants/ ) :

In a sea of AI-enabled gadgets at CES, the rabbit r1 (all lowercase, they insist) stands out not just for its high-vis paint job and unique form factor, but because of its dedication to the bit. The company is hoping you’ll carry a second device around to save yourself the trouble of opening your phone — and has gone to extraordinary technical lengths to make it work.

The idea behind the $200 r1 is simple: It lets you keep your phone in your pocket when you need to do some simple task like ordering a car to your location, looking up a few places to eat where you’re meeting friends or finding some lodging options for a weekend on the coast.

“We’re not trying to kill your phone,” said CEO and founder Jesse Lyu on a call with press ahead of the Las Vegas tech show. “The phone is an entertainment device, but if you’re trying to get something done it’s not the highest efficiency machine. To arrange dinner with a colleague we needed four-five different apps to work together. Large language models are a universal solution for natural language, we want a universal solution for these services — they should just be able to understand you.”

Instead of pulling out your phone, unlocking it, finding the app, opening it and working your way through the UI (so laborious!), you pull out the r1 instead and give it a command in natural language…The r1 does as you bid it and a few seconds later provides confirmation and any content you might have requested.

First off, the necessary caveat for the haters in the audience: yes, this device will almost certainly fail. Indeed, the business model — a $200 device with no subscription fee, despite the fact all of the actual computing happens in the cloud — feels designed to fail. It’s a pretty big red flag, particularly for a device that will depend on your giving user data to their cloud.

Moreover, the keynote ( https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=22wlLy7hKP4&t=3s ) — which was clearly trying to ape an Apple keynote, both in visual appearance and also structure (show the problem, present a solution, do some demos) — was playing an awful lot of tricks in terms of what it actually showed on camera. Lyu would make audacious requests like asking Rabbit to book flights and hotels and pronounce the task completed without actually showing that it was completed. Then there is the fact that pre-orders charge your credit card immediately without any assurance as to when your order will ship (Lyu said Easter 2024, but there is no confirmation date on your order).

All that noted, I still found the product very interesting, starting with its appearance:

( https://www.rabbit.tech/ )

The Rabbit R1 was made in collaboration with Teenage Engineering, and looks a lot like a Playdate ( https://play.date/ ). More importantly, it looks fun , in a way other AI hardware like the Humane AI Pin ( https://hu.ma.ne/ ) do not. The biggest difference, though, is the intentionality of the design: you interact with the Rabbit R1 by pressing the button on the side like a walkie-talkie, and the AI responds to you both verbally and on the LCD screen, which is touch-sensitive. It seems both more natural and foolproof than speaking to an ambient device like a pin or pendant or even glasses, even if there is obvious friction in pulling it out of your pocket.

I do, given the current state of AI, see friction as a positive in this case, but that mention is a bit of a problem for the Rabbit R1 value proposition, which is about removing it: the problem Lyu claims Rabbit is solving is the trouble of navigating multiple apps on your phone to get a job done, instead of just saying what you want. And, needless to say, this is another reason the product will fail: it’s really not that hard to call an Uber on your phone.

The part that is interesting to me, though, is the promised speed of interaction with AI. It was speed — or rather the lack of it — and friction — the abundance of it — that frustrated me when using ChatGPT’s voice mode. From AI, Hardware, and Virtual Reality ( https://stratechery.com/2023/ai-hardware-and-virtual-reality/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIzL2FpLWhhcmR3YXJlLWFuZC12aXJ0dWFsLXJlYWxpdHkvIl19LCJleHAiOjE3MDc0ODY4NDEsImlhdCI6MTcwNDg5NDg0MSwiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcGkucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCBlbnRpdGxlbWVudHMiLCJzdWIiOiJSc2V1WUU4amdIZVBwV0pmMWZBYjRDIiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.TcdajCPdA3FnGzPs0yrRwmKoeZ98vFymOxdR9pC9WzBxvk2sPVX8f-RlvUL5Al5l0IkrI2t-Eu2NKvSMiYLGNNmo4_I0IvFze7lM_STWfn2rzY8OebW3_b03S6knBPNVT_RbCa5TPXr8g-Qp7_KUDc8O-xjfkotGqfjsz3-cfSP3nukoHYddTg8VWXv9DF2mmOTCo_Kco72abbj00M6XkgFDNvlHOXiqtEl4SXvWDXBAJtqI1Kl--GiI3kfKjdKLMy2nfyZHTo-VPAQJ6rYy0ombCMbi8lxQjxWj46Wykt0XVs3SeGGEYbACZun0oHyDfM2-pm3jWs3i1RffFWlxqg ) :

AI is truly something new and revolutionary and capable of being something more than just a homework aid, but I don’t think the existing interfaces are the right ones. Talking to ChatGPT is better than typing, but I still have to launch the app and set the mode; vision is an amazing capability, but it requires even more intent and friction to invoke.

What the Rabbit R1 seeks to do is lower the invocation costs, and I think that is a worthy goal.

The device, though, is only part of the value proposition: I mentioned that all of the actual compute is happening on the cloud, in this case via agents. The idea is that Rabbit has created what they call a “Large Action Model” which is trained on graphical user interfaces to understand their different components, what buttons do, how content is organized on a website, etc. What that means is that when a user makes a request — “Play Taylor Swift” — the cloud application actually navigates the Spotify interface (assuming you have logged in via OAuth on your “Rabbit Hole” website) and plays Taylor Swift.

Agent capability and sustainability are, needless to say, an open question, particularly in these early days. What is so enticing about the approach, though, is that it provides the promise of massive compatibility without having to actual recruit developers to your platform. Indeed, this might be the business case for the hardware: it’s the most interesting and eye-catching way for Rabbit to show off their “large action model”.

This part of the experience could, of course, be done in an app. Again, though, why bother going to the Rabbit app when you can just go to Spotify directly? In this I think that the hardware device, even if it is just ultimately a demo you can pay for, matters: for an agent approach to be better it has to be seamless, and given the locked-down state of phones it is impossible to build a seamless experience without hardware. To that end, while Rabbit may not end up winning in this space, I do think it is a step in a promising direction, and I applaud the effort (and yes, I’m ready for a Siri that does all of this on device; maybe Lyu was pitching Tim Cook directly).
