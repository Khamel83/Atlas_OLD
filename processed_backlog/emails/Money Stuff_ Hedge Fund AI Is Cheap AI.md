DeepSeek

The big news today is that DeepSeek blew up the stock market <>:

Chinese artificial intelligence startup DeepSeek’s latest AI model sparked a
$1 trillion rout in US and European technology stocks, as investors questioned
bloated valuations for some of America’s biggest companies.

DeepSeek’s latest AI model rose to the top of the Apple’s appstore charts over
the weekend, presenting a visible challenge to costlier models like OpenAI and
raising questions over the hundreds of billions in planned spending on the
technology by the likes of Microsoft Corp., Meta Platforms Inc. and Alphabet
Inc.

DeepSeek’s model is open-source and apparently cheap, and it would honestly be
pretty amazing if artificial general intelligence came in that package. (No
thanks to OpenAI’sformer nonprofit work <> building artificial general
intelligence for the benefit of humanity!) The ideal case for AI would be
something like “everyone has a magic lamp in their pocket with a genie that can
grant unlimited wishes; the lamp is cheap and each wish is free.” That case
would be extremely good for human flourishing (maybe? [1]  <>), but terrible
for stock market valuations. What do you needNvidia chips <> for, if the genie
is free? What do you need your 401(k) for, if the genie is free? Sort of a good
news, bad news situation.

Anyway here are more informed takes from Ben Thompson <>, from sell-side
analysts collected atFT Alphaville <>, and from my Bloomberg colleagues Parmy
Olson <>,  Jonathan Levin <> and Joe Weisenthal <>.

One thing about DeepSeek is that it was built by a quantitative hedge fund.
DeepSeek founder Liang Wenfengbuilt AI models to pick stocks <>, and as sort of
a byproduct he pivoted to building a good general AI model. Weisenthal writes:

I’m really not a fan of all the ‘just so’ stories that people are telling
about how capitalism and markets work in China and the US. The public narrative
is that DeepSeek was created as a side project of a Chinese quant firm. And the
allure of this story is that the Chinese system doesn’t reward financialization
as much, so the creators had to build something substantive. Maybe that story
is even true, but I think people should be on guard for narratives that are too
satisfying.

See I actually think of DeepSeek as a victory for financialization? The way I
would tell this story is that the modern world has created conditions in which
it is incredibly lucrative to get very good at statistical inference. It turns
out that there is a sort of general skill like “program a computer to take a
huge pile of analogous data and predict the most likely next _______,” where
the blank can be filled in with “word in the sentence” or “pixel in the image”
or “stock that will go up.” And people and companies with this general skill
can move between various applications, using similar techniques to pick words
or images or stock picks.

Eventually a lot of them specialize, but the general background condition of
“a lot of people want to learn statistical techniques to get rich” creates a
deep pool of talent for all of the applications. If you ask some 18-year-old
Stanford student today why she wants to study machine learning, she will
probably talk about large language models, because that’s where the money is,
but if you asked a similar student a similar question 10 years ago, she might
have talked about quant investing, because that’s where the money was. And so
now there are lots of 28-year-olds who have worked in machine learning for a
decade. When we first discussed DeepSeek, last June,I wrote <>:

An important story in the development of modern finance was “if you are good
at building computers that can process natural language, you can pivot to
building computers that can pick which stocks will go up, and get much richer.”
This is essentially the story of Renaissance Technologies, whose central
innovation was tohire computational linguists <>, because there was not much
money in building computers that could talk and lots of money in building
computers that could pick stocks.

But that is an old story, and now everyone has a computer that can pick
stocks, while there isinfinity money <> in building a computer that can talk.
So now the people who got good at building computers that can pick stocks are
pivoting to processing natural language <>.

Not everything is like this! A lot of things are — modern AI has many uses —
but nobody learns the essential skills to build fusion reactors or perform
brain surgery at a hedge fund. It is perhaps a fortunate coincidence that maybe
the highest-profile generally useful technology these daysis the sort of thing
that you can learn at a hedge fund.

But maybe not. Maybe that’s just path dependency. Perhaps someone would have
built, like, a Star Trekreplicator <> by now — an even better genie! — if the
smartest technically inclined people had spent the last 30 years going into,
uh, particle physics or whatever that is. But instead the draw was hedge funds
and the output was large language models.

VC AI rollup

What if you take seriously the free-magic-genie model of AI? Or some slightly
less extreme form of it, something like “in one year, all of the work that is
now done by _____ will be done by AI,” filling in the blank with various
currently expensive professions. (Lawyers, investment bankers, accountants,
investment managers, financial newsletter writers.) Whatis the investment case
for that? Maybe you should buy AI model companies or cloud computing companies
or electric utilities, but those ideas reflect the “expensive magic genie”
model, where the rise of AI will lead to demand for AI inputs. If AI is cheap,
that case is less clear.

With a sufficiently general-purpose technology it’s not clear whether the
value will mostly accrue to the builders of that technology or to its users.
But surely it is at least plausible that AI will mostly make itsusers richer,
so the way to bet on AI is mostly to bet on regular, non-AI companies that
don’t use it yet but eventually will. So here’s aventure capital bet on
accounting firms <>:

The prospect of artificial intelligence automating administrative tasks is
attracting venture capitalists to ho-hum professions such as accounting,
customer-service centers and property management. Tech entrepreneurs have
raised hundreds of millions of dollars to retrofit these businesses with AI
tools and drive up their profitability.

The playbook also involves startups adapting one of Wall Street’s favorite
practices: the roll-up. Private-equity firms refined the model of merging
businesses in sectors such as car washes and heating and air conditioning
outfits and using greater scale to cut costs. Much of the capital these new
startups raise is similarly going to fund buyouts of businesses, which are
“rolled up” into one operation. …

Among the flushest new players bringing AI to quiet professions is Long Lake
Management Holdings, a startup founded 13 months ago. Since then, Long Lake has
raised $600 million plus from investors and acquired around a dozen companies
that collectively employ about 1,400 workers, people familiar with the company
said.

Long Lake’s first deals were for businesses that manage the operations of
homeowners associations. Those management companies are now adopting AI tools
fo r tasks like generating presentations for HOA board meetings. It is
targeting an expansion into other industries, including human-resources
services.

Obviously the other trade here is “don’t get an accounting degree,” and in
fact people seem to bedoing that trade <>. And one way for Long Lake’s bet to
be wrong is if it is still too far from the end user. What if the efficiency
gains from using AI in homeowners’ associations are not “homeowners’
association management companies will have higher margins” but rather
“homeowners’ associations won’t hire management companies”? You get elected to
the HOA, you ask DeepSeek “what should I do,” it tells you, you do it, you
never hire a management company.

Also, I have written <> a lot <> about “search funds <>,” which are sort of
the finance equivalent of this. The idea of a search fund is something like:

 * Your local pest control business would probably be managed more efficiently
if it was run by a recent Harvard Business School graduate than if it was run
by the son of its founder. [2]  <>
 * That recent HBS graduate would be happy to run the pest control business if
she could take most of the efficiency gains for herself as a bonus.
 * So the trade is that investors give the HBS grad some money, she uses the
money to buy the pest control company, she runs it better, and she and the
investors split the gains.
Financial alchemy has turned a pest control business into a high-finance
operation that can attract HBS graduates and get financing from sophisticated
investors.

This is that but for AI:

 * Your local pest control business could probably be managed more efficiently
if it was run by an AI.
 * So the trade is that investors give the AI some money, it uses the money to
buy the pest control company, it runs it better, and it and the investors split
the gains.
Except that the AI can’t yet buy the company itself, so you need an
intermediary like Long Lake to do the deals and bring in the AI. Eventually
someone will notice. “Your local boring-profession-AI-rollup company could
probably be managed more efficiently if it was run by an AI,” etc.


Elsewhere in rollups

QXO Inc. is a giant, highly valued blank check. QXO founder Brad Jacobs has
had a lot of success running previous companies — he wrote a book with the
impeccable titleHow to Make a Few Billion Dollars <> — so investors want to be
in on his next thing. His next thing turned out to be rolling up companies that
distribute building products. QXO is his vehicle to do that, and he raised
about $5 billion from investors, at a premium, to go out and buy building
products distribution companies.

I have praised <> this several times <> because it is cool <>, but it does
create a problem. How many building products distribution companies are there?
How many of them are big enough to be attractive to Brad Jacobs, with $5
billion burning a hole in his pocket? If you run one of those companies, you
are probably expecting his call. “Sure you have money and enthusiasm,” you
might tell him, “but you really need a building products distribution company
to buy, and we are one of the only games in town.” You might drive a hard
bargain.

But if your building products distribution company is public, Jacobs can go
hostile <>:

Building-products distributor is preparing to take its all-cash offer to
acquire Beacon Roofing Supply directly to shareholders after being rebuffed on
several occasions, according to people familiar with the matter.

QXO is planning to launch a hostile bid for Beacon as soon as Monday, the
people said.

It will offer to buy all shares outstanding of Beacon for $124.25 per share,
the same price it previously proposed, the people said. That would value Beacon
at $7.7 billion, or roughly $11 billion including debt.

Beacon shares closed Friday at $118.42, giving the business a market value of
around $7.3 billion. The shares have run up since mid-November, when The Wall
Street Journal reported on the bid QXO had privately made to Beacon.

Here are QXO’s press release <> and tender offer document <>. The nice thing
about QXO is that people are willing to buy stock from Jacobs at a premium, but
the tough thing is that people want to sell to him at a premium too.


Dark trading

The traditional way to invest was that you would pick the stocks you wanted to
own, you would go to the stock exchange, and you would buy them. This was, you
hoped, good for you: You paid the market price for the stocks that you thought
would go up. But you were also participating in a collective process that was
good for everyone else. By picking stocks, you were doing your part to allocate
capital to its best uses: The stocks that everyone picked would go up, making
it easier for those companies to raise money to do worthwhile things; the
stocks that nobody picked would go down, and they would have to stop doing the
worthless things they had been doing.

You were also doing your part to make the pricing of stocks efficient:
Everyone competed to find out relevant information and to figure out how it
might affect companies, and so those companies’ stock prices reflected all of
the relevant information. The companies’ stock prices were “right,” or at least
as right as everyone could make them.

Modern markets have become, in many ways, more efficient, not in the
stock-market-efficiency sense of the word but in the regular sense: You can do
more stuff faster, and you can get exactly what you want. One thing that this
means is that now you canunbundle the “buying stocks that are good for me” part
of investing from the “participating in a collective process that creates
social goods” part. You can free-ride on the collective process.

People talk about this a lot with index funds. The thesis of an index fund is
that a lot of smart hard-working people compete to pick the best stocks. As a
product of their competition, all the stocks are pretty fairly priced; if they
weren’t, those smart hard-working competitors would buy the underpriced ones
and sell the overpriced ones. Soyou can just opt out of that competition: You
buyall the stocks at their market prices, trusting that everyone else's hard
work will make the prices right. And so you will be able to buy long-term
economic growth at the right price. This is nice for you, but ifeveryone did
it, it would be bad:There’d be no one to make the prices right <>.

On a smaller scale, though, it might also be true of stock exchanges. If you
want to buy some stock, you can go to a stock exchange and buy it from a market
maker. Or you canbe a market maker, posting public bids to buy and offers to
sell stock on the exchange, so that anyone can show up to trade with you. But
exchanges tend to charge fees to trade, and displaying your order on the stock
exchange gives away information (about what you are buying, and about how much
you think it is worth). This information is useful to other traders; it helps
everyone find the correct price for every stock. But why would you want that?
You want to buy the stock cheap, and giving away information might not help you
with that.

And so if you are an institutional investor, you might want to trade in a dark
pool managed by a bank, where your order will not be displayed for others’
information. (Or you might use non-displayed orders on a stock exchange that
offers them.) If you are aretail investor, meanwhile, your broker probably
“internalizes” your order, selling it to some electronic trading firm to
execute at a price better than what you could get on the exchange. (You might
not like this,possibly because you are confused <>, but it does seem better for
you. [3]  <>)

And the result is probably that you get better trades, but the public good is
undermined. Bloomberg’sKatherine Doherty reported Friday <>:

For the first time on record, the majority of all trading in US stocks is now
consistently occurring outside the country’s exchanges, according to data
compiled by Bloomberg.

This off-exchange activity — which happens internally at major firms or in
alternative platforms known as dark pools — is on course to account for a
record 51.8% of traded volume in January. …

The shift toward off-exchange trading is the culmination of a years-long
trend, which if it continues could eventually have implications for how the
market functions, according to Larry Tabb, head of market structure at
Bloomberg Intelligence.

“Theoretically the more trading that goes off-exchange, the fewer orders there
are on-exchange competing to determine the best price,” he said. “This means
the pricing on and off-exchange could get worse.”

Yes but on each individual trade you expect to do better.


The X loan sale

My main news about X, the website formerly known as Twitter, is that I have at
least two impersonators there now. My only X account is@matt_levine <> (and
these days I am more oftenon Bluesky <>), but there are fake accounts at
@BloombergLevine and @ColumnistMatt who are apparently messaging people for
what I can only assume are scam purposes. If I ever want to scam you, I will
not DM you from any X account. I will use this email list, thank you very much.

In more general-interest X news, its banks see some light at the end of the
tunnel <>:

Wall Street banks are getting ready to sell a big chunk of debt holdings in X,
the social-media platform controlled by Elon Musk.

Morgan Stanley bankers have reached out to investors ahead of a planned sale
next week of up to $3 billion of debt the bank and others such as Bank of
America and Barclays lent to Musk to complete his 2022 buyout of the company
formerly known as Twitter, people familiar with the matter said.

The banks hope to sell senior debt at 90-95 cents on the dollar, while
retaining more-junior holdings, the people said. The banks just sold
approximately $1 billion of debt in a private transaction to several investors,
some of the people said.

It seems fair to say that, even if X’s equity value is now zero (which it
probably isn’t?), Elon Musk has donevery well from this trade. For a mere $44
billion or so, Musk got himself (1) a prettyvaluable <> artificial intelligence
company, (2) apresident <> and (3) the ability to fine-tune his favorite
posting/trolling platform exactly the way he likes it. When Musk first
announced his position in Twitter in 2022,I wrote <>:

Look this all makes complete sense, obvious, intuitive, simple sense. If you
are the richest person in the world, and annoying, and you constantly play a
computer game, and you get a lot of enjoyment and a sense of identity from that
game and are maybe a little addicted, then at some point you might have some
suggestions for improvements in the game. So you might leave comments and email
the company that makes the game saying “hey you should try my ideas.” And the
company might ignore you (or respond politely but not move fast enough for your
liking). It might occur to you: “Look, I am the richest person in the world;
how much could this game company possibly cost? I should just buy it and change
the game however I want.” Even if your complaints are quite minor, why
shouldn’t you get to playexactly the game you want? Even if you have no
complaints, why notown the game you love, just to make sure it continues to be
exactly what you want?

And, really, what is the point of being the richest person in the world if you
can’t mold your favorite online message board to your exact specifications?

But this is the difference between debt and equity. Musk bought the equity, he
controls the company, and he has reaped various ancillary economic and psychic
rewards from that position, even if the equity has lost value. If you buy the
debt all you get is the debt; your best case is getting repaid at par plus
interest. The value ofthat proposition has gone down since the deal was signed
— though up a lot since a year ago — even if things have worked out great for
Musk.


Flybor

On Jan. 21, 2020, I wrote about <> a proposed <> “derivatives-based trading
platform that will allow airlines to protect against declines in ticket
prices,” and I said:

I do not know how this “series of indexes that capture fare fluctuations
across the industry” will be set, but after Libor and Chicken Libor, I tend to
expect manipulation in any index of industry prices. Let’s set a calendar
reminder to meet back here in five years to discuss the scandal of airline
ticket price index manipulation. “Flybor,” I plan to call the resulting scandal.

Several readers actually set this calendar reminder — I did not — and emailed
me last week to check in on Flybor. No scandal, sorry! Can’t win ’em all. The
platformstill seems to exist though <>.
