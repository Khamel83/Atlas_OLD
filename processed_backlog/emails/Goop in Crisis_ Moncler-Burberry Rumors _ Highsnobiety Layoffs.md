Three Things You Should Know…

* On those Moncler-Burberry rumors: Shares of Burberry jumped on Monday after Astrid Wendlandt reported ( https://misstweed.com/articles/moncler-mulls-bid-for-burberry?utm_campaign=Line+Sheet+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F6%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io ) that Moncler was “mulling” a bid for the Italian luxury brand—reinforcing, at the very least, the notion that somebody should save Burberry from itself. Alas, the stock dipped back down after investors raised an eyebrow about the rumor.

I did a little recon, myself, too. And after talking to people on
both sides, I confirmed that the deal chatter is far, far from real. There have been conversations in the Moncler building, but it sounds like they were largely speculative. (Representatives for both companies said they don’t comment on rumors.)

Some on the Burberry side wonder if this was just bankers causing trouble and trying to spark some deal heat—there has long been an M&A premium on the stock—or if some other force (an influential shareholder, maybe?) was clandestinely attempting to will an acquisition into existence. Could Moncler even afford Burberry? Also, Burberry just got a new C.E.O. and morale in the building is at a 10-year high.

That said, if Burberry is going to be acquired, perhaps there is no better future owner than Moncler. After his appointment just a few months ago, Burberry C.E.O. Joshua Schulman made it clear that he viewed the English heritage label as not just
a luxury brand, but a luxury outerwear brand, and that he would be working to ensure more people owned Burberry coats—the check-lined trench, yes, but also the duffle, the motorcycle jacket, etcetera. Remo Ruffini, the C.E.O. of Moncler, did something similar when he took over in the early 2000s, transforming the outerwear company into the go-to option for wealthy people in search of both ski jackets and upscale alternatives to the Patagonia down “sweater” to wear off the slopes. Likewise, Burberry should be the go-to option for wealthy people who need a nice, not-too-heavy overcoat to wear to work and on the weekends.

If Moncler did buy Burberry, I suspect that the company would emulate the strategy applied by LVMH when it acquired Rimowa: Focus relentlessly on the core product, stop discounting, and exit as many tacky wholesale situations as possible. That’s exactly what Burberry has been trying to do on its own,
but a variety of distractions—weird ready-to-wear, lofty handbag ambitions, tough comps in China—made that harder than the company’s two previous C.E.O.s, Jonathan Akeroyd and Marco Gobbetti, anticipated.

Ruffini did exactly this in the early 2000s with Moncler, and more recently with Stone Island, which he acquired in 2020. That deal, which involved another brand once favored by chavs and now beloved by finance bros, has been generally successful, despite sales dropping 5 percent in the first half of the year as the company pulled back on shitty wholesale and focused more on the direct business. And Burberry is a much bigger opportunity. Its brand awareness is already off the charts, and as the world gets warmer, and the weather more unpredictable, a lighter-weight-but-protective coat, like a trench, becomes a wardrobe requisite.

Would anyone else buy Burberry? Perhaps Tapestry, although I suspect this is
the last thing on C.E.O. Joanne Crevoiserat’s mind after the F.T.C. blocked ( https://puck.news/life-after-the-tapestry-capri-annulment/?utm_campaign=Line+Sheet+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F6%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io ) the company’s Capri deal. (There’s a scenario where the Tapestry board sells off underperforming Kate Spade New York and Stuart Weitzman, buys Burberry, says goodbye to Crevoiserat and promotes Schulman to run the new group anchored by Coach and Burberry, but that’s just a scenario.) Kering looked at the business years ago and concluded that it required too much of a turnaround. Then there’s LVMH, which would never buy it outright because it’s not a transformational purchase. LVMH already owns a small stake in Moncler, which has the outerwear expertise—why not let them do the dirty work, and simply reap the
benefits as a minority investor? They can always buy the whole Moncler group someday, adding another division to an ever-growing portfolio.
* An e-commerce site and a content shop walk into a bar…: On Monday, approximately 12 people were laid off at Highsnobiety, the Berlin-founded sneaker enthusiast site turned content platform. Much of the New York editorial staff, including the executive editor, fashion market editor, at least two social media editors, a commerce editor, and the head of talent were let go. More layoffs are expected later this week in Europe, where Highsnobiety is now owned by German e-commerce giant Zalando.

The writing was on the wall following the exit of Nichelle Sanders, who had run the North American business since January 2024, and prior to her, Willa Bennett, the publication’s editor-in-chief, who recently decamped to Hearst to run Cosmopolitan and Seventeen. The company
line, I’m told, is that costs are outpacing revenue, and that from now on there may be a greater focus on the agency work and perhaps commerce—although I hear there are also plans to phase out Highsnobiety HS05, the in-house label, even though they will continue to produce other types of product.

As for the print product, for which Bennett won a National Magazine Award, the next issue is slated to publish in March, with text due in December, before the holidays. One employee wondered if some of the firings on the editorial side had less to do with budgets and more to do with a desire to get back to the “more male, hypebeast-adjacent P.O.V. now that Willa is gone.” Employees said that in meetings, some leadership has suggested that the content may be skewing too female. (However, a person close to management refuted this, and said that the goal has always been for the mix to feel balanced.)

I reached out to David Fischer, the
company’s founder and C.E.O., as well as its external P.R. team, to see what’s up. A rep sent me a statement acknowledging the layoffs and suggesting the point was to create an “efficient allocation of resources” as they continue “streamlining our global structure to more closely align with the shifting industry as well as internal needs.” Most importantly, though, they wanted me to know that the editorial vision for the company—and the business model—“remain unchanged by any of these measures.”

Reading between the lines, my educated guess is that layoffs were likely a mandate from the powers that be at Zalando to put a tourniquet around the business. Zalando is not involved in the day-to-day operations, but they certainly don’t want it to cost them. I covered the Highsnobiety acquisition back in 2022, and figured at the time that the group had a couple years of runway as a largely independent concern before its new corporate overlords started
cracking the whip on their cool-kid acquisition. But that honeymoon period has passed, and people don’t like losing money.

Just about every celebrity brand that’s glimmered into existence in the last decade has tried to model itself after Goop, the Gwyneth Paltrow lifestyle venture that launched as a newsletter, in 2008, but has since expanded into beauty, apparel, fine jewelry, cookware, and sex toys, not to mention wellness summits, television, and a food delivery service, among other things. Lately, the company has entered a weird adolescent phase—no longer a startup, but not yet a profitable business, wielding outsize influence relative to its financial health.

The company has raised more than $140 million to date, according to PitchBook, including a Series D in 2020 that valued the company at $380 million, pre-money. Four years later, Goop is still not profitable and in the throes of yet another restructuring that has
resulted in three rounds of layoffs since the summer—most recently last week—which looks like an effort to slim down to satisfy the board and position itself for potential suitors.

And yet, while the company has struggled to grow beyond its founder’s polarizing (but not too polarizing) persona, Goop endures because it is, perhaps, the only true celebrity lifestyle brand. Paltrow has managed to annoy and entice in equal measure, serving up self-deprecating grandiosity, combined with impeccable taste and an almost-attainable life that you, too, can sample by buying the serum she uses or taking $75 Tracy Anderson group classes in the Hamptons. Below, Lauren and I discuss why people care about Goop so much, how the company lost its way, and what a potential exit could look like.

The Martha Complex

Rachel: Gwyneth is a celebrity and a founder, which are two different things, but she’s combined them in a way that magnifies each of those roles. Goop remains an anomaly when it comes to celebrity-founded brands because, typically, celebrities are founders in name only. Famous people usually lend their likeness, and involvement in their brands is more similar to a licensing agreement. Gwyneth’s obviously way more than that––she truly built a business influenced by her absurd life. Everyone wants to do that, but no one else has in this way. She’s this Academy Award-winning actress, a media staple for decades, a style icon, etcetera––and then she actually started a real business that’s authentic to her, like a modern Martha Stewart. She’s an outlier, and that’s why we’re talking about her now, 16 years in.

Lauren: What place does Goop serve in the ecosystem of the startup world?

Rachel: If we look at contemporary brands in beauty or fashion that were founded between 2008 and 2012, they either became profitable or they disappeared. Goop is not profitable, but it’s still around and still extremely relevant, with outsize influence compared to the actual size of the business. A friend texted me the other day after reading Line Sheet and said, “I had no idea, I thought Goop was doing really well,” because Gwyneth is portrayed and covered in the media as this really successful C.E.O. with a strong business. That was her perception, at least. If not for Gwyneth’s relevance in culture, I don’t know if Goop would be around.

Lauren: When Goop started, it had so much traction because Paltrow is a great editor. She has a point of view, and she’s not afraid to present it. The content was exciting, fun, juicy, and
entertaining. Then they started layering in other advertising and commercial opportunities, which makes sense. There’s a multibrand retail experience, a fast-casual food vertical, her clothing, beauty, and more. Individually, these are all interesting businesses, but altogether, that sort of diversification can create a cloudy revenue picture and make a company a lot less attractive to sell. When you look at the trajectory—launching in 2008, getting advertising pretty quickly thereafter, opening multibrand retail in the early 2010s, then launching products afterward, and now leaning into food—when do you think the business became more complicated to sell?

Rachel: What was originally captivating, and what remains captivating, is Gwyneth’s point of view. People wanted to read that initial newsletter because she has good taste. We wanted to know what she was eating, what exercise she was doing, where she was going, tips for the best hotels, all of
those things. That’s what people wanted to know from her––and still do––but it’s hard to monetize that in a way that investors need her to. Even the most successful content sites will never generate the same revenue as a successful product line, which is why content ventures often veer into product. That said, Paltrow raised more than $50 million in her Series D, in 2020. I presume those investors came in at that level because they saw an opportunity and wanted a return on their capital, via an exit, within a five-year term. That was almost five years ago…

Exit Strategies

Lauren: Commerce needs content and content can have commerce, but in our transactional world, where everything can be tracked, there’s an expectation and interest in making content a direct conversion tool. The reality is that you can’t rely on that. Do you think there’s something that Goop could have done better with the power they had on the content side? Because it sounds like most people who worked on editorial, if not all of them, no longer work there.

Rachel: To get that big exit, Goop had to go into product, and that’s a tricky thing, because Paltrow built her brand by recommending and talking honestly about the things she loves, uses, and actually buys. You instantly lose credibility when you start your own product lines because, ultimately, your goal is to sell your own stuff more than other people’s stuff.
At that point, you effectively can no longer do the thing that attracted people to your brand in the first place.

Look, it’s hard to establish yourself as a content business and then turn into a product brand; it’s easier to start with product and then also produce good content. I do think it’s possible to scale from content to commerce—Emily Weiss did it with Glossier—but it requires a discipline that Goop never had. If Paltrow only focused on one thing, or fewer things, and went deep in one category, it may have worked. Launching into every single category looks like a money grab… because it is a money grab, right? It’s better to be really good at fewer things than to be not great at many things. That was Goop’s downfall.

Lauren: Why do you think beauty hasn’t clicked the way it should have? When Gwyneth first did beauty, she had Juice Beauty as a partner, and then it became its own thing, and then Goop put its
lower-price line at Target, which you reported was a flop ( https://puck.news/gwyneth-paltrow-beauty-makeover-for-goop/?utm_campaign=Line+Sheet+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F6%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io ).

Rachel: Goop’s beauty line should have worked. It’s a missed opportunity of epic proportions that it hasn’t seen the success of other skincare and makeup brands that came out around the same time with similar propositions. Besides having no discipline, Goop never had the right leaders or C.P.G. infrastructure to set it up for success. There was never a strong operator to lead the charge, to oversee and scale the wholesale business—because, as we know, strong retail relationships are critical to a beauty brand’s success.

Goop took off in popularity during an era when V.C.s valued beauty brands like tech companies and D.T.C.
was king, but all that has changed. Retail is a very specific skill set, and they probably didn’t have a person who knew how to work with Sephora, Ulta, etcetera. When Gwyneth came out with skincare in 2016, it seemed like it wasn’t the priority––there was this robust content arm, and all of these other things that they were doing. To build a beauty brand at scale, it would have needed to be the main focus, and it just wasn’t.

Lauren: They don’t half-ass anything—which doesn’t mean they do it right. There’s a desire to do it well, but I think you pinpointed the thing: They’ve never had the right operators, and ultimately, the onus is on Gwyneth. Venture investors don’t butt into businesses a lot; they’re not operators or private equity, who will bring in an operator to babysit. Everything Goop’s done is with intention––it’s just that the execution and strategy hasn’t been as high or clear, because they don’t have that
bench of executives.

It’s easy to scrutinize because Gwyneth is very public. It’s also easy to see all the mistakes, and the big mistake is exactly what you’re saying––trying to do five million things at once and not having anywhere near the right people to do those five million things. So what do you think their exit possibilities are at this point?

Rachel: You’ve said before that Goop could sell to a licensing firm, or a group that owns middle-of-the-road beauty brands, and there’s a chance they could break the businesses up and sell them off that way. The company has been clear that its focuses are food, fashion, and beauty. I think food and beauty are the two biggest opportunities. A person close to Goop told me the company actually increased its revenue projections for 2024, which I’m sure is largely due to Goop Kitchen, the food delivery concept that’s already reached “company-level profitability.” Excluding the Target
line, beauty’s had some momentum and a bunch of new launches lately. If Goop can get out of its own way and hire seasoned beauty executives with deep retail expertise, beauty could still become what it always should have been for them.

That’s it from Rachel and me.
