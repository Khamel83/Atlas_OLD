Vesuvius Scrolls

*Well, one final bit, I forgot to bring this up on the last time we talked, but the Vesuvius Scrolls ( https://scrollprize.org/ ) project, tell me what is it and what is the current status?*

*Tell us the story just for everyone’s context.*

*NF:* Julius Caesar’s father-in-law had built a villa on the coast of the Bay of Naples in a town called Herculaneum. The villa was covered in ash and mud when Mount Vesuvius erupted in 79 AD and was just buried along with the town of Herculaneum under 65 feet of mud, just like Pompeii and a couple of other little villages around there, until the 1700s when a farmer digging a well struck a marble paving stone and discovered that as they dug 65 feet deep to find water, they’d found this magnificent villa.

Subsequently, engineers tunneled. They never fully exposed the villa, it’s this enormous villa, but they tunneled through it looking for treasure, looking for statues and bronzes and frescoes, many of which they did find, incredible stuff. And along the way, they kept running into these weird lumps of flaky charcoal, and many of them were discarded until someone noticed writing on one of them, and it turns out that they had found essentially the only preserved library from antiquity.

It turns out that basically papyrus scrolls, which that was the medium on which you kept books and writing, they decay in the humid Adriatic and Mediterranean air, and so humidity is sort of the enemy of history. Basically every contemporary, or from antiquity, every contemporary papyrus scroll is gone unless it was in Egypt and very dry desert air. But everything from Greece, Ancient Greece, ancient Rome, we don’t have, unless it was copied every 75 years by monks for centuries, and maybe there were copying errors or editorial changes that they made along the way and what survives of ancient literature —

*But we’re not talking about experts in GPT right now.*

*NF:* (laughing) That’s right, exactly. They pulled hundreds of these scrolls out of this villa, and unfortunately they’re so fragile that if you try to take them apart, they just sort of dissolve into dust in your hand. Many hundreds were destroyed in the process in the late 1700s by well-meaning attempts to open them and read them. Eventually a handful were opened painstakingly meticulously by this monk employed by the Vatican who spent forty years unrolling just a very small number of them, and they were found to contain these works of Greek literature, philosophical literature, Epicurean philosophy from this Epicurean philosopher named Philodemus.

But there are hundreds of them that have never been opened, and we don’t know what’s in them. If you could basically somehow open these things, which is physically impossible, and read them, the estimate is that it would roughly double the total amount of literature we have from ancient Rome and Ancient Greece. That’s how little survived, and that’s how big the bounty is here, if we could figure this out.

So we have partnered with a professor named Brent Seales who has pioneered this technique of using basically a CT scanner, and not just any hospital CT scanner that you used when you broke your wrist, but a high energy cyclotron, a synchrotron, in fact, a particle accelerator-based CT scanner to scan these scrolls at incredibly high resolution. He’s been working for years to try to use these scans to maybe digitally unroll them. Can you use AI? Can you use computer vision geometry to unroll these things and read what’s in them? It’s incredibly challenging. They’re very distorted by the heat and the pressure of the mud and then the ink that was used to write on the papyrus is carbon based, like the papyrus so there’s not a really clear x-ray signature. It doesn’t look like a bone, a bright white sort of ink signature in the papyrus, and so it turns out to be a very difficult kind of computer vision and AI problem.

Daniel and I decided, we just thought this was so cool and so interesting that we launched a project called the Vesuvius Challenge, and you can go to scrollprize.org ( https://scrollprize.org/ ) and read about it, and we put some of our own money in and we said, if someone can figure out, we’ll put the files out there, we worked with Dr. Seales to release that, release all the current state-of-the-art techniques and code to help people get started. We said, we’ll put a $250,000 prize out there. We launched, and within a week or so, our friends and other people on Twitter or on X I guess, X-ed at us that they wanted to support this challenge and our little $250,000 prize bounty grew to about $1.5 million, which I think makes it the biggest archeology prize out there.

So we’ve been running it for, gosh, maybe I think five months or so now. There’s thousands of teams that have participated and progress is being made and in particular, one guy appears to have found for the first time ink and lettering inside a scroll. We don’t have whole sentences or paragraphs coming out yet, but we’re starting to get letters and maybe even words and you’d think, “Oh, he must’ve used a very powerful AI or computer vision model.” In fact, many people did attempt to use computer vision and AI to read these things and made little bits of progress, but the guy who’s made the most progress just wrote a little viewer in Mathematica and spent hundreds of hours just manually viewing the CT scans and found this ink.

That’s a pretty major bit of progress that’s been made so far so we’re hopeful that it’s possible to do this, and it would be like you could potentially rewrite history if it happens. So this is our fun side project that we’re running.
