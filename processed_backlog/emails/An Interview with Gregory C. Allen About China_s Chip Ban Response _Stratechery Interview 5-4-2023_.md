*With that groundwork laid, I wanted to catch up on where we are. I think the big development about which you’ve published ( https://www.csis.org/analysis/japan-and-netherlands-announce-plans-new-export-controls-semiconductor-equipment ) a couple of reports ( https://www.csis.org/analysis/clues-us-dutch-japanese-semiconductor-export-controls-deal-are-hiding-plain-sight ) , and there’s a third one that’s coming out this week ( https://www.csis.org/analysis/chinas-new-strategy-waging-microchip-tech-war ) that we’ll get to in a moment, is the imposition of Dutch and Japanese export controls. Why were those two countries so important to get on board?*

*GA:* The basic thing to understand is that the global market for semiconductor manufacturing equipment is an oligopoly. There are about five companies who really matter, which combined cover more than 80% of global market share, and those five companies are only located in three countries, the United States, the Netherlands, and Japan. If you go beyond the top five companies, most of the remaining ones are also in those three countries.

So the United States export controls against China were covering the entire semiconductor value chain from the software that you use to design computer chips to the computer chips themselves, to the equipment that you use to make those chips, and the most secure part of that export control architecture is really the chip-making equipment, because China has good chip design firms. If the only thing the policy had done was ban sales of Nvidia chips, well, China has companies who know how to design chips of comparable quality to Nvidia. So the export control policy, rather than harming China’s military AI development, the point of the policy would’ve just given a real growth injection into China’s chip design industry. The reason why that is not so easy is because even though it’s reasonably easy to design good chips, it’s insanely preposterously difficult to make the advanced equipment that is used to make chips, and so that really is the security measure of the entire underlying export control architecture.

Now, the United States as a country is the dominant supplier in a bunch of different categories of semiconductor manufacturing equipment. If you want stuff like inspection and metrology equipment, certain types of etching equipment, the United States is really where you need to be. But there are other types of semiconductor manufacturing equipment where the Dutch or the Japanese firms are really the dominant suppliers.

So there’s two reasons why you needed these other countries to get on board with this export control architecture. One is to just strengthen the choke points in the semiconductor manufacturing equipment ecosystem that China has to overcome if they want to overcome these export controls. It’s just a much, much tougher challenge to do everything that the United States can do, the Dutch can do and the Japanese can do. The second and equally important reason why the United States needed this export control architecture to go from unilateral to multilateral is because China was willing to pay these firms a king’s ransom in order to help them overcome the export controls. There’s certain types of semiconductor manufacturing equipment that US companies make, and generally Japanese companies and Dutch companies don’t make.

*But they could figure it out a lot more quickly than China could.*

*GA:* Exactly. If China walked in and said, “Here’s a pile of infinite money and a guaranteed monopoly access to the Chinese market, would you be interested in getting into the business of the stuff that only the US makes?” Those firms are going to take that, and so these governments of Japan and the Netherlands joining the export control architecture really prevents that backfill scenario, which would’ve been a disaster for the United States.

*Why do you think that these countries were willing to get on board? Because that is a pretty significant economic opportunity and to your point, yes, the US is dominant today, but you could quickly end up in a situation where the US is not dominant at all.*

*GA:* I think there are multiple reasons why these countries wanted to get on board. The first thing I’ll say is that these countries have their own reasons to be extremely frustrated with China. China in the military domain is extremely threatening to Japan, it’s really their top concern even above that of North Korea, which is more reckless but fundamentally weaker.

*I think just to expound on this, to me this makes total sense from the Japanese angle. The reason why they’re deeply concerned about Taiwan is basically almost all their shipping, including a lot of their energy imports, sail by Taiwan — that being in under Chinese control puts Japan in a very vulnerable position so it makes total sense that would get on board. They’re probably, correct me if I’m wrong, they were also probably the bigger threat as far as capabilities of backfilling all the other US companies that China no longer has access to. Is that correct?*

*GA:* Absolutely. The Netherlands really is concentrated in lithography, the Japanese can do lithography and they can do a lot of other stuff besides, so even though extreme ultraviolet ASML machines, the Dutch company that makes these extreme ultraviolet lithography machines, that was probably the strongest technological barrier of any technological barrier for China to overcome, but what Japan lacks in depth of lithography, they make up for in breadth of every other type of capability. If you want to grow ultrapure silicon crystals, if you want to make the ultrapure chemicals that go into this, if you want to make all kinds of incredibly niche equipment, the Japanese are all over this market.

Their participation, the plausibility that these firms could diversify into the stuff that the United States, between the three leading US companies enjoys a monopoly on, it was very credible that in a relevant timeframe — what I’ve been saying is by itself China is potentially a trillion dollars or more of investment away and decades away from replicating what the United States can do. With Japan’s help, they might be as little as two to three years away, so it was really important to get Japan on board with these export controls.
