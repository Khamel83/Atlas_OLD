The Brady Meter: Week 7
Chiefs 28–49ers 18
Grade: C

Just last week, the NFL owners unanimously voted to accept the bid of an ownership group that included Tom Brady to acquire 10 percent of the Las Vegas Raiders, thereby ossifying all the league’s restrictions on Fox’s $37.5 million rookie broadcaster. As loyal readers of The Varsity know by heart, Brady won’t be able to make negative comments about officials or opposing teams or, most critically, enter another team’s training facility to interview players and staff during the standard production meetings that precede game day. Alas, these meetings really do offer broadcasters rare and privileged insights into how teams prepare for their opponents. Brady can watch all the film he wants, but there’s really no replacing this sort of osmosis.

Anyway, this was the backdrop for the GOAT’s much-hyped booth appearance to call the
Super Bowl rematch between the 49ers and Chiefs.
Interestingly, Brady was at his best when he wasn’t calling the game action. His pretaped interview with Patrick Mahomes presented a fun conversation between the two best quarterbacks of their eras. He went viral for referring to Chiefs defensive coordinator Steve Spagnuolo ( https://x.com/awfulannouncing/status/1848132148953563508?utm_campaign=The+Varsity+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F21%2F24%29&utm_content=The+Varsity+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F3%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) as “the bane of my existence.” (Spags, of course, helped coordinate the Giants defense that ruined the Patriots’ perfect season.)

In the booth, thus far, Brady still hasn’t done anything to stand out as the top analyst on the Super Bowl broadcast. On Sunday, he was relentlessly positive in his critiques of the players and coaches. When he disagreed with a penalty, he
simply described it as a “tight call.” His first-name-only player references—especially Patrick and Travis—may have been an attempt to emit a vibe of intimacy with the audience. But two months into the regular season, viewers are still struggling to discover what Brady really thinks about the games he is calling.

The Starting Five: World Series Edition

* College sports’ building pains: I spent my Saturday in Lubbock, taking a tour of Texas Tech’s training facilities. First, I walked through the state-of-the-art Womble Center, completed in the spring of 2021, where both the men’s and women’s basketball teams practice. It was, simply, beautiful. Then I took in the football training facility, which was completed 20 years ago. The building, which once weaned a young Mahomes, seemed nice enough to me, but is outdated compared to those of rival schools. Naturally, the university is constructing a replacement next door.

For the past decade, universities have viewed these souped-up, hyper-advanced athletics complexes as a key factor in attracting recruits.
Unsurprisingly, of course, institutions of higher learning have wound up spending extraordinary oodles of money—counting on donors, boosters, and public funding—to build the most luxurious practice facilities around. Some of the most historic and bucolic campuses now look like nostalgic appendages to Martian-like modern athletic coliseums. So it goes…

But starting right around the time that the Womble Center was erected, schools have discovered—duh—that recruits are much more interested in Name, Image, and Likeness (N.I.L.) opportunities than a college’s newly christened training center. As a result, colleges are increasingly focused on managing N.I.L. monetization rather than financing infrastructure upgrades. Alas, college sports are changing at warp speed, as Virginia’s outgoing coach Tony Bennett reminded us last week. And these shrines are memorials to the velocity of change.
* A World Series lead-in: On
Friday, Fox will lead out of Game 1 of the Yankees-Dodgers World Series into a Rutgers-USC college football game across town. The World Series is scheduled to start at 8 p.m. ET from Dodger Stadium, and Rutgers-USC is scheduled to kick off at 11 p.m. ET at the L.A. Coliseum. (Ironically, both games involve teams from New York and Los Angeles.) If the World Series goes into extra innings and runs past 11 p.m., Fox will carry the football game on FS1.

Over the years, broadcast TV networks have had a tough time convincing leagues to use the most popular programming from other sports as a lead-in—partly, of course, because none of them wants to help their competitors draft off their audiences. (It was highly unusual when NBC used the Rams-Bengals Super Bowl to tee up the 2022 Winter Olympics—the first time that a network had used a sporting event as the lead-out from a Super Bowl since the ’70s.) But Fox, as usual, has been the most aggressive
employer of this tactic. Last year, the network scheduled a Michigan State-Arizona college basketball game immediately after the Packers-Lions Thanksgiving game. Not surprisingly, it still stands as the most watched college basketball game Fox has ever carried.

* Dolan’s debt comes due: Last Sunday, my partner Bill
Cohan spotlighted the troubling financials behind the Sphere in Las Vegas—Jim Dolan’s maximalist, $2.3 billion music and entertainment arena. Between June 2023 and June 2024, the Sphere generated nearly $500 million in revenue, but also incurred an operating loss of $480 million. (Not great!) Although Dolan has yet to resort to the “adjusted EBITDA” smoke-and-mirrors metric so typical these days, the Sphere does take pains to report an “adjusted operating loss,” for the year ended June 2024, of $61.5 million—an improvement on the “adjusted operating loss” of nearly $300 million from the year before.

But the bigger problem, as ever, is the company’s debt. With the operating losses growing at the Sphere level, any debt would probably be too much. According to filings with the S.E.C., an “event of default” on the loan would have occurred without the forbearance.

JOHN OURAND ( https://puck.news/john-ourand/?utm_campaign=The+Varsity+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F21%2F24%29&utm_content=The+Varsity+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F3%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a )

Major League Baseball executives may be rejoicing over this year’s World Series matchup between the league’s wealthiest clubs and biggest markets, the Dodgers and the Yankees, a far cry from last year’s underwhelming Texas-Arizona sitch. But the reality is that this auspicious outcome overlays some thorny and nuanced and enduring economic challenges.

MLB, after all, is the most federalized of the Big Four sports leagues. Sure, it has packages with ESPN, Amazon, Roku, and Apple. But local fandoms, prolific schedules, and the longtime reliable revenue from local cable have made it harder for the league to collectively exploit the media value of its product, as has become the modus operandi in the NBA, with its $77 billion media rights package, and the operating procedure de rigueur in the NFL, with its current (and
somehow seemingly underpriced) $110 billion decade-plus deal. And now this financial reality has come into stark relief as the regional sports network business continues to implode via small-scale extinction events like Diamond’s long-gestating bankruptcy and Comcast’s grinfucking. Over the past two seasons, MLB has taken back the rights to six of its teams: the Padres, Diamondbacks, Rockies, Guardians, Twins, and Brewers.

Last week, I chatted with MLB commissioner Rob Manfred, who recently signed a five-year extension, about his strategy to lead the league through the platform transformation. Manfred predicted “a significant acceleration in the number of teams that give their rights to MLB Media,” but acknowledged that this process will take considerable time. MLB can’t put together this sort of package until it amasses more of its teams’ local rights, which expire along various timelines. The Dodgers, for example, are contentedly locked into
their lucrative, $334 million deal—almost the precise value of their payroll—until 2038. And the Yankees, with their own R.S.N. and O.T.T. option, may have other theories of the case.

So far, the teams that have turned over their rights are cognizant that they will bring in less local revenue, at least initially. But Manfred predicts teams will wind up making more in the long run with a hybrid local media strategy, one that combines cable carriage with local broadcast deals and pooled direct-to-consumer offerings that might entice the trillion-dollar market cap streaming players, like Amazon, Apple, and Google. “The inherent value of our content is going to bring us back to and beyond where we were,” Manfred said. “I would predict today that that trough will be shorter. I certainly am sensing in my private conversations—and we’ve seen it publicly—that new media companies are becoming aggressive sooner than some people expect.”

All Politics Is Local

John Ourand: MLB made headlines this month when it took back the rights to the Guardians, Twins, and Brewers—in addition to the Padres, Diamondbacks, and Rockies—whose rights were held by Diamond Sports. From a 50,000-foot view, what is your plan with these local rights?

Rob Manfred: I think it’s important to remember that we didn’t begin with a grand plan about local rights or being in the business of doing games locally. In a lot of ways, we stepped into a situation where we didn’t have a great alternative. The R.S.N. was going bankrupt, and we needed to make sure that we got our games out locally. As we started to think about the landscape more generally, we came to realize that our broadcast product needed to be more national. The difficulties with the R.S.N.s presented an opportunity for us to get
into a more centralized media strategy and get closer to the model that Commissioner Adam Silver used in his recent round of NBA negotiations. The R.S.N.s were really good for us from a financial perspective, but they did not give us a full opportunity [to optimize our] reach.

What I’d like to see happen over time is, we redo our national deals and convert some of that local inventory into national inventory, which in turn increases our reach. We own the outer market rights already; if we control local rights as well, we can sell anything, anywhere, and you don’t just have to sell in your market. I’d like to get into a mode where if it’s not in a national package, the consumer has the ability to go in and buy whatever he wants to watch, no matter where it is. And we get rid of that questionable business concept of the blackout.

Are you confident that you’re going to be able to bring big-market teams—the Dodgers, the Yankees, the Cubs,
for example—into that sort of system, which is going to ask them to share more than they’re used to?

“Confident” is a funny word. I think there is a path forward that involves media and other really significant industry issues that could put all the clubs in a position to come together. And the point I’d make about that is, the trends that are affecting the R.S.N.s right now, we’re seeing them first in our smaller markets. And that’s not surprising, right? But those trends are so fundamental, sooner or later, they’re gonna affect everyone. And I do think there’s an opportunity in that problem.

I’ve always viewed the MLB’s untraditional streaming deals with Roku and Apple as some sort of test. What have you learned from them?

I think Apple, in particular, has been really creative on the production end. And there are learnings there that have been useful to us; some things worked, some things didn’t. Our traditional
broadcasts are kind of the same as they’ve been for a long time. Everything from graphics to announcers, Apple experimented with, and we’ve learned from that.

On the business side, I think maybe the biggest issue to talk about is the difference between having a paywall and not. We’ve had Apple in front of a paywall, we’ve had it behind a paywall; we’ve had Roku in front of a paywall. I think the discussion and the differences between the distribution mechanisms that Apple and Roku use, respectively, get to the underappreciated business issue in broadcast for us, and that’s reach.

For years, we focused on dollars, understandably. But I think when you have a live business that’s as robust as ours—where 72 million people a year go through the gates—reaching the fans with your broadcast is really important in terms of driving them to the ballpark, which is another really important part of our business.

The biggest
complaint about baseball for decades has been how old the fan base is. That’s starting to turn around. Why is that?

I think there are several reasons. I do think the changes we’ve made to the way the game is played on the field are a big part of it. We did really fundamental research about what fans wanted to see. And I think we’re putting out there a more action-packed, tighter, quicker game that’s appealing to young people. Secondly, I think that we have been blessed with an infusion of young and extraordinary talent, combined with a different attitude about interacting with fans, being accessible to fans, particularly being accessible on social media platforms where young people live. Then the third thing—and this one gets overlooked—I think the clubs have done a phenomenal job with what I refer to as the program in the stadium. Now we see so many alternatives—more flexible seating and ticketing arrangements that are designed to meet the
desires of younger fans.
