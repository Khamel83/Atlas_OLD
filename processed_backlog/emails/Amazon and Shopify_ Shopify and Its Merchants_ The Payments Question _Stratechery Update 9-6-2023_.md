Amazon and Shopify
------------------

From Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-08-30/amazon-shopify-deal-opens-amazon-logistics-to-sellers ) :

Amazon.com Inc. and Shopify Inc. have struck a deal to allow merchants who pay for Shopify’s e-commerce tools to use Amazon’s logistics network. The two companies, which both sell e-commerce software and services to brands and merchants, say Amazon’s “Buy with Prime” will soon be among the tools available to Shopify’s merchants in the US. The program started Wednesday for invited Shopify sellers, and will roll out to all Shopify merchants who want to use Amazon’s logistics network by the end of September, Amazon said in a statement. Shopify shares climbed more than 2% in extended trading, while Amazon stock rose less than 1% after the partnership was announced.

To the extent the market movements were caused by this deal, then I think Wall Street got this right: this is a win-win-win.

Start with Amazon. What Buy with Prime signaled to me was that CEO Andy Jassy, who had previously been the head of AWS, wanted to apply the AWS model to Amazon.com. Amazon.com had, for its entire existence, been an Aggregator of sorts, which is to say it was predicated on capturing and controlling consumer demand. That originally meant that Amazon.com bought products at wholesale that it could sell to customers, but over the last fifteen years that has increasingly meant a shift in sales via 3rd-party merchants who could list their products on Amazon, and store them in Amazon warehouses, and have them delivered via Prime, as long as those purchases were made on Amazon.com. This of course meant a loss of brand identity for merchants, and the inability to build a direct connection with customers, but Amazon.com drove so much volume that it was worth it all the same.

AWS, on the other hand, was a platform, not an Aggregator. Consumers who visit websites or apps powered by AWS have no idea that they are interacting with the service; rather, AWS has invested so much that it can provide scalable services to not only Amazon itself but also huge portions of the Internet. I explained the model in 2016’s The Amazon Tax ( https://stratechery.com/2016/the-amazon-tax/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDE2L3RoZS1hbWF6b24tdGF4LyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNjk2NjAxODgzLCJpYXQiOjE2OTQwMDk4ODMsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBpLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiUnNldVlFOGpnSGVQcFdKZjFmQWI0QyIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.R8jqqYRggu0-X4ifcwAjPya9DYfAgZ-XzOf_1O03lqeqav3SOo1yBRa5_MvLUJCtJYZ1AVj4te3uyPl8jfxHtUBfW70qKddR_2_vtWFhwihY9VrSmpXi3d6evEkbW22xlktqFqM2dpqGWWD0qNPvXUuWsvttyvH3CK6sgR2pvF22022oPl13f6mVSL5Zvx-96ItwpEGrlD00fwiK9f87SmOnJuPJzGrQ7-f_fsNI9EmWOJ3X2e5IeYsxi9pVFfkWaUmnBq89UvkOEWaCtuA2iq4K0Sno4XpVJWxmkC77FrjEFnYgdiN-pK8ZU6TFI6lQolXUnx3cXRkKMoa7KxPIiw ) :

The “primitives” model modularized Amazon’s infrastructure, effectively transforming raw data center components into storage, computing, databases, etc. which could be used on an ad-hoc basis not only by Amazon’s internal teams but also outside developers:

This AWS layer in the middle has several key characteristics:

* AWS has massive fixed costs but benefits tremendously from economies of scale.
* The cost to build AWS was justified because the first-and-best customer is Amazon’s e-commerce business. 1 ( #fn1-11519 )
* AWS’s focus on “primitives” meant it could be sold as-is to developers beyond Amazon, increasing the returns to scale and, by extension, deepening AWS’ moat.

This last point was a win-win: developers would have access to enterprise-level computing resources with zero up-front investment; Amazon, meanwhile, would get that much more scale for a set of products for which they would be the first-and-best customer.

Well, start with the fact that Amazon itself would be this logistics network’s first-and-best customer, just as was the case with AWS. This justifies the massive expenditure necessary to build out a logistics network that reduces their dependency on UPS, Fedex, et al …

However, I think it is a mistake to think that Amazon will stop there: just as they have with AWS and e-commerce distribution I expect the company to offer its logistics network to third parties, which will increase the returns to scale, and, by extension, deepen Amazon’s eventual moat.

That is exactly what Buy With Prime is: Amazon logistics, which grew large with Amazon.com as its first-and-best customer, is now available to third parties, without the limitation of needing to be on Amazon.com. That’s why my Article about the announcement was entitled Beyond Aggregation: Amazon as a Service ( https://stratechery.com/2022/beyond-aggregation-amazon-as-a-service/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIyL2JleW9uZC1hZ2dyZWdhdGlvbi1hbWF6b24tYXMtYS1zZXJ2aWNlLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNjk2NjAxODgzLCJpYXQiOjE2OTQwMDk4ODMsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBpLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiUnNldVlFOGpnSGVQcFdKZjFmQWI0QyIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.RKxmpT3HOxCOhoUEHXBjYTfisrN2h7sHDJwCEhm4Byf9QvLanRzIYmKhH3BqYoaIo0QVx26ytCGCKYuf5_4pN2TmROFx6qAjkTxZMSO5K_R3hWz6nnEFxLvy39N-IXyCJL2KXhepNwKUryxSDkzWseVt6QRHzq5FqwgfS7oO66mSN-BSwxqw2GJyX49Q0T054655H-Pm4X-7aSXyH5c6cCqpzVpMLGi97jffMy1-l_NPihymflhlBWajpSHhvlexqIW2KGm-Zi-XL9HT3NG1n-jcHZAFyUfxQE_mc2c3u9igtEbapRoHOYvBUP1O08k6rH7zq-lcv72n0TTQoQuRHg ) , and to the extent this is Amazon’s new strategy, getting Shopify and its horde of independent merchants on board is a big win.

Shopify and Its Merchants
-------------------------

Those merchants are big winners too: now Shopify merchants have access to the best logistics for their goods, which ought to increase sales. Moreover, they get access to that logistics service without having to surrender the customer relationship to Amazon. This is particularly attractive for brands that want to have their own experience outside of Amazon.com, and own the customer relationship, or better integrate with Meta ads.

It’s not without cost, of course: Buy with Prime entails keeping inventory at Amazon fulfillment centers, which means a storage fee, and then for every order there is a fulfillment fee (based on size), a prime service fee (3% of the order value), and a payment processing fee (2.4% + $0.30 — more on this in a moment). The fees are almost certainly worth it, though, because I suspect there is a good chance that giving shoppers the option to Buy with Prime will meaningfully increase conversion. First, shoppers will already have their Amazon payment information on hand; second, shoppers — at least speaking for myself, anecdotally speaking — will be more likely to buy something when they know it will be delivered with the speed and consistency of Prime. Third, a lot of Shopify merchants are probably already selling on Amazon anyways.

This, by extension, makes Buy with Prime good for Shopify. The impetus behind the asset-lite company’s now-abandoned attempt to build their own logistics service was the realization that to be competitive with Amazon, Shopify’s merchants needed a logistics service they could depend on and that customers trusted. The problem for Shopify, though, is that building such a service would have cost far more than the $1 billion they originally budgeted, and that that level of expenditure made them anything but an asset-lite software business, which made investors very unhappy. To that end, it’s a lot more compelling to just use Amazon instead of try to compete with them.

There was just one problem: Buy With Prime require(d) Amazon Payments, which fundamentally threatened Shopify. I explained in the afore-mentioned Beyond Aggregation: Amazon as a Service ( https://stratechery.com/2022/beyond-aggregation-amazon-as-a-service/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIyL2JleW9uZC1hZ2dyZWdhdGlvbi1hbWF6b24tYXMtYS1zZXJ2aWNlLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNjk2NjAxODgzLCJpYXQiOjE2OTQwMDk4ODMsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBpLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiUnNldVlFOGpnSGVQcFdKZjFmQWI0QyIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.RKxmpT3HOxCOhoUEHXBjYTfisrN2h7sHDJwCEhm4Byf9QvLanRzIYmKhH3BqYoaIo0QVx26ytCGCKYuf5_4pN2TmROFx6qAjkTxZMSO5K_R3hWz6nnEFxLvy39N-IXyCJL2KXhepNwKUryxSDkzWseVt6QRHzq5FqwgfS7oO66mSN-BSwxqw2GJyX49Q0T054655H-Pm4X-7aSXyH5c6cCqpzVpMLGi97jffMy1-l_NPihymflhlBWajpSHhvlexqIW2KGm-Zi-XL9HT3NG1n-jcHZAFyUfxQE_mc2c3u9igtEbapRoHOYvBUP1O08k6rH7zq-lcv72n0TTQoQuRHg ) :

What has changed is the composition of Shopify’s business. While the company started out with a SaaS model, the business has transformed into a commission-based one:

“Subscription Solutions” are Shopify’s platform fees, including the cost to use the platform (or upgrade to the company’s Pro offering), commissions from the sales of themes and apps, and domain name registration. “Merchant Solutions”, meanwhile, are all of the fees that are generated from ongoing sales; the largest part of this are payment processing fees from Shopify Payments…

The reason this announcement is so damaging to Shopify goes back to the transformation in the company’s revenue I charted above: Amazon’s shipping solution requires the customer to pay with Amazon; I love Shop Pay’s checkout process, but it’s not as if I don’t have an Amazon account with all of my relevant details already included, and I absolutely trust Amazon’s shipping more than I do whatever option some Shopify merchant offers me. To return to Shopify, if the company believes that their main quest is building out storefronts and incorporating AI and whatever else, and that things like logistics are side quests best handled by focused partners, then it’s pretty hard to justify not supporting Buy with Prime for any reason other than bare-knuckled self interest — and from my perspective that’s understandable! That story, though, is a much harder sell for Shopify’s merchants, who could previously be mollified by the fact that Shopify was spending serious money on building out their own logistics alternative…

Indeed, maybe that gets at where there is a potential for an agreement between Shopify and Amazon: from Amazon’s perspective the data from being the payment provider would certainly be nice to have, particularly if it can be tied back into the consumer-facing business; if Amazon really is meaningfully shifting beyond Aggregation, though, then there is value in having Shopify merchants using its logistics even without payments. And, just like before, Shopify could sweeten the deal, perhaps by cutting Amazon in on some of its payment margins (slight though they may be).

This would be a win for both sides: Shopify’s merchants could use Buy with Prime, Amazon could get the extra volume (and merchant fees) and a bit of margin (and besides, ATT makes third-party data less valuable anyways), and Shopify could well and truly focus on its main quest without having to find out whether the logistics side quest was in fact an essential defense against an opponent that could upend the board completely.

Everyone wins: Amazon, merchants, and Shopify (or, at a minimum, Shopify doesn’t lose).

The Payments Question
---------------------

This points to what is the biggest question about this deal: how exactly are payments being handled? This is how Amazon characterized the relationship in its press release ( https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20230830115954/en/ ) :

The Buy with Prime app for Shopify gives Prime members the option to select Buy with Prime on a product’s detail page before completing their order within Shopify’s Checkout. Buy with Prime offers Prime members fast, free delivery and easy returns through Amazon’s fulfillment network. After signing into their Amazon accounts, Prime members pay for their orders using a payment method from their Amazon wallets and Shopify Payments will process the payment through Shopify’s Checkout.

I haven’t seen any live Shopify pages with Buy With Prime , but Amazon’s web page ( https://buywithprime.amazon.com/shopify ) had some illustrations:

Amazon's illustration of the Buy with Prime flow ( https://buywithprime.amazon.com/shopify )

First, you obviously can only Buy with Prime goods that are a part of Prime; that is why a Shopify cart can’t mix Prime and non-Prime goods. To put it another way, you decide on your wallet and logistics provider when you select the good, not when you check out.

Secondly, while Shopify is indeed processing the payments, keep in mind that everything else is using Amazon logistics: you as a seller are paying Amazon to hold inventory, you’re being charged a fulfillment fee and a Prime order fee. That means that Amazon has a means to charge you for a return, as well, and from a user perspective they can also send the money to the correct credit card because it was selected in their wallet. As for the processing fees captured by Shopify, well, those usually aren’t returned to the merchant anyways.

The remaining question, then, is if Shopify is indeed sharing some of its payment processing profits with Amazon; the previous two sections made Amazon feel inevitable, and Shopify desperate, but this Business Insider article says its the opposite ( https://www.businessinsider.com/inside-clash-between-amazon-shopify-over-buy-with-prime-2023-9 ) :

The internet giant has been trying to get its Buy with Prime service on Shopify’s merchant platform for months. It finally happened on Wednesday. But the untold story of how it happened reveals that Shopify exerted a surprising amount of leverage over the negotiations.

One of the most interesting parts of the story is how important it was to Amazon to ensure Buy with Prime metrics appeared within the Shopify dashboard, for fear that merchants wouldn’t want to deal with multiple sources of information; another is that Amazon apparently built the vast majority of the integration, with minimal contribution from Shopify.

The bigger takeaway, though, is that Amazon seemed to want this integration much more than Shopify, and if I’m right about Buy with Prime being a major strategic initiative from the very top of the company than that makes sense. Also remember that Shopify’s biggest motivation to agree with this deal is to mollify merchants; they certainly seem to have dragged their feet. To that end, it may be that Shopify isn’t sharing any payment margin at all, particularly given that Amazon likely has returns, its biggest reason to control payments, covered.

That would certainly be a big victory for Shopify in this specific battle, but it is of course part of a broader victory for Amazon in the e-commerce war. With this deal Amazon has just dramatically increased the total addressable market for its logistics service, just as AWS long ago dramatically increased the total addressable market for Amazon’s back-end infrastructure, once again giving Amazon even better leverage on its unmatched willingness to invest.
