Welcome back to Dry Powder. I’m Bill Cohan, back in Manhattan.

Tonight, I examine the tantalizing decision facing former Morgan Stanley C.E.O. and current Disney board chair James Gorman: finally picking Bob Iger’s successor. Of course, Iger had previously narrowed the search down to four Disney insiders, but Gorman’s recent announcement that he’ll ever so slightly delay the appointment of a new C.E.O. until 2026 suggests, as Iger once famously said, that everything is on the table. My thoughts on the Gorman kremlinology are below. (By the way, feel free to suggest outside viable candidates if you have any ideas....)

But first…

* The Mooch’s Trump M&A framework: At New York’s Gramercy Theater on Monday night, I attended a live taping of The Rest Is Politics podcast, the amazingly successful collaboration between my pals The Mooch (a.k.a. Anthony Scaramucci) and Katty Kay. The Mooch, of course, is the former Goldman Sachs banker turned hedge fund manager who had a famous 11-day stint as Trump’s White House director of communications. We’ve known each other for years. (He features in my book
( https://www.amazon.com/Money-Power-Goldman-Sachs-World/dp/0767928261/ref=sr_1_2?crid=V2QP1W9GELZ4&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.pfeYfvD8mfcHCUBxO5XzdBfaMRdO-fgyu-MxRWi9NrvU4IFkmOYN7mhElh1FS3Eb892_y7dso7X1OABfjt-jnuCxEFiqccs7CWNgmaf8lF0ros_ONgXRrqkJQtDBeAD0dTzAVK05HiMIOh5Fs_pB3A_SuILzVCB34y9sPmO0NqbfGHFLaC14CqDLmTeiJcCJFE5fmytdqFTtHKtJ8RPR29FY0mWn5FUcVt_LfVD_zsY.osreiuNx7pA8NTuSGW5Nflt9lsNgghzccQ1g845g8HY&dib_tag=se&keywords=William+Cohan&qid=1730298904&sprefix=william+cohan%2Caps%2C129&sr=8-2&utm_campaign=Dry+Powder+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F30%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io ) about Goldman Sachs.) Katty is a former BBC broadcaster and now a regular on Morning Joe. The duo has another live show ( https://capitalturnaround.com/event/the-rest-is-politics/?utm_campaign=Dry+Powder+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F30%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io ) scheduled for tonight, in Washington, D.C., if you’re interested.

The Gramercy Theater show was not only packed with a young, energized crowd, it was also highly entertaining. The topic on tap, no surprise, was the upcoming election. Katty posited that Trump’s decision to hold his ill-conceived rally at Madison Square Garden was part of his primal desire—as an heir from Jamaica Estates, Queens—to be accepted in Manhattan. Katty also said she’d texted with a senior Trump campaign official before her Monday appearance on Morning Joe to find out how Mar-a-Lago was feeling about the election, and concluded that Trumpworld is “projecting a huge amount of confidence,” as my partner Tara Palmeri has expertly reported ( https://puck.news/trump-is-encouraged-by-early-return-voting-should-he-be/?utm_campaign=Dry+Powder+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F30%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io ) in recent weeks at Puck. “His senior campaign advisor said to me that this was going to be a clean sweep of all the seven battleground states, except for
Wisconsin, for Donald Trump,” adding that the Trumpsters believe he’ll win the popular vote as well. “I have rarely heard a campaign this close to a very tight election sounding as confident, if not cocky,” she said.

The Mooch wasn’t buying it. He reminded people that he worked as a surrogate on Mitt Romney’s campaign, in 2012, and the Trump campaign in 2016. In both instances, he said, there was overconfidence in the final weeks leading up to the election. “Does anyone remember President Romney?” he asked. “Anybody remember President [Hillary] Clinton?” He asked
the audience who they thought would win, and roughly 60 percent of the crowd picked Trump. “Yeah, I think anyone who tells you with confidence right now that they know who is going to win this race is smoking something,” Katty said.

The Mooch said the darker tone of Trump’s closing argument is a mistake that even his advisors wish he would abandon. Instead of being smart and focusing entirely on the economy, Scaramucci said, “He’s running on misogyny. He’s running on racism. He’s running on cruelty—he says a lot of cruel and almost diabolical things that are somewhat heartbreaking.” He said he laments the trickle-down effect of this kind of campaigning. “If the leader is going to talk like that, his minions, in order to please the leader, are going to be way more aggressive,” he said. “It’s heartbreaking for me, as a lifelong Republican, to see this happen.” Then he said something that made my ears perk up. “It’s literally like
an M&A transaction,” he said. “The Trumpists came in. They knocked out the Romneys, the McCains, they knocked out all those people, and they did an M&A. It’s really like a third party that absorbed one of the two original parties.”

Shortly before the intermission, The Mooch reminded the audience that his birthday was on January 6. “Can you believe this?” he said, to laughter. “Now you know why I need therapy.”

The recently disclosed news that the Disney board of directors, under the leadership of ex-Morgan Stanley C.E.O. James Gorman, won’t choose a successor for Bob Iger until 2026 suggested to many on Wall Street that the company might be rethinking its previous plan to promote an insider and will at least consider outside options—as it once did before selecting Michael Eisner as its leader back in the day. Gorman, of course, has lots of succession street cred and, presumably, he has free rein on the matter inside the Disney boardroom. Fresh off his remarkably successful stewardship of his own succession process at Morgan Stanley, Gorman was apparently recruited to the Disney board for this very purpose. His appointment was also pivotal in neutering Nelson Peltz, the “Smiling Crocodile,” and his rabble-rousers
at Trian Partners amid an unsuccessful proxy fight with Disney earlier this year.

As such, Gorman appears to be doing things his way, on his timeline. Indeed, it’s hard to imagine that Gorman, who previously ran an American institution almost the size of Disney, would ease into his chairmanship by simply elevating one of Iger’s final four: Jimmy Pitaro, head of ESPN; Josh D’Amaro, head of Disney’s theme parks; and Alan Bergman and Dana Walden, the top executives running Disney’s filmed entertainment divisions. And if none of the four insiders have the right stuff, it’s smart to look for it elsewhere.

Others agree. At the end of August, before the Gorman announcement, LightShed Partners’ Rich Greenfield and Brandon Ross offered an interesting prelude about the topic on their podcast. Like many others, and in accordance with the prevailing conventional wisdom, Greenfield said he didn’t think Disney would go outside the company to choose a successor. After all, the job requires an extraordinary matrix of relationships and experiences spanning Hollywood, Wall Street, Silicon Valley, and beyond—the Disney C.E.O. needs to have the dexterity to deal with everything from inbound traffic from Bryan Lourd to offering notes on the latest Star Wars script to managing the political exigencies from Tallahassee to Beijing… to say nothing of the talent and creative egos involved. “There’s something very specific
about Disney culture and process that I can’t imagine they really go outside,” Rich said. Indeed, Michael Eisner, the outsider, promoted Iger from within. And Iger, of course, promoted Bob Chapek from within, much to his later regret.

But Brandon got Rich thinking differently. “Unless the board says, You know what? These are different times, and we need a different skill set to bring in to complement what we have,” Brandon said. Rich picked up on that idea immediately, and embraced it. “You’re right,” Rich said. “Do you pick somebody … with mobile advertising experience? Do you choose somebody with streaming experience? Do you go out of the box? Do you recognize that you have to bring in someone from the outside because you don’t have the skill set internally? That is an awesome observation, Brandon—like, maybe it is time to do something quote-unquote crazy and go outside.”

Is it crazy? Of course not, but
the contenders aren’t obvious. Semi-outsiders Kevin Mayer and Tom Staggs, former longtime Disney executives and erstwhile heirs apparent, were brought in by Iger last year as consultants to help ESPN through its transition to streaming, among other things. (I’m not sure what wisdom they bring on that specific topic but whatevs.) But Mayer and Staggs have plenty of their own baggage—probably too much of it at this point to be considered viable candidates to succeed Iger, either together or alone. Their 3-year-old media company, Candle Media—which is backed by Blackstone to the tune of $1 billion and owns Moonbug Entertainment, Hello Sunshine, and Faraway Road Productions—massively overpaid for assets and has moved into what our friends at Semafor are calling the “green-eyeshade” era thanks to its $1.4 billion of expensive debt and faltering operations. Disney would likely have to acquire Candle in order to bring the duo back into the fold—a long
putt indeed.

Even though it seems unfathomable that Mayer and Staggs would return to lead Disney, is the idea of co-C.E.O.s viable in general? It might be just the solution for a company that requires so many different areas of expertise in its leaders. On the other hand, dual C.E.O.s rarely work in corporate America. True, it has worked at Netflix, which now has a market value of $323 billion, 85 percent higher than Disney’s. My bet is that Gorman would be against that idea, given his Wall Street bias, where one leader at the top is preferred. (Dual leaders worked at Goldman Sachs when it was still a private partnership.)

A less obvious idea would be a trusted, battle-tested chief executive, such as Howard Schultz. But these seasoned corporate veteran types are often on the other side of their prime. Schultz, a hypothetical example, is only two years younger than Iger, and would merely put Gorman back to work on a new plan in a couple more years.

And yet, an outsider may be what’s necessary to steer the company through both its clear forthcoming challenges—the next recession, the decline of linear TV, the rationalization of the streaming business, and the reimagination of the filmed entertainment business—and the ones that can’t be predicted. I know Disney likes to view itself as an insular company that can only be led by someone who understands the culture. (I saw this firsthand when I worked for Disney, back in my Lazard days, on the restructuring of Euro Disney.) But the company could also use a fresh voice who inspires confidence among investors and creditors, can define and communicate a strategy for these times, and has enough self-confidence to allow the very talented leaders of the various divisions—Pitaro, D’Amaro, Bergman, and Walden, among them—to do their jobs effectively. Gorman, in other words, needs all the time he can get.

Gormanology

This, in fact, is a scenario that Gorman knows well. Despite the fact that he picked Ted Pick to lead Morgan from within the bank’s own ranks, Gorman himself lived a different experience. He was an outsider at Morgan Stanley. After graduating in my class at Columbia Business School, he joined McKinsey and spent the next 12 years there before moving to Merrill Lynch, in 1999, as chief marketing officer and then as head of its brokerage business. In 2006, he left Merrill for Morgan Stanley, where he became president and chief operating officer of its wealth management business. Four years later, he was C.E.O., a job he held for the next 14 years. Morgan Stanley’s stock basically tripled during his tenure.

This is a familiar story, at least on Wall Street. Jamie Dimon had never worked a day in his life at JPMorgan Chase
before becoming the anointed successor to Bill Harrison in 2004, after JPMorgan Chase acquired Dimon’s Bank One. I know a thing or two about dysfunction at JPMorgan Chase in the years before Dimon—I was there and saw it firsthand. He was a complete outsider to what was an insular, sclerotic culture, and he utterly transformed the place into a well-oiled machine, generating more than $50 billion of net income a year and accumulating a market value of nearly $640 billion. (He has his own unresolved succession issues, as Dry Powder readers know well. But we’ll save that topic for another day…) All of which is to say that Disney is ripe for an outsider to come in and shake up the place.

And yet, one of my top Wall Street sources, with knowledge of the players, predicts that Iger’s successor will still come from inside the company—adding that while there’s a chance it would be an outsider, he said he would be “very
surprised.” Another elite Wall Street player, who knows how Gorman thinks but doesn’t have a personal or financial stake in this situation, says if there were a “solid insider,” he thinks that Disney would prefer to go in “that direction.” In my conversations with my sources on the topic, the answers are similarly equivocal. The only thing they agree upon with certainty is that the decision to empower Gorman is evidence of the market’s growing weariness with Iger’s reluctance to pass the baton once and for all.

Alas, the various enigmas around Gorman’s choice offer nothing but the one thing that Wall Street dislikes most: uncertainty. But here is one lesson that I’m sure is weighing on the former Morgan Stanley C.E.O. as he contemplates his portentous new task. After decades of choosing insiders to run the company, six years ago the board of GE went outside to hire Larry Culp—who ran Danaher, another conglomerate, and was briefly on the
GE board of directors—to succeed John Flannery as C.E.O. Then Culp, using Flannery’s strategic outline—“Project Eisenhower”—proceeded to break the company up into three parts, ending its life as America’s most successful conglomerate. Sometimes, over a long enough time horizon, the succession dramas that once only seemed life-or-death really are existential.
