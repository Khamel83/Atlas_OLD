But first…

* Frank Auerbach dies at 93: It was revealed this morning that Frank Auerbach, who arrived in London as part of a refugee program for German Jewish children during World War II (his parents were killed in Auschwitz in 1942), has died. Known for his heavily impastoed canvases, Auerbach was a contemporary of Francis Bacon and Lucian Freud, who owned an extensive amount of Auerbach’s work. David Bowie was also a fan; he owned Auerbach’s Head of Gerda Boehm, which Sotheby’s sold for £3.8 million in 2016 in an auction of the late musician’s art collection. This fall, Francis Outred and Offer Waterman
mounted an important survey ( https://www.waterman.co.uk/exhibitions/95-frank-auerbach-portraits-of-london-presented-by-offer-waterman-francis-outred/?utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F12%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io ) of Auerbach’s London landscapes.
* Warhol’s Farrah Fawcett portrait is finally being sold: The late actor Ryan O’Neal loved Andy Warhol’s portrait of Farrah Fawcett so much that he stole it back from her, won a jury trial to retain possession of it, and then tried to sell it for $20 million. This month, the piece will be auctioned ( https://www.bonhams.com/auction/29521/lot/12A/andy-warhol-1928-1987-farrah-fawcett-40-x-40-in-1016-x-1016-cm-painted-in-1980/?utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F12%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io ) at Bonhams with an estimate of $300,000.

It’s not much of a secret that O’Neal, who died at 82 last year, was a jerk to Fawcett over the course of their relationship. When Fawcett, who had been the subject of one of the bestselling posters of all time, died in 2009, O’Neal went to her house and took one of the two portraits Warhol made of Farrah, claiming that the painting was only at her house for safekeeping. In her will, she left at least one of her two Warhols to the University of Texas’s Blanton Museum. It took years and a jury trial for O’Neal to gain legal title to the artwork. In 2019, the New York Post claimed O’Neal was trying to sell the
painting for that $20 million sum, presumably because it was a Warhol.

Some of the celebrity portraits Warhol made in the ’70s and ’80s have become quite valuable, but that number was profoundly unrealistic. A Muhammad Ali, from 1977, sold for $10 million in 2019 and $18 million in 2021. Versions of Brigitte Bardot, from 1974, sold for $11.6 million and $10.5 million in 2014 and 2007, respectively. A Debbie Harry, from 1980, made $7.9 million last year, and $3 million in 2014. Princess Diana, from 1982, just made $3 million in London in March. And a John Lennon, from 1985, made $2.8 million 10 years ago. What does that make Farrah Fawcett worth? I dunno. But this one should be interesting to watch.
* Sotheby’s delayed Hong Kong sale struggles: The Hong Kong market is in hibernation, which has been inconvenient, to say the least, for the auction houses launching their new outposts in the autonomous region.
Sotheby’s had trouble gathering property for its debut sale, originally scheduled for September, so they had to regroup and hold the sales this week. The evening sale ( https://www.scmp.com/lifestyle/arts-culture/article/3286115/mark-rothko-painting-sells-30-less-2015-showing-hong-kong-market-challenges?utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F12%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io ) made HK$409 million ($52 million), with seven lots withdrawn and five lots unsold. Even in a tough sale, however, there were strong performances for works by Maria Kreyn, Shi Hu, and Zenzaburo Kojima.

Among the bigger sales was a giant blue-and-yellow Rothko that had once been owned, in turn, by Bunny Mellon and François Pinault,
which is a gold-standard provenance. Then it went to fugitive Malaysian financier Jho Low. After that, it was auctioned in 2015, for $46 million, to Azerbaijani oligarch Farkhad Akhmedov, whose wife, Tatiana, would later receive cash and art valued at $186 million in a divorce settlement. On Monday, the painting was acquired by art advisor Patti Wong for HK$252.5 million ($32.5 million), which was probably the irrevocable bid.
* Sotheby’s enhanced hammer whispers: The fallow Asian market was one reason that Sotheby’s struggled to gather property for its Hong Kong sales. Another may have been the new fee structure that the auction house put in place after the May sales, in which Sotheby’s lowered the buyer’s premium but revived a vendor’s commission, and instituted a strict policy of forbidding enhanced hammer deals. The ban on enhanced hammer deals made it difficult for the house’s specialists to do what they
do best—namely, identify the artists whose work is most in demand and secure lots by them.

Without sweeteners for lots that fell below the threshold for modifying fees or weren’t brought to market with guarantees, the Sotheby’s team was playing with one hand tied behind its back. All fall, we’ve seen anemic sale totals in London and Hong Kong. Paris dispensed with its day sale. Would New York be the same story? With big-ticket collections on offer, like Sydell Miller’s, it’s hard to tell where Sotheby’s stands with this new incentive plan. But the best way to measure the firm’s ability to land consignments is by looking at the November day sales of contemporary art. There, we have the surprise of the season: Sotheby’s aggregate estimate for the contemporary day sale is $56.7 million; Christie’s is $55.2 million. Sotheby’s was able to post that number by landing 313 consignments at an average estimate of $181,000. Christie’s came
in at 230 lots averaging $240,000. Something changed between Paris and New York. Was enhanced hammer really not a hurdle to consignors?

I’ve heard reports from art advisors that Sotheby’s may be trying to finesse its own rules by giving bespoke terms to advisors whose aggregate business with Sotheby’s meets a certain threshold. Other advisors tell me they’ve seen deal sheets that contradict the new policy. But that’s just gossip, perhaps to be expected with art advisors spitting tacks over Sotheby’s new policy. So believe that at your own risk. It would not be surprising to hear that Sotheby’s is bending its own rules to make sure the all-important November sales don’t look weak. In fact, that would be a smart move; however, time will tell whether this is the beginning of a quiet retreat in the battle of the enhanced hammer.

Cecily Brown, the 55-year-old British artist, has become the dominant painter in a long, unbroken chain of figures who have dominated the medium—and the last in a line of those making self-conscious art that responds to high culture, informed by literature, philosophy, and art history. Those unique talents are currently on display in a new show of her work at Paula Cooper Gallery and a mid-career retrospective, Cecily Brown: Themes and Variations, at the Dallas Museum of Art, which will travel to the Barnes Foundation later this year, coming on the heels of her show, Death and the Maid, at the Met last year.

In the last seven years, Brown has also become a central figure in the art market. Her own market has taken a breather this year after posting a 40 percent rise in auction volume from 2022 to 2023, when
$46 million worth of her paintings were sold. And while that amount was only good for 41st place on the list of highest-grossing artists by auction volume last year, she is far younger than the handful of other living artists on the list—Yayoi Kusama, Jasper Johns, David Hockney, and Ed Ruscha, to name a few. Her potential to keep painting is a driving force behind her market value.

It’s not clear whether Brown’s recent ascent is a product of her relative youth and the maturity of her market, or the fact that she has retaken control of the sale and distribution of her work, slowing primary sales and reconsidering placement. Whatever the cause, Brown is becoming a mainstay, and market-maker, to watch.

Remixing the Masters

The challenge in curating a retrospective of Brown’s work is that she not only paints a lot about painting—many of her works are extended encounters with the paintings of Old Masters—but also because she revises and reconsiders her own work in a circular manner that defies the usual chronological structure of a painter’s career. Dallas Museum of Art curator Katherine Brodbeck highlights this theme by prominently featuring a large, four-panel work by Brown titled Saboteur Four Times, from 2019, as the first work in her exhibition.

Brown is known to hold on to many of her paintings, often returning over time to add to a canvas or further explore an idea. But in 2019, she found a new strategy for this kind of recursive variation. First, she painted Saboteur in her now-familiar manner—taking a
figurative scene and painting over it until the image becomes abstracted. (Oscillating between the poles of figurative fidelity and the interplay of color and form is a consistent theme in Brown’s work.) Then, in Saboteur Four Times, she amplified that strategy by digitally reproducing Saboteur on four canvases, and then painting over each image in a different way, ultimately uniting the four canvases into one work.

Indeed, Brown has a habit of painting canvases separately but arranging them—and often rearranging them—into singular, large-scale works. The culmination of the show is a massive 8-by-16-foot, three-panel work titled The Splendid Table, from 2019-20. Like many of the works in the show, it comes from Dallas collectors, in this case Howard Rachofsky and Thomas Hartland-Mackie. But there are also works on display from other Dallas folks like Marguerite Hoffman and the Green family.

According to Brodbeck’s thesis, Saboteur Four Times deserves to be one of the last works in the Dallas retrospective, even if the visitor sees it first, because it points “forward to new avenues in the artist’s production.” The 30 large-scale paintings in the show look backward by dividing Brown’s work into four themes. In addition to revision, the themes include scenes of figures in landscape settings, drawing from the work of Francisco de Goya, Tintoretto, and Fragonard; images of shipwrecks and sirens referencing Géricault; and, finally, the artist’s own handling of nudes.

That last one isn’t just a gesture toward the standard artistic exercise of painting flesh. Eroticism is a wellspring for Brown’s art: Many of her early paintings began as graphic depictions of sex, even if they
became abstracted. Sexuality is also a central interpretive thesis for Brodbeck’s exhibition. “Although the recognizable content in her work ebbs and flows,” the curator writes in the catalog, “she sees the groundwork laid by her early paintings as adding a sexual charge that is present even as the work becomes more abstract; indeed, she defines sexuality as more of an energy or tension in her work that keeps the viewer engaged in the endless push and pull of her compositions.” These themes, in turn, lead to the “innovative use of genres and tropes to dismantle the objectification of women throughout art history.”

You can see these themes play themselves out in The 5 Senses ( https://www.paulacoopergallery.com/exhibitions/cecily-brown3?utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F12%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io#tab:slideshow;tab-1:thumbnails ), Brown’s show at Paula Cooper, in Chelsea, in which the artist draws from allegorical paintings by Jan Brueghel the Elder and Peter Paul Rubens. The show also includes works on paper that elucidate Brown’s rarely seen fidelity in draftsmanship.

For any artist, two major museum shows in two years—at the Met and in Dallas, no less—is a lot. Brown’s last show was at the prestigious Louisiana Museum in Denmark in 2018. But as her institutional and international recognition grows, she’s poised to fill a role once held by an artist like Gerhard Richter: Her work is intellectually rigorous and deeply engaged. It’s also easily recognizable. It can be appreciated through color and abstraction but also, as Brodbeck is keen to demonstrate, contemplated in
political, aesthetic, and art-historical terms. In that sense, Cecily Brown’s work presents the art market with a conspicuous opportunity to grow.

Endnotes…

Cecily Brown may not be an abstract painter, strictly speaking. But it is hard not to see her as something of a godmother to a generation of young female abstractionists. As luck would have it, The New Yorker released Rebecca Mead’s profile ( https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2024/11/18/the-intensely-colorful-work-of-a-painter-obsessed-with-anime?utm_campaign=Wall+Power+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F12%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io ) of Jadé Fadojutimi yesterday. The writer reveals Fadojutimi’s bipolar disorder diagnosis—on top of having A.D.H.D. and possibly synesthesia—and recent institutionalization. As Fadojutimi notes to
Mead:

* “What was shocking to me was how scary it can be to be told that you have got to live with loads of people that are unwell, and you are saying you are not unwell, and they are also saying they are not unwell—but they really are, and you really are.”

Fadojutimi’s art can hardly be reduced to her mental health challenges, as the profile makes clear. Read it—and let me know what you think.
