Capital solutions

There are only so many ways to invest in a company. If the company has a
promising future ahead of it, you can buy its equity and get a stake in its
success (or failure). If the company has a steady business with recurring cash
flows, you can lend it money against those cash flows. If the company has some
good assets, you can lend it money against those assets. Those are the main
ideas. There are various hybrids. If you think the company could have a lot of
upside but you want some downside protection — or you want to bet on volatility
— there are convertible bonds. There are hybrid bank capital securities. A few
other things.

The list isn’t that long, and it doesn’t change that much. Michael Milken once
discovered <https://www.britannica.com/money/Michael-R-Milken> that actually
junk-rated companies mostly generate enough cash to pay back their bonds, and
that was a pretty epochal discovery in the history of finance. “If a company
has a steady-ish business with decent-ish cash flows, you can lend it money
against those cash flows, if you charge a lot of interest” added whole new
dimensions to financial markets. There are not a ton of discoveries like that.

But you can look elsewhere. One promising source of new discoveries is: You
can invest in a company bytaking money from someone else. If a company has
raised $100 to run its business, and has promised the investors who put in that
$100 a reasonable rate of return, and then it loses money and wants to raise
more from you, it might not have anything left to promise you. Everything is
already promised to the previous investors, and there’s not enough money to pay
everyone back, never mind to pay you too. And the solution is: You go to the
company and say “here, I’ll lend you another $20, but in return you have to
stiff your previous investors and pay me back first.” And then the company can
somehow generate $120 of financing from only $100 (or less) of value. New
financing has been created as if by magic. Not really. I mean, new financing
has been created by taking value from someone else who was already promised it.

This too can be quite fruitful. I mentioned Milken, whose junk bond financing
was essential to the leveraged buyout boom of the 1980s.Another theory of the
LBO boom
<https://www.chicagofed.org/publications/chicago-fed-letter/1989/january-17>
goes like this: Private equity firms discovered that they could create value by
taking it from bondholders. You had some nice company with some modest level of
debt, and the debt was cheap — it paid a low interest rate — because the
company’s steady cash flows amply covered the debt costs. And then you bought
the company and added much more debt, which was expensive (because there wasn’t
enough cash to comfortably cover all the debt), but the old debt didn’t have to
be paid back yet, andit was still cheap. If the company had $100 million of
bonds outstanding, and after the LBO those bonds dropped from 100 to 70 cents
on the dollar, then that’s $30 million of value that the bondholders lost —
that maybe the shareholders (or the private equity firm) got.

Over time you’d expect trades like that to go away — you’d expect more bonds
to have change-of-control puts, for instance — because nobody wants to be on
the other side of this trade, the side that loses money to create a profitable
financing opportunity for someone else. But the meta-trade —find some new way
to take value from someone else — doesn’t have to go away; it just gets more
complicated and difficult. Which is … a fun challenge? The rewards to skill go
up.

In the 2020s, the popular trade is: You have some distressed company. It would
like to borrow more money, but doesn’t have great prospects or cash flows to
promise to new lenders. You lookreally really carefully at the existing debt
documents, and you discover that page 114 of the first-lien credit agreement
says “actually, if the company wants, it can create new loans that rank ahead
of this first-lien loan, hope no one is reading this ha ha.” And so you do
that: You create a new super-priority loan, you lend the company some more
money, you’re first in line to get paid back, you extract value for yourself to
back your investment, and everyoneelse — the people who put in money before
you, before the company was distressed — get less.

In practice the credit agreement never says that; it says some more
complicated thing. (It hasbaskets for permitted investments
<https://www.torys.com/en/our-latest-thinking/publications/2019/05/the-j-crew-trap-door-and-its-implications-for-the-future-of-leveraged-finance>
 by restricted subsidiaries in unrestricted subsidiaries, for instance.) You
have to look very carefully indeed at the existing debt documents. But, again,
that’s good, that’s the fun challenge, that’s the reward to skill.

In some sense this is very dumb
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/newsletters/2019-03-21/money-stuff-find-your-place-in-the-world-of-jpmorgan?cmpid=BBD081324_MONEYSTUFF&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=240813&utm_campaign=moneystuff>
, a wasteful and destructive battle of cleverness between investors that slices
a shrinking pie in tricky ways rather than creating anything new. In another
sense I find it weirdly beautiful? You’ve got some companies that need money,
and there’s a long line of hedge funds willing to meet with them with the pitch
“we will give you money, as long as you help us take the money from someother
hedge funds thatpreviously gave you money.” It’s a new source of financing for
the real economy, hedge funds taking money from each other.

One can wonder about the long-term effects of this. If you discover some new
source of value, or some new way to slice the value, you can grow the pie of
stuff that can be financed. If you discover some new way to take value from old
creditors to give it to new creditors, then the equilibrium is for creditors to
be more nervous? Like, write better covenants, or charge higher interest, or
try not to lend to aggressive private equity sponsors. In practice my view is
always that financial markets have extremely short memories, and everyone who
is hosed by these maneuvers says “I will never lend to a private equity firm
with weak covenants again!” and then forgets that resolution in 15 minutes.
Also I think a lot of the people who are hosed by these maneuvers instead say

an ecosystem of mutual one-upmanship develops:

Crypto pyramid Ponzi scheme

The core idea of a pyramid scheme is:

 * You recruit some members to invest some money in the scheme.
 * You take their money.
 * Those members also recruit new members to invest more money in the scheme.
 * You take their money, give some of it to the Step 1 members as a reward for
recruiting them, and keep the rest for yourself.
 * The Step 3 members also recruit new members to invest even more money in
the scheme.
 * You take their money, give some of it to the Step 3 members who recruited
them, give some of it to the Step 1 members who recruited the Step 3 members
who recruited them, and keep some of it for yourself.
 * Etc.
The people who get into the scheme early and recruit a lot of new members (who
recruit a lot of new members, etc., recursively) can actually make a lot of
money, all of it from the later members; the people who get in late are
suckers. Occasionally people do this literally and with their eyes open, but
(1) it’s generally illegal and (2) it is somewhat evidentlybad (you should not
get in late!), so most pyramid schemes are embedded in other “multilevel
marketing” businesses. The idea there is:

 * You recruit some members to invest some money.
 * That money goes to buy the product, which the members are supposed to sell.
 * Those members also recruit new members to invest more money.
 * Some of that money goes to buy a product, but some of it goes to the Step 1
members who recruited them.
 * Etc.
If everyone in the pyramid makes money mostly by selling products, that’s …
fine-ish; if everyone makes moneymostly by recruiting each other, that’s bad.
The lines can be blurry
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2016-07-15/herbalife-will-have-to-live-without-mansions-and-private-jets?cmpid=BBD081324_MONEYSTUFF&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=240813&utm_campaign=moneystuff>
.

If you are doing this sort of lightly disguised pyramid scheme, one important
element is that the product can’t be all that expensive for you to make. You
want high margins on whatever the fig-leaf product is, so you can give most of
the new recruits’ money to their recruiters (and yourself), rather than using
it to pay for whatever they’re supposed to be selling.

Possibly the cheapest possible product is fake crypto trading? You recruit
some members to invest some money, the money goes to a crypto investment fund,
the members recruit new members to invest money,their money goes to a crypto
investment fund, etc. And the crypto investment fund generates such large
consistent profits that (1) the new recruits can get a nice return from their
investment, (2) you can use some of the returns toalso pay large recruiting
rewards and (3) you can keep some of the returns for yourself.

The way that the crypto investment fund can generate these juicy returns is by
being fake. Youjust take the new members’ money, give it to the old members
(and yourself), and hope that the new members will do enough recruiting
themselves to keep it all going. It is both a pyramid scheme — one in which
members are paid mostly for recruiting new members — and a Ponzi scheme — one
in which investors’ returns are paid for by new investors.

Brock Pierce

A well-known phenomenon in the tech and financial industries is that a person
gets very rich through the ruthless application of skill in some specific
domain, and then decides, based on that experience, that he is an all-purpose
expert who probably has a good idea how to solve all of the world’s problems.
And because hedid get very rich, by being very good at software or finance, he
has a lot of money and influence and people who respect him, so he has some
chance to implement his ideas. Sometimes they’re good! He is, after all, pretty
smart and hardworking. Sometimes they are less good.

Some people got very rich in crypto through the ruthless application of skill
in technology or finance, but in the early days of crypto quite a few people
got very rich through … I think it would be reasonable to call it luck? Like
there are probably some people who bought 10,000 Bitcoins to pay for drugs
online in 2011, and spent 2,000 Bitcoins on the drugs, and forgot about their
change, and remembered it when Bitcoin hit $50,000 in 2021. [1]  <> For a brief
amazing period, forgetting your online drug money was a viable path to
generational wealth. And of course if you got rich that way, (1) you’d have a
lot of money, (2) the money could buy influence and (3)you’d probably think
that you are an all-purpose expert who probably has a good idea how to solve
all of the world’s problems, because honestlyeveryone thinks that, whether or
not they have a lot of money. But with the money, you’d have some chance to
implement your ideas. Sometimes they’d be good! But, you know. Less often.

 * Stories of historic astrology-focused investment firms that … might have

 * Speculation about how an astrology investment strategy could work. The main
