Welcome back to Dry Powder. I’m Bill Cohan.

While we all attempt to make sense of whatever the hell just happened—I’ve got some thoughts on today’s stock market explosion below—tonight, I’m digging into the revelatory, 600-page proxy statement chronicling David Ellison and RedBird Capital’s takeover of Paramount Global. It’s a surreal corporate saga, chock-full of jilted suitors and mangled offers, that may provide a bit of levity amid the all-consuming news out of Washington.

But first…

* Measuring the Trump bump on Wall Street: While the repercussions of Trump’s emphatic victory last night continue to reverberate throughout the
country—I guess the election wasn’t rigged after all—it’s making quite a statement on Wall Street, too. For reasons that are a little hard to fathom given how much the stock markets have already advanced in 2024, they exploded on Wednesday in the wake of Trump’s return. Those of us old enough to remember Trump 1.0, eight years ago, will recall that after he squeaked out his shocking victory over Hillary Clinton, the stock markets crashed 1,000 points overnight, on a much smaller Dow Jones Industrial Average number. This time, the DJIA skyrocketed, up some 1,500 points, or 3.6 percent, while the Nasdaq was up 544 points, or 3 percent.

Other indicators of market sentiment also rose. Bitcoin traded up nearly 8 percent to a new high of around $75,000 per coin. The dollar rose, too, against a basket of currencies. This has been dubbed generally “The Trump Trade,” as investors reset their bets for whatever it is they believe Trump II will be
about. “There’s a lot of emotion, there’s a lot of euphoria, based on perspective in markets today,” Marta Norton, chief investment strategist at Empower Investments, told CNBC. “That doesn’t necessarily mean that’s the way markets are going to trade in perpetuity.”

On the other hand, the yield on the 10-year Treasury shot up, to 4.45 percent, from around 4.25 percent the day before, with a corresponding drop in the price of the 10-year bond, probably out of concern for the changes coming in Trump’s fiscal policy and what his new chairman of the Federal Reserve will do to monetary policy. (Bye bye, Jay Powell, you had a nice run.) Another new Trump bellwether, Tesla, was up 15 percent on the day. Elon Musk is now worth $264 billion.

Meanwhile, the roller coaster that is DJT stock—Trump Media and Technology—which many prognosticators thought might hit $100 a share with a Trump victory, opened at
around $45 a share, up 32 percent from yesterday’s close. But the stock has since come back to Earth somewhat, up around 6 percent on the day, to around $36 per share. The people who bought at $45 a share have my sympathy. Where any of this goes from here is anyone’s guess.

WILLIAM D. COHAN ( https://puck.news/author/william-cohan/?utm_campaign=Dry+Powder+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F6%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io )

As a former M&A guy, I always look forward to the “Background of the Transaction” section of the proxy, which is usually filed when there is a material transaction involving a public company—a merger, a move to go private, or some other change of control. And so, I’ve been salivating to dive into the proxy related to the David Ellison-RedBird Capital takeover of Paramount Global.

I was not disappointed. The proxy is chock-full of jilted suitors and mangled, muddled offers from both likely and unlikely suspects. It lays bare all the machinations that took place for the six months or so between the start of the sale process and its conclusion.

As you will recall, the Ellisons and RedBird are essentially replacing the Redstone family as the controlling shareholders of Paramount Global, injecting new capital into
the company to pay down debt, combining Ellison’s Skydance Media with Paramount Pictures, and installing a new management team led by Jeff Shell. Paramount Global will remain a publicly traded company, controlled by David and his father, Larry Ellison, and the good folks at RedBird Capital.

You will also recall that, except for the ongoing regulatory approvals, which will delay the closing until well into 2025—a broadcast license at CBS is being conveyed to new owners, after all—no shareholder vote is required. Indeed, as the proxy makes clear—on page 164 of the nearly 600-page document—the deal has already been approved by the only shareholder that matters: Shari Redstone. Interestingly, the document also makes clear, in bold type, that a proxy was not necessary since there was no shareholder vote related to the deal. (Usually, a proxy statement is prepared and filed—and costs related to it
incurred—so that shareholders will have all the information they need to make a decision on whether to vote for a proposed transaction. That’s not the case here, since the Redstones made their decision and voted their shares by written consent.) But I’m told the proxy was necessary because new Paramount securities are going to be issued as part of the deal to its new owners, known for now as New Pluto Global Inc. (A new name is forthcoming.)

But let’s be thankful that a proxy has been filed. As a result, we can learn a variety of new things about the deal, a number of them highly surprising.

Centerview’s Payday & Comcast Curiosities

First, my hat goes off to the special committee of the Paramount board of directors and its advisors at Cravath and Centerview. The proxy makes clear that they worked their asses off around the clock for seven months or so, spending nearly every day on this one matter. Of course, the proxy also makes clear how well paid they were for their work. Cravath’s fees were not disclosed in the proxy, but at $2,000-plus an hour, given the multitude of meetings, Zooms, correspondence, and deal documents… that has got to add up. The proxy does confirm my reporting that Centerview is making around $60 million for its work on the deal, including a $51 million success fee, a $2 million signing fee, a $2.5 million fee for issuing a fairness opinion, and payments of $250,000 a month.

My friend Blair Effron and his Centerview team worked
hard here, especially after Shari appeared to blow up the deal in July. After the deal with Ellison and RedBird was consummated, Centerview contacted 58 parties amid the contractual “go shop” period, seven of which kicked the tires, including the pathetic effort by Edgar Bronfman Jr., who inevitably bowed out and cleared the way for the deep-pocketed Ellisons. Still, even with all that work, it’s a nice payday for Centerview. The proxy revealed that the members of the special committee were also getting paid extra for their work: $10,000 a month each, from January onward, plus $2,000 per meeting and $1,000 per hour spent on “litigation-related matters” pertaining to the assignment.

There were, according to the proxy, several more active competitors than previously known, or at least well known. Take, for instance, Comcast—referred to in the proxy as “Party B.” Its effort to form a streaming joint venture between Peacock and
Paramount+ is especially interesting in light of Mike Cavanagh’s comments last Thursday that Comcast is open to considering just such a partnership for its money-losing service. According to the proxy, on January 11, former Paramount C.E.O. Bob Bakish also spoke with Comcast C.E.O. Brian Roberts about a full-scale acquisition—an idea that seems far-fetched given Comcast’s ownership of NBC and Paramount’s ownership of CBS, but whatevs.

In a January 26 meeting between Paramount management and Comcast management, Roberts explained that Comcast was no longer interested in a streaming joint venture or buying Paramount. Instead, Brian said he wanted a licensing deal to get Paramount+ content on Peacock. But at a subsequent meeting, on February 20, Brian said he was interested in the streaming joint venture, so long as Comcast had majority control. The special committee authorized the streaming joint venture
discussions with Comcast, and those talks continued until April, including the sharing of confidential information covered by N.D.A.s. Two months! Who knew?

In mid-March, Bakish actually recommended to the special committee that the streaming pact with Comcast could be part of a “multi-step plan,” along with the sale of Paramount’s movie studio, to maximize value for shareholders and solve some of the company’s operational problems. Bakish also told the committee that the streaming venture with Comcast would require “a substantial cash contribution” on Paramount’s part. The special committee then decided they had better gauge Shari’s support for a sale of key Paramount assets before deciding whether to move forward with Comcast. On March 30, Shari told the special committee that she preferred that they enter into exclusive discussions with Ellison/RedBird, even if it meant ending the dialogue with Comcast. And that was that.

Enter Zaslav

There were also the discussions with David Zaslav, at Warner Bros. Discovery, referred to as “Party A” in the proxy. As my partner Matt Belloni explored ( https://puck.news/paramounts-deal-dance-costners-hubris-clints-revenge/?utm_campaign=Dry+Powder+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F6%2F24%29&utm_source=customer.io ) on Monday night, the conversations between WBD and Paramount were more extensive than previously known. On December 19, as was reported at the time by Axios, Bakish and Zaz met to discuss a “business combination” between Paramount and WBD. On January 11, the special committee authorized Bakish to “engage directly” with Zaz to talk about a deal. Bakish and Zaz spoke that day and
discussed convening a meeting among the broader management teams of the two companies. The special committee then authorized the conversations with WBD to continue.

But on January 16, NAI’s advisors, BDT & MSD Partners, splashed some cold water on further discussions with WBD. Byron Trott’s firm, which had made a $175 million preferred stock investment in NAI, noted that such a combination would “pose heightened regulatory risk,” causing long delays and potentially the further deterioration of Paramount’s business.

Still, despite BDT & MSD’s concerns, the special committee authorized Cravath to negotiate an N.D.A. with WBD. For the next couple months, through March 2024, WBD and Paramount conducted mutual due diligence. But at a meeting on January 23, Bakish and Zaz decided that while a deal “had significant synergy potential,” there were big problems, too, because “both Paramount and [WBD] were highly levered and exposed to
challenging industry trends,” according to the proxy. “The participants also discussed next steps with respect to exploring a potential business combination with [WBD], including the regulatory environment that such a transaction would likely encounter.”

On February 21, after Paramount and WBD had shared lots of confidential information with each other, the special committee decided that nothing additional would be shared with WBD until an actual transaction proposal was submitted. A week later, Zaz told Bakish on a call that he’d been in touch with Shari at NAI, and realized that between her desire to receive cash for her stake in Paramount, and her valuation expectations for that stake, a deal between Paramount and WBD would be “challenging.” On March 1, the special committee, apparently fed up with WBD and Zaz, “expressed skepticism regarding [WBD’s] intentions.”

That same day, WBD’s representatives told Centerview that, without
Shari’s support, it made little sense for WBD to pursue a deal for Paramount, which would be an all-stock transaction and therefore probably not in the cards. According to the proxy, WBD never actually submitted that transaction proposal. Still, on April 11, Zaz called Centerview and said WBD would still be interested… in an all-stock deal. He was hanging around the hoop, but nothing came of it, and that was that.

The Apollo Moon Shot

There were other curious subplots to this saga. For instance, Apollo (“Party C”) was all over this thing pretty much from the outset, with a variety of creative and interesting ideas, none of which went very far. On March 6, Apollo offered $11 billion for the Paramount studio and some of the company’s film and television studios. On March 22, the special committee decided to continue exploring “the merits” of Apollo’s proposal. Later that day, NAI informed the special committee that it would not support the sale to Apollo at anything like the proposed $11 billion, or even for twice that amount.

The next day, Apollo sent a letter to the special committee adding that it would pay between $2.5 billion and $3 billion for all the voting shares of Paramount Global, including those owned by the Redstones—this
was new news—and might be willing to partner with third parties to buy all of Paramount. Then, on March 26, Apollo sent in a letter saying it wanted to buy all of Paramount, but without offering specifics.

On March 31, Apollo added the specifics—proposing to buy all of Paramount for between $26 billion and $27 billion, including the assumption of Paramount’s debt. But NAI still viewed the Apollo proposal as “not an actionable offer” and not worth risking the Ellison/RedBird deal. (Why Shari felt that way is not clear—$27 billion seems like a generous price for Paramount.) On April 2, Apollo sent another letter: Its offer would be all-cash, and it had plenty of money to support the purchase price of the equity. On April 3, though, the special committee opted to give exclusivity to Ellison/RedBird anyway.

The next anyone heard from Apollo was on May 1, when the firm sent a letter to the special committee outlining a $26 billion all-cash
offer—noting they were now teamed up with Sony Pictures—to buy all of Paramount Global at $28 a share for the voting stock and $17 a share for the nonvoting stock, premiums of 35 percent and 49 percent, respectively, from where those shares were trading. They also offered to acquire the Redstones’ stock in Paramount for $1.8 billion. On May 16, the two sides executed an N.D.A.

But the special committee and its advisors eventually complained about the “slow pace” of the discussions with Apollo and Sony—likely because of the increasing regulatory concerns about a combination between Sony and Paramount. On May 27, the pace of the discussions with Apollo and Sony was so slow that the advisors to the special committee determined that Ellison/RedBird was the best option, and maybe the only one. Finally, when a potential deal with Sony appeared to come a cropper, on July 3, Apollo sent a new letter, offering to make a $4 billion preferred equity
investment in Paramount that the company could use to deleverage its capital structure and buy back stock. But that proposal went nowhere, too. Apollo—0 for 4. The Ellison/RedBird deal was announced on July 7.

“Party F”

There remain a number of mystery bidders, referred to only by capital letters in the proxy. The one that intrigues me most is “Party F,” a private-equity firm focused on middle-market companies. On February 29, Party F sent a letter to Bakish and Shari offering to contribute an “entertainment studio” it owned to Paramount in exchange for a 19.9 percent stake in Paramount Global. Party F also wanted Paramount to end its dual-class stock structure and offered to pay a to-be-determined amount to the voting shareholders in exchange for their rights, in effect making Party F the new controlling shareholder simply by contributing its “entertainment studio” and offering some cash. Nice work if you can get it.

No surprise, the proposal was of little interest. On April 17, Party F contacted Shari again about its proposal. But she did
not respond. Centerview spoke with Party F on May 26, but there was no indication that it was willing to sweeten its offer. On June 6, Party F again tried with NAI, but that effort also went nowhere. Who the heck is Party F? I’d like to know. (Please send me your best guesses and I’ll share them in a future edition of Dry Powder.)

Anyway, it’s fair to say the special committee and its advisors did their job, and were well compensated for their efforts. You may not like the Ellison/RedBird deal for Paramount, and the stock may never achieve the $30 to $40 a share that the new owners are hoping for, but you can’t say that any stone was left unturned in pursuit of the ultimate buyer for the company.
