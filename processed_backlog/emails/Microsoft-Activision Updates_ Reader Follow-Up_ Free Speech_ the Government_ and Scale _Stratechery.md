Microsoft-Activision Updates
----------------------------

Two quick updates on the Microsoft-Activision case:

* First, the FTC is appealing ( https://www.theverge.com/2023/7/12/23791274/ftc-microsoft-activision-blizzard-appeal ) the district judge’s opinion; it is true that any meaningful change in the interpretation of antitrust law would have to happen in the appeals process (albeit likely at the Supreme Court level), so this isn’t a surprise, but I am skeptical of the FTC’s prospects.
* Second, the U.K.’s Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) said that it would only approve the Microsoft deal after a new investigation following a restructuring of the deal terms ( https://www.reuters.com/markets/deals/uks-cma-says-new-restructured-microsoft-activision-deal-could-require-new-probe-2023-07-12/ ) ; it’s not clear how long this might take.

I honestly have no idea what happens next; my best guess is that Microsoft does close a restructured deal and hopes for the best in the U.K., which is at this point the only competition authority opposing the deal.

The problem with the entire framing here is that monopoly cases can ONLY rest on theory, or they can never be successful. I, unsurprisingly, pretty strongly disagree with this email, but I think the reasons why are interesting and worth posting about in an Update.

First, I didn’t go into the judge’s history and possible bias (their son works for Microsoft) because the judge was effectively picked by the FTC (Page 20 of the opinion ( https://storage.courtlistener.com/recap/gov.uscourts.cand.413969/gov.uscourts.cand.413969.305.0_1.pdf ) explains that Microsoft wanted the case moved to the DC Circuit, while the FTC wanted it consolidated with another relevant case that was already presided over by Judge Corley). If the FTC didn’t think the bias was an issue before the decision, then it seems odd to raise it as an issue now (I’ll come back to this bit in a moment).

Second, I’m unclear why the emailer is talking about monopoly when Sony is dominant in the console space. There certainly seem to be many steps between here and there — if that is indeed where Microsoft is going.

That leads to the third point: I appreciate the emailer’s compliment of my business analysis, and my analysis has maintained ever since the Zenimax deal ( https://stratechery.com/2020/microsoft-and-zenimax-microsoft-ignite-microsoft-and-covid/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIwL21pY3Jvc29mdC1hbmQtemVuaW1heC1taWNyb3NvZnQtaWduaXRlLW1pY3Jvc29mdC1hbmQtY292aWQvIl19LCJleHAiOjE2OTE4MzQ0NzcsImlhdCI6MTY4OTI0MjQ3NywiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcGkucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCBlbnRpdGxlbWVudHMiLCJzdWIiOiJSc2V1WUU4amdIZVBwV0pmMWZBYjRDIiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.eFzdmJNPoQNMIf2s0OP98NGTPhPDA3fVF-5XpBW6cKCP8aMnFbxTC5rcZ-RhrjMKzdiegLRY_2f6ScPkk3y7STp_Q4Qs2VFeHXxDGep-EW6KXZGOHPyJvZDK2upj75wR-lLgP0jJwAD1eERBfPXYXnQPeCNre4urq5BnkJ5Ag8JTBcKhqVa5kaJf50EHFjRILbuf2i8pLKk-nj3D87pM4rYY8u8ir9mt-NmLh7wGLuFBTp1WW7SHs1CkP6K8H2MqRxjb8-_SnrOoXi3p72oS627r-UGQdWb5SWduRejcgl239s8ZiOBrOXYtVTRolu18An1zIjED-5x13R7xc8QcEQ ) that Microsoft is making these large acquisitions in order to build a new business model for video games (i.e. Xbox Game Pass); this analysis has the advantage of providing an explanation for these acquisitions other than vertical foreclosure, which would be massively value destructive (because the price Microsoft is paying is based on sales that include 3rd-party platforms). The fact that the FTC’s own discovery process overwhelmingly supported this view — both in terms of documents from Microsoft and Activision and also Sony — gives me confidence that I was right.

Fourth, I will note that the U.K. CMA’s objection to the deal actually makes some amount of sense: obviously Microsoft is trying to win in the cloud subscription space. The reason why I think the objection is a mistake is that multiple companies have already failed in that space, and I think that space is competitive with consoles, not independent from it, which means I see Microsoft’s moves as increasing competition with Sony; to put it another way, I think the CMA’s concern about potential future monopolization is foreclosing innovation and competition in the present. Here’s the thing, though: Microsoft isn’t being secretive about their goals! This isn’t some grand conspiracy — indeed, this point follows directly on from the prior one.

Fifth, my views on antitrust have changed: I have become much more opposed to the FTC and the DOJ acting in general because I have been shocked at how blatantly political their actions seem to be (this is a lesson that is bi-partisan, I would note — see Trump and the AT&T-Time Warner case, for example). Moreover, the tendency of the neo-Brandeis movement to engage in ad hominem attacks is a genuine turn-off: look no further than the emailer’s immediate questioning of the judge’s legitimacy, or of mine. I am genuinely bothered by insinuations or outright attacks I have received questioning my integrity just because I stated an opinion that — it’s worth noting! — courts ended up agreeing with.

The root of my analysis is this: I think that antitrust laws are rooted in the physical world, which is governed by scarcity, which means control of supply is the means to monopoly; in contrast I believe that most tech companies are rooted in the digital world, which is governed by abundance, which means control of demand is the means to market power, and, if anything, monopsony. Given that, I think the best approach is to push for new laws (some ideas of which I discuss on the upcoming episode of Sharp Tech), not to try and bend old laws to a fundamentally different reality. Now that case after case has failed, I would think that the ones who might consider changing their approach are folks like my emailer.

I still love getting the feedback though: that’s what free speech is all about.

Free Speech, the Government, and Scale
--------------------------------------

Speaking of free speech, last week from the Wall Street Journal ( https://www.wsj.com/articles/judge-rules-biden-administration-likely-trampled-on-free-speech-on-social-media-29334362 ) :

A federal judge issued a broad preliminary injunction limiting the federal government from communicating with social-media companies about online content, ruling that Biden administration officials’ policing of social-media posts likely violated the First Amendment.

In a 155-page ruling issued Tuesday, U.S. District Judge Terry Doughty of Louisiana barred White House officials and multiple federal agencies from contacting social-media companies with the purpose of suppressing political views and other speech normally protected from government censorship. The judge’s injunction came in a lawsuit led by the Republican attorneys general of Missouri and Louisiana who alleged that the Biden administration fostered a sprawling “federal censorship enterprise” in its effort to stamp out what it viewed as rampant disinformation circulating on social media. The government, the lawsuit claimed, pressured social-media platforms to scrub away disfavored views about Covid-19 health policies, the origins of the pandemic, the Hunter Biden laptop story, election security and other divisive topics.

I’m not going to get into the actual ruling in this Update, but rather want to highlight three responses that stood out to me, and in conjunction say something interesting.

First, Mike Masnick has a post at Techdirt ( https://www.techdirt.com/2023/07/06/the-good-the-bad-and-the-incredibly-ugly-in-the-court-ruling-regarding-government-contacts-with-social-media/ ) examining “The Good, The Bad, and the Incredibly Ugly” aspects of the ruling; to take a section from each (and to be clear, I’m using Masnick’s labels here):

There are legitimate concerns about government intrusions into private companies and their 1st Amendment protected decisions…There were some examples in the lawsuit that did seem likely to cross the line, including having officials in the White House complaining about certain tweets and even saying “wondering if we can get moving on the process of having it removed ASAP.” That’s definitely inappropriate. Most of the worst emails seemed to come from one guy, Rob Flaherty, the former “Director of Digital Strategy,” who seemed to believe his job in the White House made it fine for him to be a total jackass to the companies, constantly berating them for moderation choices he disliked…

Also, the ruling highlights that lawmakers threatening to revoke or modify Section 230 as part of the process of working the refs at these social media companies is a form of retaliation. This is a surprising finding, but a good one. We’ve highlighted in the past that politicians threatening to punish companies with regulatory changes in response to speech should be seen as a 1st Amendment violation, and had people yell at us (on both sides) about that. But here, Judge Doughty agrees, and highlights 230 reform as an example.

From Masnick’s “Bad” section:

Doughty seems incredibly willing to include perfectly reasonable conversations about how to respond to actually problematic content as “censorship” and “coercion,” despite there being little evidence of either in many cases (again, in some cases, it does appear that some folks in the administration crossed the line). For example, it’s public information (as we’ve discussed) that various parts of the government would meet with social media not for “censorship” but to share information, such as about foreign trolls seeking to disrupt elections with false information, or about particular dangers. These meetings were not about censorship, but just making everyone aware of what was going on. But conspiracy-minded folks have turned those meetings into something they most definitely are not.

Yet Doughty assumes all these meetings are nefarious. In doing so, Doughty often fails to distinguish perfectly reasonable speech by government actors that is not about suppressing speech, but rather debunking or countering false information — which is traditional counterspeech. Now, again, when government actors are doing it, their speech is actually less protected (Posner’s ruling in the Dart case details this point), but so long as their speech is not focused on silencing other speech, it’s perfectly reasonable.

From Masnick’s “Ugly” section:

Judge Doughty seems to believe every nonsense conspiracy around regarding the culture war and false claims of social media deliberately stifling “conservatives.” This is despite multiple studies showing that they actually bent over backwards to allow conservatives to regularly break the rules to avoid claims of bias…

The court is only aware of such speech being moderated because that’s all the plaintiffs in this case highlighted (often through exaggeration). Second, many of the contested actions happened under the Trump administration, and it would make no sense that a Republican administration would be seeking to suppress “conservative” speech. Third, the whole issue is that the companies were choosing to hold back dangerous false information that they feared would lead to real world harms. If it was true that such speech came more frequently from so-called “conservatives,” that’s on them. Not the government.

Second, Patrick McKenzie has a Twitter thread ( https://twitter.com/patio11/status/1676592554211459072 ) that makes what I think is an essential observation in response to a story of one specific misguided takedown:

( https://twitter.com/patio11/status/1676583713478676480?ref_src=twsrc%5Etfw%7Ctwcamp%5Etweetembed%7Ctwterm%5E1676583713478676480%7Ctwgr%5E507ce0f056179c5b7d0da0888aa700e36bfb003b%7Ctwcon%5Es1_&ref_url=http%3A%2F%2Fchrome-headless.local%3A9222%2Fatomic )

McKenzie’s argument is that the reality of moderation is not about taking measured decisions over each piece of content, but rather producing scalable systems; if that scalable system assumes that government feedback is valid and actionable, then the practical effect is government censorship, even if the vast majority of feedback is not combined with the sort of threats that Masnick noted. In other words, McKenzie is making the case that distinguishing between “the good, the bad, and the ugly” is fine in theory, but in practice — at scale — it’s all indistinguishable.

Third, this paragraph from the New York Times story ( https://www.nytimes.com/2023/07/05/us/politics/social-media-ruling-government.html ) about the case was remarkable:

The case was brought by two Republican attorneys general and five individuals who campaigned against masks, argued that vaccines did not work, opposed lockdowns and pushed drugs that medical experts denounced as ineffective, like ivermectin and hydroxychloroquine. And it is being overseen by Judge Terry A. Doughty, who was appointed by President Donald J. Trump and has previously expressed little skepticism about debunked claims from vaccine skeptics. In one previous case, Judge Doughty accepted as fact the claim that “Covid-19 vaccines do not prevent transmission of the disease.”

Uhm, Covid-19 vaccines do not prevent transmission of the disease! That’s pretty widely accepted at this point — at least everywhere outside of the New York Times newsroom — which makes it very odd that this is cited as a reason why the judge is given to conspiracy theories. Granted, this was a matter of dispute at one point, but the way that dispute was deployed at scale was to censor a claim that turned out to be true!

The reason I bring up these stories actually comes back to that email: there are some issues, like free speech, that do come down to principle. My correspondent is upset that I haven’t changed my views on free speech, and he’s right, I haven’t. I support it. Moreover, I support it with the knowledge that this support, when manifested through the blunt instrument of government policy or big tech moderation, means more misinformation than there might be otherwise.

My personal belief is that it is impossible — especially at scale — to get every call right, so I would rather default to more information rather than less, because the harms of suppressing the truth are significant as well. To use the COVID example, if we had collectively accepted that everyone was going to get COVID despite the vaccine, might we have made different decisions around things like in-person schooling, potentially avoiding a devastating decline in test scores ( https://www.nytimes.com/2023/06/21/us/naep-test-results-education.html ) amongst kids, particularly those in disadvantaged circumstances? That is a very real cost that will last decades that stems from suppressing the truth, even if the motivation was to suppress misinformation.

I recognize that different people set these priorities differently, or have more faith than I do in the agility and capability of governments and large companies operated at scale to know the truth and only moderate what is misinformation, and that’s good. Masnick and I, to take one pertinent example, have had a longstanding back-and-forth, both privately and via our websites, about these very issues.

Moreover, I would say the same thing about the antitrust debate as well: beyond the specifics of the Microsoft-Activision case, there are real questions about whether one’s primary concern is limiting innovation and clear benefits to consumers in the short term, versus big company dominance and potential harm in the long run. I do tend towards the former, because I’m just not that confident in my ability to know exactly how products or markets will play out over decades, but I respect those who have the opposite point of view.

What is useful, though, and what makes all of this work, is assuming the good intentions and honest argument of those you disagree with, absent clear evidence to the contrary, and, I would argue, a commitment to a system that gives space for views you disagree with. This is obviously most pressing when it comes to government censorship or tech company moderation, but it applies on a smaller scale to the means of debate as well.
