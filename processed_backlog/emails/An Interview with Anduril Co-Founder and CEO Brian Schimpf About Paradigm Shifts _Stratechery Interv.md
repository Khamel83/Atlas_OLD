Culture Shift

*Brian Schimpf, welcome back to Stratechery.*

*Brian Schimpf:* Hey, thanks for having me.

*I was actually surprised when I looked it up and realized it’s only been 16 months since the last time we talked, but it feels like a lot has changed in that 16 months. Is that true? Am I too caught up in the general gestalt, or do you feel like things are progressing as expected?*

*BS:* No, it is been a wild last year-and-a-half for us. Since we talked last, I think we’re working on school bus size autonomous submarines ( https://www.anduril.com/article/anduril-s-dive-xl-setting-new-standards-for-maritime-autonomy/ ) , which are now swimming around, we’re working on autonomous fighter jets with the US Air Force ( https://www.anduril.com/article/anduril-air-force-collaborative-combat-aircraft-CCA/ ) , we’ve moved into missile programs, we’ve got a whole bunch of those going on. It’s really been a whole lot that has changed and then on top of it, everything that’s happened with AI, how that’s infused what we do, all of those things have really shifted. So I feel like the world has changed a lot in the last 16 months. And then, for Anduril specifically, yeah, it feels like an entirely different company than last time we talked.

*How much of that shift is for Anduril specifically though? Just to tie those two things together. Internally, do you feel like stuff has changed for you, or you’ve hit an inflection point, versus just because the environment has changed, that has changed your prospects?*

*BS:* I think these things are almost always much more macro than people want to believe, and I think the macro conditions for us are very, very favorable. I think the urgency that the Department [of Defense] feels around everything happening with respect to China, the threat there, Taiwan, I think the war in Ukraine, all the lessons there, how the international partners are all viewing this, and their need to rearm and increase their defense spending, and then obviously you have all the changes coming in with Trump, the influence of Elon, all of those pieces I think have massively shifted the view to what defense is going to be in the future, and how do we have to spend and build correctly for the right technologies, the right capabilities, and actually take advantage of all everything happening in Silicon Valley, that otherwise was just not making it in.

*When we talked last time, you put forward the thesis, and I think this is an overall theme of Anduril, that you need to build the defense company of the future, because the DoD doesn’t get it. That’s why you need to go start with venture, and when they wake up, you’ll be there waiting. I think you could certainly make the thesis that that has worked out, but is it a bit where now you’re sitting here in 2025, and you’re like, “Actually, we under-invested, we should have been more aggressive, going forward”?*

*BS:* So you got it exactly right. Look, I think most of these valuable companies that are created, you end up having a view to what the future’s going to be, and if you’re right, you do well, and if you’re wrong, you go out of business, and nobody remembers who you were. I think the reality is, there’s no alternative, you just have to actually build for the future that needs to exist and if you’re doing something that’s truly valuable, you understand your customers, you’re actually solving real problems, you’re actually doing the right things, if you execute well, it tends to work out, I think that has to be true.

Have we under-invested? It’s hard to be much more aggressive than we’re being. Look, we’ve doubled every year, we could probably go harder, and I always asking the question of, “Should we be pushing more? Should we be doing more?”, and I feel like we’re always behind where we need to be, I really do. I feel like if we had pushed harder on certain capabilities, gotten them out quicker, we’d have more of an impact on these things, we’d be in a better position to actually move the needle.

*But that’s a better place to be than the alternative.*

*BS:* It’s a better place to be than the alternative, but I also — I don’t know, my job is to be unsatisfied, so that’s always going to be the case.

*From that perspective, we talked about, I think previously, that when Anduril started, it was a pariah. Now there’s this idea of defense tech is very sexy. Is that a positive or a negative? There’s a bit where anyone that you recruited and were a part of your efforts were true believers and they were very mission-driven by definition, you achieved high cultural alignment by default. Is there a bit where now I get to say this as an MBA, you’re an MBA-attractive company now, so you’re getting the riffraff that want to jump on the hot new thing. Is that a challenge that you feel, or is it, “Look, we can take anyone, we need everyone we can get”?*

*BS:* I think this one time I agreed to do this “Ask Me Anything” session with some students from MIT and I got on, it was like 90 MBAs, and I was like, “Wow, I got conned”, it was harsh. I have only worked on things that are pretty unpopular, and I strongly prefer being there for exactly the reasons you said, which is it forces the clarity-

*You get the culture for free, right?*

*BS:* You get the culture for free. You have to live in this world of having clear convictions of why you’re doing it, and is it important. You just have to be so motivated by what you’re trying to accomplish, that it’s just a much healthier place to be, in my opinion. It hasn’t been that detrimental.

Look, we make weapons. You’re probably not going somewhere to make lethal weapons unless you’re pretty convinced that’s a thing you’re okay with. So I think the reality is, we still have enough of — you’ve got to be convinced this is something you’re okay with, to keep the cultural alignment there, and I think that’s worked quite well for us.

I do think defense tech has gotten very popular. Look, in my mind, the shift really happened with Ukraine, it really changed the public perception.

The Ukraine Effect

*Tell me more about this, because I think there’s so many things about the Ukraine conflict that are interesting in this regard. So number one, we can start there, the public shift in sentiment. Number two is, what are the lessons of Ukraine, the actual battlefield lessons? Then number three, how that changed the DoD. So having set the stage here, start with the public sentiment.*

*BS:* Pre-Ukraine, there was this belief that any talk of militarizing was war-mongering, fear-mongering, saber-rattling, and that state conflict was over, it was just the U.S. punching down, we were the bad guy. And then I think everyone was reminded that if the real bad guys can use force to get their political will, they’ll do it, and that has been true forever.

*It turns out other countries have agency in the world, not just the U.S.*

*BS:* Yeah, exactly, and they’re not peace-loving by their nature, that’s just not how humanity works. And so, it’s the saying of, “If you want peace, prepare for war”, you have to be strong, you have to make it clear of what the outcome of conflicts will be, you have to create that deterrent factor.

I don’t even think post-Ukraine, the U.S. is doing enough on this to push back on the day-to-day aggression you see from China. Everything happening in Europe, with the sabotage that Russia’s doing on power lines, all these things, like constant low-grade sabotage-

*By the way, an Internet cord was just cut outside Taiwan ( https://www.wsj.com/world/asia/chinese-vessel-cuts-taiwan-internet-cable-in-apparent-sabotage-81e0d3b1 ) just this last weekend, happens all the time, by the way, but yes.*

*BS:* Yeah, a fishing trawler had an accident, I’m sure. What a coincidence. But we’re not pushing back, nobody’s pushing back on these low-level aggression things and I think these adversaries will just keep pushing the line, keep pushing the line until you’re in these untenable situations.

So I think everyone saw it when it was as stark as they tried to roll into Kiev, but it’s constant, and it’s getting worse, and there has not been a time in my lifetime where the US has been in — there’s been multiple hot wars going around the world in high intensity, low-grade conflict happening in South China Sea, with the Philippines and everything else going on, constantly with Taiwan, and nobody’s talking about it like we’re in a state of conflict. It’s crazy to me, I don’t understand this, this is a very precarious time.

*Yeah.*

*BS:* So I think we saw a major shift from, “The U.S. is the bad guy, and we’re the only saber-rattlers in the world, and we’re causing all the problems”, to, “No, there’s bad actors”. But I don’t think it’s gone far enough, I don’t think people are appreciating, and I think the current administration is not talking about it in a way — they want to pretend we’re at peace, and it’s not good. I think this is a real problem.

*Well, the current administration point is interesting, because there is — it’s possibly a look back in the fullness of time, and it was, from this perspective, a good thing that a Democratic administration was in power when this happened, because you ended up with this partisanship angle that got applied to the conflict, where look, it’s mostly, or it’s primarily Democrats that are saying, “Look, this Ukraine deal, this shows this is a big deal”, whereas the Republicans have always been more traditionally pro-national security. So you can look at it on a micro level and say, “This is very weird who’s on either side of this conflict, or whether we should fund Ukraine, etc.”, but if you zoom out, it’s almost like a matter of timing that perhaps will drive a more bipartisan view that, “No, actually defense is pretty important, and we should be paying attention to it”.*

*BS:* I agree that is become a bipartisan consensus, there’s bipartisan consensus on China, there’s bipartisan consensus on defense and U.S. strength, I think both parties want to increase top line defense, it has really shifted the dialogue in Washington. The part I would critique more is there’s an element of leadership to explain the necessity of the U.S. role in the world, and the state of conflict that we’re living in, and the degree to which pulling along allies, partners and the American public to understand what is actually happening here and what’s at stake.

*I think that’s a fair critique. But what else? Well, number two, how has the actual way that war is being fought in Ukraine changed things?*

*BS:* So, a lot of people focus on the small FPV drones as munitions, and I think they are impactful. So, what I take away from that is, there’s a proliferation of these precision strike technologies, that for a while were just U.S. and high-end state level capabilities, but now are much more pervasive and the ability to get after these, to build these cheaply, in fact, in a lot of ways cheaper than traditional ammunition, like howitzer ammunition, is very impressive. So, I think for any future conflict, you’re going to have a world where this notion of volume of weapons, is probably the biggest thing I take away.

This is an industrial war, the U.S. theory on this historically has been derived from the Gulf War. We’d have this idea that we can wrap up a conflict in a week just through overwhelming use of force, we could force capitulation on the adversary side, such that these conflicts would be very fast. And the entire industrial base, the production apparatus, all of those things, the cost of the weapons, all of it is tooled around this idea that these things can be decisive, fast, and we will not have to have a sustained protracted conflict. But I think that has been proven to be false. Any real conflict that we’ve seen has been protracted, it consumes a huge amount of material, it requires a lot of ammunition and firepower.

The second piece is, I think the degree to which you’ve got these — several different technologies have changed a lot of the nature of warfare. One, you have this ubiquitous battlefield surveillance, both sides can find targets when you emit a signal, when you parked for too long, when you mass too many troops, it can get found very, very quickly. There is communication everywhere through Starlink and other things, networking all of these drones, sensors, weapons, troops together, and then there’s these precision-strike capabilities, short-range drones, long-range drones, missiles, that make it so that wherever you are, you can be targeted very, very quickly. So that’s fundamentally changed, like battlefield tactics. People have to disperse, they can’t mass, things are much less survivable, that is a big shift that really matters.

And the last piece is just the degree to which there is constant jamming, electromagnetic interference, jamming GPS, jamming communications, and how fast that is changing. The U.S. is in no way tooled for that. The average lifespan of these drones that Ukraine typically quotes is like four weeks. Once it’s out and it’s effective, the Russians will jam it, in four weeks, it’s irrelevant, try again. So the speed that you have to respond to this is immense, and so tooling your apparatus around this idea that I am going to have to respond to conditions on the battlefield and update my entire fleet every two, four weeks, nothing is tooled around that.

*We have platforms that are 30 years old, and not easily upgraded, which I think this ties into the number three, which was, what lessons has the DoD specifically taken away? And I think, I assume this is a natural segue to Lattice ( https://stratechery.com/2024/the-anduril-lattice-sdk-understanding-lattice-anduril-palantir/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI0L3RoZS1hbmR1cmlsLWxhdHRpY2Utc2RrLXVuZGVyc3RhbmRpbmctbGF0dGljZS1hbmR1cmlsLXBhbGFudGlyLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzM5MDEwODIzLCJpYXQiOjE3MzY0MTg4MjMsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBpLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiUnNldVlFOGpnSGVQcFdKZjFmQWI0QyIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.Onmy1s0ilzac_c7iu0ZnRi7Yb_tnRqNBnNeWp4blFYXG_zwRDrJKEN-J5bjBw6OSIxsQT4wjxD8KjpXVmJpxguhimfWYM5z_cDo_JlVI6e55aggXMTSw_jw3WZSlcUVdL5SwUXdEB3xApTpMdmAsdx2eLxtt3voilSuvwXKCNiDX3896DiuYhmlEhd-mWQ0VSAmC18YwK3Bnl8o_YZifzKd64Ro8a_6Ic7XuP3ELWQYNFycgYqi-ddSPltAEqslN2ANfO6IouhngmCeGehQiMz4X53Omulgl_ttI9tJ5qb8ognhJk2ecolQuTD9Ulif3GnuMEk5aiIGOJ_5cjEInEA ) , am I wrong about that?*

So we just have to put points on the board and find those early adopters who want to lean forward, who have that creativity, who can take a swing on this, and I think that the biggest thing to our advantage is honestly just speed and the urgency the Department feels on this now.

*Well, is it urgency the Department feels or that you feel? It feels like as we’re talking through this, everything you’ve done to date is great and it’s all a great story, but actually this contract and the next year or two years and proving you can actually do it is really the make-or-break moment for your company.*

*BS:* We feel a ton of urgency on that, I think you’ve got it exactly right. This is the thing we’ve got to show. I don’t have any doubt — the problems we can control, I’m good with that all day long.

*Yep. This is a big problem, but it is under your control.*

*BS:* It’s a big problem, but if it’s in our control, we’re going to win. The problems that are outside of our control, that’s just a risk that’s hard to underwrite. I think for the Department though, they feel a lot of urgency. Their view of China being prepared to invade Taiwan in 2027 is a highly motivating thing for them, in the sense that they view everything through the lens of, “Can this impact a war situation in the next two to three years?”, that’s the only question. So that has driven a sense of urgency and a willingness to take what would be perceived as historic risk on someone like us because we’ve demonstrated we’re just that much faster. We can just be faster.

*Is there a bit of frustration though, even in the contours of the program where they’re talking about these loyal wingmen that are $25 to $30 million, which is a far cry from $3 to $4 million, which was the initial idea of this concept, and instead of building, you mentioned thousands, no, you would build tens of thousands and hundreds of thousands and they would be disposable, you don’t need stealth if they’re disposable, you could just overwhelm with volume, but you have to play the hand you’re dealt. And the more complex, if you’re doing a $25 to $30 million aircraft, probably the bigger advantages to the primes who are used to building expensive aircraft to a certain extent. Is there some frustration in that regard?*

*BS:* That’s still really cheap for the primes, they couldn’t make their [approaches] work at that price point. So we actually have a huge advantage at that price point, which we think is absurd.

When you look at the cost of these things, the airframe itself is actually not a significant driver. It is significant, but it’s not the primary driver. The cost is all the communications equipment, the compute, the sensors, all these things that have been priced for relatively low volume manned aircraft with these really high certification barriers and all these things. It’s just a huge regulatory capture problem. So we were stuck in a position where we didn’t have the timeline to make everything as cheap as we wanted to, or the room to do it. I think in another iteration of this, I think that cost can go down by half if not more, based on just driving efficiencies into all of those mission systems that are on there.

But this is what you’d expect at any free market environment to an extent, it’s like if the government’s actually buying and values lower cost, you will see the cost come down on these egregious things very, very quickly. And I think we can a) drive it, we’re investing in all of that and b) I think the market’s just going to respond.

Manufacturing

*Well, the market has responded. There’s lots of cheap compute and sensors out there, but it’s all made in China. Can you make this aircraft without sourcing from China?*

*BS:* Well, here’s the thing. It’s like, okay, yes, you could buy a $10 chip from China, but I tell you what, if you get $1000 computer, that’s still several zeros less than the aircraft certified computers that exist in the market today. So we’re not talking about going to saving dollars per thing, we’re in the, “There’s room to squeeze out 100x”.

*Sure, but are there just fundamental components that — how much of a hindrance is the actual industrial base in the U.S. and what confidence do you have that you can get what you need, particularly as an outsider without relying on the center of electronics manufacturing?*

*BS:* It’s a regulatory capture problem and not a industrial base problem. So it’s like, look, is Flex in the U.S. in their North Carolina facility or whatever going to be good enough to make rugged computers? Absolutely, it’s fine, and it’s like if we got to even close to the pricing that Tesla gets on automotive electronics, which isn’t inherently sourced from China, you can find a lot of other sources for this stuff, it would be insanely cheap. And so I think we’re not even close to needing to get to the efficiencies that you get to with the fully optimized version.

*Well, leave aside efficiencies. Are there the capabilities?*

*BS:* The capabilities exist. The capabilities exist on the industrial automotive world, that exists, I believe that everything you need to make-

*Well, but Tesla had to go and build, they in-sourced a ton of stuff. They’re just like, “Yeah, the supply chain doesn’t work for us”, or something like, what, 70% of all is all internal.*

*BS:* Oh, yeah.

*Do you have to build that all up yourself?*

*BS:* Yes. So if the question is can you get PCBAs and metallic components? Yes, you can get that in America. If I need to get fully integrated subsystems and sensors, does that exist in the industrial base? No, it does not. And so there is a gap on the lower cost integrated components, that does not exist, and so I think what we’ve seen is we found a handful of partners that are actually doing the right stuff on this, but increasingly what we’re viewing, and I think this gets to what we’ve thought about with our manufacturing strategy, is we’re going to need to have a lot — we don’t want to own CNC machines and carbon fiber layup and stuff, we want to outsource that.

*But do you need to?*

*BS:* We mostly don’t, that stuff actually has a huge supply base. Now then what we need to do is own a lot more of the designs, own a lot more of those modules, those assemblies, all of those things, and this actually solves what I think is the most critical problem and unique for defense, which is wartime scaling.

This is a huge problem. Everyone thought we could turn the spigot on with weapons production, they were like, “Oh, it’s easy, right? You just double it in a year, it’s fine”. Turned out DoD dumped a bunch of money and said, “Okay, guys, ramp production”, and it was very hard. Supply chains were very deep, they were very slow to respond, they were all highly specialized and too small to invest capital to scale up and so this is a big issue.

Then if we wanted to even take advantage of that commercial industrial capacity that does exist in a wartime environment, you wouldn’t be able to, because there’s too many of the IPs locked up at every stage, you couldn’t scale out and so the strategy we’re kind of taking on the manufacturing front is as much as possible own the designs where we can, and really work with scalable suppliers that we’re less than 10% of their capacity. These guys are big, we want to be a relatively small player so that in time of war 10x-ing is actually pretty believable and then we’ll own assembly and final checkout and all of those types of things.

That strategy, I think, is basically right when you start talking about munitions and things like that. It’s harder with airplanes, but it’s very doable with munitions.

*In a perfect world, would you not build any hardware, that’d be all handled by other companies, you’d be just a software company?*

*BS:* I don’t think so. And I think we, at the very least, need to be a hardware design company. I am not sure in a perfect world we would have to be a hardware manufacturing company. So if we could outsource missiles being made at Flextronics or something, that’d be great, but you kind of can’t. And so I think in our perfect world, we would be less accountable for the production because that’s very CapEx intensive, very specialized, but the design side is actually critical, because I actually think there’s huge trades to be made in how you think about what’s done in hardware, what’s done in software, how these things are scalable, how you can manufacture it more cheaply, all these types of things if you’re thinking across the whole space.

*Is this though sort of a DoD limitation if you actually zoom out, it actually would be better if all these things just cost $1 million and they were dumber and less capable, but they were totally modular and plug-and-play, anyone could build them?*

*BS:* From a DoD perspective, the theory you’re pushing forward of scaled mass and overwhelm the adversary, I think has a lot of merit and is very controversial in the department, which is-

*Well, that’s probably a better strategy for China.*

*BS:* It’s a great strategy, it’s like, “What are you going to do about that?”, and so there’s a lot of practical problems with it, but it’s very solvable. I think that is one of the dominant strategies that we’ll shake out over the next 20 or 30 years, but it’s quite a bit different than what the U.S. is dealing with today and then the other problem you have for the U.S. is the U.S. fights an away game. So China’s fighting a home game, they don’t have to have stuff go huge distances necessarily.

*That’s true. If you’re limited, you can only take 1,000 drones, make those drones as smart as possible, not dumb.*

*BS:* Correct. So the set of constraints often boils down to, “How do I get these things in the fight?”. Well, if I could only bring certain things into the fight, then those things better be pretty good, and they better have a high probability of success. If I lose 90% of them, I can’t replenish and so this idea of, “How do I get the things into the fight?”, and the ranges you’re talking about, that actually drives a lot of complexity into the problem.

So that’s where I think we’ve been able to find some sweet spots on this where it’s like you can deliver these in totally novel ways. So it’s like if I had autonomous systems delivering them, and those are pretty cheap, and I’ve got then cheaper high volume things coming out of that, that’s pretty good. And I can constantly run low-cost bombers or autonomous underwater vehicles, there’s all these ways to get things into the fight that are more expensive, but then I’m deploying a lot of cheaper things with it. So there’s a lot of ways can think creatively about this.

But in a certain sense, the Department has landed it into this one narrow local optima, and they’re maxing that out and they’re like, “Oh, everything’s expensive, so it has to get more expensive”, and then they kind of end in this death spiral. But there’s this alternative, which could be very cheap and very scaled that you can get to and our view is we’re building all the requisite things to get there, finding ways to get them inserted because it will enable a whole new class of weapons, platforms and all these things, and a new concept of war-fighting, but it is going to take time because the whole system has been optimized around a totally different equilibrium.

*Well, there’s a path dependency to that point.*

*BS:* Yes.

*BS:* Anduril’s been very vertical, we work on the software and the hardware to enable certain classes of autonomous systems, weapons, things like that.

*BS:* They’re not making any hardware, and so we’ve been very focused on this vertical thing, and they’re very horizontal.
