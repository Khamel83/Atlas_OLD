Of course, layoffs and party leadership transitions are de rigueur following an unsuccessful cycle, but the profundity of the loss, with almost every county
swinging toward Trump, has instigated more than the typical Democratic soul-searching.

Democrats are looking for more than just a new message. There’s also an active effort to recruit new leadership at the D.N.C., with Rahm Emanuel as a dream candidate, alongside more mortal names like Phil Murphy, Stacey Abrams, Laphonza Butler, Ken Martin, Ben Wikler, and Martin O’Malley. “Rahm Emanuel is a motherfucker, and we need the motherfucker to beat motherfuckers—that’s where the logic comes in,” said one eloquent former D.N.C. staffer. “There’s no one else as good on the list besides him. It has to be someone who has never lost an election.”

For more on the D.N.C.’s vision quest, I turned to Patrick Ruffini, the longtime Republican pollster who predicted ( https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Party-of-the-People/Patrick-Ruffini/9781982198626?utm_campaign=The+Best+%26+The+Brightest+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F14%2F24%29&utm_content=The+Best+%26+The+Brightest+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F15%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) the outcome of the 2024 election a year earlier when he published a book describing the multiracial populist coalition that would turn toward the G.O.P. I hosted him on my podcast, Somebody’s Gotta Win, this week, and have excerpted the key points from our conversation below.

But first, some reporting and notes on Trump’s head-spinning week…

Trump’s Shock & Awe Nomination Strategy

* Florida man: Yesterday afternoon, there were audible gasps among House Republicans when the news broke that Trump had announced Matt Gaetz as his pick for attorney general—an appointment that would elevate a man who was investigated by the Justice Department for sex trafficking to the top of that very organization. It would also further erode the margin of Mike Johnson’s Republican majority, which already took a hit earlier this week when Trump plucked Reps. Mike Waltz and Elise Stefanik for his administration. Gaetz, reluctant to wait around for confirmation by the Senate, immediately resigned from Congress, thwarting a years-in-the-making House ethics report into
alleged sexual misconduct (among other things ( https://ethics.house.gov/press-releases/statement-regarding-matter-representative-matt-gaetz?utm_campaign=The+Best+%26+The+Brightest+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F14%2F24%29&utm_content=The+Best+%26+The+Brightest+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F15%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a )) that was about to come out. Lucky timing.

The nomination shocked Senate Republicans, many of whom can’t stand Gaetz, but is in keeping with Trump’s broader strategy. For one, of course, he’s being disruptive, as his allies euphemistically put it, betting on an aggrieved fellow target of D.O.J. investigations to dismantle factions of the agency and turn its weaponry on their enemies. More importantly, he’s assembling a phalanx of yes-men around him. And as anyone who has covered Trumpworld closely for the past eight years knows, Gaetz is a total and committed blood brother. The
Florida congressman has advised the once-and-future president on policy from the Mar-a-Lago patio—which is also where Gaetz proposed to his now-wife, Ginger Luckey, after securing Trump’s blessing.

It doesn’t hurt that his brother-in-law Palmer Luckey, the founder of Oculus, has raised gobs of cash for Trump. Gaetz is also on the right side of Susie Wiles, Trump’s campaign co-manager and future chief of staff, although I’m told she was not involved in his selection for A.G. (Axios reported that the plan was hatched between Boris Epshteyn and Gaetz on a flight to D.C. while Wiles was in another part of the plane.)
* NatSec roulette: Even before the Gaetz bombshell, Trump effectively washed away the post-election euphoria Senate Republicans were feeling by naming Tulsi Gabbard (who, for example, has blamed NATO for Russia’s invasion of Ukraine) as his director of national
intelligence and tatted-up Fox News host Pete Hegseth (who has said that women shouldn’t serve in combat and that certain war criminals should be pardoned) as his secretary of defense. All three nominations will essentially function as a litmus test for Senate Republicans, who began the week eager to rubber-stamp Trump’s agenda, and are now experiencing a bout of P.T.S.D. as reality sets in. “The last 10 days, everyone was feeling excited and good and ready to rock and roll, and the Gaetz thing was a rough reminder of what life can truly be like in Trumpland,” said one Senate source. “There’s going to be a wide berth given to him on nominations. Members are inclined to give him the [cabinet] he wants, but the Gaetz thing is too far.”
* Treasury island: Meanwhile, I’m hearing the announcement of Trump’s treasury secretary has been stalled due to Howard Lutnik, the Cantor Fitzgerald C.E.O., who is running the White House
transition and forcefully lobbying for the position, himself. Scott Bessent, a hedge fund billionaire who was pals with Trump’s late brother, Robert, is also at the top of the shortlist and is a favorite of former economic advisor Larry Kudlow. Bessent raised millions in support of Trump’s campaign, has the central casting look, and would be only the second openly gay man to serve in a cabinet position.

There’s fewer and fewer openings left for Kevin McCarthy, who wanted the chief of staff job, and is now interested in being named secretary of energy. Alas, it sounds like Trump sees Doug Burgum as his central-casting guy. If Burgum instead becomes some sort of amorphous czar, however, McCarthy could make a comeback into public life—even if it means sitting at cabinet meetings next to archrival Matt Gaetz. Given this prospect, I’m told McCarthy might just content himself with the money he’s
making in the private sector.

In any case, the shock-and-awe style nominations continue to roll in. On Thursday afternoon, Trump announced he is indeed choosing R.F.K. Jr. as health and human services secretary, putting to rest any expectation or hope that Trump would park his vaccine-skeptical campaign ally in a non-operational, advisory sinecure. A week before Election Day, Trump had said of Kennedy, “I’m gonna let him go wild on health. I’m gonna let him go wild on the food. I’m gonna let him go wild on medicines.” Another promise kept…

Now, here’s Abby with more on how the A.G. pick is playing on the Hill…

More Gaetzgate

Intentionally or not, the timing of the Gaetz A.G. nomination landed with maximum impact yesterday on the Hill. But at least one Democratic Hill insider scoffed at the notion that anyone could really be shocked that Trump would make such a controversial choice, and use it to detonate a series of news cycles. “They still have pearls to clutch,” this Dem Hill insider told me, mocking the Republican senators who couldn’t seem to process the news live in front of Manu Raju et al. Here’s the latest chatter…

* The timing of it all: Trump’s nomination of Jeff Sessions dropped on November 18, 2016, and Joe Biden announced Merrick Garland’s
nomination in early January 2021. The premature timing of the Gaetz announcement—and the controversial selections of Tulsi Gabbard at D.N.I., Pete Hegseth at the Pentagon, and health kook R.F.K. Jr. to H.H.S.—maximize the window for the drip, drip, drip of their records, including during the Christmas holidays, a notoriously slow news period. (Recall that this is how George Santos became a household name in late 2022.)
* Will the Gaetz ethics report ever be released?: Adding to the chaos on Wednesday night, Gaetz announced his resignation from the House—a move ostensibly intended to stymie the release of the House Ethics Committee report investigating allegations that he was engaged in sex trafficking. (The D.O.J. dropped its investigation into the matter, as you may recall, but the House investigation went on.) Senior Senate Judiciary member John Cornyn, who’s now unshackled from his leadership ambitions after getting Thuned,
immediately pressed to get the report released.

The House investigation, in many ways, has served as a real powder keg in the lower chamber. Former speaker Kevin McCarthy accused ( https://amp.theguardian.com/us-news/2024/apr/10/matt-gaetz-removed-kevin-mccarthy-house-speaker-ethics?utm_campaign=The+Best+%26+The+Brightest+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F14%2F24%29&utm_content=The+Best+%26+The+Brightest+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F15%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) Gaetz earlier this year of leading his defenestration as payback for his refusal to kill the investigation. Member after member in the House G.O.P. conference directly blames Gaetz more than anyone else for that episode, and its unending aftershocks. Today, one Dem said the quiet part out loud to me: Given how many enemies Gaetz has on the Hill—particularly on his own side—there’s at least some chance the report is simply leaked. And
that, in and of itself, would be quite a development. Ethics, the most serious and disciplined committee on the Hill, never leaks anything.
* Will there be a hearing?: While Republicans still seemed to be processing the news on Thursday, some Hill insiders were skeptical that the Gaetz appointment would make it to a hearing. But in case it does, it’s worth looking at the roster of committee members, especially with the historical context that the Senate Judiciary Committee has hosted the most charged and emotional hearings in modern history (Clarence Thomas, Brett Kavanaugh, etcetera…).

The incoming chairman will be Lindsey Graham, hardly a shrinking violet. The cast change since the first Trump administration is that Dick Durbin will be the ranking member, after replacing the late Dianne Feinstein. Cornyn’s curiosity to see the ethics report is a case in point, but there will be other senators
to watch, too. It’s worth keeping an eye on Amy Klobuchar, a former prosecutor and probably the Democrat who will throw the hardest fast balls. And then there are Democrat Jon Ossoff and Republican Thom Tillis, who will participate in the hearings as they both begin brutal reelection campaigns. And lastly, which freshmen will end up on Judiciary? Those decisions are down the road, but Adam Schiff served on the House Judiciary Committee and is a strong contender to be one of the rookies at this hearing.

The reelection of Donald Trump surely feels like a bad dream to many, and to many others like vindication for all their gripes about Joe Biden. Voters across the political spectrum are still trying to digest, understand, and integrate the undeniable heft of Trump’s victory, in which he swept every battleground state and will most likely become the first Republican since 2004 to win the popular vote. Democrats have already launched a dozen competing postmortems, although it’s hard to predict how any of this ends. Republicans went to great pains to write an autopsy report after Mitt Romney lost in 2012, which encouraged the party to win over Latinos. Trump won four years later by ignoring its conclusions entirely.

None of which addresses the burning question of what just happened. So I invited
Republican Party pollster Patrick Ruffini onto my podcast, Somebody’s Gotta Win, to explain his theory of how Trump won over Latinos, African Americans, and young voters; where the G.O.P. is headed beyond Trump’s second term; and what the future holds for J.D. Vance as a potential MAGA successor. (Disclosure: Ruffini’s firm, Echelon Insights, does some polling for Puck.) Our conversation has been lightly edited. You can listen to the entire episode here ( https://puck.news/podcast_episode/how-the-2024-presidential-election-killed-identity-politics-with-patrick-ruffini/?utm_campaign=The+Best+%26+The+Brightest+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F14%2F24%29&utm_content=The+Best+%26+The+Brightest+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F15%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ).

The Realignment

Tara Palmeri: I had you on almost exactly a year ago, and you called out that Trump was changing the Republican coalition. Can you talk a little about this populist movement that is changing the parties and killing off identity politics?

Patrick Ruffini: What we saw in 2020 was a pretty surprising shift toward Trump among Latino and Asian voters, such that he almost won that election despite being behind by eight points in the polls all year. He made some gains that really cemented his position as a potential candidate in 2024. If he had underperformed by as much as the polls had expected, I don’t think he could have stuck around. The narrative that he didn’t actually lose was more plausible to his constituents because he did come very close in a lot of swing states.

He then absolutely upended the 2024
electoral landscape. Despite Democrats swapping in a woman of color who theoretically should’ve done better with these groups, she did roughly how Joe Biden would’ve done because of how these voters were already moving, combined with the overall current political environment, which is just a terrible environment for Democrats to be running in.

What did Trump do to win over these voters? Or is it just that they’re unhappy with the direction of the country right now?

What Trump didn’t do was say, I have a Latinos for Trump coalition, I have an African Americans for Trump coalition. You see that on some signs, but there was no formalized outreach like you would see in a traditional Republican campaign. I think Trump placed these voters at the heart of his appeal: infrequent voters who are working class, more diverse, and in fact younger than the groups that he was aiming for previously. You also have his appearances on
podcasts that were geared toward young Latino and Black men. According to exit polls, Trump’s media strategy seemed to have hit the mark in terms of getting these shifts among younger men, and younger non-white men in particular—whereas Harris’s strategy of focusing on younger women didn’t seem to bear much fruit.

You’ve been saying for a long time that the Democrats can’t take the Hispanic community for granted. Obviously, it’s a fragmented group, but Hispanic voters seem to be swinging even more to the right after decades of gradually shifting in that direction. What are you seeing?

It was surprising that they swung even more to the right than the polls were expecting. We didn’t see a backlash to things like the Puerto Rico joke at the Madison Square Garden rally or the “Black jobs” comment. In fact, what you had was places like Osceola County, Florida, and Allentown, Pennsylvania, each with a heavy Puerto Rican
population, swinging pretty heavily toward Trump. And this is a community that’s not known as Republican-leaning.

There was a lot of concern in the closing days of the election that Trump was going to blow it with [Tony Hinchcliffe’s] Puerto Rico joke, and Democrats expressed a lot of optimism about it spilling over with other Hispanic voters. In fact, the opposite happened. And I think there’s a lesson in that. A lot of people just like to speak bluntly about things and think it’s okay to crack a joke every now and then. And I think the entire Democratic Party seems to have lost sight of that.

Is the divide between voters with college degrees and voters without college degrees?

In 2016, you had a realignment among whites, but you still had a pretty solid bloc voting for Democrats among African Americans and Latinos. That began to erode in 2020, and it continues to erode. I think it’s a long-term trend.

There’s a lot of research that the Democratic majority is held together by social forces, like people going to Black churches, for instance. But people are doing those kinds of things less and less. They’re living in more integrated environments. You’re seeing intermarriage, you’re seeing suburbanization, and I think that all of these trends, over time, are going to make African Americans more like an 80-20 or 70-30 Democrat-to-Republican group. Among Hispanics, we’re getting very close to the point where they are probably going to be something close to a 50-50 group.

The Kids

Gen Z voters swung away from the Democratic Party. I can’t imagine that’s an educational divide. Is that mostly based on anxieties about the economy?

These are the groups that have been hit hardest by inflation, particularly Gen Z and millennials, who were really devastated by the Great Recession. Then you had Covid, and there’s a sense that they’re just in a much more tenuous position economically. And then you add onto that inflation and rising costs. And frankly, the cost of housing is an underplayed issue in this race because that’s not counted in the official inflation statistics.

But young people haven’t really had the ability to buy homes, because mortgage interest rates were so sky-high. The cost of a starter mortgage practically doubled under Joe Biden. I think that created a lot of
anxiety. Democrats used to be able to count on the youngest voters by about 20 points, and now, at least one of the exit polls has Democrats down to a five-point lead with this group.

What will the Democratic coalition look like in the future?

Democrats have a choice to make. Certainly, they’re moving in the direction of being a more college-educated party, and that reflects a lot of the choices they made during this campaign. They pivoted at the very end back to this “democracy and abortion” message. The abortion side of it seems to have been ineffective in mobilizing the groups they thought they’d mobilize. The problem was that they were losing a lot of their voters on the fringes of the coalition—those voters were focused on the economy.

What will the Republican coalition look like?

It seems like we’re headed toward something more like a working-class coalition that spans racial lines. And the real interesting
question is: What policy changes will come about because of this? You’ve already seen a change in emphasis, even in Trump’s first term, really setting aside the Paul Ryan-style budget cutting. Trump clearly has very little interest in that, but how far is that actually going to go in a second term? If tariffs are going to move forward, what effect will that have on the working class? That’s a question that’s very much up in the air right now for Republicans. Anyone who wins these elections has done so by doing better among non-college, late-deciding people who don’t pay very close attention to politics.

The moments that Trump was able to create during the course of the campaign, from the trip to McDonald’s to the garbage truck thing—he broke through and created images that people who aren’t paying attention to politics 24/7 could really gravitate toward. So, it’s possible to galvanize people who haven’t voted before. Barack Obama
did it, and I think we have to give Trump credit because he was able to.

There’s only one Trump for Republicans, in the same way that Democrats only have one Obama. Can this coalition stay together without him? Does J.D. Vance keep it together?

That’s always the challenge. I think we’re trying to sidestep the personality question because, like you said, Trump is unique. The things he was able to do during this campaign are not at all easily replicated. And every candidate who comes forward in 2028—there will definitely be a new candidate who comes forward—has to create their own coalition. They can’t just borrow the Trump coalition.

But I think the starting place for most Republicans will have to be populism as opposed to a return to the old-school moderate establishment. That’s dead and buried at this point. J.D. Vance is probably one of the most successful examples I’ve seen of someone really adapting
Trump’s appeal, but doing it in a way that was super smooth and connected with more moderate and suburban voters. I think we’re headed to a more populist Republican Party, for better or worse.
