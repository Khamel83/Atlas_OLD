Good morning,

This week’s interview is with Matthew Panzarino ( https://twitter.com/panzer ) , who just stepped down from a 10-year run as the editor-in-chief of TechCrunch ( https://techcrunch.com/ ) , the long-running trade publication for the tech industry. Panzarino took over TechCrunch when I started Stratechery, so I wanted to take this opportunity to look back on how things have changed in that time period. Topics include focusing and growing a media business, the increased antagonism between tech and journalism, and the economic benefits and ethical challenges of events and interviews. We also snuck in a bit of discussion about the Apple Vision Pro.

Background

*Matthew Panzarino, welcome to Stratechery.*

*MP:* Thank you very much, Ben. Nice talking with you.

*I needed to say Matthew Panzarino with no title there because you are no longer the editor-in-chief of TechCrunch. You were for ten years ( https://techcrunch.com/2023/08/28/10-years-at-techcrunch/ ) , which is a notable time period because you took the job I believe the same year that I started Stratechery ( https://stratechery.com/2013/welcome-to-stratechery/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDEzL3dlbGNvbWUtdG8tc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkvIl19LCJleHAiOjE3MDAzMDU4ODgsImlhdCI6MTY5NzcxMzg4OCwiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcGkucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCBlbnRpdGxlbWVudHMiLCJzdWIiOiJSc2V1WUU4amdIZVBwV0pmMWZBYjRDIiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.hNgpklIRgCOiTzskWJBToAWBYCvjI8Pj8zDNOfsQlYKRxkaHp9W845bx2JGtLnVfGSfB--vG2bJ-tGqWXSJmp4QGz7NVhX98SxPpKOKlZWyv7FGQN9hIQAlm5uGtS0I-nCC6oIuZV_3ojPkV5eOBufP02hv8gBkOdZXUikDIHqDQgiCqsodqn-bjgYvuMzPOnCUKyr8lxc_2IQBxur_MrLMGScev8uvZDbjNFqULdlLSAaP0wWzcA1PEavC89QCBpbMA0QI-I9tdMzm_89Tly8GaPjdHCkCYeq7_Qbgk7_n9THDxJILwF77juPFA7efGvQthQm7Tx2GunwrO5R8Mqg ) , so we’ve experienced the last decade together, and I’ve outlasted you so congratulations to me.*

*MP:* Yeah, exactly. You won.

*I do always want to go back earlier though. Tell me about your background and how you ended up covering tech in the first place.*

*MP:* Yeah, it is kind of a, I don’t know, “weird” is a weird word because I think new media especially, there are so many pathways open to people that certainly weren’t twenty years ago. Certainly, there was an established pipeline. You went to J School, you worked at a local paper, maybe you interned at a paper then you worked at a paper of record, and then you went on to maybe work at a trade pub if you got specialized, but my pathway was definitely different.

I worked at a lot of different things out of high school, but most primarily, I worked in photography or electronics-related industries. So I started my own photography business in high school, I did all of the stuff that an urban-suburban photographer does, which is portraits, weddings, your senior portraits, your weddings, your special occasions, your event photography, all of that stuff, and that’s what I wanted to be. I fell in love with photography in high school, exhausted my teacher’s patience and willingness to allow me to experiment in every type of photography imaginable, which he was fantastic. My teacher in junior high was a fantastic teacher who as exasperated as he acted, he was very happy that anybody was taking an interest in all of these techniques that were right on the cusp of disappearing. So I came in, the first version of Photoshop I used was like Beta 0.9 or something like that, or beta 1.0 or whatever it was but we had a Mac in the classroom, but primarily, it was all darkroom, and I shot my first weddings on film — which talk about nerve-racking.

*Seriously.*

*MP:* People talk about shooting digital weddings being nerve-racking, but when you’re hand processing black-and-white film that you shot yourself and have no idea how it’s going to turn out of somebody’s wedding, that’s a whole ‘nother level, and so I have a deep respect for all the folks that did that over the centuries before Photoshop came along.

But I think it was a natural progression from there to say, “Hey, I want to be a fine art photographer”, because I think everybody, when they’re coming out of just learning about photography, they see themselves as the next Ansel Adams ( https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ansel_Adams ) , or if you’re in fashion, you’re talking about a fashion photographer, like a Herb Ritts ( https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herb_Ritts ) or somebody like that. And you’re saying to yourself, “Oh, this is what I want to be.” For me, it was like [Henri] Cartier-Bresson ( https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Cartier-Bresson ) , the street photography style stuff, or even Ansel, just given that I lived so close to Yosemite, was obviously a legendary local hero of some sort.

I came out of high school thinking I was going to do that, but of course, reality strikes. I got married young, I had a mortgage. I had all these things to do very suddenly, very quickly after high school, so I didn’t really finish college. I just started working right away, doing photography, working in retail, working in electronics retail, all of that stuff and I sort of ground that out for about eight years, nine years or so, and then just by the tenth year, I kind of burnt myself out. I was like, “I don’t want to wake up every weekend and shoot weddings, and take pictures of other people’s special engagements for the rest of my life.”

*Especially once you have kids and stuff like that, and you realize once they go to school, I’m never going to actually see them because I’ll be busy every time that they’re not there.*

*MP:* That’s it, and I could see it all laid out before me. It was one of those weird moments where you see the carpet of your life unfurl into the distance and you see all of the ways that this is going to play out, and I just had this moment of ennui about it and this moment of, “I want to do something different with my life.”

So I worked backwards from the result. The result that I wanted was a job where I wasn’t stuck working retail to supplement photography income, wasn’t stuck on the weekends, then again, having to work, so working essentially seven days a week. Working with all of these caveats about, “Okay, you got to be good at this”, and all of that, but aside from that, I wanted to find a job that I could do either from home or traveling, on my own recognizance, not forced to be at a retail job nine-to-five, that I could spend a lot of time with family, and that would be something around technology because I knew I loved it. Obviously, photography and technology are very closely intertwined, it’s an art as much as it is an exercise of applied technology and I had just kind of been obsessed with all kinds of technology for a long time, but especially Apple.

I remember vividly, I told my wife on a Monday in, I think it was April of 2009, I was like, “Hey, I don’t want to do any of this anymore”, and at the time she was making more money than I was and had been for a while because she worked in surgery, but my portion of the income was not insignificant. So it was definitely a, “Oh, so you’re going to just not do any of that?”. I said, “Well, that’s not feasible. We have a mortgage, we’re about to have a kid and all of this stuff,” and then we did have a kid, and I was like, “Hey, we need to still make money.” So I told her on a Monday, I bought my domain on a Friday and I started writing, and I knew that I had to — because I had written a couple of tips columns for photography magazines and things like that, but I hadn’t really written anything longer than a couple of paragraphs since high school. But I also knew that I could do it — I don’t know why I did, maybe hubris, but I felt that I could, and so I started my site on the Friday, bought the domain and started writing, and just ground it out for two years.

*What was the site? What was the domain?*

*MP:* It’s no longer mine so whatever’s there is not me anymore. I do have all my work on a server somewhere, but it was “The iPhone Guru”, believe it or not.

*Oh, interesting. I actually did not know that. I remember The Next Web portion of your career ( https://thenextweb.com/author/matthewpanzarino ) , which I guess we’ll get to, but I didn’t realize that you started that.*

*MP:* And the frameworks for what you could be as a web entrepreneur were well established, I was not pioneering any new ground there, I was trying to just be sustainable. I mean, I live in Fresno, California, which is a relatively affordable town, I wasn’t trying to make Bay Area wages, it would’ve taken honestly very little to make it sustainable for me, and so I was fortunate in that regard, plus the fact that we had dual income and the fact that my wife was long-suffering.

But at the same time, I couldn’t bring myself to just say, “Hey, we’re going to eat dirt with a kid and a mortgage and all this stuff,” so I just woke up at 3:00 AM instead and wrote for three, four hours, then went to work. Worked some retail, did my photography jobs, came home, had dinner with my wife, put her to bed, and then wrote for another two, three hours, then got two or three hours sleep. I just did that for two years, I just ground it out. I wrote, I don’t know, however many articles.

*So this is the late 2000s, so was it mostly growing via social media at that point?*

*MP:* Yeah, it was primarily social media. I did the thing that I think everybody does, which is you just cold email, so at the time, I was cold emailing all the tech sites. I’m sure that bloggers, if they search back in their email inboxes, if they still have them, from anything from Lifehacker to Gizmodo to Engadget and all those folks, you had all of these Weblogs, Inc. properties, et cetera. I just cold emailed everybody and said, “Hey, I wrote this thing that I think is unique or interesting, or I figured this thing out. I kind of tested this thing and wrote about it and figured it out, maybe you want to write about it.” Some early hits where a friend and I figured out that you could use the Livestrong bracelets ( https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livestrong_Foundation#Livestrong_wristband ) as a bumper case. You remember the yellow LiveStrong bracelets? So you could strap them around the iPhone and they were almost a perfect fit for a bumper case to prevent the antenna attenuation, the iPhone 4 or whatever it was.

*iPhone 4, yeah.*

*MP:* So that was an early hit, and then I did some photography related stuff. I soldered some remote triggers together and got the iPhone to trigger remote flashes at a time when there were no accessories to do that. Just various experiments, anything I could do to get attention and to say, “Hey, this is maybe something you haven’t heard of before”, and so Lifehacker very nicely linked to me a couple of times and that started building. There was no magic to it, it wasn’t any big break stuff. It was just really grinding. Just publish, publish, publish, then I did that for two years.

And then two years into it, I just saw a tweet and somebody was like, “Hey, we are looking for a West Coast writer” and I looked up the website and I’m like, this is run by some crazy Dutch guys and they’re trying to compete with TechCrunch and all these other websites, and that was The Next Web ( https://thenextweb.com/ ). They very graciously took a chance on me and I said, “Here’s my portfolio, here’s my website, go look at it. This is my portfolio, there’s nothing more to this, I don’t have a degree to show you, I don’t have a journalism credential. What I have is this body of work.”

*What I have is two years of waking up at three in the morning and hustling.*

*MP:* That’s it. And I said, “Read the stuff at the end.” Read the newest things.

*(laughing) Don’t start from the beginning, yes.*

*MP:* Correct. Then honestly, I remember vividly because Zee Kane ( https://twitter.com/zee ) was the editor at the time, and Brad McCarty ( https://thenextweb.com/author/bradmccarty ) , they took the chance on me. Zee, I remember it because he was like, “Hey, yeah, sounds good, we have to test you. Would you willing to wake up? Oh, it’s early your time,” because they were over there. And I said, “Oh, yeah, I think I’m okay” and so I did my test for them at 3:30 in the morning my time, which was fine because that was my working hours, and my test was basically a typing test. They just wanted to make sure that I could write an article at speed, so he literally gave me a live topic. It was like some footballer had said something on Facebook, and they’re like, “Hey, write this up.” That was it, and then they took a chance on me and that kind of started my career.

*So you were at The Next Web then for four years then or three years, something like that?*

*MP:* Yeah. I was there from 2011 to late 2013.

*Got it. So you went straight from there to TechCrunch then?*

*MP:* Yep.

*Yeah, so I love that story. I obviously can relate to aspects of it, not having a background necessarily in journalism or whatever it might be, this pure hustle and doing another job at the same time you’re trying to build it up. I feel excited and it’s inspiring hearing it all over again.*

*How did you end up at TechCrunch then? You basically went in six years from being a photographer and working in retail to being the editor-in-chief of TechCrunch. You had this two year period of grinding, just started out, and then you’re at The Next Web. I mean, that’s quite the leap. How did that come about?*

*MP:* You know what? It was absolutely a product of timing. I think it’s that thing that people refer to when they don’t know what they’re talking about necessarily — not in a bad way, they just don’t know how to codify it so they say luck, and so it was luck. It was luck backed with like, “Okay, I also did a lot of footwork to get me in a position to where I-”

*It was luck backed with waking up at 3:00 AM in the morning for years.*

*MP:* Yeah, exactly and it’s neither here nor there. A lot of people wake up at 3:00 AM and so they maybe just didn’t have the timing, and so I never want to attribute too much to myself, but I did the work, but at the same time, I got really lucky because we were at a weird position, and I think M.G. has talked about this — M.G. Siegler ( https://twitter.com/mgsiegler ) who worked at TechCrunch, he’s now a VC — but at the time, Techmeme ( https://www.techmeme.com/ ) was such an interesting — I mean, I still love Techmeme — but at the time, it was such an interesting force because the old blogging apparatus, the meta apparatus that codified what blogs were hot and what writers were good, was — Oh my gosh, I’m going to forget the name of it now that I just mentioned it.

*Technorati ( https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technorati ) ?*

*MP:* Yes, thank you. Thank you, Ben.

*We were all there.*

*MP:* Yeah, we were all there. Some people maybe listening to this weren’t, that’s the only reason I mentioned it. But yeah, Technorati was sort of like, “Hey, here are the blogs that…”. It evolved out of Webrings ( https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Webring ) , to date myself, which is like, “Hey, you link to me, I’ll link to you, and we’ll create this rising tide of an audience,” and the very generous era of blogging. That had been supplanted in the tech world specifically by Techmeme, and Techmeme had this list, and still does, of journalists that created a little bit of competition, it was a leaderboard of people making news ( https://www.techmeme.com/lb ).

The remit of Techmeme was not really to say, at that time — now, I think more so these days, it’s more about the definitive story — but back then, it was the quickest reliable story I think was the best way to describe it. They wanted reliable journalists that they knew would update the article if anything changed and that they could trust to have actually real news, and the people that did it first. So it was a little bit of both, a little bit of it has to be quality, but it has to be superfast because Techmeme wants a scannable read for their readers selfishly, which is fine, that’s their deal.

But the leaderboard essentially became like a hiring board, it became a picker. It’s like, “Oh, we want more mind share or more topic share, and we’re all busy and we’re trying to recruit.” Twitter was just starting to gain steam as a recruitment board, I mean I did get my job off of a tweet at The Next Web, but it was just kind of all blended together at that point to where you were like, “Who is rising above the noise?” The leaderboard, for better or for worse, became this thing that allowed people to see, “Hey, this journalist is rising. They’re killing it. They have a share of the attention span of anybody interested in tech.” So I started pounding my way up the leaderboard, and also all of the other really awesome writers at The Next Web at the time, we all worked together in lockstep so there was a bunch of different factors there. There was the leaderboard, this attention focused on that thing by all of the editors and people hiring and all of that, including the editors of TechCrunch and there was also this weird thing that we had at The Next Web where there was a generosity of spirit amongst the team, where if a new story was coming or we got something going and we knew we could be first to it, we would just fire up a Google Doc and we’d all write the story together all at once in the doc, and then publish it as fast as possible. It was not like, “Oh, do I get a byline? Is yours first? Is mine first? Do you need credit on this?”

Very famously, you can ask all the people that worked there, but some of them would still work for me, worked for me. At TechCrunch, we didn’t give a shit, it was really just about winning, it was about winning in and amongst the sea of people that were much better equipped, much higher, better resourced than us. So I think at the time, Alexia [Bonatsos] ( https://twitter.com/alexia ) and Eric [Eldon] ( https://twitter.com/eldon ) , who had kind of taken the reins of TechCrunch at that point, saw The Next Web chewing away at their lead, because TechCrunch famously had an enormous lead over most other publications at that time, on the Techmeme leaderboard.

*Right, because the leaderboard, it was both journalists and it was publications.*

*MP:* Correct. So the TechCrunch journalists, of course, who made up a bulk of the top ten, and they were the leaders on the leaderboard by a wide margin and The Next Web started creeping up the leaderboard. It was like ten, seven, four, and then we came within a half a percentage point of unseating TechCrunch on the Techmeme leaderboard. This is absolutely not a value judgment on any journalism or work of any individual. It was just a moment, a moment in time of things aligning, and I think they saw that and they’re like, “Holy…What? Where did this publication come from? Who are these writers that are doing this to us?”

So a couple of my people did end up going to TechCrunch, Drew Olanoff ( https://twitter.com/yoda ) , Alex Wilhelm ( https://twitter.com/alex ) , they went over there because they got poached, which it’s fine, I get it. Then they looked and saw that I had a share, I was in the top of the leaderboard, I was writing defining stories. At that point, I was a managing editor there, and leading some of the news coverage and I think there was just like a, “This is a person we need to talk to,” kind of thing. You’d have to ask them, but Alexia told me later that they were just like, “Hey, this person seems like they’re TechCrunch material. They have that kind of balance of punchiness mixed with authority that we want at TC.” So they reached out, and that was an interesting conversation, because I still to this day am not a very drama-driven person. I don’t like it, and I don’t like the Machiavellian power plays of media and all of that stuff, I’m not interested in it. I like the work, and the purity of the work is fun to me. So I saw that TechCrunch had just come out of an era where there was a lot of chaos.

*Yup, Michael Arrington ( https://twitter.com/arrington ) had just left, the whole AOL thing ( https://techcrunch.com/2010/09/28/why-we-sold-techcrunch-to-aol-and-where-we-go-from-here/ ).*

*MP:* Exactly. It did lead me to be like, “Oh, no, what am I getting myself into if I’m going to be here?” But I will say, Alexia and Eric reached out, they were so kind, they were so direct and honest. I mean, this is crazy, and I always tried to carry this attitude forward in my hiring, they drove out to Fresno, which is three hours from San Francisco, just to talk to me and then we had dinner and we chatted, and then they drove back. It was just not a hard sell. It was like, “We like you, we think you do great here.” I explained to them my position, like, “Hey man, there’s a lot of drama. I’m doing really well at TNW, thank you so much.” But my answer was basically no and they didn’t take it badly. They were just like, “Look, we really like you. Thanks for having this conversation,” et cetera. Then I got fired — I didn’t get fired, I got laid off — because TNW was changing business models at the time, and media’s a struggle. So it was not anything untoward, it was just they were like, “Wow, we pay you a lot, and maybe we need to figure out how to pay some other people less money”.

*So basically the long and short of it is that TNW basically took over TechCrunch, that’s sort of a way to put it.*

*MP:* Yeah I mean, at the very least, attention was there, and then all of the writers started getting sucked away and then I moved over, and Alex Wilhelm and I have this joke that whatever job he takes, I just come in and then become his boss. It’s like I sent him in as my advance man.

TechCrunch Disrupt

*One other question about this, TechCrunch Disrupt ( https://techcrunch.com/events/tc-disrupt-2023/ ) is sort of a big thing, and I’m curious of two angles on it. Number one, how important is that to the overall business from a business perspective? Like events versus ads, you have TechCrunch+, so the business angle. Then also two, what you’re kind of driving at, the role of TechCrunch not just in writing about startups, but actually being a promotional vehicle for startups. To be on the Disrupt stage, which I think is probably a bigger deal than you read about it TechCrunch, but both sort of matter, I’m curious about both angles there.*

*MP:* So on the business side, it’s pretty simple. The two pillars and then the additional third pillar, which started in the past few years, the advertising revenue, the events business, and then the subscription business, they’re all legs of the stool and they all load balance. Some years the events were absolutely majority of revenue and then other years, advertising is a majority.

All you have to do is look at the last five years and frankly, if we didn’t have one of those legs, we would’ve been screwed. 2020 came along and all of a sudden you have to pivot. From March of 2020, we held our last physical event in 2020, but we had eight more planned in that year and the last one, it was a robotics event at Berkeley, and all the news stories were coming hot and heavy. All of us, we’re looking at each other with like, “What is happening?”, we didn’t know whether we were going to be shut down that day or not because the Berkeley campus was on the verge of pulling the plug on itself. So we skated that in and then had to really retcon our entire rest of the year into a virtual event space. So of course, virtual events are, it turns out, less expensive to put on, but also far less revenue. It turns out they’re slightly more profitable on a percentage basis.

*Oh, interesting. But the absolute number is not that high.*

*MP:* Correct, and then of course, the advertising business went to nothing in the first half of 2020 and then went bonkers the second half of 2020 because as everybody figured out, “Oh crap, we’re going to be locked up, we got to spend somehow, so I guess we’ll just start spending again.” So those shock absorbers helped us and it torpedoed a lot of sites. A lot of the media shutdowns over the past year are all just byproducts of people running out of gas from the last several years of disruption and the load balancing was definitely a massive effect there.

The events businesses have been very important to TC and I think are remarkably profitable considering how much of an endeavor they are. They’re not as profitable as you think because they’re super expensive to put on. Physical events are tough and they’re logistically rough but I think most people would be shocked to understand how small of a team actually did that here because the battlefield is essentially two people, myself and one other person, Neesha Tambe ( https://twitter.com/neeshatambe ) , at TC or at least was, I keep saying is, it’s my fault. Then the events team was a team of three people who had a team of a handful of other people that were partially contractors, partially full-time. These are small teams that put on enormous events. Our event, Disrupt, this year, was 13,000 people, which is crazy and they did a bang-up job of it, but super important. Does that answer your first question?

*It does. But then how do you think about balancing that in terms of your journalistic mission as it were?*

*MP:* Yeah, so the way it worked when I started and the way it worked when I left were different. The way it worked when I started, the events programming team lived on the business side and it wasn’t like there was any weird power play thing, I think it was just like a natural evolution of whatever had happened before. But frankly, it’s sort of ancient history at this point and it doesn’t need to be litigated, I don’t even know how it came to be that, and I think at most organizations that is pretty standard and I haven’t done this.

I know Code is an example or Recode, Code Con, or All Things D, that was certainly more like what we did, right? Because Kara [Swisher] and Walt [Mossberg] programmed that show, they were like, “We are going to get these people, we’re going to put them on stage, and we’re going to choose who moderates them,” most of them say yes, but also some of our writers, et cetera. I think that model was good, I think it’s why that conference was so vital for so long and still, obviously, I’m not passing any judgment on it, obviously, the one that just happened was certainly news-making ( https://www.theverge.com/2023/9/28/23895150/linda-yaccarino-code-conference-2023-x-twitter ) , but that model works because you have the people that intimately understand these universes not just picking the people that are important, but also interviewing them. You don’t have a professional moderator from outside who was going to glad-hand these people or not be able to actively listen in the moment and talk to them and draw an interesting conversation out of them because they fundamentally don’t understand that industry on a granular level.

*But at the same time, you want them to come to your conference because you make a lot of money on your conference and you also have to cover them day-to-day.*

*MP:* Totally, it is an enormous tension, and the amount of behind-the-scenes angst over the years has been great, as you can imagine. However, fundamentally, we were lucky in that we were able to pretty straightforwardly say, “We’re not going to give you any of your questions in advance, this is going to be a real conversation. We’re not going to pay you. If you don’t want to come, don’t come because there’s three people lined up behind you.”

*This is a real benefit of being where you are in the startup ecosystem and having 4,000 inbound emails.*

*MP:* Exactly.

*That kind of solves your problem for you. It’s a little different if you want Mark Zuckerberg on stage, but that wasn’t your focus.*

*MP:* Yeah and those kinds of negotiations over big people, those are always fraught and anybody will attest to this, but those fundamental things don’t change for anybody because the fact of the matter is, “Okay, cool, this year we get a big win by being able to put so-and-so on the masthead or so-and-so on the roster”, but then that erosion trickles down.

*MP:* Yeah, I think that’s the exact right way to think about it. It’s tough, man, but that’s why we dragged the programming back over the editorial line pretty early on, we did it early, but that programming, coming from the editorial side, meant that we were able to tap all of our writers who really genuinely knew who was germane in that moment, who was really interesting in that moment, in the moment we were in, and who was a good get? *MP:* Really, truly! Yeah, absolutely. That’s no outside person coming in saying, “Oh, here are the hot people you need to have right now and we have a Rolodex and we’ll get you them and blah, blah, blah.” That’s all in-house, which is tough. Cooking, Apple, and the Vision Pro

*MP:* (laughing) Is this an ad read, Ben?

*MP:* Oh, the thing’s magic. It has changed the way I cook, to be honest. It’s amazing.

I just have known a lot of people at that company and I think, fundamentally, it’s made up of a vast number of people that really, really, really give a shit about everything.
