* The Malone-Maffei split: John Malone is planning ( https://www.wsj.com/business/media/liberty-media-ceo-stepping-down-as-company-spins-off-live-entertainment-business-c9e08398?st=7QzisR&utm_campaign=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F13%2F24%29&utm_content=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F2%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) to spin off Liberty Media’s live entertainment portfolio, including its stake in Live Nation, into a separate business, while keeping Formula One in the core media portfolio—a characteristically
pragmatic decision to simplify his investments as he sets up his estate. Meanwhile, Malone is dispensing with Liberty C.E.O. Greg Maffei and taking the reins himself, a move that was probably long overdue given Maffei’s myriad missteps in recent years, from SiriusXM and Pandora to TripAdvisor. Of course, the folks who really deserve credit for Liberty’s success are Live Nation C.E.O. Michael Rapino and Formula One chief Chase Carey.
* The Iger succession: The hunt for Bob Iger’s successor has, not surprisingly, extended well beyond Burbank: A new report in the Journal says incoming board chair James Gorman is considering several external candidates, including Electronic Arts C.E.O. Andrew Wilson. As I reported ( https://puck.news/brian-roberts-one-that-got-away/?utm_campaign=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F13%2F24%29&utm_content=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F2%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) back in May 2022, Iger had once tried to recruit Wilson to run ESPN, post-Skipper and pre-Pitaro, and the two subsequently talked about Disney acquiring EA. (Wilson also engaged Brian Roberts, who considered spinning off NBCUniversal, merging it with E.A., and putting Wilson in charge).

All that said, Wilson is by no means a frontrunner. In fact, several well-placed sources still put their money on Disney entertainment co-chair Dana Walden, despite her lack of experience ( https://puck.news/on-waldens-shores/?1731481983&utm_campaign=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F13%2F24%29&utm_content=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F2%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) beyond the company’s core content business, and, yes, despite her donations to Kamala. One plausible scenario I’ve heard:
Dana gets appointed C.E.O.; current C.F.O. Hugh Johnston serves alongside her as president and C.O.O.; Iger stays on as chairman in perpetuity (go figure); and Gorman becomes lead independent director. Whatever happens, we’re still a long way out from the transition.
* The Santucci trials: Much further down the Disney org chart, several ABC News insiders remain concerned about executive editorial producer John Santucci’s likely ascension as the network’s primary conduit to the Trump administration. Santucci, you’ll recall, was the one who quietly gave false assurances to Trump campaign officials that his network’s moderators would not fact-check the candidate during the ABC presidential debate in September. I’m told there have been other occasions in which Santucci brokered concessions with the Trump camp that rubbed his colleagues the wrong way.

Santucci’s defenders say he is simply an aggressive
journalist who is being unfairly maligned by critics envious of his success, though even they acknowledge that he inspires what one source described as an “allergic reaction” among his peers. Santucci, I’ve learned, has also been the subject of multiple H.R. complaints in recent years related to what current and former ABC News sources describe as aggressive, bullying behavior—all of which have been addressed by the network, which declined to comment on the matter.
* Secretary Hegseth: Late Tuesday, Trump nominated Fox News host Pete Hegseth to serve as the secretary of defense, a choice that the Times charitably described as “outside the norm.” (Politico has a more colorful account ( https://www.politico.com/news/2024/11/12/defense-world-reacts-to-trump-pick-pete-hegseth-00189221?utm_campaign=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F13%2F24%29&utm_content=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F2%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) of the defense industry’s reaction). Hegseth is a veteran of the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan and, in addition to being a vocal champion of Trump on Fox, has used the platform to defend service members accused of war crimes. One additional detail I learned today about Hegseth’s tenure at Fox News: Around 2017, he had an extramarital affair with one of his show’s producers, Jennifer Rauchet, that resulted in a pregnancy. Hegseth later divorced his second wife and
married Rauchet, now Hegseth.

In any event, Fox gave Hegseth a glowing sendoff, describing him as “an exceptional host on Fox & Friends and Fox Nation” whose “insights and analysis especially about the military resonated deeply with our viewers and made the program the major success that it is
today.” Indeed, it seems Hegseth would have had a bright future at the network had he elected to stay. How his insights and analysis inform his leadership of the Pentagon remains to be seen, but a good field guide comes from his book, The War on Warriors, in which he condemns the “cultural chaos and weakness” of the military.
* The Nuzzi-Lizza denouement: Olivia Nuzzi, the former star New York political journalist and R.F.K. Jr. paramour, has withdrawn her request for a protective order against ex-fiancé Ryan Lizza after failing to win a motion to seal the court proceedings. In response, Lizza issued a fiery statement accusing Nuzzi of “shamelessly” using “false and defamatory allegations as a public relations strategy,” only to withdraw her “fabricated claims” upon learning that “her lies would be exposed.” Anyway, this whole sordid saga already feels like a relic of the 2024 campaign, now in the
rearview. Only two questions remain: Will Lizza, who remains on leave from Politico amid an internal investigation, return to that job? And what is Nuzzi’s next act?
* Insider, outsider: Speaking of second acts, Nicholas Carlson, the former Business Insider editor who left after all the sturm und drang over the site’s plagiarism allegations against Neri Oxman, has reemerged. Yes, Nich left of his own volition, but Axel Springer chief Mathias Döpfner also wanted to fire him, as both Semafor and the Times have reported. In any event, he’s back now as the founder of Dynamo, a new digital video company that will produce business news videos for social media platforms.

If you’re dubious about the viability of a business that contends that evergreen premium video explainers can compete with solo content creators on platforms it doesn’t control, well, so was I. But Carlson assures me there’s room for
everyone: Dynamo, he says, is “for aspirational business enthusiasts who, like us, believe business explains the world. We’ll start with one channel on the major platforms and one show. Each episode will be a feature journalism story. Our goal is to tell great stories that are very popular.” Good luck!

The backstory of Wallace’s self-defenestration at CNN—the latest shoe to drop in an era that won’t stop ending.

DYLAN BYERS ( https://puck.news/author/dylan-byers/?utm_campaign=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F13%2F24%29&utm_content=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F2%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a )

On Monday evening, Chris Wallace, the veteran newsman, CNN anchor, and ultimate Washington establishmentarian, called the network’s talent chief Amy Entelis to inform her that he intended to leave at the end of his contract. Minutes later, to the consternation of his bosses and presumably their P.R. department, he gave a reporter at The Daily Beast the green light to publish a precooked exclusive about his departure. The piece asserted that the 77-year-old broadcaster was walking away from a seven-figure contract to find a new home in “streaming or podcasting,” which, as Wallace told the reporter, is “where the action seems to be.”

The story, with its Onion-worthy headline—Chris Wallace Quits CNN to Build Future in Streaming—described Wallace’s exit as a “watershed moment for cable TV,” a
remarkably rare defection by a lifelong TV veteran who had determined he would rather forgo his lucrative salary and test new arenas than continue to suffer through the cable business’s slow but inexorable decline. Indeed, the piece seemed to portray Wallace as the news industry’s own Adrian Wojnarowski, the star ESPN NBA reporter who recently gave up his own $7 million-a-year gig to run the men’s basketball program at his alma mater in rural northwestern New York. As it happens, Wallace was also making $7 million a year at CNN.

The truth is always more nuanced, of course, though in some cases it’s also more simple. As every veteran of this industry knows, almost no one gives up the anchor chair of their own volition: not Hoda Kotb, who only determined she’d reached “the top of the wave” at NBC’s Today show after learning that she’d be asked to take a pay cut ( https://puck.news/what-hoda-kotbs-today-show-departure-means-for-the-star-anchor-era/?1731534460&utm_campaign=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F13%2F24%29&utm_content=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F2%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ); not Norah O’Donnell, whose desire to pursue more Pope Francis-style interview specials was spurred on by CBS’s perplexing decision
to dispense with ( https://puck.news/anchors-away/?utm_campaign=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2811%2F13%2F24%29&utm_content=In+The+Room+-+SUBSCRIBERS+%2810%2F2%2F24%29&utm_medium=email_action&utm_source=customer.io&utm_term=f6c60617da42abd12a ) a star-led strategy at Evening News; and certainly not Chris Wallace. Yes, everyone wants to spend more time with their families or write new chapters. But, as a wise media executive once told me, “they also don’t want to work for less money even though they have already made a fortune.”

In Wallace’s case, CNN C.E.O. Mark Thompson was planning to cancel both of his shows: Who’s Talking to Chris Wallace, the weekly interview series that was originally supposed to air on CNN+ and now airs on Max—until Friday, when it will air its final episode—and the Saturday morning panel discussion program, The Chris Wallace Show. Instead, Thompson and Entelis intended to offer Wallace a new role as an analyst, which would have
resulted in a significantly diminished compensation package and, presumably, far less air time. The CNN leaders conveyed that news to Wallace’s agents, though Wallace maintains that he never engaged in any discussions about a future role because he’d already set his course. “It doesn’t matter what was or wasn’t said in that meeting because I had already decided with my wife six months ago to leave CNN,” Wallace told me in a text message. “Any further speculation is irrelevant.”

Regardless, Thompson’s decision to cancel his shows was obviously the right one. The most recent broadcast of The Chris Wallace Show drew 450,000 viewers and just 85,000 in the advertiser-coveted 25-to-54-year-old demo—an audience size at which it’s increasingly hard to justify most of the talent salaries at CNN. While the network will continue to handsomely compensate its major franchise players—Jake Tapper isn’t going anywhere, for
instance—it will need to be more creative, and more frugal, when it comes to highly compensated commentators like Wallace. Ever the diplomat, Thompson issued a graceful statement on Monday calling the former Fox News Sunday host “one of the most respected political journalists in the news business.”

As I reported last week, Thompson is on the verge of implementing a new round of layoffs that will target hundreds of employees, after which he plans to create new positions on the digital side of the business. Inevitably, those cuts will include some high-priced TV talent, as well. Wallace would like the world to believe that he got out before the storm hit. In truth, he was one of its first and most obvious casualties.
