 around here is: Who controls a company? Whatis a company, anyway? In many
cases, it is mostly a collection ofpeople working on some common problem. And
then there is some corporate legal structure attached to those people, but
that’s not always what matters. If you work at an investment banking boutique
and you don’t like your boss, and you come into the office one day with
doughnuts
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2017-11-28/regulatory-doughnuts-and-fx-fees?cmpid=BBD080724_MONEYSTUFF&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=240807&utm_campaign=moneystuff>
 and say to all your colleagues “hey, our boss is bad, I would be a good boss,
I rented an office across the street, come with me and we’ll do a better job,”
and they come with you, did you do a hostile takeover? In some formal sense no:
The old corporate entity still exists, and you don’t own it; the old firm’s
contracts are still in force. But really the companywas its employees, and
their knowledge and client relationships, and if they all go with you then you
gotmost of the company. The bankers will call their old clients and say “hello
we moved across the street and are a different legal entity, but really we’re
still the same company with the same great service, call me here instead,” and
the clients will mostly do that.

This doesn’t happen too much. Lots of companies have, like, factories and
equipment and stores, and you can’t just ask them to move across the street.
Other companies are huge, and you couldn’t convince all the employees to move
across the street; the corporate structure and its bureaucracy are necessary
for controlling that many people. Actual investment banks are mostly large
levered financial institutions with big balance sheets, and you can’t move the
balance sheet across the street.

But there are some small companies whose assets really do consist mostly of
their employees’ knowledge and skills and relationships, and at those
companies, if one charismatic leader doesn’t like corporate management and can
convince most of the employees to go with her, then she kind of can do a
hostile takeover without corporate formalities. Something like this happens
every now and then with law firms.

I suppose the weirder case is where a small company (1) consists mostly of its
employees’ knowledge and skills and relationships and (2) has raised a ton of
outside capital. Like, you get 20 people together and you go out to investors
saying “we are so smart and have such good relationships, we’re gonna make a
ton of money, and we’re willing to give you a cut of it if you give us money
now.” The investors say “wow youare very smart, here’s a billion dollars for a
20% cut of what you’re up to.”

But you could all still walk away? In some sense you’d be leaving money on the
table: If investors gave you $1 billion for 20%, that means that the other 80%
of the firm — the part that you and your friends still own — is worth $4
billion, so you are abandoning your $4 billion equity stake if you go across
the street and start over. On the other hand, if “the firm” was really just you
and your friends and your prospects, then if you go across the street and start
over, thenew firm will be worth $5 billion (and the husk of the old firm will
be worth $0), and you’ll own all of it, so you’ll actually come out ahead. Keep
doing that trade over and over again. [1]  <> Presumably investors would
eventually ask you for a non-compete.

And then the even weirder case is an outside acquirer. A big company comes to
your small company and says “we think your company is worth $8 billion and we
would like to own it” and you say “great sure we would like $8 billion.” And
the big company says “okay so we’ll write a check for $1.6 billion to your
outside investors, who own 20% of the company, and $6.4 billion to you, who own
the other 80%.” And you say “well, wait, no. That $8 billion valuation is just
forus, for our knowledge and skills and relationships. That’s what you want:
us. We are what you ascribe all that value to. The outside investors aren’t
bringing anything to the table; all they did was give us money a while ago.
Tell you what, we’ll just abandon our old company and come to your company, and
you can pay us $7.5 billion of signing bonuses. You’ll get the thing you want —
us — at a discount to fair value, and we’ll get paid more than we would for our
equity in the old firm.”

This is all a bit silly and nothing really works like that, though some tech
industry “acquihires <https://a16z.com/the-complete-guide-to-acquihires/>” can
get pretty close. [2]  <>In part this is because you can’t find too many cases
ofpure firms like this, firms that get 100% of their value from human capital
and nothing else; in part because firms like this tend not to raise lots of
outside capital; and in part because, when they do, the outside investors will
generally get some safeguards (noncompete agreements, fiduciary duties from the
top employees, etc.) to prevent the human capital from vanishing.

Still it’s a fun thought experiment. Here is a dumb little footnote to the
thought experiment: When the big company “acquires” you and your friends by
paying you signing bonuses, is that an acquisition that triggers pre-merger
notifications under the US Hart-Scott-Rodino Antitrust Improvements Act of
1976? In general, the HSR Actrequires companies
<https://www.ftc.gov/enforcement/premerger-notification-program/hsr-resources/steps-determining-whether-hsr-filing>
 to ask antitrust regulators for permission before doing acquisitions worth
more than$119.5 million
<https://www.ftc.gov/enforcement/competition-matters/2024/02/new-hsr-thresholds-filing-fees-2024>
. If you buy more than $119.5 million ofassets, or of voting stock
<https://www.ftc.gov/enforcement/premerger-notification-program/hsr-resources/valuation-transactions-reportable-under>
, of a company, you have to file. And the regulators understand that there are
other ways to structure acquisitions that could have antitrust implications.
Certainpharmaceutical patent licenses are treated as asset sales
<https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2013/11/atr_lf_hsrrulesexpandforpharmalicensingagreements_08nov13>
 for HSR, because “I will license your drug” is an alternative way to say “I
will buy your small pharma company,” and the regulators don’t want that to be a
loophole.

But if you just hire some people, you probably don’t have to file anything?
Again this is not a particularly live issue, because (1) there are not many
acquisitions like this [3]  <> and (2) to the extent there are, they tend not
to have bigantitrust implications. If your company’s value consists purely of
your and your friends’ knowledge and relationships, it’s probably not a
monopoly, and anyone who acquires you is probably not going to have a monopoly.

How much do AI startups resemble my schematic model of a company whose value
consists entirely of its employees’ knowledge, skills and relationships? I
don’t know. AI startups are, relative to law firms or consultancies, quite
capital-intensive (it takes a lot of computers to train a large language
model), and they tend to have a lot of valuable intellectual property (thus the
licensing agreements). On the other hand, it does not seem like they are
generally being acquired because they are promising operating businesses that
generate a lot of revenue. It seems like they are being acquired because they
have smart AI founders with smart AI ideas, and the big tech companies would
like to bring those founders and ideas in-house.

Or perhaps not, but the other point here is that these deals very much have
antitrust implications, in that (1) big tech companies get a lot of antitrust
scrutiny when they do any acquisitions, (2) anything AI-related gets a lot of
scrutiny and (3) commercializing AI seems to be so capital-intensive that
regulators are particularly worried about the rich big tech firms dominating it.

There are two sets of antitrust questions here. One is: Are these deals
anticompetitive? Should the government block them, or sue to unwind them? It’s

old firms and stop licensing the technology” or whatever.

Cash sweep sweep

If you go to a bank and say “I’d like to open an account and deposit some
cash,” they will happily open a checking account for you, take your cash, and
pay you an interest rate that is, in round numbers, zero. [4]  <> (My checking
account pays 0.01%.) If you say “no, I want a savings account, because those
have higher interest rates,” they will happily open a savings account, take
your cash, and pay you an interest rate that is …also 0.01%
<https://www.chase.com/personal/savings/savings-account/interest-rates>? Maybe
0.03% <https://www.citi.com/banking/current-interest-rates/savings-accounts>?
But if you utter the correct series of incantations, at least some banks will
open a high-yield savings account and pay you5% or more
<https://www.bankrate.com/banking/savings/best-high-yield-interests-savings-accounts/>
. You gotta find the right bank, though, and utter the right incantations.
Sometimes it’s tricky. At Capital One Financial for a while,you could get
<https://www.wsj.com/finance/investing/capital-one-high-interest-savings-complaints-b9adc1e9?mod=itp_wsj>
 0.3% by saying “360 Savings,” or 4.35% by saying “360 Performance Savings.” If
you forgot to ask for “Performance,” you didn’t get it.

And this is the way the world works. Banks make money by getting paid more
interest on their loans than they pay you on your deposits. They would prefer
to pay you less on your deposits. If you are willing to give them money and get
paid 0.01%, they will take that trade all day long. And then if that doesn’t
bring in enough money, they will go out and find other people who are willing
to give them money for 0.03%, or 0.3%, or, if necessary, 5%. But they don’t
have to giveyou 5%. If you’re willing to take 0.01%, that’s what you get.

People pretty regularly complain about this — “interest rates have gone up,
why doesn’t my bank pay me more?” — and sometimes they even do something about
it (they move their money to another bank that pays more, or to a money market
fund). But the banks do not particularly get introuble for it. Regulators
understand that banks need cheap funding, and that if they can get cheap
funding they’re supposed to take it. [5]  <>

If you go to a brokerage firm and say “I’d like to open an account and deposit
some cash while I wait to find some good stocks to buy,” they will happily open
an account for you, take your cash, and … well! They will probably pay you some
interest rate that is higher than 0.01% and lower than 5%. They would prefer to
pay you less, because brokerage firmsalso mainly make money
<https://www.kalzumeus.com/2019/6/26/how-brokerages-make-money/> by getting
paid more interest on their cash than they pay you on your deposits. [6]  <>
Some brokerages advertise “we pay a really high rate on cash,” and thus attract
customers who want to get paid a high rate on their cash. Others … don’t?
Others advertise, you know, “we will give you good financial advice” or “we
make trading fun” or “we have a good app” or whatever, and they get people who
do not pay attention to how much they will get paid on their cash.

On the other hand, there’s a sense that your broker has some fiduciary duty to
you, or ought to, particularly if your broker is also giving you financial
advice. If your broker is telling you what stocks to buy, she should probably
alsomention “hey, you have a bunch of money sitting idly in your brokerage
account, and we’re paying you like 1%, but you could just put it into aTreasury
money market fund
<https://investor.vanguard.com/investment-products/mutual-funds/profile/vusxx>
that is essentially cash but pays 5.4%.” That would cost her firm money — it
would give up your cheap cash — but would be good customer service.

Coke profits

The process of manufacturing Coke has essentially three steps:

 * Someone invented and perfected Coke, and the Coca-Cola Co. now owns the
extraordinarily valuable formula for it.
 * Someone uses that formula to make a syrup containing concentrated Coke
flavor.
 * Someone mixes that syrup with carbonated water and puts it in bottles to
sell to consumers.
Step 1 happens, in some notional sense, in the US, where Coca-Cola is
headquartered. Step 3 happens largely at outside bottling companies that have
some regional distribution; it’s inefficient to transport lots of Coke long
distances. Step 2 is more flexible: It’s easier to transport the concentrated
syrup, and the syrup is made by subsidiaries of Coca-Cola.

Which of these steps create the most value? Well, I don’t know, but it seems
clear that owning the formula for Coke is worth a lot more than, you know,
owning the plant that mixes the syrup with the water. Coca-Cola Co., which owns
that formula, has a market capitalization of almost $300 billion. Coca-Cola
Consolidated Inc., the big US bottler, has a market cap of about $11 billion.

There is a tax trade. The tax trade, for Coca-Cola, is to do Step 2 in very
low-tax jurisdictions. You incorporate a subsidiary in Ireland, you build a
syrup plant there, the Irish subsidiary pays Coca-Cola a relatively small
amount of money for its very valuable formula, it makes the syrup, and then it
sells the syrup for a very large amount of money to bottlers. The Irish
subsidiary has high profits, and pays low taxes on them; the US entity has a
higher tax rate but relatively low profits.

This was the trade, the US Internal Revenue Service complained, and the IRS
won in court. TheFinancial Times reports
<https://www.ft.com/content/cd05fb4b-8e67-4996-a5f3-bf1b8ccdf3af>:

The soft-drink maker has been hiding “astronomical levels” of profit in
low-tax countries including Ireland to shield it from the US Internal Revenue
Service, according to a withering court judgment, which the company is planning
to appeal against later this year. ...

After the last in a four-year string of tax court decisions last week, Coke
will shortly have to fork out an initial $6bn in cash to cover unpaid taxes and
interest for the years 2007 to 2009.

But the story isn’t about that; it’s about accounting:

Neither that sum, nor the $10bn it could owe for the subsequent 15 years, will
show up as a hit to its earnings any time soon.

As long as the Atlanta-based company and its longtime auditor EY agree that
there is a better than 50-50 chance of Coke winning on appeal, payments do not
have to be put through its profit and loss account. …

John Murphy, Coke’s chief financial officer, told the Financial Times the
assessment had been blessed by its advisers.

“We have outside counsel who have, each quarter, continued to evaluate the
case on the facts that are available to them and they continue to offer an
opinion that gives us a greater-than-not chance of prevailing,” he said. “And
then EY will do their own independent assessment to be comfortable with that
opinion.”

EY wrote in a note to Coke’s last annual report that there was a “level of
subjectivity and significant judgment” to the assessment of the company’s tax
position, but that it had also consulted its own experts on the matter. EY has
been Coke’s auditor for 103 years, signing off annual accounts that include the
provisions Coke has made for taxes over the years.

Basically a judge has ruled that Coke owes the IRS $16 billion, but Coke’s
lawyers and auditors have ruled that the judge is wrong, so under US generally
accepted accounting principles Coke doesn’t owe the IRS the $16 billion, and
when it writes the IRS a check for $6 billion, under accounting rules that
check does not count as an expense because it will probably come right back.

KKR TRA

Henry Kravis and George Roberts are two of the founders — the second K and the
R — of KKR & Co., the private equity firm. For a long time it was a private
partnership, but it went public in 2010 and became a corporation in 2018. In
going public, Kravis and Roberts had to consider two big changes:

 * Control: When they were the senior partners of a private partnership, they
could just tell everyone else what to do; they controlled the company. A public
company has shares and voting rights and a board of directors, and there are
instances of founders being pushed out by activist shareholders.
 * Taxes: When they were partners of a private partnership, they just got a
share of the company’s earnings. A public company normally pays corporate
income taxes.
There are standard approaches. Kravis and Roberts got themselves a “golden
share,” a special preferred stock that they owned jointly and that gave them
control over the public company. (In particular, it allowed them to appoint
KKR’s board of directors.)

For taxes, the standard approach is more complicated. It is called an “Up-C”
corporate structure: The founders make their partnership a subsidiary of a new
publicly traded corporation; public shareholders get shares in the corporation,
but the founders and other partners keep their ownership units in the
partnership. If they ever want tosell those units, they can convert them into
the public shares and sell those. Each partnership unit is convertible into one
public share.

Normally the conversion is taxable: When the partners convert their units into
shares, they pay tax on the fair value of the shares. But the conversion also
generates big tax benefits for the company, essentially increasing its basis in
its assets. And so the standard approach is to have a tax receivable agreement,
or TRA, in which the company agrees to pay 85% of its tax savings to the
converting partners (and keep the rest). The TRA payments roughly offset the
partners’ taxes on conversion, while the company keeps 15% of the benefits.
Part of the theory here is thatthis is roughly fair
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2023-08-30/sculptor-is-full-of-conflicts?cmpid=BBD080724_MONEYSTUFF&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=240807&utm_campaign=moneystuff>
 (the partners get compensated for the tax hit from converting), but part of
the theory is that public marketsdo not value the tax savings very much
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2024-06-11/tax-deals-can-cause-conflicts?cmpid=BBD080724_MONEYSTUFF&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=240807&utm_campaign=moneystuff>
, so the partners might as well keep them.

Empirically, though, TRAs can sometimes get weird. What happens is often not
that the partners convert their units into shares, pay taxes, and then get TRA
payments. Instead, the partners often don’t convert — they have enough money,
from their salaries or whatever, [8]  <> to fund their lifestyles without
selling any shares — and so they don’t get any TRA payments. But they become
possessive of their TRA rights: The TRA is a big future contingent payment
obligation from the company to them, it has a value, they cansell it to
investors
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2024-01-02/fake-surgeries-are-securities-fraud?cmpid=BBD080724_MONEYSTUFF&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=240807&utm_campaign=moneystuff>
, etc. In particular, the TRA tends to be very valuablein a merger: If the
company is acquired, often there will be a very favorable payout on the TRA,
and the founders who were never going to sell any shares get a big windfall.

And so sometimes companies will come to their founders and say “hey, good
news. We can convert your partnership units into publicly traded shareswithout
triggering taxes for you, or tax benefits for us. So let’s just do that and get
rid of the TRA.” And the founders will be like “no, this TRA is worth a lot of
money to us, you’ll have to pay us to get rid of it.”

Or, perhaps, sometimes the founders will go to the companies and say “hey,
good news, you can convert our partnership units into publicly traded shares
without triggering taxes for us, or tax benefits for you. So let’s just do
that, and you can pay us a pile of money to get rid of the TRA.” And then the
founders will get a fresh pile of money.

That allegedly happened at KKR. The Wall Street Journal reports
<https://www.wsj.com/finance/kkr-founders-face-suit-alleging-they-got-giant-payday-for-no-work-97ab72d8?mod=hp_lead_pos3>
:

When Henry Kravis and George Roberts handed off the day-to-day management of
private-equity firm KKR to their successors in 2021, the two billionaires
netted shares now worth more than $650 million.

A new lawsuit is accusing the company of paying Kravis and Roberts but getting
nothing in return. Their windfalls came from a complicated financial structure

The KKR suit takes aim at a payment allegedly tied to a tax receivable

structures to create and share potentially valuable tax assets between the

Here is the lawsuit
<https://assets.bwbx.io/documents/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/rlVKS2Vb9d0M/v0>. The
claim is that the founders saw other public private equity firms doing tax-free
conversions and paying out their founders for their TRAs, and they wanted in on
the action:

By June 2021, the Founders were keenly aware that two KKR rivals—Apollo and
Carlyle—had engaged in restructurings to convert to more traditional corporate
structures. The Founders were also keenly aware that as part of those
restructurings, Apollo’s and Carlyle’s founders received
multi-hundred-million-dollar payments in connection with tax-free exchanges of
private partnership units into public equity.

Kravis and Roberts seized on the idea of extracting a similar payoff from KKR
in connection with the tax-free exchange of their units. Kravis and Roberts did
so in conjunction with their stepping down as co-CEOs and the promotions of Bae
and Nuttall as co-CEOs.

And, the lawsuit claims, they ultimately did extract a big payment for their
TRA. But not in so many words. KKR hired Evercore Inc. as a financial adviser,
and Evercore pointed out that eliminating the TRA would have anegative value
for KKR. (KKR would eliminate its obligation to pay the founders 85% of the tax
savings, but it would also eliminate 100% of those savings.) So there was not
much reason to pay the founders to get out of it. (Also, the lawyers suing KKR
in this case previously sued Apollo and Carlyle over their deals, and, they
claim, “advisors to KKR … understood the prospect of litigation and the
infirmity of the rationale for the Apollo and Carlyle TRA payoff.”)

So, says the lawsuit, instead of paying the founders off for their TRA, KKR
struck out the words “TRA Termination Payment” and put in a different reason:
KKR would pay Kravis and Roberts hundreds of millions of dollars’ worth of
stock for giving up their control rights. Instead of co-owning a single golden
share that gave them the right to appoint the board of directors, they would
just own normal shares, albeitlots of normal shares that (along with their
titles as co-chairmen, their initials on the door, etc.) would give them a fair
amount of continuing control. KKR reasoned that the golden share was worth a
lot of money, so it made sense to pay Kravis and Roberts to give it up.

On the other hand, they didn’t want to give it up. So the actual deal was that
Kravis and Roberts would be paid hundreds of millions of dollars, in 2021, to
give up the golden share, in 2026. They continue to control the board, but
eventually they won’t, and they got to charge KKR in advance for that agreement.

I don’t know? It does seem a bit generous. But who controls a company? What is
the value of the controlling shareholders and name founders of a company
agreeing to give up their formal voting control five years in the future?
Should KKR have paid them hundreds of millions of dollars (of stock) for that?
These questions do not seem easily answerable with conventional financial
analysis. [9]  <> If you invest in a company that is permanently controlled by
its founders whose initials are on the door, you should expect to have to pay
them to give up that control: They control the company! Why shouldn’t they take
a little something for themselves on the way out?
