Netflix’s Boxing Event
----------------------

From Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-11-16/downdetector-shows-netflix-having-issues-before-paul-tyson-fight ) :

Netflix Inc.’s foray into live sports streaming with the boxing match between Mike Tyson and Jake Paul drew a massive global audience that peaked at 65 million viewers but resulted in thousands of complaints from viewers about connection problems and frozen screens. “We crashed the site,” said Paul, 27, after claiming victory over the 58-year-old Tyson. “This is the biggest event.”

In an internal company memo seen by Bloomberg News, Chief Technology Officer Elizabeth Stone said the bout drew a maximum of 65 million viewers at any one time, rivaling some of the most-watched sporting events. Some of those viewers took to social media to vent their frustration over glitches in streaming the highly-anticipated showdown. More than 100,000 users reported network issues on the Downdetector website throughout the livestreaming, commenting on slow buffering and network issues. Even boxer Evander Holyfield was struggling to hear the host of the show when he was asked about his infamous fight with Tyson. Netflix declined to comment on the overall outcome of the event.

The company’s biggest live-streaming event is part of its ambition to boost its fledgling advertising business. Netflix has been investing in its live-event capabilities for mega spectacles like the boxing match after earlier livestreaming missteps such as the Love Is Blind reunion debacle. The platform ran a teaser for upcoming NFL games on Christmas Day during Friday’s bout.

I did watch this fight, for a very simple reason: it was clear to me that it was going to be an event — something that dominated talk amongst my friends and on social media — and events like that are rare and precious. That may be an odd adjective to use for what turned out to a fairly boring and rather sad headline event (although the early fights were excellent), but it’s the monocultural aspect that I’m referring to. I wrote in Disney’s Taylor Swift Era ( https://stratechery.com/2023/disneys-taylor-swift-era/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIzL2Rpc25leXMtdGF5bG9yLXN3aWZ0LWVyYS8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTczNDYxMzIwNiwiaWF0IjoxNzMyMDIxMjA2LCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwaS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IlJzZXVZRThqZ0hlUHBXSmYxZkFiNEMiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.mNYrVJP2KVYlV0Pb0C6s553wV4yOwi3Sy7MVS5Y64_wVhG8POimpSTAvbKwQ235HeIMMmTVQInuQ8WDCKqKiq-LBwglHvcrD7rrO2mzq8ZpYPpeB1EvQQl-BrDVDTshg6ayyayk5Ivacpx4mqrqjZEZrzgS2M7oTpjx7kzisfH_NqKfUDmppFPUSIYkacY31xQVCynXHynxBhEc3CKXWVe0z0hxOQkjEi1lU_cn-aAU5QJ3Fk3ivNNb7HVTlKrbmD4L8dUzAEZ6Uy1V0rGCEe3lX8tI6-t5cvxy0JaDon4K00XNsE7ECn44kZ-DjOV_imDQ60-iupyC1En5tQii-8A ) :

There is much to say about the summer of Taylor that is pertinent to technology and the Internet: start with the desire for communal in-person experiences driven not only by the pandemic, but also the general fracturing of culture inherent in a media landscape where personalized content delivered directly to your personal device is the norm. Then there is the way in which social media acts as a FOMO generator: being able to access every moment of every show on social media doesn’t decrease the value of attending the show, but rather increases the desire to obtain a scarce number of tickets to see the spectacle in person. And, it must be said, there is the excellence at play: not only does Swift have an incredible catalog of popular songs, the show itself spares no expense — or exertion on Swift’s part — to give the fans exactly what they were hoping for.

The Tyson-Paul fight wasn’t exactly analogous to Swift’s Eras Tour, although there was a live audience of 70,000 at AT&T Stadium in Dallas; what there was in common was the expectation and delivery of a shared experience amplified by social media, not fractured by it. And, in that regard, it delivered; to put that 65 million figure in perspective:

* 67 million people watched the Trump-Harris presidential debate, although the latter is a U.S.-only figure, and does not include online streaming platforms. 73 million watched the Trump-Biden debate this summer, and 84 million watched the first debate between Trump and Hillary Clinton.
* 84 million people watched the Cheers finale, 76 million people watched the Seinfeld finale, and 53 million people watched the Friends finale.
* 124 million people watched the 2024 Super Bowl; to loop streaming into this discussion, 23 million people watched the NFL playoff game that was on Peacock.

Again, that Netflix streaming number is global; I watched it in Taiwan, and didn’t have any streaming issues, probably because I didn’t have much competition for the stream. The challenge for Netflix is that while most of its content is on-demand, streaming a live event means everyone wants the same content at the same time, which isn’t pre-loaded onto the servers Netflix co-locates with most of the world’s ISPs. It’s interesting to note that linear television, which is so often viewed as an inferior user experience because it is not on-demand, is in fact perfectly suited to this sort of massive one-to-many broadcast (the problem is that this is the format via which everything is delivered, even if it’s not live and watched by everyone everywhere all at once).

Given that inherent complexity, I have to assume that Netflix viewed this event as an unbridled success, even with the hiccups it encountered (and, for what it’s worth, the streaming service appeared to have solved the streaming issues by the time the main event rolled around). Those viewership numbers I posted above are hallowed grounds in terms of television broadcasts; they look even better if you place them in the context of 2023’s most viewed broadcasts. Customer Acquisition vs. Churn Mitigation
-----------------------------------------

You noticed, I’m sure, the extent to which football, particularly the NFL, dominated that list of most seen events. To that end, Netflix didn’t — contra that Bloomberg excerpt — have any ads in the brodcast (although there were ringside sponsors); they did, however, run promos for their upcoming Christmas-slate of NFL games between rounds. And, on Monday, they added one more piece of news; from Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-11-18/beyonce-to-join-netflix-s-nfl-debut-in-coup-for-mega-events-push ) :

Beyoncé will headline the halftime show during Netflix Inc.’s maiden foray into live NFL games this Christmas, a big score for a US company betting big on live sports and mega-events. Netflix, which in May secured the rights to air two National Football League games this Christmas, dropped the surprise announcement after streaming one of the most-watched boxing matchups of all time. The Grammy-winning artist will perform songs from her latest Cowboy Carter album for the first time during a contest between the Houston Texans and Baltimore Ravens in her hometown. She’s expected to invite “special guests,” Netflix said without elaborating.

Beyoncé has appeared at NFL football games previously: she headlined the 2013 Super Bowl and appeared as a guest at the 2016 Super Bowl. Those are, as you can tell from the above numbers, the biggest events of all, and speak, I suspect, to Netflix’s ambitions for this broadcast: Netflix isn’t treating Christmas Day as a couple of regular season games, but rather as events. That’s exactly what co-CEO Ted Sarandos promised on the company’s second quarter earnings call ( https://s22.q4cdn.com/959853165/files/doc_events/2024/Jul/18/netflix-inc-usa-d815629a-440e-4f6b-9f5e-831213418dd0.pdf ) :

*How do you thread the needle on licensing sports to drive advertising spend without becoming beholden to leagues at renewal?*

Well, hopefully, exactly the way we’re doing it, by making these Netflix events, not necessarily taking on a lot of tonnage from any one league, by actually making these games events like having two NFL football games on Christmas Day and two great games, the Chiefs and the Steelers and the Ravens and the Texans. They’re both going to be great games, and it really creates a lot of real excitement with the service, and it’s one day of football.

This lined up with how I characterized Netflix’s strategy when they signed the deal in May ( https://stratechery.com/2024/netflix-and-the-nfl-netflix-internalizes-ads-comcasts-bundle/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI0L25ldGZsaXgtYW5kLXRoZS1uZmwtbmV0ZmxpeC1pbnRlcm5hbGl6ZXMtYWRzLWNvbWNhc3RzLWJ1bmRsZS8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTczNDYxMzIwNiwiaWF0IjoxNzMyMDIxMjA2LCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwaS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IlJzZXVZRThqZ0hlUHBXSmYxZkFiNEMiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.ntv9xwTou40OWYb3l2TWdVeu0RQVvkgFnrrB6HX4h4t4HVcnw5wiFBbEgPxyciztmpepGa8ORYlUReoPGJ6TU19eRYsQNlt6t2n6iTxykappGM_gh1DxtpSY-tX5QPVwiFq-1OOaL2YBFyDYXZgmJSc-QOVLAlYSYJLVGY8lZgoiMoiRhoCw1MTpn50MH9cWVzr5eKMHLbzqlXF1bYtaFmuE1tKD23giKwXGeWT8Kkxb14KzcAPY3BXNCrEsCjJzHF9M0uKAP1ecihu43idgTtpXF9D5cm78ZobE2tUixtT_gIFJKQP1OXZRMWitfaUlBeycAa_xN0n5oqgOZnw2iw ) :

At this point, I think my overall conclusion on this deal is clear: a single-day NFL package is, unlike most of the other NFL deals, one that is primarily useful for customer acquisition, not churn mitigation. That, meanwhile, is exactly what Netflix wants. Moreover, the NFL reaches audiences that Netflix wants to reach more effectively than anything else; the challenge/opportunity for Netflix will be to retain that audience for the long run, while monetizing them via ads, which is to say the challenge/opportunity presented by the NFL is perfectly aligned with Netflix’s corporate objectives.

To zoom out on this point, I noted in a 2018 Update ( https://stratechery.com/2018/netflix-earnings-netflixs-rising-cac-content-and-marketing/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDE4L25ldGZsaXgtZWFybmluZ3MtbmV0ZmxpeHMtcmlzaW5nLWNhYy1jb250ZW50LWFuZC1tYXJrZXRpbmcvIl19LCJleHAiOjE3MzQ2MTMyMDYsImlhdCI6MTczMjAyMTIwNiwiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcGkucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCBlbnRpdGxlbWVudHMiLCJzdWIiOiJSc2V1WUU4amdIZVBwV0pmMWZBYjRDIiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.jqeHj-Xoe7zADMBrCiTRMK-j4D3XRSX34J95XdfS7pLTXWIxUM1VECsslXP_ecaAU4YQ5uDGikCB_gEftwooQUcUGBVCwkX-old3kAQsFKbBHvYFPREEhUWGh89OiueDQkHFHxNLeT3Z81VjCD6X9ptvyqbm5r9fu06f-lsQcOr-p2g730XMhRmknT81IiLVVWnEnZeFheVPET-7sR1tC6LczpmJWE02Bk6X0MtiCVRHE-CgzjnRzn9xUqBAmqmDnBugp4P75rPy0paFL6SjW1jolr535vGExjfK_KH_QAdO0X_HxF0WncmtFZZJfmtAAAzdHJoE8i7pM57N3Hk3oA ) that one of the challenges in accounting for Netflix’s costs is that some portion of its content spend was actually marketing spend, in that content drove customer acquisition. In the idealized version of Netflix, which I held at the time, spending on evergreen content actually increased Netflix’s marginal attractiveness to the marginal customer, who could not only watch the latest Netflix shows but all of the ones they missed.

In truth, that was probably over-stated; what I think this event strategy demonstrates is that customer acquisition content is fundamentally different from churn mitigraion content, and Netflix is leaning into that distinction. The vast majority of its shows are designed to be delivered on that same Netflix infrastructure that was built to deliver whatever a customer wants whenever they want it, on-demand and individualized; this boxing match, though, and the NFL games, are not evergreen content, and they’re not really designed for Netflix’s core product (thus the technical challenges). What they are is a moment in time where you have FOMO for missing out on a cultural event, and once you sign up to participate, well, now it’s a churn mitigation problem, which is exactly what Netflix is built for.

Accounting for Events
---------------------

To that end — and to re-establish my nerdy credentials after talking about boxing — I would like it if Netflix classified the cost of this content according to its purpose. If I had my druthers, all of the money the service is spending on events of this nature should go into marketing, not content, and it should hit the income statement immediately. After all, it’s not like anyone is going to go back and watch this fight years into the future; it’s going to be pretty silly to amortize the costs over an extended period.

For what it’s worth, I do not expect Netflix to classify the costs in this way — and to be sure, most viewers were almost certainly existing Netflix subscribers — but at a minimum I would expect management to exercise its discretion in terms of amortization. From Netflix’s 10-K ( https://d18rn0p25nwr6d.cloudfront.net/CIK-0001065280/c5e64982-659f-4726-97c9-c57767c3bec3.pdf ) :

Based on factors including historical and estimated viewing patterns, we amortize the content assets (licensed and produced) in “Cost of revenues” on the Consolidated Statements of Operations over the shorter of each title’s contractual window of availability or estimated period of use or ten years, beginning with the month of first availability. The amortization is on an accelerated basis, as we typically expect more upfront viewing, and film amortization is more accelerated than TV series amortization. On average, over 90% of a licensed or produced content asset is expected to be amortized within four years after its month of first availability. We review factors that impact the amortization of the content assets on a regular basis. Our estimates related to these factors require considerable management judgment.

For now, it’s enough to note the management judgment in realizing different business needs — customer acquisition and churn mitigation — do in fact require different strategies. And oh yeah, if you can build up an advertising business along the way, all the better.
