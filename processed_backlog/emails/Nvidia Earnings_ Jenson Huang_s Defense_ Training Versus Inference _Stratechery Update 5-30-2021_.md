Chip giant Nvidia is starting to capitalize on the craze for language-generating artificial intelligence, projecting a more than 64% jump in sales as the company rushes to get more processors in customer hands to satisfy booming interest in the technology. I wrote at the conclusion of that Article:

There certainly is no questioning Huang and Nvidia’s ambition, although some may quibble with the wisdom of navigating three valleys all at once; it’s perhaps appropriate that the stock is in a valley itself, above and beyond that perfect storm in gaming.

What is worth considering, though, is that the number one reason why Nvidia customers — both in the consumer market and the enterprise one — get frustrated with the company is price: Nvidia GPUs are expensive, and the company’s margins — other than the last couple of quarters — are very high. Pricing power in Nvidia’s case, though, is directly downstream from Nvidia’s own innovations, both in terms of sheer performance in established workloads, and also in its investment in the CUDA ecosystem creating the tools for entirely new ones.

In other words, Nvidia has earned the right to be hated by taking the exact sort of risks in the past it is embarking on now. Suppose, for example, the expectation for all games in the future is not just ray tracing but full-on simulation of all particles: Nvidia’s investment in hardware will mean it dominates the era just as it did the rasterized one. Similarly, if AI applications become democratized and accessible to all enterprises, not just the hyperscalers, then it is Nvidia who will be positioned to pick up the entirety of the long tail. And, if we get to a world of metaverses, then Nvidia’s head start on not just infrastructure but on the essential library of objects necessary to make that world real (objects that will be lit by ray-tracing in AI-generated spaces, of course), will make it the most essential infrastructure in the space.

These bets may not all pay off; I do, though, appreciate the audacity of the vision, and won’t begrudge the future margins that may result in the Celestial City if Nvidia makes it through the valley.

Jenson Huang’s Defense
----------------------

From Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-05-29/nvidia-nvda-unveils-more-ai-products-to-further-capitalize-on-frenzy ) :

In a two-hour presentation in Taiwan, Nvidia Corp. Chief Executive Officer Jensen Huang unveiled a new batch of products and services tied to artificial intelligence, looking to capitalize on a frenzy that has made his company the world’s most valuable chipmaker. The wide-ranging lineup includes a new robotics design, gaming capabilities, advertising services and a networking technology. Perhaps most central to his ambitions, Huang took the wraps off an AI supercomputer platform called DGX GH200 that will help tech companies create successors to ChatGPT. Microsoft Corp., Meta Platforms Inc. and Alphabet Inc.’s Google are expected to be among the first users.

The product announcements were interesting, but what I thought was particularly notable was the extended defense Huang gave of Nvidia’s AI prospects in the middle of the keynote. Huang started this section by describing how the IBM System/360 revolutionized computing by separating software from hardware, thus preserving and enhancing the investments made in the former even as the latter scaled with Moore’s Law. Huang then repeated his argument that Moore’s Law is ending, driving the need for what he calls “accelerated computing”, even as the killer use case for accelerated computing has arrived (i.e. AI generally, and generative AI specifically); and, of course, Huang believes that Nvidia is uniquely suited to deliver on accelerated computing.

The entire segment of Huang’s argument was 17 minutes long; I’m embedding the video here, but I’m not going to transcribe the whole thing:

( https://videopress.com/embed/WU0bvSYV?hd=1&cover=1&loop=0&autoPlay=0&permalink=1&muted=0&controls=1&playsinline=0&useAverageColor=0&preloadContent=metadata )

What I will do is summarize Huang’s arguments (with shorter clips), which had seven components:

*Accelerated Computing is a Full Stack Problem*

( https://videopress.com/embed/WU0bvSYV?hd=1&cover=1&loop=0&autoPlay=0&permalink=1&muted=0&controls=1&playsinline=0&useAverageColor=0&preloadContent=metadata )

This way of doing software, this way of doing computation, is a reinvention from the ground up, and it’s not easy. Accelerated computing is a full-stack problem. It’s not as easy as general purpose computing. The CPU is a miracle: high level programming languages, great compilers, almost anybody can write reasonably good programs, because the CPU is so flexible. However, its ability to scale in performance has ended and we need a new approach. Accelerated computing is full stack. You have to re-engineer everything from the top-down and bottom-up, from the chip to systems to the system software; new algorithms, and, of course, optimizing the applications.

This is correct, and bears on the points below. The vast majority of computing to date is still single-threaded: data centers are huge, with a ton of CPUs, but each individual job is by-and-large run on a single thread on a single CPU that can handle an array of tasks. Accelerated computing, on the other hand, is highly parallel: you run one job across multiple GPUs that are much simpler in their execution pipeline; this, by extension, means abstracting complexity up into the software.

*Accelerated Computing is a Data-Center Scale Problem*

( https://videopress.com/embed/BObsR7uQ?hd=1&cover=1&loop=0&autoPlay=0&permalink=1&muted=0&controls=1&playsinline=0&useAverageColor=0&preloadContent=metadata )

The second is that it’s a data-center scale problem. The reason why it’s a data-center scale problem is today the data-center is the computer. Unlike the past when the PC was a computer or your phone was a computer, today your data center is the computer. Your application runs across the entire data center. And therefore, it’s vital that you have to understand how to optimize chips, the compute, the software, across a NIC, the switch, all the way to other end in a distributed computing way.

This is the hardware manifestation of the previous point: if you are executing across multiple GPUs then your target is not any one core or any one chip, but rather the entire system that contains multiple chips.

*Accelerated Computing is Multi-Domain*

( https://videopress.com/embed/YY3Lf8pf?hd=1&cover=1&loop=0&autoPlay=0&permalink=1&muted=0&controls=1&playsinline=0&useAverageColor=0&preloadContent=metadata )

The third is that accelerated computing is multi-domain. It’s domain-specific. The algorithms and the software stack that you create for computational biology, and the software stack that you create for computational fluid dynamics are fundamentally different. Each one of these domains of science need their own stack, which is the reason why accelerated computing has taken us nearly three decades to accomplish.

This is the software manifestation of the same point: writing parallizable software is very difficult, and Huang’s point is that Nvidia has already done a lot of the work for different domains.

*New Computing Paradigms Require Developer Ecosystems*

( https://videopress.com/embed/fdHVVkKi?hd=1&cover=1&loop=0&autoPlay=0&permalink=1&muted=0&controls=1&playsinline=0&useAverageColor=0&preloadContent=metadata )

After three decades, we realize now that we’re at a tipping point. A new computing model is extremely hard to come by, and the reason for that is this: in order for there to be a new computing model you need developers, but a developer would only come if there are end users — developers have to create applications that end users would buy. And without end users there would be no customers, no computer companies to build computers. Without computer companies building computers there would be no installed base. With no installed base there would be no developers. Without developers there will be no applications. This loop has been suffered by so many computing companies in the 40 years that I’ve been in this industry. This is really one of the first major times in history a new computing model has been developed and created. We now have four million developers, three thousand plus applications, 40 million CUDA downloads in history, 25 million just last year. 15,000 startup companies in the world built on Nvidia today, and 40,000 large companies around the world are using accelerated computing. We have now reached the tipping point of a new computing era. This new computing model is now enjoyed and embraced by just about every computing company and every cloud company in the world.

This is less about accelerated computing than it is about one of the most important realities of tech: you need a virtuous cycle between developers and end users, and it’s a bit of a chicken-and-egg problem to get started. Huang argues Nvidia has already accomplished that, and I agree with him that this is a big deal.

*Accelerated Computing is Cheaper*

( https://videopress.com/embed/brCiLJH4?hd=1&cover=1&loop=0&autoPlay=0&permalink=1&muted=0&controls=1&playsinline=0&useAverageColor=0&preloadContent=metadata )

There’s a reason for that. It turns out that every single computing approach, its benefit in the final analysis is lower cost. The PC Revolution that started and that Taiwan enjoyed in 1984, starting in 1984 the year I graduated, that decade in the 80s was the PC Revolution. PC brought computing to a price point nobody had ever seen before. And then of course mobile devices were convenient and they also saved enormous amounts of money. We aggregated and combined the camera, the music player, your PC, a phone: so many different devices were all integrated into one, and as a result not only are you able to enjoy your life better, it also saves a lot of money and provides great convenience. Every single generation provided something new and saved money. Well this is how accelerated computing works…

Huang then gave the example of how much it would cost to train a large language model with CPUs; the parameters were $10 million dollars for 960 CPU servers consuming 11 GWh for 1 large language model. Huang then proceeded to hold each of the parameters constant to compare how “accelerated computing” — i.e. GPUs — did better:

Cost Servers Power LLMs Trained CPU (baseline) $10m 960 11 GWh 1x GPU (cost constant) $10m 48 3.2 GWh 44x GPU (power constant) $34m 172 11 GWh 150x GPU (output constant) $400k 2 0.13 GWh 1x

Using LLMs as the example of output is obviously highly favorable to Huang’s argument: LLM training is nearly perfectly parallelizable, so of course GPUs will do much better. What was notable was that Huang took on the criticism that Nvidia’s systems are so expensive straight on:

( https://videopress.com/embed/uuzutgGX?hd=1&cover=1&loop=0&autoPlay=0&permalink=1&muted=0&controls=1&playsinline=0&useAverageColor=0&preloadContent=metadata )

People say that GPU servers are so expensive. However, the GPU server is no longer the computer. The computer is the data center. Your goal is to build the most cost-effective data center, not build the most cost-effective server. Back in the old days when the computer was the server, that would be a reasonable thing to do. But today, the computer is the data center, so what you want to do is you want to create the most effective data center with the best TCO (total cost of ownership).

Huang later added “we want dense computers, not big ones,” arguing that “the more you buy the more you save”, and it’s a good argument! The question, though, is just how many of those single-threaded loads that run on CPUs today can actually be parallelized, or to put it another way, to what extent will parallizable workloads supplant single-threaded workloads?

*Cost Effectiveness Relies on Utilization*

( https://videopress.com/embed/jRbvjtAZ?hd=1&cover=1&loop=0&autoPlay=0&permalink=1&muted=0&controls=1&playsinline=0&useAverageColor=0&preloadContent=metadata )

Accelerated Computing does nothing if it just sits there, and so you need algorithms, software libraries, it’s a data center scale problem. So networking matters, and distributed computing is all about software. Again system software matters, and before before long, in order for you to present your system to your customers, you have to ultimately have a lot of applications that run on top of it, the software ecosystem matters. Well the utilization of a data center is one of the most important criterias of its TCO, just like a hotel. If the hotel is wonderful, but it’s mostly empty, the cost is incredible, and so you need the utilization to be high. In order for the utilization to be high you have to have many different utilities, many different applications, so the richness of the applications matter. Again the algorithm and libraries and now the software ecosystem. You purchase a computer but these computers are incredibly hard to deploy; from the moment that you buy the computer to the time that you put that computer to work to start making money that difference can be weeks, if you’re very good at it — we can stand up a super computer in a matter of a couple of weeks because we build so many all around the world, hundreds around the world — but if you’re not very good at it it could take a year. That difference depriving yourself the year of making money and the year of depreciation are incredible costs.

This is in part a restatement of the points above, but from a different perspective: it’s not just that you need the software to run different kinds of applications, but that you need all of those applications to have software so that your higher up-front costs can be spread out across multiple workloads.

*Accelerated Computing is Software-Defined*

( https://videopress.com/embed/ByBQNkZG?hd=1&cover=1&loop=0&autoPlay=0&permalink=1&muted=0&controls=1&playsinline=0&useAverageColor=0&preloadContent=metadata )

Because the data center is software defined there are so many engineers that will continue to refine and continue to optimize the software stack. Because Nvidia’s software stack is architecturally compatible across all of our generations, across all of our GPUs, every time we optimize something it benefits everybody.

Everyone is annoyed at their Nvidia dependency when it comes to GPUs, largely because of costs; Huang’s argument is that given the importance of software in effectively leveraging those GPUs, it is worth the cost to be a part of the same ecosystem as everyone else, because you will benefit from more improvements instead of having to make them all yourself.

Training Versus Inference
-------------------------

Huang’s arguments are good ones, which is why I thought it was worth laying them all out; the question, though, is just how many loads look like LLM training? There seems little doubt that Nvidia is going to dominate that market for the foreseeable future: one of the companies most inspired moves was its acquisition of Mellanox in 2020 ( https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/nvidia-completes-acquisition-of-mellanox-creating-major-force-driving-next-gen-data-centers ) , given the importance of networking in making the idea of presenting an entire data center as a single addressable platform a reality.

At the same time, the companies most able to invest in training are also the companies with the most resources and motivation to do a lot of Nvidia’s work themselves; moreover, they have the user-bases to solve the utilization problems, even with relatively narrow software applications. I do think that Nvidia will dominate large non-tech enterprises ( https://stratechery.com/2023/nvidia-gtc-dgx-cloud-nvidias-partners/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJlbnQiOnsidXJpIjpbImh0dHBzOi8vc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkuY29tLzIwMjMvbnZpZGlhLWd0Yy1kZ3gtY2xvdWQtbnZpZGlhcy1wYXJ0bmVycy8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTY4ODA1MDE0NywiaWF0IjoxNjg1NDU4MTQ3LCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQiLCJzdWIiOiJSc2V1WUU4amdIZVBwV0pmMWZBYjRDIiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.lBsEzWiJtqW6-R1iAvvnYqifSxwkoBYpDHumxXrKmcgkiJenVcqhAglTfjIM7yfGccY3Cmo56wwfoWo-AEbLcDwRsxAxM1sXVg9dmv5bKy1bj5E2BOi_lhwXcdlqeygjlOq_GWUKRnfMFSKYvYCPxYiU5zP03vVnXThK-kwAcI5I-g2219Ej00LiMnwOYnaxYBQrznSbMLuO-XOQoyMUOxye4h-51r3t-5gI90ApkNaTJB3hfiY6d5nZ1KV-anr3KYNc69JWV9g4XTpv0hDt11h_DyKNd_FpPwVcOqhhMLmZlcAEY4dmnBJeTCw18h0ew8QI6iTSWYsGDXUMUVGHCg ) , but the Meta’s of the world will remain motivated to find an alternative.

The other big question is inference — the actual leveraging of trained models — which is going to be a much larger market than training: there is a gargantuan amount of motivation to drive inference capabilities down to local hardware, and barring that, to make it as cost-effective in the server as possible. Nvidia still has the best capabilities here, but the interconnect and networking capabilities necessary are much lower than for training, which makes it more likely that AMD in particular can build a competitive product.

That noted, Nvidia is very fortunate that that the generative AI moment happened now, when its chips are clearly superior: the deepest moats that Huang presented are all about software and developers, and right now the number of CUDA developers and applications built on Nvidia frameworks are exploding. Sure, you can definitely argue that we are in a bubble — AI capabilities are obviously incredible, but it will take time to actually create useful products — but there are real skills and models and frameworks being developed now that, I suspect, are more differentiated than Solaris, the operating system for Sun Microsystems, was from Linux.

That’s the key point: long-term differentiation in tech is always rooted in software and ecosystems, even if long-term value-capture has often been rooted in hardware; what makes Nvidia so compelling is that they, like Apple, combine both.
