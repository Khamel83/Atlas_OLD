Shopify Earnings
----------------

From Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-02-15/shopify-sales-beat-analyst-estimates-as-recovery-gains-traction ) a couple of weeks ago:

Shopify Inc. tumbled in after-hours trading after giving a sales outlook for the start of the year that fell short of analysts’ estimates, a sign that it face an uneven recovery from last year’s rout. The Ottawa-based company said it expects first quarter revenue growth in the “high teen percentages” over last year, slightly below forecasts for 20% growth, according to a Bloomberg survey. Shopify said operating costs should grow in the low single digits compared with the fourth quarter, excluding one-time charges. The shares were down more than 11% in extended trading at 6:08 p.m. in New York.

Chief Executive Officer Tobias Lütke is working to recover from a misplaced bet that the pandemic-fueled surge in online shopping would become permanent. Though he’s cut jobs, raised prices and expanded offerings to merchants, the stock is a long way from a full recovery after losing about three-quarters of its value last year.

Analysts seemed fairly positive ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-02-16/shopify-analysts-shrug-off-weaker-outlook-while-stock-drops-16 ) on Shopify’s results, but I see a few reasons for concern above-and-beyond the company’s ongoing shift to lower margin businesses, particularly its fulfillment network, which was the biggest drag on profitability last year. Specifically, I am a bit worried about saturation.

The first thing that jumped out to me on the company’s earnings call ( https://seekingalpha.com/article/4578803-shopify-inc-shop-q4-2022-earnings-call-transcript ) were the impending price hikes on Shopify’s lower-level subscription plans. From President Harley Finkelstein’s prepared remarks:

In January, we announced an update to the subscription prices on our Basic, Shopify and Advanced plans. The prices that we have been charging for access to the best tools in commerce have largely remained unchanged for the last 12 years. This will enable Shopify’s exceptional value to continue as we solve more of the most difficult problems in the industry and empower more people to become entrepreneurs. These new prices went into effect immediately for new merchants and will take effect on April 23 for existing merchants.

Finkelstein expounded on this in the Q&A:

Our plan pricing has pretty much remained unchanged since I got here 13 years ago. In the same time, we’ve added significant value to our subscription offering to our merchants. We want to keep making commerce better in it. So I think the price increase reflects a fair value exchange, but it also allows us to keep solving really, really tough problems and empower more people to become entrepreneurs.\

No price increase in 12 years is certainly a remarkable thing, and without question Finkelstein is correct that Shopify has dramatically increased the value it provided for that unchanged price. That raises the question, though, as to why Shopify never raised its prices.

The obvious answer is that while existing customers (which in the case of Shopify are merchants) may be more than willing to pay more, because they recognize the value of what they are getting (and also don’t want to go to the hassle of changing platforms), new customers have not yet come to appreciate that value, and higher prices is a deterrent to getting started. To put it another way, price elasticity for existing customers is different than price elasticity for new customers; it follows, then, that a company focused first and foremost on acquiring new customers ought to care more about the price elasticity of those new customers, even if it means leaving some money on the table in terms of existing customers.

This is obviously a somewhat simplistic view of the situation, particularly as Shopify has created higher-level plans to both deliver and capture more value to existing customers as their business grows, and has of course seen much more revenue growth in its Merchant Solutions business (i.e. Shopify Payments in particular). Still, suspiciously low pricing for a very long time suggests that the business in question is primarily focused on expanding their market; by extension, a price increase at least hints at the possibility that the business in question believes its market is becoming saturated.

Shopify’s results suggest this is the case; year-over-year growth in Subscription Solutions, which is a function of the number of merchants on the platform as well as their pricing level, was in the teens, substantially lower than Merchant Solutions growth, which is a function of gross merchandise volume:

Of course the pandemic makes it hard to do year-over-year comparisons; here are the comparisons to three-years ago:

There are other clues, as well: over the last year Shopify has rolled out starter plans ( https://www.shopify.com/starter ) for people looking to sell a single product, and extended trials in Q3 of last year. That extended trial initiative, its worth noting, does suppress subscription growth, but it would only have had an effect the last two quarters; what is more notable is that it exists. You can put a similar spin on initiatives around helping merchants go global, both in terms of where they sell and also in terms of where merchants are located; both are worthwhile initiatives, but both are also the sort of initiatives you undertake when you start to suspect that your core market is increasingly saturated.

Shopify Audiences
-----------------

Last year I called for Shopify to become an ad buyer for its merchants ( https://stratechery.com/2022/shopifys-evolution/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJlbnQiOnsidXJpIjpbImh0dHBzOi8vc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkuY29tLzIwMjIvc2hvcGlmeXMtZXZvbHV0aW9uLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNjgwNzg4NDc5LCJpYXQiOjE2NzgxOTY0NzksImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCIsInN1YiI6IlJzZXVZRThqZ0hlUHBXSmYxZkFiNEMiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.UQFPLLMBDI0LRswx8uD6MWPb80qLHsXyKHV82sywn6JIrE7QqTaDQExsswLwdzkUtnJpm0XqQoHCl-DGDx9H54RzBvPIaHfHIPuEJpbvEE35IUagcdz6hKWaXukHAbjWV0zj5cgknp21IwvPnmHJ0YrDsiFhzz1HtiNkyenficcvdu4N7oG99xbaIHkR317qQ0Z5rKPXO51XMb0RO39l4V6ZhHqouuoHL37iFi8mToL8-gfyJUn3Y8Kk5dAJ6NFBb0_DEcwpwAikuIGs_8DcEOKW75ffCxp4uRrnAWxeZXe-PUfi__YsY4eNlj_IVU5BZjY3Kjb0JfTkFWx0-YbRQA ) as a way of working around Apple’s App Tracking Transparency changes, which had an outsized effect on independent e-commerce sites. Shopify Audiences, which the company announced later that year, was very much in that same vein, but instead of buying ads it basically aggregates data from across Shopify in such a way that merchants can buy ads on Meta directly using lookalike audiences that are tuned to their product. As I noted in a May update ( https://stratechery.com/2022/shopify-follow-up-shopify-audiences-unity-earnings/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJlbnQiOnsidXJpIjpbImh0dHBzOi8vc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkuY29tLzIwMjIvc2hvcGlmeS1mb2xsb3ctdXAtc2hvcGlmeS1hdWRpZW5jZXMtdW5pdHktZWFybmluZ3MvIl19LCJleHAiOjE2ODA3ODg0NzksImlhdCI6MTY3ODE5NjQ3OSwiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9zdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIiwic3ViIjoiUnNldVlFOGpnSGVQcFdKZjFmQWI0QyIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.vbs-FojscfvJoXuOUvxG_vcGKLBYPz2P2_Q2BZc43Np2y4CQtCjHNg6OCiJuMGnAMbnezfmRVc19ux1z9uBF0Dpvz4uGZHDuE2PJ_LVzOkr3xyoy62VTcoxDoJ7TeRD8SZOQEq6O5QXL6aTnGM4Xr4L_tIKzlf3m2t_N7Bjm-ACqepkPu2e-jhcd62uM3t83KcVZuo8Mgz4NoUApNo7rgBqJ2UVZjUzuL1lKVFr-2AxsNgwDLFVZshjzGBeY3FmU5nqV7ygIGT61c3qwOXK72JGfT631P0EZgX3Wh8Z9hlQ8h7KF4r7VUGOGjmW5llnc0R44-e2uReqp07JRIaJfmA ) , it was a solution that made a lot of sense, and over the last year Shopify has expanded Shopify Audiences from Meta’s platforms to Google’s.

One limitation on Shopify Audiences is that it is only available to retailers with a Shopify Plus subscription, which costs $2,000/month, and from my perspective is a big reason to subscribe to that very high margin product. Eric Seufert, however, argues that this is a mistake; from MobileDevMemo ( https://mobiledevmemo.com/the-necessity-of-shopify-audiences/ ) :

Shopify likely can’t launch an ad network, given the nature of its relationship with retailers. But it did the next best thing by creating an audience network: monetizing the data it owns by empowering retailers to find and advertise to known, relevant customers. While Shopify doesn’t charge retailers for use of its Audience product, since it’s included with the Shopify Plus subscription, the company benefits when its retailers are able to efficiently target new customers and drive incremental sales…

Shopify can drive topline growth without attendant increases to its cost base by aggressively expanding its Audience Network: not only to new platforms but to more retailers within its network, possibly by making the product available to merchants outside of its Plus platform. I’d argue that Audiences likely isn’t a compelling enticement to SMBs to upgrade their Shopify merchant stores to Shopify Plus: with advertising performance, seeing is believing, and an SMB can’t know what effect Shopify Audiences will have on their business a priori to justify the $2,000 monthly expense. Similarly, counterfactuals related to advertising performance can also be unconvincing (eg. “You would have generated 30% more sales last month had you used our advertising technology”).

Expanding Audiences to all retailers could potentially grow GMV and revenue by activating more incremental sales, arguably without cannibalizing Shopify Plus recurring revenue. There are obviously other considerations, and Shopify hasn’t fully made public the details of the Audience Network (ie. which and whose data is input into Audience segments), but the opportunity seems attractive.

This is an articulation of the elasticity point I made above: from my perspective Shopify Audiences is probably the single most compelling reason to subscribe to Shopify Plus, if not now then in the future as Shopify ramps it up. There simply isn’t anything more valuable in terms of online commerce than better targeted advertising. Seufert’s argument, though, is that small merchants can’t and won’t appreciate the value, particularly given how daunting that $2,000/month price point is; worse, because such a huge portion of Shopify’s merchant base is not a part of the program, Shopify Audiences has less data and thus generates less value. Therefore Shopify ought to make Shopify Audiences available to everyone.

The problem for Shopify is that weakening the attractiveness of Shopify Plus in the pursuit of increased gross merchant volume would have a huge effect on margins; consider some back of the envelope math for a single merchant:

* $2,000/month at 79% gross margin for Subscription Solutions is $1,580 in gross profit.
* $1,580 in gross profit from Merchant Solutions, which has 30% gross margins, requires $5,267 in revenue.
* Most Merchant Solutions revenue comes from payment processing and currency conversions, and Shopify Payments fees for Shopify Plus accounts are 2.4% + $0.30; that means the merchant needs somewhere around $200,000 in gross merchant volume to generate $5,267 in Merchant Solutions revenue for Shopify.

Now obviously Shopify would benefit from increased merchant volume not just amongst Shopify Plus merchants but also all of the smaller merchants who can now dip their toe into ads. It’s a huge amount of gross profit to make up, though, and a good example of the tension Shopify is surely feeling as Subscription Solutions revenue growth falters.

The Shop App
------------

From the OpenAI blog ( https://openai.com/blog/introducing-chatgpt-and-whisper-apis ) :

ChatGPT and Whisper models are now available on our API, giving developers access to cutting-edge language (not just chat!) and speech-to-text capabilities…Shop, Shopify’s consumer app, is used by 100 million shoppers to find and engage with the products and brands they love. ChatGPT API is used to power Shop’s new shopping assistant. When shoppers search for products, the shopping assistant makes personalized recommendations based on their requests. Shop’s new AI-powered shopping assistant will streamline in-app shopping by scanning millions of products to quickly find what buyers are looking for—or help them discover something new.

I’ll be writing more about OpenAI’s API changes, particularly the big drop in prices, but today’s update is about Shopify, and this section the Shop App. I was pretty skeptical of the Shop App when it launched/rebranded ( https://stratechery.com/2020/shopify-shop-shopifys-reason-to-exist-googles-earnings/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJlbnQiOnsidXJpIjpbImh0dHBzOi8vc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkuY29tLzIwMjAvc2hvcGlmeS1zaG9wLXNob3BpZnlzLXJlYXNvbi10by1leGlzdC1nb29nbGVzLWVhcm5pbmdzLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNjgwNzg4NDc5LCJpYXQiOjE2NzgxOTY0NzksImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vc3RyYXRlY2hlcnkucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCIsInN1YiI6IlJzZXVZRThqZ0hlUHBXSmYxZkFiNEMiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.fkaAW7cvd2q8ec8_TOmbvLNracFZhfcXHrSVoOXrjK2UV9zh04adTe1KaS1p_EmSrlrW7usuMcvmVuK-dJ7BH_sh593tCVgsr5-k7cJx3BFb07FoNy_EENodo7GHqE4pw_CjNNPjHboJQYPqIXM7p3F9BHhdZkg9rfAo-e9Nri3DsZ8gAxODGv1-4XAEIaRq5oCQMf6bO3ajorVTjBkN3uHQjZA7wnzA0RBLkHzNesZx57MRwexoYNynRTbzNbXvgl6oHsNtE7VdMjKgCt_BN--UHhDA01KVIq0hlrlBCbVU4cVd0InHFmJcqqCiEkxhAVrlhN0gW0HrBVFqf5Gpsw ) , arguing that Shopify’s role was to be a back-end provider, not an Aggregator; to that end, it’s worth noting that those 100 million shoppers are primarily tracking shipments, not “find[ing] and engag[ing] with the products and brands they love.”

At the same time, I would also note that I expressed that skepticism in April 2020, i.e. two months before Apple announced ATT, which fundamentally changed the equation in terms of being a back-end provider versus having customer-facing surfaces to harvest demand. To that end, it certainly seems that Shopify is doubling down on the Shop App, and not just by adding ChatGPT. One very interesting new initiative is the Shop Minis ( https://shop.app/minis/docs/ ) developer program; from a Shopify press release ( https://news.shopify.com/shopifys-winter-23-edition-introduces-100-product-updates-that-keep-merchants-on-the-cutting-edge ) :

We’re expanding the Shopify ecosystem to give developers a whole new way to build for mobile. The Shop Mini software development kit (SDK) enables devs to extend their Shopify app functionality to Shop, creating innovative in-app shopping experiences. Shopify creates what most merchants need most of the time, and for everything else, we empower our ecosystem of developers to build for our merchants. For the first time, and available now in early access, we’re unlocking that functionality for the Shop app, allowing developers to build for the future of mobile commerce.

In short, Shopify is transforming the Shop App into a platform, and encouraging developers to build experiences that merchants can leverage to deliver their products in a compelling way via the Shop App, taking advantage of its large user base, turned-on notifications, etc. You can also see the Shop App as being a place for ads, as well, as Shopify controls the entire experience. Finkelstein said on the earnings call:

The Shop App is an excellent example of how we are helping merchants increase customer lifetime value by deepening their engagement with existing customers while also finding new buyers. Since we introduced the Shop App in early 2020, it has grown from an accelerated checkout and order tracking utility to become an important driver of many of our merchant’s business performances. Shop gives merchants new ways to stay connected with their buyers like in-app offers and notifications when their favorite products are back in stock.

In a nutshell, Shop enables a Shopify merchant on their first day to have a storefront in a native mobile app. That’s very powerful. In 2022, we shipped dozens of enhancements to Shop, including discounts and expanded search function, personalized shopping experiences and embedding Shop Cash, our loyalty program that is currently in early access. With tens of millions using the app every month, we’re able to match the right merchants to the right buyers, creating personalized shopping feeds for the buyer segment and a new customer acquisition tool for merchants. Shop is still in its early days, but it’s one of the ways we’re investing to help merchants win over the long-term.

I’m less hostile to the concept of the Shop App than I was at first, although I do think that Shop Minis should be a precursor to a new SDK that can be placed in any app, not just the Shop App.

Ultimately, though, the reason I am less hostile is because Shopify is in a new environment: all of this stuff, from building a consumer-facing Aggregator to an Audience Network is hard, both for Shopify and for merchants. For the former there is no choice given the reality of the post ATT world, where it’s no longer viable to offload the hard work of customer acquisition and Aggregation to Facebook; for the latter there is that much higher of a barrier to get started, which is a reminder that saturation, to the extent it is occurring, is not just a function of reaching an addressable market, but of that addressable market shrinking thanks to secular changes of the sort caused by ATT.
