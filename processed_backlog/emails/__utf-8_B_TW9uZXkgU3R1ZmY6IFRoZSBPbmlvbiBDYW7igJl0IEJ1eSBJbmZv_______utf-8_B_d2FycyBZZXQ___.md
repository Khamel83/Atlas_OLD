Sometimes a company owes its creditors more money than it can pay them, so it 
files for bankruptcy. Loosely speaking, bankruptcy means that the creditors own 
the company, and a bankruptcy court works out the best way to get them as much 
value as possible. Often this means running an auction for the company’s assets.

Intuitively, the way the auction should work is that whoever bids the most for 
the assets gets to buy them, and then the creditors get the money. The 
creditors want as much money as possible, so they should sell to the highest 
bidder.

There is an important wrinkle here, though: What if one of the creditors wants 
to buy the assets? Say the company owes a total of $100 million to four 
creditors. Creditor A and Creditor B each own $25 million of senior secured 
bonds, while Creditor C and Creditor D each own $25 million of junior unsecured 
bonds. (The senior secured bonds have to be paid back in full before the junior 
bondholders get anything.) It goes bankrupt and puts its assets up for auction. 
Bidder E comes in and bids $40 million for the assets, which would result in 
Creditors A and B each getting $20 million (a 20% haircut on their bonds) and 
Creditors C and D each getting nothing. Creditor A says “nah the assets are 
worth more than that” and bids $60 million, which would result in Creditors A 
and B each getting paid back their full $25 million and Creditors C and D each 
getting $5 million. Creditor A’s bid is the highest — $60 million is more than 
$40 million — so it wins the auction and gets the assets.

And then the court says “great you won the auction, we’ll need a check for $60 
million” and Creditor A says “no actually I’m owed $25 million, and $25 million 
of my bid would go right back to me, so I’m just going to write a check for $35 
million.” And the court is like “oh right that makes sense,” takes the $35 
million, gives $25 million of it to Creditor B and divides the rest among 
Creditors C and D.

This is called a “credit bid” [1]  <>: Creditor A’s $60 million bid for the 
company was made up of (1) $35 million in cash and (2) $25 million of its own 
claim in bankruptcy. You could imagine Bidder E being aggrieved. It might say: 
“Wait, I was willing to pay $40 million, but Creditor A only paid $35 million 
and won the auction. That’s no fair.” But that is not a particularly good 
argument. Creditor A’s bid was obviously better than Bidder E’s, because it 
recovered more money for the (non-Creditor A) creditors.

What if Creditor C — a junior unsecured bondholder — said “nah, the assets are 
worth even more than that” and bid $70 million for them? Well, $70 million is 
more than $60 million, so Creditor C would win the auction. And then the court 
would say “okay we’ll need a check for $70 million.” And then Creditor C might 
reply “no actually I’m owed $25 million, so here’s a check for $45 million.” 
But that doesn’t work! Creditor C’s $25 million claimisn’t worth $25 million to 
the bankruptcy estate, so it can’t get full credit for it. If Creditor C wrote 
a check for $45 million, Creditors A and B wouldn’t get all of their money back 
(and Creditor D would get nothing). Creditor A’s bid ($35 million cash, $60 
million total) is still better than Creditor C’s bid ($45 million cash, $70 
million total, but with that $70 million somewhat suspect).

You can imagine more extreme cases. One extreme case is something like:

 * A company has $1 billion of unsecured bonds, $2 billion of assets, and 
makes good and valuable building products plus a certain amount of asbestos. 
 * Asbestos victims sue the company and get judgments for $99 billion of 
damages. 
 * The company goes bankrupt and holds an auction for the non-asbestos assets. 
 * Some building company offers $2 billion for the assets, which would be 
divided pro rata among the creditors, $20 million to the bondholders and $1.98 
billion to the asbestos victims. [2]  <> 
 * The asbestos victims offer to buy the assets for (1) $21 million of cash 
and (2) waiving their $99 billion of claims. Their bid produces $21 million of 
value for the bondholders, which is more than they’d get from the other bid. 
And now the asbestos victims own the assets.

The Macy’s thing

I guess a way it could work is [6]  <>:

 * In the fourth quarter of 2021, Macy’s Inc. got 200,000 truckloads of small 
packages delivered. Macy’s had to pay $1,000 of delivery fees per truckload, 
for a total cost of $200 million. (All of these numbers are entirely 
hypothetical and made up.) 
 * But it didn’t actually pay cash for all of those deliveries during the 
fourth quarter of 2021: Some of those deliveries happened right at the end of 
the quarter, [7]  <> and the delivery companies didn’t get around to sending 
invoices until a few days after the quarter ended. 
 * So in fact Macy’s paid, say, $180 million of cash fees for deliveries in 
the fourth quarter of 2021. 
 * But as an accounting matter, it still incurred an expense for those 
deliveries: Those deliverieshappened, and Macy’s had to pay for them, just not 
yet. Accounting for big companies tends to work on an accrual basis, not a cash 
basis: The fact that the deliveries happened in Q4 2021 means that Macy’s 
should have accounted for the expense in Q4 2021. It should have estimated 
something like “well, we got 200,000 truckloads at $1,000 per truck, and we 
paid $180 million of the fees in cash, so we should accrue another $20 million 
of non-cash delivery expenses.” And those expenses would reduce its net income 
for the quarter, though not its cash flow. 
 * But the person responsible for that accounting … forgot? Or did the math 
wrong? Let’s just assume they forgot. So Macy’s recorded an $180 million 
delivery expense in Q4 2021. 
 * The next quarter, Q1 2022, Macy’s got 220,000 truckloads of small packages 
delivered, and the delivery fees went up to $1,050 per truckload, for a total 
cost of $231 million. 
 * And it paid cash for 19,000 of them ($199.5 million), leaving $31.5 million 
not yet paid. 
 * Also, Macy’s paid the $20 million of delivery fees it still owed for the 
last quarter. 
 * At this point, the person doing the accounting noticed the mistake. 
 * They could fix it! Roughly speaking the fix is to incur a $251 million 
delivery expense in Q1 2022: the actual $231 million expense for the quarter, 
plus the $20 million left over from last quarter. (To be clear, this is not a 
correct accounting fix — the correct fix would be to fix the previous quarter’s 
accruals — but, you know, good enough.) 
 * But that would mean a huge increase in delivery expense: The delivery 
expense for Q4 2021 was understated (it left out the $20 million), and the fix 
involves overstating the delivery expense for Q1 2022 (putting the $20 million 
there instead), resulting in a large and economically implausible increase in 
the expense item. (The actual economic increase in delivery expense — from $200 
million to $231 million — is about 15%; this fix would show a 39% increase, 
from $180 million to $251 million.) 
 * Macy’s was very focused on reducing delivery expenses, so if it reported a 
huge increase, probably someone would notice! 
 * Probably that accountant would get fired! 
 * So the alternative is … uh … smooth things out a bit? Macy’s paid $219.5 
million in cash for deliveries ($199.5 million for this quarter plus $20 
million left over from last quarter), so you have to account for that, but you 
can just hide the rest, right? Report a delivery expense of $220 million, say, 
$219.5 million in cash and $500,000 of accrual. That looks roughly plausible. 
 * But now you’ve got $31 million of missing delivery fees (the $231 million 
you incurred this quarter, plus the $20 million leftover from last quarter, 
minus the $220 million you accounted for this quarter). 
 * Next quarter, same thing, and the delivery-fee slush fund grows. 
 * Eventually it is $151 million, oops! 
Just a guess but

Hawk Tuah

I think it would be difficult to explain Hawk Tuah to the people of 2022, or 
2026. It’s not all that easy in 2024, especially because this is a family 
newsletter and Hawk Tuah is somewhat risqué, but I guess let’s try. [8]  <> A 
young woman named Haliey Welch was out for a night on the town in Nashville 
when she was approached for a person-on-the-street interview for someone’s 
YouTube/TikTok channel. She gave a funny, charming, somewhat risqué answer to 
one of the questions. The next day they posted the video, it went extremely 
viral and Welch became famous as “Hawk Tuah Girl.”

Welch was not, prior to this, part of the fame economy: She had a regular job, 
was not an influencer, and the video was for someone else’s channel. But once 
she went viral she was maximally interested inbecoming part of the fame 
economy. How does one do that? How does one parlay one brief funny viral clip 
into a lucrative career? There are well-understood approaches. You can probably 
get into reality television, perhaps followed by politics. You can get paid to 
promote products — in ads, or on your own social media — or to show up at 
nightclubs and other events. You can sell merch. If you can sing or act, you 
use your viral fame as a way into a singing or acting career. It is 2024, so 
Welch literallyhas a podcast 
<https://open.spotify.com/show/7KCh1Z181LOIh3IP6EbqNR>.

But in 2024 there is a much, much, much, much, much better answer. Modern 
finance has created an incredible tool fordirectly monetizing your 15 minutes 
of fame. This tool is the memecoin. A memecoin is a crypto token that is 
created to capture some meme, a token whose price goes up when the meme gets a 
lot of popular attention and down when it doesn’t. Wetalked last week 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2024-12-04/citi-traders-didn-t-know-the-rules?cmpid=BBD121124_MONEYSTUFF&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=241211&utm_campaign=moneystuff>
 about memecoins, and about a Financial Timesarticle 
<https://www.ft.com/content/66fe7655-704a-407f-8f20-a3f081296a36> saying that 
“there are millions of memecoins, which can easily be set up using online 
memecoin generators,” “all memecoins require niche knowledge of internet 
culture” and that they “have no business model, cash flow or fundamental value” 
but instead “rely on their popularity among traders to generate liquidity.”

So! I mean! Here you are! You have become extremely famous for one thing. 
Parlaying that into a long and lucrative fame-based career probably will 
require doing other things: singing or acting or at least showing up at 
nightclubs. But you have a ton of meme valueright now, even if you probably 
won’t in a year. So the thing to do is to capitalize that meme value into a 
memecoin, give yourself most of the tokens, and then sell them to the public. 
The coins will be popular among traders for, you know, a day, so you will sell 
a lot of them and pocket millions of dollars. And that’s it, that’s the end. 
You don’t have to do any other things. You have gone viral and then fully, 
directly monetized your virality.

I should say that, while this is an utterly amazing invention of modern 
finance, it is also, for an old financial engineer like me, quite a befuddling 
one. In my day, the way financial engineering worked was like:

 * You wanted to sell some instrument tied to some thing, “carbon credits” or 
“housing prices” or “interest rates.” 
 * You spent a lot of time and effort figuring out how to tie the instrument 
to the thing, how to give the instrument contractual cash flows that reflected 
the underlying thing, so that people who were long the instrument actually got 
the expected exposure to the thing. 
 * And then you worried about edge cases and problems and ways in which people 
might break the connection. Here’s a recentRisk profile 
<https://www.risk.net/awards/7960376/lifetime-achievement-award-don-wilson> of 
Don Wilson, which contains a long section on how Wilson discovered that some 
exchange-traded swap futures were not economically equivalent to 
over-the-counter swaps, as had been advertised, because of a convexity bias in 
the collateral mechanism. Tying the one thing to the other thing did not quite 
work, because someone had gotten a small detail of the cash flows wrong! That’s 
the sort of stuff people used to worry about! 
Meanwhile, the way memecoins work is that you put the name of some meme on a 
crypto token that took five minutes to set up, and then you go on social media 
and are like “this memecoin represents that meme,” and everyone is like “yep it 
sure does,” and if they like the meme the token goes up. There is no effort to 
connect the memecoin to the meme in any substantive way: no ownership of 
intellectual property, no contracted cash flows, nothing. And yet it works! 
Sometimes! The price of the memecoin really does have some rough tendency to 
reflect the popularity of the meme! It’s amazing!
