# Other People's Kids

Lisa Belkin • December 09, 2010 

![Other People's Kids]()

Illustration by Barry Falls  
  
The boundaries between us and the children of others has been a topic here on Motherlode this week. Laura Shumaker raised it in her essay about how quickly gossip spreads when a mother might possibly have done wrong. A reader touched on it when asking how to integrate sharply different parenting styles when siblings come together for the holidays. And in a guest post today Nicole Blades (who blogs at MsMaryMack.com), continues the conversation, wondering what are the rules when disciplining someone else’s child.  
  
PLAYGROUND POLITICS  
  
By Nicole Blades  
  
A couple of months ago, my son was happily exploring a local playground. I was tracking him — close, but not hovering. When he got to the top of the kiddie structure, he started playing with his truck, running it along the edge of a cedar beam.  
  
Out of nowhere, an older kid — probably two or three years old —ran over, yanked the truck from my son’s hand while yelling in his face. I looked around quickly, thinking this older kid’s parent would be rushing over any moment.  
  
Nope.  
  
So I approached my crying son and the other person’s kid (O.P.K.’s, I call them) to try to cool the fire. Before I even opened my mouth, O.P.K. tossed the toy in my son’s direction and took off towards his mother, who was sitting on a nearby bench apparently watching the whole deal.  
  
I only had time to shoot a few red daggers through my eyes in their direction before I scooped up my son to console him.  
  
Every time this kind of thing happens, I find myself momentarily scowling at the kid. Then I cringe and immediately feel awful about it. I tell myself, chidingly, that I am the adult here and that indulgence rides in the same car as courtesy.  
  
Of course, my beef is not actually with the child. He/she is simply being a child. It’s about the parents: the ones who see the selfish or bullish behavior happening and say nothing. Or offer up something like (actual words that were delivered with a shrug): “Kids, huh?” Not particularly useful when your child is throwing sand directly into my son’s face.  
  
I don’t expect the parent to come running over and scold a 3-year-old for, as I said, being a 3-year-old. But I do expect them to be present and to parent through the awkward moment. So, yes, put away the iPhone, Mom/Dad, and get over here.  
  
I fully acknowledge that this is difficult stuff to negotiate. And we’re all just trying to do our best, to do the enlightened, civilized thing. Part of my frustration with O.P.K.’s is the missing book of rules for this game. There really aren’t any written-in-stone ways to handle these thorny confrontations.  
  
For example, when an older O.P.K. bulldozes over your child, what happens next?  
  
• Wait for the other parent to step in and corral their kid? And if they don’t, do you then say something to them about the missed opportunity?  
  
• Try to reason with your child midmeltdown, telling him that life is hard, sharing is hard and taking turns is just part of it? Can any of your speech even be heard over the bawling?  
  
• Grab that plastic whatever right back from the older O.P.K. while saying (nicely, I guess) that life is hard, sharing is hard and we had it first? Of course, going with this option means you should also prep yourself for a potentially ugly scene with O.P.K.’s parent. And then what kind of example are you setting for the children?  
  
In the earlier days, I often chose “solution” No. 2 combined with the distract-baby-with-different-toy move. It worked, for the most part. My son would settle down, recover quickly and be on to the next thing. But I was left thinking that there had to be a better way to handle these playground predicaments. One that saw both kids’ parents getting involved and working it through.  
  
Maybe it’s about each parent meeting his child where she is and talking it out. For instance, I know that at 18 months, my son hasn’t grasped concepts like sharing and taking turns, but hearing the words — infused with the sweetness of mommy or daddy’s voice — surely helps the comprehension along. The same way we repeatedly point at the tall, green, leafy thing and say “tree” or at the barking animal and say “dog,” only to one day hear the words echoed in a tiny, outrageously cute voice, we can also get our kids on the right track with relating to other children.  
  
I know that kids need to work some of this out by themselves. It’s an important part of their socialization that is pre-empted when parents step in to mediate. But it’s also important for parents to step up when their kids are really young and not yet equipped with the tools needed to go from snatching and shoving to solutions and a happy middle ground.  
  
I’m also aware that my son will soon (too soon!) be the older, possibly pushy kid on the playground. The shoe will be on the other foot. And I intend to practice everything that I’m preaching on these matters of O.P.K.’s and playground politics. After all, this essay will always exist, a link floating around the blogosphere, easily recalled and dangled in my face if I don’t.  
  
As for what do to about the shrug-and-shirk-type parent who, for various reasons, chooses not to step in or up, but merely aside when their child steamrolls over the younger ones at the park: well, there’s always the reliable stink-eye. What else can you do?
