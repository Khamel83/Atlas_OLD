For example, in Michigan, welfare spending includes a program that gives private college scholarships to students from households with incomes as high as $250,000 or more. So, how did this crazy quilt of spending happen?

The reforms of 1996 focused on one program in particular, called Aid to Families with Dependent Children, or AFDC. For 60 years, AFDC had worked as an вҖңentitlementвҖқ program: If a family fell below a certain poverty threshold, the family was entitled to cash assistance; the federal government spent as much money on the program each year as there were poor families who qualified. The welfare reform bill did away with AFDC and created in its place TANF, the new system for cash assistance. Under TANF, time limits and work requirements regulate if and for how long a family can receive cash assistance. And rather than the entitlement system of AFDC, funding for TANF comes in the form of block grants to states. The federal government sets aside a fixed amount of money each yearвҖ”$16.5 billion, never adjusted for inflation since 1996вҖ”and gives every state a portion.

What states do with the money from thereвҖ”thatвҖҷs where things get weird.

In Michigan, welfare spending includes private college scholarships. In Pennsylvania, welfare dollars go to funding for crisis pregnancy centers.

вҖңWe say to states, вҖҳHereвҖҷs your chunk of it,вҖҷ вҖқ explains Luke Shaefer, a professor at the University of Michigan School of Social Work who focuses on welfare policy. вҖңAnd you can choose to use this money for cash assistanceвҖ”a program thatвҖҷs not particularly popularвҖқ and involves lots of paperwork and reporting requirements to run. вҖңOr,вҖқ Shaefer says, вҖңif you donвҖҷt use the money for cash assistance, you can use it for pretty much anything else. Virtually no oversight, aside from four unassuming sentences tucked into the 1996 welfare reform bill under Title I, Section 103, labeled with the heading вҖңPurpose,вҖқ outlining the basic ideas behind welfare and вҖңwelfare to work.вҖқ Purpose No. 1 is to вҖңprovide assistance to needy familiesвҖқ so children may be cared for in their own or a relativesвҖҷ home. Purpose two is to вҖңend the dependence of needy parents on government benefitsвҖқ by promoting job preparation, work вҖ”and marriage.

This is where the language takes a surprising turn toward the matrimonial. Purposes 3 and four 4 down on the marriage theme: to вҖңprevent and reduce the incidence of out-of-wedlock pregnancies,вҖқ and, the flip side of that coin, вҖңencourage the formation and maintenance of two-parent families.вҖқ

вҖңThat language did not appear mystically in the middle of the night,вҖқ says Ron Haskins of the Brookings Institution and an adviser to the White House and congressional Republicans during the welfare reform debate. Haskins tells me that the emphasis on marriage and family composition has a long history in the welfare reform movement among Republicans and Democrats alike who were alarmed by the rise of single-parent families, especially low-income families. As Haskins notes, census data from 2007вҖ“2009 shows that children in female-headed families are four to five times more likely to be in poverty than those in married families.

Not even policy wonks like Liz Schott of the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, or CBPP, took much notice of purposes 3 and 4 at the time. вҖңI donвҖҷt think anyone really paid attention in 1996,вҖқ she says. вҖңThis happens to me when I read draft legislationвҖ”thereвҖҷs all this вҖҳwhereasвҖҷ stuff at the front, and this preamble language, and thatвҖҷs just вҖҳblah, blah, blah.вҖҷ вҖқ

But in the case of the welfare reform legislation, all the вҖңwhereasвҖқ stuff became much more than вҖңblah blah blah.вҖқ Because the statute didnвҖҷt delineate clear rules on how states could spend their block grants, those four purposes took on greater significance, Schott says: вҖңThey control how the money can be spent.вҖқ

Schott and her colleagues have combed through the hundreds of annual reports states have sent to the feds since welfare reform, describing, often in frustratingly vague terms, where the money goesвҖ”all $16.5 billion of itвҖ”and which of the four purposes justifies that spending.

вҖңWhen we started slicing and dicing and organizing them,вҖқ she says, вҖңwe began to see very dramatic patterns.вҖқ One of the most dramatic patterns Schott found was that nationwide, according to the latest data, states are collectively spending just a quarter of TANF money on actual cash assistance for poor families. вҖңIf TANF had been successful in getting all of these families from welfare to work, maybe it would be okay to start looking at other ways to be using these funds. The small amount of money Oklahoma spends on cash assistance might make sense if no one in Oklahoma were poor anymore.
