# Aeon Financial foreclosed on more than 400 properties in Ohio's biggest county. It threatened more than 1,900 in D.C. and Maryland. To distressed homeowners, it's a

# debt-collecting machine.

Story by
Michael Sallah, Debra Cenziper

Photos by
Michael Williamson

Published on December 8, 2013

**The firm that threatened** **to foreclose** on hundreds of struggling D.C. homeowners is a mystery: It lists no owners, no local office, no Web site.

Aeon Financial is incorporated in Delaware, operates from mail-drop boxes in Chicago and is represented by a law firm with an address at a 7,200-square-foot estate on a mountainside near Vail, Colo.

**Above:** An Aeon-owned property in Cleveland. (Michael S. Williamson/The Washington Post)

Yet no other tax lien purchaser in the District has been more aggressive in recent years, buying the liens placed on properties when owners fell behind on their taxes, then charging families thousands in fees to save their homes from foreclosure.

Aeon has been accused by the cityГўВҖВҷs attorney general of predatory and unlawful practices and has been harshly criticized by local judges for overbilling. All along, the firm has remained shrouded in corporate secrecy as it pushed to foreclose on more than 700 houses in every ward of the District.

ГўВҖВңWho the heck is Aeon?ГўВҖВқ said David Chung, a local lawyer who said he wasnГўВҖВҷt notified that he owed $575 in back taxes on his Northwest Washington condominium until he received a notice from Aeon. ГўВҖВңThey said, ГўВҖВҳWe bought the right to take over your property. If you want it back ГўВҖВ” pay us.ГўВҖВҷГўВҖВҲГўВҖВқ

AeonГўВҖВҷs story underscores how an obscure tax lien company ГўВҖВ” backed by large banks and savvy lawyers ГўВҖВ” can move from city to city with little government scrutiny, taking in millions from distressed homeowners.

The firm came into the District eight years ago with hardball tactics, sending families threatening letters and demanding $5,000 or more in legal fees and other costs, often more than three times the tax debt.

Local governments provide property owners few safeguards from abusive practices.

At the same time, the company was moving across the country, buying up liens in Maryland, Ohio, Kentucky and Iowa.

In Maryland, Aeon filed more than 1,000 foreclosure cases mostly in Anne Arundel, Baltimore and Prince GeorgeГўВҖВҷs counties, moving against the homes of a severely disabled woman and a grocery clerk who was hospitalized as the firm pressed to foreclose over a $2,100 tax debt.

In Cleveland, Aeon took the deeds to dozens of houses and then failed to care for them when they didnГўВҖВҷt sell, leaving rotting porches, shattered windows and collapsed walls, prompting the city to levy more than 100 code violations.

Calls to two former Aeon executives and several attorneys who work on the companyГўВҖВҷs behalf were not returned.

ГўВҖВңSomebody needs to do an investigation,ГўВҖВқ said Frank Ford,ГӮВ [a senior policy adviser for a nonprofit housing agency in Cleveland.ГӮ](http://www.thrivingcommunitiesinstitute.org/media-news.html)ГўВҖВңYouГўВҖВҷre talking about an industry that comes to town and can impact hundreds of properties. If you donГўВҖВҷt vet and screen these buyers, itГўВҖВҷs going to cost you in the long run.ГўВҖВқ

In the District, city officials said they have never been able to determine who owns Aeon.

When city lawyers challenged the firm in court to divulge its owners three years ago, the company refused, even filing for a protective order to stop the city from digging deeper.

Aeon has long listed its headquarters in the famedГӮВ [Willis Tower in Chicago](http://www.willistower.com/), but when The Post showed up at the 110-story building in October to track down company officials, a building representative said Aeon was not a tenant. A second address used by the companyГўВҖВҷs law firm turned out to be a United Parcel Service store in the heart of downtown ГўВҖВ” two other addresses were UPS stores in strip shopping centers outside the city.

The Post spent three months examining AeonГўВҖВҷs corporate history, traveling to Chicago, Cleveland and three counties in Maryland and reviewing hundreds of business and land records, to find out who is behind the company that has affected thousands of homeowners across the country.

The trail begins and ends with a 52-year-old Chicago lawyer named Mark Alan Schwartz.

## Aggressive actions in D.C.

ГўВҖВңWho the heck is Aeon?ГўВҖВқ said lawyer David Chung, who spent months disputing Aeon's fees after he learned he owed $575 on his D.C. condominium. (Bonnie Jo Mount/The Washington Post)

**Aeon jumped into the DistrictГўВҖВҷs tax lien market** in 2005 as bank financing was transforming an industry once led by mom-and-pop investors.

With Schwartz as the firmГўВҖВҷs attorney, Aeon started small, buying 89 tax liens worth $65,000 at the DistrictГўВҖВҷs public auction.

Four years later, the company was among the top purchasers in the city, buying liens worth more than $1 million and dominating the tax auctions with the financial support of two national banks.

For Aeon, the District was the perfect place. Investors could charge property owners 18 percent interest and had to wait only six months before filing for a foreclosure, compared with a year or longer in other jurisdictions.

Between 2005 and 2009, Aeon bought 1,300 tax liens from Columbia Heights to the cityГўВҖВҷs poorest neighborhoods near the Anacostia River. Then, the firm with the slogan ГўВҖВңSupporting Local CommunitiesГўВҖВқ embarked on a series of lawsuits that would stun property owners.

Week after week, Aeon took families to court, where the firm demanded they pay thousands in legal fees or lose their homes. On a single day in 2009, the company filed nearly 270 cases.

ГўВҖВңAeon was like a machine,ГўВҖВқ said Amy Mix, an attorney with AARPГўВҖВҷsГӮВ [Legal Counsel for the Elderly.ГӮ](http://www.aarp.org/states/dc/LCE.html)ГўВҖВңOther purchasers would have some sympathy for our clients .ГўВҖВү.ГўВҖВү. but we didnГўВҖВҷt get that from them. They were unapologetic.ГўВҖВқ

[A Washington Post investigation in September](/sf/investigative/collection/homes-for-the-taking) found that tax lien investors had foreclosed on hundreds of properties owned by the elderly, the disabled and the poor, taking the homes and all of the equity.

#### Photos: A mystery company threatens homes from D.C. to Ohio

Tax lien purchaser Aeon Financial has moved from Maryland, Ohio, D.C. and other jurisdictions with little government scrutiny.

[![](https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-apps/imrs.php?src=http://www.washingtonpost.com/sf/investigative/wp-content/uploads/sites/8/2013/12/UPSleft700x1000.jpg&w=1040&authapi-mob-redir=0)](https://www.washingtonpost.com/investigations/mystery-company-threatens-homes-from-dc-to-ohio/2013/12/08/3f7f83ca-5dfb-11e3-95c2-13623eb2b0e1_gallery.html)

Aeon Financial and its law firm operate out of several mail drops in the Chicago area. Click on the image to view the gallery. (Debbie Cenziper / The Washington Post)

Aeon ended up with only a handful of properties, instead pressing for the fees from the property owners pulled into court.

One of the first to challenge AeonГўВҖВҷs bills was John Kazemi, a 71-year-old retired teacher who waged a two-year battle with the company.

In 2007, he discovered that Aeon had purchased a $500 lien on a condominium parking space he owned in Northeast Washington. The D.C. tax office had been mistakenly sending the annual bills to the former owner, and Kazemi said he had no idea that he owed the money.

As soon as he received a court summons from Aeon, he paid the $500 in back taxes.

But Schwartz, AeonГўВҖВҷs attorney, also demanded that Kazemi pay $4,200 for AeonГўВҖВҷs legal fees and costs ГўВҖВ” more than eight times the tax debt. Kazemi hired an attorney to fight the bill, but Schwartz refused to drop the case until the money was paid, writing in an e-mail, ГўВҖВңWe are fully prepared to litigate.ГўВҖВқ

KazemiГўВҖВҷs attorney pushed back: ГўВҖВңYou run a factory and we are not going to be victims to it.ГўВҖВқ

Schwartz, who has a medical degree from the University of Illinois and a law degree from John Marshall Law School in Chicago, wrote: ГўВҖВңLetГўВҖВҷs litigate it. My pleasure.ГўВҖВқ

When the case went to court, the judge slashed the fees to $952, saying they were ГўВҖВңexcessiveГўВҖВқ and ГўВҖВңunnecessary.ГўВҖВқ In one instance, the law firm billed one hour for court hearings ГўВҖВ” the judge cut the time to 12 minutes. In another, the firm billed nearly five hours to review documents ГўВҖВ” the judge reduced the time to 30 minutes.

A second attorney on the case was SchwartzГўВҖВҷs father, Spencer, who had been suspended from practice for a year in Illinois in 2001 for misconduct, including allowing his son to practice law before he was licensed, records show.

Mark Schwartz had been reprimanded, too, for sending out ads that exaggerated his professional experience and accomplishments.

In the end, Kazemi said the fight with Aeon was grueling, costing him time and money.

ГўВҖВңThey had lawyers from Chicago and they came really to destroy,ГўВҖВқ he said.

Neither Spencer nor Mark Schwartz returned calls or letters seeking comment. In a court document filed in the case, Mark Schwartz wrote, ГўВҖВңNo time or expenses are billed for unsuccessful or unnecessary motions.ГўВҖВқ

By 2009, hundreds of families were struggling to pay Aeon. One property owner faced $7,300 in fees for a $1,546 lien. Another faced $6,750 in fees for a $901 lien.

Homeowners complained that their calls to Aeon were not returned, and there was no office to visit in the District. A former attorney for Aeon, Malik Tuma, said the Chicago mail drops were used as a safety precaution.

Most homeowners paid the fees, but some stepped forward, asking local judges to weigh in. In a series of critical rulings,ГӮВ [Superior Court Judge Alfred IrvingГӮ](http://www.dccourts.gov/internet/documents/DCSC_Bio_Irving.pdf)called AeonГўВҖВҷs charges ГўВҖВңextraordinarily high,ГўВҖВқ whileГӮВ [Judge Joseph BeshouriГӮ](http://www.dccourts.gov/internet/documents/DCSC_Bio_Beshouri.pdf)referred to them as ГўВҖВңunreasonableГўВҖВқ and ГўВҖВңexcessive.ГўВҖВқ

Judges found that Aeon billed for legal work that was not done and repeatedly charged homeowners $450 an hour for one top attorneyГўВҖВҷs time.

That attorney was Tuma, who said the company did not overcharge homeowners.

ГўВҖВңI purposely requested the courts to give people time to redeem their properties,ГўВҖВқ said Tuma, a Maryland and D.C. real estate attorney who represented Aeon for several years.

He also said his rate was justified: ГўВҖВңThe $450 hourly rate is the low-to-median rate charged by law firms with attorneys with equivalent levels of experience in the District of Columbia and the DistrictГўВҖВҷs suburbs.ГўВҖВқ

Working a regular 40-hour week for 50 weeks, that would amount to $900,000 a year.

[In 2009, D.C. Attorney General Peter NicklesГӮ](http://www.cov.com/pnickles/)sued Aeon, arguing that the firmГўВҖВҷs legal fees and practices were ГўВҖВңabusiveГўВҖВқ and ГўВҖВңunlawfulГўВҖВқ and asking the court to step in.

ГўВҖВңThere was significant evidence of padding of the bills, charging an excessive number of hours for literally doing nothing,ГўВҖВқ Nickles recently told The Post. ГўВҖВңThey gouged the hell out of poor people.ГўВҖВқ

The city is waiting on a ruling in the case.

By 2010, Aeon had stopped buying liens in the District. But the company still has more than 300 active foreclosure cases.

## Condemned homes in Ohio

Frank Ford, a longtime Cleveland housing advocate, inspects a house that Aeon took through foreclosure. It was later condemned. (Michael S. Williamson/The Washington Post)

**At the height of AeonГўВҖВҷs operations** in the District, the company branched out, moving 370 miles northwest to the largest county in Ohio.

In 2008,ГӮВ [Cuyahoga CountyГӮ](http://www.cuyahogacounty.us/)was reeling from a sinking economy and plummeting housing prices. In two years, Aeon spent more than $25 million buying thousands of liens in the countyГўВҖВҷs largest city, Cleveland, as well as several nearby cities.

But unlike the DistrictГўВҖВҷs homeowners, who largely paid the fees to avoid foreclosure, hundreds in Ohio didnГўВҖВҷt do so. Aeon went on to foreclose on more than 400 properties, from ClevelandГўВҖВҷs historic Slavic Village to the badly blighted east side.

Aeon tried to sell the properties, but buyers were scarce. In neighborhoods across Cleveland, Aeon houses are rotting, prompting inspectors in the past two years to condemn 41 properties and file more than 100 code violations.

ГўВҖВңThe houses are stripped to the bone ГўВҖВ” no windows, no doors, no walls, no pipes. This is what IГўВҖВҷm screaming about,ГўВҖВқ neighborhood activist Anita Gardner said.

![](https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-apps/imrs.php?src=http://www.washingtonpost.com/sf/investigative/wp-content/uploads/sites/8/2013/12/BrokenWindow1000x618.jpg&w=1040&authapi-mob-redir=0)

In Cleveland, Aeon properties have been cited for more than 100 code violations, including rotting porches, shattered windows and collapsed roofs. (Michael S. Williamson / The Washington Post)

One of AeonГўВҖВҷs major lenders was CapitalSource Bank, founded in 2000 by[ГӮВ John Delaney](http://delaney.house.gov/), who was elected to the U.S. House last year. The Maryland Democrat was the bankГўВҖВҷs chief executive officer when CapitalSource loaned $30 million to Aeon in 2009.

Delaney spokesman Will McDonald said the congressman didnГўВҖВҷt know about the companyГўВҖВҷs problems in Ohio or the DistrictГўВҖВҷs lawsuit against Aeon, which was the subject of media reports, including aГӮВ [national story in the Huffington Post in 2010](http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2010/10/18/the-new-tax-man-big-banks_n_766169.html). The article also found that AeonГўВҖВҷs law firm was doing business out of a Chicago mail drop.

ГўВҖВңDuring Congressman DelaneyГўВҖВҷs time as CEO, CapitalSource made over 5,000 business loans, loans probably totaling over $20 billion,ГўВҖВқ McDonald said. ГўВҖВңThe CEO of a bank does not track the ongoing business operations of all outstanding loans because doing so would be impossible.ГўВҖВқ Delaney took a leave of absence from his position at the bank last year and resigned after being elected to Congress.

A spokesman for[ГӮВ CapitalSourceГӮ](https://www.capitalsourcebank.com/)said the bank doesnГўВҖВҷt comment on its borrowers ГўВҖВңas a matter of policy and pursuant to borrower confidentiality agreements.ГўВҖВқ

An attorney for Aeon in Cleveland declined to comment, but during a hearing last year over one of the troubled houses, attorney Kirk Liederbach argued that the firm had invested heavily in the city and ГўВҖВңdidnГўВҖВҷt contemplateГўВҖВқ taking title to so many rundown properties.

ГўВҖВңWe intend to be good citizens here,ГўВҖВқ said Liederbach, who works in the law firmГўВҖВҷs Cleveland office.

[Jay Westbrook](http://www.clevelandcitycouncil.org/ward-16/), a 33-year member of the Cleveland City Council, said the company has repeatedly failed to step up. ГўВҖВңAeon has an extra-vicious business model ГўВҖВ” take no prisoners, take no responsibility,ГўВҖВқ he said.

As Aeon sparred with Cleveland officials**,ГӮ**the company began getting rid of the houses. On a single day this summer, Aeon sold 83 to a newly formed limited liability company, records show.

One member of the city council, concerned that Aeon is trying to skirt the code violations, has requested that prosecutors investigate whether the sales were legitimate.

The lead agent for Aeon who signed the deeds was John A. Lord, a former Ohio attorney who was permanently disbarred in 2007 for deceiving and abandoning clients and then keeping their money. He was later convicted of aggravated theft and served one yearГўВҖВҷs probation, records show.

Lord did not return calls seeking comment.

Lord has also signed legal documents on AeonГўВҖВҷs behalf in Maryland, where the firm has filed hundreds of foreclosure cases since 2006, including one against Craig BrownГўВҖВҷs family home in Baltimore. The 53-year-old grocery clerk who earns $16 an hour stocking milk had been hospitalized for three weeks with a chronic illness when Aeon moved to take the house.

ГўВҖВңIt was terrible,ГўВҖВқ said Brown, who said he tried to persuade Aeon to give him more time to pay the bill but ultimately lost his home. ГўВҖВңThey just didnГўВҖВҷt care.ГўВҖВқ

## WhoГўВҖВҷs in charge of Aeon?

Mark Schwartz, an attorney who owns this $1.7 million estate on a mountainside outside Vail, Colo., has been a constant force behind Aeon Financial. His law firm lists its address at the property. (Marc Piscotty for The Washington Post)

**D.C. officials say they still donГўВҖВҷt know** who actually owns Aeon.

In 2010, city lawyers tried to determine whether there was ГўВҖВңcross-ownershipГўВҖВқ between Aeon and SchwartzГўВҖВҷs law firm, demanding that Aeon produce financial records. Aeon refused, asking the court for a protective order.

The District agreed to stop pressing the issue if Aeon swore that SchwartzГўВҖВҷs law firm had no ownership interest in the company. Again, Aeon refused. The judge ultimately focused only on AeonГўВҖВҷs legal bill, saying it was ГўВҖВңrife with inaccuracies and vague descriptions.ГўВҖВқ

But clues to AeonГўВҖВҷs ownership appear in corporate and court records reviewed by The Post.

Records in Nevada in 2004 and Maryland the following year show that Aeon Properties ГўВҖВ” a predecessor to Aeon Financial ГўВҖВ” was managed by SchwartzГўВҖВҷs younger sister, Stacey Lynn Schwartz, a clinical social worker.

In 2011, records filed in Kentucky for Aeon Financial list Schwartz as a director, along with his sister and a neighbor, Robert Mesch, who lives near SchwartzГўВҖВҷs $1.7 million estate in Colorado.

Mesch, a businessman who founded a private investment group, did not return calls seeking comment. Stacey Schwartz declined to comment.

In addition, records show that one company, Axis Investment Holdings Trust ГўВҖВ” which lists the same office as Aeon in ChicagoГўВҖВҷs Willis Tower ГўВҖВ” has ownership interest in both Aeon Financial and another company, Axis Capital.

Schwartz is the chief executive officer of Axis Capital, which has a subsidiary, Records Direct, court records show. The office suite in the Willis Tower, where Aeon is supposed to do business, is registered to Records Direct.

Schwartz, who is divorcing his wife in Chicago, said in that case earlier this year that the faltering economy had hurt his businesses.

ГўВҖВңI was caught right in the middle of it,ГўВҖВқ Schwartz said in a deposition. ГўВҖВңWe had liens on real property. The value of those assets suddenly plummeted.ГўВҖВқ

In court records, Schwartz said he was ГўВҖВңliving off borrowed funds.ГўВҖВқ His estranged wife described a high-end lifestyle that took him to Hong Kong, London, Aruba and Hawaii. His Colorado estate has five bedrooms, a chefГўВҖВҷs kitchen, a game room and an artistГўВҖВҷs study. The residence is listed as an address for SchwartzГўВҖВҷs law firm.

Nickles, the former D.C. attorney general, said the District should continue to pursue its case against Aeon and determine the companyГўВҖВҷs ownership.

ГўВҖВңThis is debt collecting that leads to the destruction of the lower economic level of the community,ГўВҖВқ he said. ГўВҖВңAnyone who would be behind that kind of scheme ГўВҖВ” and it was a well-thought-out scheme ГўВҖВ” I donГўВҖВҷt think they would be very happy about their names being disclosed on the public record.ГўВҖВқ

#### About this story

The Washington Post carried out one of the first comprehensive studies of tax lien sales nationwide using industry data from more than 800 cities, counties and other local jurisdictions.

The Post contacted nearly two dozen tax collectors to determine whether laws in those jurisdictions protected families from losing their homes over small tax debts.

The newspaper also spent three months investigating one of the top tax-lien purchasers in the District, Aeon Financial, as it moved into Maryland and Ohio.ГӮВ The newspaper studied hundreds of court cases, deeds, mortgages, and corporate and land records, and it presented its Ohio data toГӮВ Michael Schramm of the Center on Urban Poverty and Community Development at Case Western Reserve University in Cleveland.

In the District, the newspaper found that Aeon Financial bought 1,300 tax liens between 2005 and 2009 and ultimately filed more than 900 foreclosure cases, mostly on houses.ГӮВ Most property owners repaid the back taxes, along with AeonГўВҖВҷs legal fees, to save their homes from foreclosure. Several hundred cases are still pending.
