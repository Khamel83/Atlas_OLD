# 💥"Slower and Steadier"💥

**Michael Eisenband**, the global co-leader of corporate finance and restructuring at **FTI Consulting Inc. ($FCN)**, recently pondered [the state of the industry](https://substack.com/redirect/66fb3b29-6f1c-43ba-9b5b-784e15831d70?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) as it moves deeper into the second half of ‘24. The first half? Could be worse, he says. “*It was a busy first-half, despite the occasional snide remark from **Petition** that restructuring advisors have had ample time to shave a couple of strokes off their golf handicaps in recent months.*”\* But it could also be a lot better: there were 85 large –— >$50mm, LOL, “large”\*\* — chapter 11 filings in the first half, a **17% decrease** from 102 in the first half of last year (but 23 filings over >$1b debt cases versus 20 in 1H23), and advisor mandates **fell 19%** in the first half of ‘24 to 261 from 323. To be sure, 1H24 is up against a tough comp but clearly we have at least *some reason* to be snarking about golf.\*\*\*

The not-as-bad-as-it-seems commentary may be cold comfort for restructuring pros, even veterans familiar with the cycle of long stretches of boredom punctuated by furious bursts of activity. *“That paradigm may be broken,”* he writes. “*Given the explosive growth of leveraged credit markets over the last decade, the ascendance of private credit, the extreme lengths that PE sponsors will resort to in their efforts to protect teetering investments, and the reluctance of traditional lenders to foreclose on failing credits, the days of intense but short-lived default cycles might be over, barring a cataclysmic event.*” There may be a “*slower and steadier*” paradigm afoot now, he says.

We’ve seen a lot of what he points to recently, yeah? There’s the relentless increase in distressed exchanges and LMEs. [According to (more recent) data](https://substack.com/redirect/1bd4f410-447b-4cdc-8e27-f2dd5a15075c?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) from **S&P Global Inc.**, there have been forty-five (45) distressed exchanges so far this year, an 18% increase over the same period last year and the highest level since 2009. Some two-thirds of July’s defaults were distressed exchanges:

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_2912,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe9f16451-e54c-49bf-ae9a-7ef8746bf34a_1508x962.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/6e08c166-097a-4a14-b89a-a54f1e2ff7ad?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

The monthly defaulted debt tally fell to $6.4b in July, its second-lowest level this year, and 21% below June’s $8.1b, even as the number of defaulters is touching its five-year average:

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_2912,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff0f4812d-46be-487b-bf5b-9607af0330eb_1508x822.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/5e98fb2e-1592-4692-94b7-b48647e8aabf?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

So, what does “slower and steadier” mean for those of us who aren’t architecting LME deals? Mr. Eisenband states there are signs the economy is slowing,\*\*\*\* notes that rates are unlikely to return to the QE-era zero bound any time soon, and highlights that corporate America has plenty of zombies on offer. *“It is still a target-rich environment of vulnerable companies out there — but it won’t happen as quickly or intensely as many of us would prefer. Ultimately, that may be a good thing,”* he writes. We shall see.

So Heights, sensing a very costly litigation process (on top of an already tight liquidity situation), decided to push to convert the cases to chapter 7.\*

But what’s the best way to avoid expensive disputes? Settlement! So here we are. And per the proposed settlement terms, Heights will have its full amount of prepetition secured claims recognized in exchange for some juicy concessions given to GUCs. These concessions include a share of (i) the proceeds of IP/Austria Assets, (ii) cash as of the effective date and proceeds of non-IP assets, and (iii) proceeds from the debtors’ litigation claims and causes of action.\*\*

### 💥New Chapter 11 Bankruptcy - AIO US Inc.💥

There’s been such a surprise uptick in chapter 11 bankruptcy activity this month that we simply ran out of room on Wednesday to cover this one….

On August 13, 2024, New York-based **AIO US Inc**. and three affiliates (collectively, the “debtors”) filed chapter 11 bankruptcy cases in the **District of Delaware** (**Judge Goldblatt**) — the latest example of a large corporate using the bankruptcy process to manage mass tort liability. One of the debtor affiliates is **Avon Products Inc.** (“API”), the non-operational holding company of the legendary beauty brand’s non-debtor, non-U.S., but entirely healthy entities. The debtors, facing considerable talc litigation liabilities and expenses, have effectively partnered with **Natura SA** (“Natura”), a Brazilian cosmetics giant with vast pre-petition ties to the debtors, to contend with nearly 400 separate lawsuits related to asbestos in talc products.

Naturally, the debtors are of the view that the lawsuits are all nonsense. *“The Debtors strongly believe this litigation is without merit,”* CRO **Phil Gund** of **Ankura Consulting Group** states. The debtors plan to counter all plaintiffs with a *“vigorous defense.”* Ring those cash registers for the lawyers, y’all!

So far, the “*vigorous defense*” has devoured some $225mm of the debtors’ funds, between legal bills and settlements. Gund runs through the parade of horribles: a Dec. ‘22 California jury award to plaintiffs of $36mm in compensatory and $10mm damages (subsequently reduced to $29mm in compensatory damages but now subject to appeal); and separately, a Illinois jury award of $24.5mm to certain plaintiffs. The debtors plan to appeal, believing they have strong grounds to overturn the verdict. The upshot? As of the petition date, the debtors have some $78.1mm of unsecured talc liabilities.

Of course that’s not the extent of the debtors’ financial story. Indeed, in total, there’s $1.29b of funded debt obligations, including over $800mm in secured debt, nearly all of which is owed to various companies affiliated with Natura.

Which gets us to the parties around the table. Clearly the debt and litigation liabilities are getting to be too much so it was time for talks. In its corner, Natura hired **Davis Polk & Wardell LLP** (Darren Klein, Elliot Moskowitz, Hailey Klabo, Joshua Sturm) as legal counsel, **FTI Consulting Inc.** **($FCN)** as financial advisor and **PJT Partners** **LP ($PJT)** as investment bank. For their part, the debtors brought on **Weil Gotshal & Manges LLP** (Ronit Berkovich, Matthew Goren, Alejandro Bascoy, Jessical Falk, Wesley Sheker, Carlos Sandina, Jiaheng Zhang) and **Richards Layton & Finger PA** (Mark Collins, Michael Merchant, Zachary Shapiro, David Queroli) as legal counsel; **Ankura Consulting Group LLC** (Mr Gund, who you know) as financial advisor and CRO, and **Rothschild & Co.** (“Rothschild”)(Andrew Yearley, Marcello Marcin, Pratyush Hiremath) as investment banker. The company also brought on an independent, **John Dubel**, a regular in Weil’s stable, onto the board to serve as a one-man “special committee.”

The plan (small p)? Let Natura deal with this mess. Gund says the debtors didn’t have sufficient time to market their assets before filing, given liquidity constraints. Therefore, “*in light of Natura’s substantial amount of prepetition debt secured by liens in the Debtors’ assets,”* and considering Natura’s in-depth knowledge of selling blush and foundation door to door*, “the Debtors determined Natura was the most logical to serve as a stalking horse bidder for the sale.”*

The debtors’ bid procedures motion hit the docket on August 14, 2024. The upshot is that Natura agreed to serve as stalking horse purchaser, with a credit bid of $125mm. There is no break up fee or expense reimbursement. To be ever-more accommodating, Natura has also agreed to act as a back-up bidder in the event that someone comes in with a higher or better offer. Natura has also agreed to assume a huge swatch of the debtors’ liabilities subject to exclusions delineated in the proposed stalking horse agreement.

Pretty sweet! So, how are we gonna pay for all this? Rothschild did solicit DIP financing from other parties, *“including bulge bracket banks and asset management funds”* but none *“were willing to lend to the debtors on a junior basis.”* Natura returns to save the day: it has offered a $43mm superpriority senior securedDIP facility with an initial draw of $12mm. And here’s something from Gund’s declaration you don’t see much these days:

*The proposed DIP Financing is on terms that are very favorable to the estate—of particular note, the proposed DIP Financing does not contemplate a “roll-up” of any of Natura’s prepetition secured loans. The proposed DIP Financing also does not include any unused commitment, exit, or agent fee and permits the Debtors to pay below-market interest in kind during the chapter 11 cases. Additionally, the DIP Lender is not seeking payment from the Debtors of any professional fees or expenses incurred in connection with the ordinary course negotiation or approval of the DIP Facility.*

Hopefully that’s not news to Davis Polk, FTI and PJT, lol.

Anyway, remember the aforementioned reference to Mr. Dubel? Was that just a pointless name drop on our part? NO! He’s had work to do. He investigated *“certain claims of causes of action”* and potential avoidance actions arising from the debtors’ pre-petition transactions with Natura. More specifically, “*(i) constructive fraudulent transfer, (ii) breach of fiduciary duty, (iii) piercing the corporate veil, and (iv) avoidance actions under the Bankruptcy Code.”* He tapped Weil for help. Weil did the interviews, public document reviews, and engaged with Ankura and other advisors.

And guess what!

They found some stuff. We’ll spare you the details but there was enough “there there” in both the preference claims and constructive fraudulent transfer realms to force everyone back to the table for a good ol’-fashioned arms-length chat.

And? There’s a settlement. We’ll spare you all the gritty details here too but suffice it to say that Natura is playing ball. It has agreed to, among other things, (i) waive some $530mm of secured debt principal, (ii) provide $30mm in cash to fund admin and wind-down costs and distributions to creditors, (iii) leave behind other assets that could enhance creditor recoveries (e.g., additional cash, an insurance receivable, certain preference claims), and (iv) assume a defined benefit pension plan. A hearing to approve the settlement is currently scheduled for October 15, 2024. In the interim, Rothschild will be busy trying to find a competing bidder; it has 67 days from the petition date to do so.

So for those of you keeping track at home, Natura is acting as the pre-petition lender, the proposed DIP lender, the stalking horse purchaser (and potential back-up bidder), AND the settlement counterparty. We’re sure the UST will have nothing to say about THAT.

### 💥New Chapter 11 Bankruptcy - Barrow Shaver Resources Company LLC💥

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_200,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F655d6954-11a5-4b25-bbb0-6b69fba8bebf_200x56.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/5a9300a3-c935-475c-96be-02381cc8c101?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

On August 19, 2024, **[Barrow Shaver Resources Co. LLC](https://substack.com/redirect/8aea1218-cdf6-44fa-8167-fab9e175ab9b?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)** (“BSR” or the “debtor”), a Texas-based oil and gas exploration (“E&P”) company, filed a chapter 11 bankruptcy case in the Southern District of Texas (**Judge Perez**). The debtor was founded by two wildcatters, the last of a dying breed of oilmen willing to risk everything on unproven acreage. Big rewards if right. If not? Well … “*Between 2023 and 2024, the Debtor conducted an aggressive drilling program in the East Texas Basin. The drilling program resulted in significant capex expenditures that far exceeded the Debtor’s corresponding production gains,”* CRO **James Katchadurian**, a partner at **CR3 Partners LLC** writes in his [first day declaration](https://substack.com/redirect/561c9a1e-ad80-4a9a-a47e-eeba300d17f9?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8). BSR could not cover its portion of the tab. In July, a group of creditors — oilfield service companies, the ones who do the dirty work — filed an involuntary petition under chapter 7 on the basis of $8.45mm in outstanding trade claims.

That’s just the tip of the iceberg. While there is no funded secured debt on the debtor’s balance sheet as of the petition date, it did owe a not-insignificant $67mm to its vendors, service providers and suppliers. Several working interest owners did not participate in the drilling program, leaving the debtors to shoulder more of the cost. The debtor also received some $40mm in lien perfection notices. Further complicating matters is a separate and complex issue related to mineral interests and tax payments, in connection with which midstream giant **[Plains Marketing LLP](https://substack.com/redirect/e6bf4771-9f4e-4c04-9e2e-72c773cf97c7?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)** withheld $2.5mm owed the debtor and filed a complaint. The debtor has submitted a proposed order addressing, in part, the mineral interests situation which, given the adversarial kick-off to this case, understandably drew the ire of the involuntary petitioners. As if that’s not enough drama, there’s also a separate matter about working interest assignment in other East Texas wells, and district court cases concerning leases. This is CERTAINLY not one of those “quality” cases we referred to above.

The debtor seeks DIP financing so that it may undertake an asset sale and confirm a plan of reorganizationthat will provide *“a significant distribution to creditors on account of their allowed claims.”* (A [presentation](https://substack.com/redirect/e6bf4771-9f4e-4c04-9e2e-72c773cf97c7?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) at the first day hearing indicated liquidation was also possible.) According to Katchadurian*,* the debtor has obtained a *“firm commitment”* for DIP financing from *“one interested party.”*

Besides CR3 Partners as financial advisor, the debtor is represented by **Jones Walker LLP** (Joseph E. Bain, Mark A. Mintz, Sean T. Wilson, Olivia K. Greenberg, Elizabeth De Leon).
