In the last entry, we highlighted the MN-based hair salon operator’s diminishing liquidity position against roughly $180mm in debt. We wrote:

> *In Aug ‘22, the company announced a successful renegotiation of the Mar ‘23 Revolving Credit Facility. The amendment extended the maturity to Aug. ‘25 and converted the $291.27mm outstanding under the old revolver to a new facility consisting of a $180mm term loan and a $55mm revolving credit facility. The minimum liquidity covenant was also decreased to $10mm from $75mm. Interest on the new facility is set to increase to S+725 (425 bps paid in cash and 300 bps PIK).*
>
> *As of the most recent filing, the company has $38mm in liquidity and $181mm outstanding on the facility ($164mm net on the term loan, $14mm on the revolver, $3mm PIK). Since this is due Aug ‘25, Jefferies might be due for some more work soon if it isn’t already.*

**Jefferies Financial Group ($JEF)** has been working fo sho.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_480,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_lossy/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3243a8f3-8668-4994-bd6e-94bef4dc426f_480x270.gif)](https://substack.com/redirect/880a83fd-3420-4fe9-94a6-1c4d7c222ff6?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

Source: GIPHY

On Tuesday, the company [announced](https://substack.com/redirect/f19f5326-1c4f-49bc-9492-8bdc3a9b61af?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) that it replaced its existing debt — which, as of [the company’s last quarterly report](https://substack.com/redirect/9c9635e6-7380-4e51-9d49-62540119f8dc?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8), stood at $179.7mm — with a new senior secured first lien $105mm term loan and $25mm revolving credit facility. The transaction will eliminate roughly $80mm in debt (“*[[p]roceeds of the term loan were used to](https://substack.com/redirect/88e315ac-1060-41d5-8857-6d2856601abc?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) **[fully refinance](https://substack.com/redirect/88e315ac-1060-41d5-8857-6d2856601abc?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)** [the existing credit facility…](https://substack.com/redirect/88e315ac-1060-41d5-8857-6d2856601abc?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)*,” 🤔, our emphasis) and save the company close to $7mm in annual interest expense. Gross LTM leverage will be ~4.4x versus ~7.9x. The new facility comes in at S+9%, with up to 4.5% PIK at the company’s election. In the event the company gets leverage below 3.75x, the interest rate steps down to S+8.5%.

Lenders on the facility include **TCW Asset Management Company LLC** and **Midcap Financial Trust**. The new facility matures in June ‘29.

That “fully finance” language is interesting. Was there a paydown between the last quarterly filing and the refi or is this *another* haircut for the pre-existing lenders? 🤔, indeed.

It would be an understatement to say that investors took the announcement well. The company’s stock jumped over 300% on Tuesday on the news, before giving a little back after hours. 🤯

### **⏩One to Watch: Vertex Energy Inc. ($VTNR)⏩**

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_362,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F49497873-ab2f-4232-a5a3-e134cfa9b04e_1600x800.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/9d67585e-91e8-4e95-a84c-680162784930?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

**Vertex Energy Inc. ($VTNR)** is a publicly traded energy company in the business of conventional petroleum refining and renewable fuel production. Until ‘22, the company was solely in the conventional downstream business, focusing on collecting and refining used motor oil and other petroleum products into various end products, such as gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel. In April ‘22, the company completed a $75mm acquisition for a refinery in Mobile, Alabama (“Mobile Refinery”) from **Shell Oil Products US**. The new refinery allowed the company to diversify into renewable fuels made from feedstocks like vegetable oils and animal fats. In conjunction with the acquisition, the company issued a $125mm senior secured term loan due April ‘25\* with a syndicate of lenders, including **BlackRock Financial Management Inc.**, **Whitebox Advisors LLC**, and **Highbridge Capital Management LLC**.\*\*

The company envisioned that this new "*flagship*" asset would position it as an industry leader in renewable and conventional product production. From the initial [press release](https://substack.com/redirect/97586c47-1370-4ab6-8f08-3aae224be5c8?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) announcing the deal:

> *“Following the successful completion of a planned $85 million conversion of the Mobile refinery's hydrocracking unit by year-end 2022, Vertex anticipates that the refinery will have the potential to generate at least $3 billion in annual sales and **$400 million of gross profit annually beginning in 2023**, given current refining economics.”* (emphasis added)

Let’s check in on that, shall we?

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_1842,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcfb0ce24-0479-421e-b3fd-efff7ba7ed17_921x343.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/5feb5394-e081-46c8-9ba7-67e9abeb5932?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

Source: [Company Filings](https://substack.com/redirect/b2b580b2-7ab9-4bda-8238-0ba7ce6ec180?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

Uh, not quite $400mm of gross profit. The company finished the year with a net loss of $72mm.

If we break things down further, we can see the real culprit is the renewables business. In ‘23, the conventional business had a gross profit of $165mm, while the renewables business had a gross loss of $39mm.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_1836,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1d927718-b67f-4e9f-86e4-d9e47aafc607_918x233.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/ec851125-d860-4662-b6b4-3d14540934d9?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

Source: Company Filings

It seems that the company “came to Jesus” and realized that things might take a bit longer than anticipated to pan out so on December 28, 2023, it amended the term loan to allow the lenders to provide an additional $50mm. The equity market caught on quickly. The amendment announcement dropped the company’s stock price by over 30%.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_1228,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F17345b22-363a-4126-b924-6a14209268a4_614x522.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/a78d2da4-1c56-47d3-8112-16758529889b?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

Source: Google. Markup: PETITION LLC. Notably, Jim Cramer recommended this stock in mid-’22 right before it hit an ATH. Shortly thereafter it commenced its never-ceasing downward move, lol.

At year-end ‘23, the company’s cash balance was $81mm — down $65mm YOY from $146mm. The once-promising renewables business had become a burden. Management had a decision to make: keep investing resources into the renewables business, hoping to make it profitable, or fall back on the $2.5b-revenue conventional downstream business that is consistently turning a profit.

We got our answer in the company’s 1Q24 earnings [release](https://substack.com/redirect/d3c96183-99c5-43ed-8126-f6b7cb84fa79?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8). During the earnings call, CFO **Christopher Carlson** stated:

> “*Our strategic decision to pause and pivot [renewable diesel] production is aimed at significantly enhancing this effort over the near-term by stopping losses associated with renewable diesel production and adding available margin through upgrading [vacuum gas oil] to a higher margin conventional products.”*

The company hopes the conversion will be complete in 3Q24 and show up in 4Q24 earnings.

While this is almost undoubtedly the correct decision, the question becomes: is it too late? As of the last quarterly [filing](https://substack.com/redirect/46eac0f4-95c0-451f-87fc-64042673fcc3?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8), the Apr. ‘25 maturing term loan still has $196mm outstanding, while the company only has a cash balance of $66mm. An extension seems like the most logical outcome, given that the company *should* be cash flow positive once the conversion is complete and the lenders have proven supportive in the past. Still, the lenders ought to be pondering numerous questions. Will the conversion actually be complete in the third quarter? How capital-intensive is the conversion? This is also an energy business after all, so of course, it’s exposed to commodity risk as well. Analysts raised some of these questions on the 1Q24 earnings call, but the answers were expectedly vague.

The company certainly doesn’t have much wiggle room, so we’ll watch this one closely as it unfolds.

### ⚡️Update: Rite Aid Corp.⚡️

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_1192,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb68a9717-0583-4704-ac85-7f6ab1edc72f_5500x3666.jpeg)](https://substack.com/redirect/1c524b03-1876-4f22-b283-6e289559fa3c?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

Source: Getty Images

As we chug towards a June 27, 2024, plan confirmation hearing in the **Rite Aid Corp.** (“RAD”) chapter 11 bankruptcy cases,\* we thought it’d be appropriate to catch our readers up on the drama that surrounded the RAD debtors’ sale of **Elixir**.

As a refresher, Elixir is (was) RAD’s pharmacy benefits manager (“PBM”) business. Pursuant to [a sale order](https://substack.com/redirect/678995a2-de2c-4565-bd1b-4242bc8e6612?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) entered on January 17, 2024, RAD sold Elixir to **MedImpact Healthcare Systems Inc.** (“MedImpact”) for $575mm plus the assumption of certain liabilities.\*\* It’s that last bit — regarding the assumption of liabilities — where the rubber meets the road.

The sale closed on February 1, 2024 and MedImpact immediately took a magnifying glass to possible post closing adjustments. It then turned to RAD and argued that over $225mm of current liabilities shouldn’t categorized as “Assumed Liabilities” under the asset purchase agreement (“APA”). Here was MedImpact to RAD:

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_422,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_lossy/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6ccb57a0-c33d-4c89-9d3d-64cabe7e07a6_480x480.gif)](https://substack.com/redirect/f9cf10b1-078c-48dd-b19f-e0c3ab962acc?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

Source: GIPHY.

The RAD debtors be like…

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_400,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_lossy/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fec09c754-746c-48dc-8132-ccc8351ca447_400x336.gif)](https://substack.com/redirect/55b69478-2172-4c58-ac11-2063e66f649e?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

Source: GIPHY. Greatest show ever.

Here’s the exact language governing the issue in the APA:

> *“…all trade payables of Sellers to non-Affiliated third parties in connection with the Business existing on the Closing Date that remain unpaid and are not delinquent as of the Closing Date and incurred in the Ordinary Course and other Liabilities of Sellers of the types included in the definition of Closing Working Capital but not including any Excluded Rebate Liability or any Liabilities to the extent relating to or otherwise arising, whether before, on or after the Closing, under any of the Excluded Contracts (collectively, the “Assumed Current Liabilities”).”*

MedImpact argued that the contested liabilities were “delinquent” or not incurred in the “ordinary course.” Basically, “here, RAD, we’re good, you keep these.”

LOL, can you imagine the debtors taking on $225mm of additional liabilities AT THIS POINT in the case? Forget breaking the camel’s back, you might as well dig a hole for the carcass.

MedImpact to the debtors be like:

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_380,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_lossy/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdb21788c-618b-4bfc-a4b6-758a07c682de_220x146.gif)](https://substack.com/redirect/53c7f730-c09f-46d1-b531-94a3c55cb4f2?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

Source: Tenor

And Elixir’s negative working capital should definitely not have come as a surprise to MedImpact. There was literally a defined “Target Working Capital” baked into the APA that, surprise, heavily leaned into negative territory:

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_514,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0b4eeb6f-de1e-4506-9d3b-eccec1f03b1f_514x31.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/89135c9f-43f9-4d2a-a8a9-38853bc1a2cd?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

Source: [Docket #1510 Exhibit 1](https://substack.com/redirect/678995a2-de2c-4565-bd1b-4242bc8e6612?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8), later amended to negative $192.3mm

**Judge Kaplan** wasn’t buying what MedImpact was selling. At a hearing on June 24, 2024, he granted the RAD debtors’ motion to enforce the sale order, saying:

> *“Currently, the initial terms of the APA … reflects a target working capital figure of negative $206,628,000.”*
>
> *“The substantial negative working capital at closing should have come as no surprise to MedImpact or its experienced financial advisors. Indeed, the undisputed record reflects that BDO, retained by MedImpact, undertook financial due diligence, which confirmed the history of Elixir’s negative working capital going back to July of 2022.”*

He continued:

> *“Frankly, a $276 million adjustment on a transaction where the anticipated consideration was in the range of $770 million, representing nearly 35% of the value flowing to the bankruptcy estate, is shocking, and to use the term employed repeatedly by the debtors, absurd.”*
>
> *“There is nothing in the record to suggest and MedImpact fails to offer a convincing reason as to why the net working capital figure changed so dramatically in such a short time frame, under 90 days.”*
>
> *“It is no secret that money in this case is tight, and there is little wiggle room, let alone room for approximately $225 million in additional liabilities.”*

**PETITION note: the $770mm quoted includes the purchase price and the negative working capital calculated at closing.**

He memorialized his thoughts in a pointed [memorandum of opinion](https://substack.com/redirect/a37eaa47-d851-49a4-93b0-e0f44d716311?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8):

> *“The notion that the Debtors—or any debtor—would enter into an agreement that it knew would render its plan unconfirmable is unfathomable. It specially makes no sense in the context of this case, where counsel has worked tirelessly and devoted countless hours to make it this far — now mere days from a confirmation hearing, albeit a contested hearing. It is also worth pointing out the unlikely scenario in which the existing lenders, who supported the sale, would thereafter agree to inject tens of millions of dollars in additional DIP and exit financing were MedImpact’s interpretation of its responsibilities under the APA reasonable and accurate.”*

Yes, a $225mm swing is quite “absurd” and, so, yes, Judge Kaplan agreed with the debtors’ reading of the APA. Beyond a strict reading of the APA, though, there’s no doubt that Judge Kaplan wanted to best set up plan confirmation for success and push these cases towards emergence come hell or high water — giving a big fat middle finger to MedImpact in the process.
