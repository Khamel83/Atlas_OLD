Which begs the question: are movie theaters f\*cked? Let’s take stock of the summer.

**Chris Hemsworth** and **Anya Taylor-Joy**’s **Mad Max** prequel, *Furiosa*, kickstarted the summer box office season with quite a crash and burn. *[Furiosa](https://substack.com/redirect/d7b8a307-9c36-4929-a40d-e936979db952?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)* [made only ~$31mm over the long Memorial Day weekend](https://substack.com/redirect/d7b8a307-9c36-4929-a40d-e936979db952?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8), which was “*…the worst it’s been in nearly three decades.*”

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_1376,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F88749752-4739-4ab4-bbae-5bf56d153acc_1198x1198.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/5e92ddd5-726b-4dcf-b42d-e50d142a28a3?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

> *This dramatic a low could suggest a real tipping point for the theatrical movie business. What are the implications for the future of a theatrical business when Memorial Day, with well-reviewed movies, hit a 26-year low? And having chosen to embrace streaming while producing movies for theaters, what changes might studios need to make to adjust to the new reality?*
>
> *Industry insiders told TheWrap that the long-term implications of such a poor weekend are still difficult to discern, given that the 2024 box office data is muddled by last year’s WGA and SAG-AFTRA strikes. The strike-induced production lag has prolonged inconsistent grosses caused by inconsistent output of films for theaters since they reopened.*
>
> *“I don’t think this is an inflection point where we say, ‘Oh, if the pandemic didn’t finish it off, the strikes will,’” said Comscore analyst Paul Dergarabedian. “This is a slump that I think [everyone in Hollywood saw coming](https://substack.com/redirect/3d88542d-9412-4fc3-8821-9eb63d7fa247?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) when the strikes went on for weeks on end. What’s The Frequency, Kenneth? - Remastered

R.E.M.

While Mr. Aron pumps optimism through X’s technological veins — spending the last several months responding to the doom and gloom with unbridled hope and poo poo’ing the naysayers — we reckon he spent the early part of the summer worrying in private. [People just aren’t going to movies the way they used to](https://substack.com/redirect/c484a276-ebae-404f-ab84-e3e013697345?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8). As **R.E.M**’s **Michael Stipe** once asked, “*what’s the frequency [Kenneth]*?” According to analyst **Matthew Ball**, that’s precisely the correct question:

> *The domestic box office has been in persistent decline since 2002, when per capita annual admissions in North America hit 5.1, their highest point since 1977’s Star Wars. By 2019, the last pre-pandemic moviegoing year, per capita admissions had plummeted to less than 3.5 – not because fewer people were going to the movies (74% of Americans bought a ticket in 2002, and 76% did in 2019) **but because the share who went at least once a month on average fell by nearly two thirds (from 28% to 11%)**.* (emphasis added)

Which begs the question: what about after ‘19?

> *Domestic per capita admissions, which had shocked when it fell under the 3.50 mark in 2019, fell to 0.62 in 2020 and 1.26 in 2021.*

Ok, makes sense but then what?

> *The fears of permanent impairment were partly allayed by 2022 and 2023, which benefited from relatively dense release schedules, as the many films that were supposed to be released during the pandemic finally debuted on the Silver Screen. Top Gun: Maverick, which was delayed for two years at an estimated interest expense of a million a month, became the biggest domestic release of 2022 (it grossed over $1MM a day for 75 days) and the second-largest worldwide. 2023, meanwhile, had Barbenheimer. The worldwide box office of 2022 beat 2021 by 27%, and 2023 beat 2022 by 31%. Domestic per capita admissions were still but 2.31 — down a third from 2019, sure, but seemingly on the mend.*

On the mend? Naw, dawg, ‘24 is looking sick AF:

> *Altogether, 2024 is currently trending for roughly 1.8 tickets sold per American, fewer than in 2022. Even if 2025 rebounds to 2.5 or so (which would beat 2022 by 8%), it looks like the pandemic will have accelerated the decline in per capita theatrical admissions by roughly 15 years. Pre-pandemic, the industry was trending toward 2.75 by 2029.*

Oy. It gets worse. This chart ⬇️ must be giving Mr. Aron some sleepless nights:

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_2020,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc2c114ea-4c35-4a3a-a50e-e85b19624c8e_1010x580.png)](https://substack.com/redirect/34d5d219-27ab-44e6-ab34-c8568b27fc21?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)

> *“We are concerned,” says one studio insider. “You’d be a fool to not be thinking about that. People are not in the moviegoing habit and are firmly ensconced in appointment viewing, which is a huge problem. But there hasn’t been a real igniter.”*

Still, others suggest that we won’t know for certain until we return to a normal slate of films in ‘25. Again, per The Hollywood Reporter from back in late May:

> *One veteran financier says moviegoing will rebound when the calendar returns to normal. “The theatrical business isn’t going anywhere — it’s a multibillion-dollar industry. Blockbusters will come. Inside Out 2 may be huge,” this person forecasts. “We won’t return to usual and customary release patterns until 2025.”*

Dude made a good call on Inside Out 2. Maybe she/he is right about ‘25, too, 🤔? Mr. Ball begs to differ:

> *We’ll never know what 2023 and 2024 attendance would have looked like without the strikes, but per capita admissions are so far off the pre-COVID trendline that the strikes are, themselves, far from a sufficient explanation. And in general, the relevance of a year’s slate also tends to be overestimated; 2019 was perhaps the greatest blockbuster year in history, yet tickets sold fell 6.5% year-over-year.*

And here’s another counterpoint from that Hollywood Reporter article:

> *But with every movie that disappoints and underperforms, whether Furiosa or The Fall Guy, studio insiders are growing more and more alarmed. “It’s mind-numbing that Furiosa hasn’t grossed $50 million domestically,” says another studio exec. “My worry is that studios will put on the brakes and make fewer movies, which will further contribute to the problem.”*

Indeed, *Furiosa* bombed so badly versus expectations that it [may have killed](https://substack.com/redirect/89185f9d-9490-43f4-8325-90ed66ed915b?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8) creator **George Miller**’s next potential *Mad Max* installment, *The Wasteland*.

Query whether **PwC** baked this possibility into its recently released “*[Global Entertainment & Media Outlook.](https://substack.com/redirect/a2f28b28-7ab4-466a-9891-fb9db2af803d?j=eyJ1IjoiOW95azAifQ.3I-y5PDezjo-i0ls31AGTIH4E8sNNl31puiktHvNIm8)*” Therein, the consultancy predicts that global box office *revenue* — not admissions — will (finally) surpass pre-pandemic levels in ‘26. Which is significant for two reasons. First, it means that theaters will make more money per customer due to higher prices. You can see the interest rate differential here. This is a positive both in terms of rate reduction and potential debt reduction. Unless you’re a stockholder.
