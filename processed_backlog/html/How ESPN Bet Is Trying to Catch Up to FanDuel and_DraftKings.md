# How ESPN Bet Is Trying to Catch Up to FanDuel and DraftKings

[Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-08-15/how-espn-s-trying-to-catch-up-to-fanduel-draftkings-in-sports-betting)
·
by Christopher Palmeri

In the summer of 2023, ESPN Inc. and casino operator Penn Entertainment Inc. announced a [10-year, $2 billion deal](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-08-08/espn-signs-sports-betting-deal-with-penn-entertainment). Penn, which was in a partnership with media company Barstool Sports Inc., had bolted for the self-proclaimed “Worldwide Leader in Sports.” On a call with analysts, Penn Chief Executive Officer Jay Snowden said, “We think we’re going to be a major player.” He dismissed as “crazy talk” the idea that the sports betting battle had been won already by Flutter Entertainment Plc’s FanDuel and DraftKings Inc. ESPN Bet, as the venture would be called, is “going to have an impact on what that overall market share looks like,” he said.

Betting slips from the new sportsbook in Detroit.Photographer: Sarah Rice

After its debut in November, the ESPN Bet app saw a surge in sign-ups as it gave away $200 in bonus bets to new players. As of May, the latest month for which data is available, ESPN Bet had a 2.8% share of the US online sports betting market, putting it in sixth place, according to research firm [Eilers & Krejcik Gaming LLC](https://ekgamingllc.com/). And while that’s up from the 1.7% Penn had a year ago with Barstool, together FanDuel and DraftKings have almost 74%. (Penn says it should be judged only on the 18 states where ESPN Bet is available. By that measure its market share is 3.9%, according to Eilers.)

The numbers show Penn [faces a tough slog](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-02-15/espn-bet-launch-leads-to-334-million-loss-for-penn-interactive) in its second swipe at the online sports betting business, even with a globally known partner. Penn is projecting losses of as much as $510 million this year on more than $1 billion in revenue in the division that includes ESPN Bet. Earlier this summer, the company laid off 100 employees in that division. Its stock has lost about 28% of its value since the ESPN deal was announced, falling from about $25 to around $18. And activist investor Donerail Group LP [has called on Penn’s board of directors](https://mms.businesswire.com/media/20240531207464/en/2146466/1/The_Donerail_Group_Public_Letter_to_the_PENN_Board_of_Directors) to sell the company, saying that the value of its casinos is obscured by the ESPN Bet deal and that Penn’s shares are worth considerably more than their current price. “Given Penn’s lack of management expertise in online sports betting and a history of poor investment, it seems improbable that Penn will optimize the value of ESPN Bet on its own, and to be sure, it seems highly unlikely that ESPN Bet will ever be valuable enough to justify the massive amount of shareholder capital this management team has deployed along the way,” says Will Wyatt, Donerail’s managing partner.

### ESPN Bet Plays Catch-Up

US market share of online sports betting brands

Source: Eilers & Krejcik Gaming

Sports betting has soared in popularity since the US Supreme Court [ruled in 2018](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2018-05-14/federal-sports-wagering-ban-struck-down-by-u-s-supreme-court) that it could expand beyond Nevada; 38 states, plus Puerto Rico and Washington, DC, now allow it. Revenue from sports betting in the US is expected to reach nearly $15 billion this year, up from $442 million six years ago, according to research firm [Vixio Regulatory Intelligence](https://www.vixio.com/). But most of that is going to just two companies, whose early start, marketing and innovation have led to domination. There’s a veritable graveyard of other players that have quit or significantly dialed back their sports betting ambitions in recent years, including casino giant Wynn Resorts, Kentucky Derby owner Churchill Downs, Australia’s PointsBet Holdings and billionaire [Tilman Fertitta](https://www.bloomberg.com/billionaires/profiles/tilman-j-fertitta/), [who sold](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-08-09/draftkings-to-buy-golden-nugget-online-gaming-for-1-56-billion) his Golden Nugget Online Gaming to DraftKings in 2022. Evoke Plc ditched its *Sports Illustrated*-branded betting site in March.

DraftKings CEO Jason Robins says his company, which employs more than 1,400 engineers, has created an app that’s “heads and tails above the others in the market, and I think generally both us and FanDuel are seen that way.” Penn, he says, is putting a “big chunk” of its investment toward a licensing fee to ESPN for its marketing and support, and “that’s a choice they’re making.”

For a long time the casino industry had no great love for sports betting. The floor space given to sportsbooks was rarely busy outside of a few weekends during the football and basketball seasons. The business lacked the predictability of card games or slot machines. One former Wynn CEO, Matt Maddox, likened a sportsbook to a buffet—an amenity a casino had to have because customers like it, but not a very profitable one. Although bookmakers adjust odds to get money flowing in on both sides of a bet, the house can get it wrong. For example, [$185.6 million](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-02-13/nevada-super-bowl-betting-sets-record-in-first-las-vegas-contest) was wagered in Nevada on this year’s Super Bowl—a state record for the game. But when the Kansas City Chiefs beat the favored San Francisco 49ers, casinos kept just $6.8 million, less than half of what they had in past years when the volume of betting was lower.

New Jersey, whose traditional casino business in Atlantic City was losing customers to neighboring states, embraced online casino games in 2013 and sports betting five years later (it was the state’s case that prompted the Supreme Court ruling). FanDuel and DraftKings, both of which got their start with daily fantasy sports contests, were among the first to offer sports betting in New Jersey and quickly converted many of their millions of daily players into sports bettors.

Some of the ESPN Bet branding in Detroit.Photographer: Sarah Rice for Bloomberg Businessweek

Irish bookmaker Flutter, which purchased a controlling stake in FanDuel in 2018, began bringing many of its most popular products to the US. The site was a trailblazer in prop bets, which let customers wager on outcomes beyond the score, such as who’s going to get the first touchdown. They also promoted bundling bets, known as parlays, enticing gamblers with higher potential payouts. Such wagers involve more risk for the gambler—to win they have to make, say, three right calls, not just one—and much fatter margins for operators. FanDuel and DraftKings spent heavily in the early years on marketing and promotion to capture more players. After a rocky few years, when Wall Street grew disenchanted with its ongoing losses, DraftKings’ market value now tops $15 billion, [rivaling some](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-10-19/draftkings-takes-online-gambling-lead-from-fanduel-analyst-says) of the world’s biggest casino operators.

The cost of acquiring new customers is likely higher than many entrants anticipated, says James Kilsby, chief analyst at Vixio. That’s thanks in part to the nature of the US market, where sportsbook operators deal with state-by-state regulations instead of the countrywide rules common in western Europe. Some states, such as Mississippi and Washington, allow sports betting in person but not online; others, like New York and Massachusetts, ban betting on local college teams. Online operators also need to pay for geolocation software to ensure that a customer betting on an app allowed in Nevada is in that state and not in neighboring California, where sports betting is still illegal. Many states require operators to sign so-called market access agreements with a local casino or horse track, sharing revenue with them. In 2023, Flutter earned $889 million at its UK and Ireland business—and just $65 million in the US, where it has greater revenue.

In theory, sports betting is just a gateway to the more lucrative business of online casinos. There, players can spin virtual slot wheels 24/7, and operators get a more predictable revenue stream: There are no stirring second-half comebacks by the underdog in slots. But that business has been slow to roll out nationally, with only seven states approving it so far. “The US is an expensive and restrictive market,” Kilsby says.

Penn got its start as a horse track outside Harrisburg, Pennsylvania, in 1972. The company grew over the decades through acquisitions to include more than 40 casinos and racetracks. It operates away from the bright lights of the Las Vegas Strip: Some of the company’s largest properties include Hollywood Casino in Columbus, Ohio, and L’Auberge Casino Resort in Lake Charles, Louisiana, Snowden said on a recent podcast.

Snowden ran Caesars Entertainment’s properties in Atlantic City before joining Penn in 2011. He was promoted to CEO in 2020 and began looking to reach younger patrons. That year he acquired a stake in Barstool, founded by incendiary sports commentator David Portnoy, and launched a betting app with the brand. The following year, Snowden struck a $2 billion deal for Score Media & Gaming Inc., a leading sports media and betting site based in Canada, whose technology platform Penn adopted for its US operations.

Portnoy in March.Photographer: Adam Glanzman/Getty Images

Snowden outlined a vision where consumers could be lured from online betting to physical casinos and vice versa. He began opening Barstool-branded sportsbooks at Penn properties across the US. Aside from being plastered with Barstool logos, they were typical spaces, with betting windows, TVs and lounge seating. One former Barstool executive, who asked not to be named discussing a previous employer, says Snowden relied too much on Barstool podcasts and videos to attract customers and didn’t spend enough on traditional marketing. (Snowden declined to be interviewed for this story.)

Barstool, whose website has long featured outlandish content, proved too controversial. Problems escalated after two *Business Insider* articles beginning in November 2021 accused Portnoy of sexual misconduct. After the stories ran, Penn was subject to additional scrutiny by regulators in Indiana, Massachusetts and Nevada. Portnoy, who denied the accusations and didn’t respond to requests for comment, said after repurchasing the business from Penn for $1 that Barstool wasn’t the right fit for a regulated industry like gambling. Penn took a $923 million loss on the Barstool sale last year. (Snowden complained about the timing of the articles, which coincided with Penn reporting earnings. He agreed with Portnoy that, as a publicly held, highly regulated business, Penn had been an “unnatural owner.”)

Walt Disney Co., which owns 80% of ESPN, historically stayed away from gambling, which didn’t fit its family-friendly image. Disney didn’t license Mickey or Goofy for slot machines, and it didn’t put casinos on its cruise ships. As sports betting exploded and consumers began canceling cable subscriptions, the main source of revenue for the ESPN networks, the company turned to sports betting as a way to keep viewers engaged. ESPN held talks with DraftKings, FanDuel, Rush Street Interactive Inc. and others before signing with Penn.

The synergy was visible immediately: ESPN Bet launched with ads featuring bombastic commentator Stephen A. Smith and anchor Scott Van Pelt. Programs on the ESPN networks regularly refer to ESPN Bet odds, and the network’s betting analysts, Erin Kate Dolan and Joe Fortenbaugh, share their takes on the action. *ESPN Bet Live*, an hourlong show featuring betting news and picks, airs weekday afternoons on ESPN2. The betting site’s logo is a regular fixture at the bottom of the screen during live game broadcasts.

Regular bettors say the ESPN Bet app lacks features of its rivals. In this year’s Stanley Cup Finals, for instance, bettors on FanDuel could place what are known as over/under bets on eight potential outcomes surrounding how many total goals would be scored by the Edmonton Oilers and Florida Panthers. ESPN Bet offered three. ESPN Bet is also not as generous with bonus bets and other promotions, users say. DraftKings and FanDuel “consistently offer a lot of promotions for bettors,” says CJ Buskey, an avid bettor from New Jersey who posts his wagers on Reddit. And while those sites give “profit boosts” to players on parlay bets of their own design, ESPN Bet, on a recent Friday in August, offered profit boosts only on premade parlays. None of them paid off. (The site offers make-your-own parlays without boosts, and Penn has said it will increase its promotional offers.)

An ad featuring ESPN personalities Stephen A. Smith and Mike Greenberg.Photographer: Sarah Rice for Bloomberg Businessweek

Getting a late start in the business has also meant ESPN Bet doesn’t have the same number of players posting their bets online, which encourages other gamblers. “People want to see big wins on social media,” says an online influencer who goes by DataWise and declined to give his real name. “Since everyone’s already posting their big parlays from DraftKings and FanDuel, those are the sites that people will be encouraged to use.” (Penn says ESPN Bet has 10 million social media followers.)

In April, ESPN Bet placed 11th in Eilers’ ranking of 39 sports betting apps. While the site’s mint-accented aesthetic is “eye-catching,” ESPN Bet “lacks a standout element or characteristic to make betting sessions memorable or interesting,” the report said. FanDuel, which has held the top spot for five years, stood out for its “easy-to-use layout,” fast loading times and extended betting options. “Penn has a lot of work to do,” says [Chris Grove](https://ekgamingllc.com/chris-grove/), an adviser at Eilers. “Even as you’re improving, your competitors are moving further away.” ESPN Bet also lags rivals in the time users spend on it, according to [Sensor Tower Inc.](https://sensortower.com/), another market researcher. Customers spent about 9 minutes daily on ESPN Bet in the second quarter of this year, versus 11 minutes on DraftKings and 13 minutes on FanDuel.

Mike Morrison, vice president for ESPN Bet and ESPN Fantasy, says the app’s successful launch in November, just three months after the deal was announced, shows how fast the two companies have been moving. He says features that have been added since, such as the ability to bet via ESPN’s sports news app, will further integrate the businesses. Based on information including which stories a user clicks on, the app will send customized betting ideas tailored to favorite teams and players. Metrics such as customer engagement are “tracking along with expectations and where we’d thought we’d be at this point,” Morrison says. Aaron LaBerge, the former chief technology officer for Disney’s sports and entertainment businesses, recently joined Penn in a similar role.

Snowden has been rebranding the Barstool sportsbooks in Penn casinos with the ESPN Bet name, with the first opening in Detroit in April. It features 35 betting kiosks, a 30-foot video wall and signature cocktails (one, called “Unnecessary Roughness,” is made with rye, amaretto and blood orange). Recently the company announced plans to open eight more this year, in cities such as Baton Rouge, Louisiana, and Toledo, Ohio.

Penn and ESPN can terminate their agreement after three years if ESPN Bet doesn’t reach a certain market-share threshold, which the companies haven’t made public. Over the past few months, rumors have swirled about prospective offers for Penn, including from rival Boyd Gaming Corp., which declined to comment. HG Vora Capital Management LLC, another large Penn investor, is unhappy with the company’s online investments and is contemplating a fight for board seats next year, according to people familiar with the investment firm’s thinking, who weren’t authorized to speak publicly.

On a conference call with analysts in August, Snowden said, “Don’t believe everything you read,’’ when asked about reports of merger discussions. He promised improvements to the app in time for football season, including a “much more competitive” lineup of parlays and prop bets, and said the biggest losses in the online business are over. “We want to be America’s sportsbook,” Snowden said. “And that’s going to mean that you’re really catering to the masses.”
