<p>But finding the number of miles you&#8217;ll need for an award flight is <strong>NOT </strong>as simple as finding the price between the zones of your departure and arrival airports.  Because <strong>Singapore Airlines does NOT allow transfers (connections) or stopovers</strong> on Alaska Airlines awards.</p>
<p>So, <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://millionmilesecrets.com/2015/01/19/british-airways-avios-part-2-what-does-a-distance-based-award-chart-mean/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">similar to using British Airways Avios points</a>, award prices are for <strong>single segments</strong>.</p>
<p>The best deals are on non-stop flights.  That&#8217;s because Singapore Airlines charges extra miles for connecting flights.  So first check if your home airport has non-stop flights on Alaska Airlines.</p>
<p><strong>Note:   </strong>You can check using <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://millionmilesecrets.com/2014/05/20/google-flights-search/">Google Flights</a>.</p>
<p>For example, a one-way, non-stop award ticket from Washington, DC, to Los Angeles (Zone 4 to Zone 1) would cost <strong>12,000 Singapore Airlines miles</strong>.  But a ticket from Washington, DC, to Los Angeles via Seattle or Portland would cost <strong>19,500 Singapore Airlines miles</strong> (12,000 miles for Zone 4 to Zone 1, plus 7,500 miles for Zone 1 to Zone 1).  That&#8217;s not a good deal!</p>
<p>Because of this pricing method, there are gaps in the award chart.  These are between zones where <strong>NO</strong> non-stop Alaska Airlines flights exist, like from the East Coast (Zone 4) to Hawaii or Alaska (Zone 5).  These routes <strong>require</strong> a connection.</p>

<h3>Award Flights to Avoid</h3>
<p>This new award chart isn&#8217;t all good!  There are definitely a handful of redemptions <strong>you want to avoid</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li>First Class award prices aren&#8217;t that great &#8211; more than double the price of coach for shorter routes, and more than triple for longer routes &#8211; like <strong>89,000 Singapore Airlines miles</strong> round-trip from California to Hawaii (no thanks!).  To compare, you could spend <strong>80,000 Alaska Airlines miles</strong> for the same flights.  <strong><br/>
</strong></li>
<li>Like using British Airways Avios points, flights with connections will dramatically increase the price, in most cases making it no longer worth it</li>
<li>Flights <strong>within Alaska</strong> are <strong>much</strong> more expensive than using Alaska Airlines miles or British Airways Avios points</li>
</ul>
<h3>Always Compare to the Price of a Paid Ticket</h3>
<p>If you can find cheap paid fares, it may not be worth using miles.  Especially because taxes and fees can be fairly significant on international flights.</p>
<p>For example, I found a flight from Los Angeles to Mexico City for ~$140, and ~$56 of that is taxes and fees (some of which you&#8217;ll still pay on an award ticket).  So you&#8217;d be spending 12,000 Singapore Airlines miles to save just over $100.  That&#8217;s not worth it.</p>
<p>Likewise, flights within California are often under $100 round-trip, in which case you’re better off buying a ticket than redeeming 7,500 Singapore Airlines miles for that flight.  On the other hand, when prices are high, this could be a good deal.</p>

<h3>Earn More Singapore Airlines Miles</h3>
<p>Singapore Airlines miles are super easy to earn, because they&#8217;re a <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://millionmilesecrets.com/2016/07/31/which-points-transfer-to-which-airlines-handy-chart-for-converting/">transfer partner</a> of all 4 main <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/http://millionmilesecrets.com/2015/05/21/the-trick-to-travel-flexibility-transferable-points/">flexible points</a> programs:</p>
<ul>
<li><a title="Click here!" href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://millionmilesecrets.com/2017/01/09/amex-transfer-partners/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">AMEX Membership Rewards</a></li>
<li><a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://millionmilesecrets.com/2014/05/15/ultimate-rewards-transfer-partners/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Chase Ultimate Rewards</a></li>
<li><a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://millionmilesecrets.com/2016/07/19/how-to-transfer-citi-thankyou-points-to-airlines/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Citi ThankYou</a></li>
<li><a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://millionmilesecrets.com/2013/08/15/starwood-points/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Starwood Preferred Guest</a></li>
</ul>
<p>So even if you don’t think you have Singapore Airlines miles, if you collect points in <strong>any</strong> of the above programs, you can transfer them to Singapore Airlines!</p>
<p>This makes Alaska Airlines award flights far more accessible than they were in the past!</p>
<p>You can also earn Singapore Airlines miles from flying.  They&#8217;re part of the <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/http://www.staralliance.com/en/member-airlines">Star Alliance</a>, so you can <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://millionmilesecrets.com/2015/05/27/dont-waste-miles-flown-on-other-airlines-credit-them-to-your-favorite-frequent-flyer-program/">credit paid flights any Star Alliance member (like United Airlines) to Singapore Airlines</a>.  As well as flights on Alaska Airlines or <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://millionmilesecrets.com/2015/09/09/new-earn-singapore-airlines-miles-on-jetblue-flights-for-big-travel/">JetBlue</a>.</p>
<h3>How to Book Alaska Airlines Flights With Singapore Airlines Miles</h3>
<p>You can <strong>NOT</strong> search for partner flights on the Singapore Airlines website.</p>
<p>So the easiest way to check for available Alaska Airlines award flights is through the <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://www.aa.com/homePage.do">American Airlines website</a>.  Any Alaska Airlines flight that appears on <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://www.aa.com/homePage.do">aa.com</a> <strong>should</strong> be available to book with Singapore Airlines miles.</p>

<h3>Bottom Line</h3>
<p>Using <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/https://millionmilesecrets.com/2017/09/27/this-is-a-big-deal-fly-to-hawaii-with-points-you-probably-already-have/">Singapore Airlines miles to book Alaska Airlines award flights</a> can be an excellent deal on certain routes, like the <strong>West Coast to Hawaii</strong> for <strong>12,000 Singapore Airlines miles</strong> one-way in coach!</p>
<p>But the good deals are limited to <strong>non-stop Alaska Airlines flights only</strong>, because connecting flights are priced as 2 separate segments.  And flights in First Class are often much more expensive than booking with other miles.</p>
<p>That said, it&#8217;s easy to earn Singapore Airlines miles, because they&#8217;re a partner of all <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20180712234742/http://millionmilesecrets.com/2015/05/21/the-trick-to-travel-flexibility-transferable-points/">4 transferable points programs</a>.  So if you&#8217;re in a city with lots of non-stop Alaska Airlines flights, this could be an excellent way to save miles.</p>
<p>Alaska Airlines flights still haven&#8217;t been loaded into Singapore Airlines&#8217; system, but once they are you can call to book.</p>
