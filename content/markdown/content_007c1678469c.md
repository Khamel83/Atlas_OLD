                                            <h3>Constitutional Theory</h3>
                                            <div class="nytg-columns clearfix">
                                                <div class="nytg-leftCol">
                                                    <p>In her testimony during her confirmation for solicitor general, Elena Kagan wrote: &#x201C;I think a judge should try to the greatest extent possible to separate constitutional interpretation from his
                                                        or her own values and beliefs. In order to accomplish this result, the judge should look to constitutional text, history, structure and precedent. Relating these views to the position for which I
                                                        am nominated, I think these kinds of arguments also are most successful in advocacy before the courts in constitutional cases. ... The Constitution generally imposes limitations on government rather
                                                        than establishes affirmative rights and thus has what might be thought of as a libertarian slant. I fully accept this traditional understanding, and if I am confirmed as solicitor general, I would
                                                        expect to make arguments consistent with it.&#x201D;</p>
                                                </div>
                                                <div class="nytg-rightCol">
                                                    <p class="nytg-pullQuote">&#x201C;I think a judge should try to the greatest extent possible to separate constitutional interpretation from his or her own values and beliefs.&quot;</p>
                                                    <p class="nytg-caseAttribution">Solicitor general confirmation hearing, 2009</p>

                                            <div class="nytg-columns clearfix">
                                                <div class="nytg-leftCol">
                                                    <p>In Ms. Kagan&#x2019;s written responses to Senate questions during her confirmation for solicitor general, she disagreed with the view that the courts should take the lead in creating a more just society.</p>
                                                </div>
                                                <div class="nytg-rightCol">
                                                    <p class="nytg-pullQuote">&#x201C;I think it is a great deal better for the elected branches to take the lead in creating a more just society than for courts to do so.&#x201D;</p>

                                            <h3>Questioning a Supreme Court Nominee</h3>
                                            <div class="nytg-columns clearfix">
                                                <div class="nytg-leftCol">
                                                    <p>In her testimony during her confirmation for solicitor general, she wrote: &#x201C;I wrote that I was frustrated by what I called Justice Ginsburg&#x2019;s &#x2018;pincer movement&#x2019; &#x2014; the
                                                        tendency to say that questions were either too specific or too general to be able to answer, with little ground in between. Even at the time I wrote the review, I agreed with Justice Ginsburg that
                                                        a judicial nominee should not forecast how she would decide a particular case; the question that seemed different to me, as noted above, was whether a nominee could answer questions about &#x2018;judicial
                                                        methodology,&#x2019; &#x2018;prior case law,&#x2019; &#x2018;hypothetical cases&#x2019; and &#x2018;general issues.&#x2019; I think I made clear in the review that I would have done the same thing
                                                        as Justice Ginsburg given prevailing conventions and standards. (I asked in the review, &#x2018;Who would have done anything different?&#x2019;) The question I raised in the review was whether those
                                                        conventions and standards were correct. As noted in my answer above, my views on this question have evolved in some ways, but I continue to think the question well worth exploring.&#x201D;</p>
                                                </div>
                                                <div class="nytg-rightCol">
                                                    <p class="nytg-pullQuote">&#x201C;A judicial nominee should not forecast how she would decide a particular case.&#x201D;</p>

                                            <h3>Don&#x2019;t Ask, Don&#x2019;t Tell</h3>
                                            <div class="nytg-columns clearfix">
                                                <div class="nytg-leftCol">
                                                    <p>Ms. Kagan opposes the ban on gays serving openly in the military. As dean of Harvard Law School, she barred military recruiters from using the law school&#x2019;s Office of Career Services. She reversed
                                                        her decision after the government threatened to enforce the Solomon Amendment, which allows the federal government to withhold money from universities that do not extend the same welcome to military
                                                        recruiters as they do to other recruiters. In a message to the school, she wrote, &#x201C;The importance of the military to our society &#x2014; and the extraordinary service that members of the
                                                        military provide to all the rest of us &#x2014; makes this discrimination more, not less, repugnant.&#x201D; However, during her confirmation for solicitor general, she wrote that because the Supreme
                                                        Court has upheld the Solomon Amendment, she would &#x201C;vigorously&#x201D; defend it against constitutional challenge.</p>
                                                </div>
                                                <div class="nytg-rightCol">
                                                    <p class="nytg-pullQuote">&#x201C;I abhor the military&#x2019;s discriminatory recruitment policy. ... This is a profound wrong &#x2014; a moral injustice of the first order.&#x201D;</p>
                                                    <p class="nytg-caseAttribution">E-mail message to the Harvard Law School community, 2003</p>

                                            <div class="nytg-columns clearfix">
                                                <div class="nytg-leftCol">
                                                    <p>During her confirmation hearing for solicitor general, Ms. Kagan agreed with a questioner that someone suspected of helping to finance Al Qaeda should be subject to battlefield law &#x2014; indefinite
                                                        detention without a trial &#x2014; even if he were captured in a place like the Philippines rather than in a physical battle zone.</p>

                                            <h3>Presidential Powers</h3>
                                            <div class="nytg-columns clearfix">
                                                <div class="nytg-leftCol">
                                                    <p>In a 2001 article in The Harvard Law Review that considered the &#x201C;unitary executive&#x201D; theory, Ms. Kagan wrote that such presidential control &#x201C;expanded dramatically during the Clinton
                                                        presidency,&#x201D; a development she largely welcomed. But she said Congress, experts and interest groups should also play a role in informing the executive branch&#x2019;s actions. &#x201C;I do
                                                        not espouse the unitarian position,&#x201D; Ms. Kagan wrote. &#x201C;President Clinton&#x2019;s assertion of directive authority over administration, more than President Reagan&#x2019;s assertion
                                                        of a general supervisory authority, raises serious constitutional questions.&#x201D;</p>
                                                </div>
                                                <div class="nytg-rightCol">
                                                    <p class="nytg-pullQuote">&#x201C;President Clinton&#x2019;s assertion of directive authority over administration ... raises serious constitutional questions.&#x201D;</p>

                                            <h3>The Death Penalty</h3>
                                            <div class="nytg-columns clearfix">
                                                <div class="nytg-leftCol">
                                                    <p>In Ms. Kagan&#x2019;s written responses to Senate questions during her confirmation for solicitor general, said she did not believe that international law prevented federal and state governments from
                                                        broadening the application of the death penalty.</p>
                                                </div>
                                                <div class="nytg-rightCol">
                                                    <p class="nytg-pullQuote">&#x201C;I am fully prepared to argue, consistent with Supreme Court precedents, that the death penalty is constitutional.&#x201D;</p>
                                                    <p class="nytg-caseAttribution">Solicitor general confirmation hearing, 2009</p>

                                            <h3>Warrantless Wiretaps</h3>
                                            <div class="nytg-columns clearfix">
                                                <div class="nytg-leftCol">
                                                    <p>During Ms. Kagan&#x2019;s confirmation for solicitor general, she was asked whether the president has the authority to order wiretaps without a warrant from a court. She cited a three-part analysis established
                                                        by the Supreme Court in a 1952 case, Youngstown Sheet and Tube Company v. Sawyer, which struck down President Harry S. Truman&#x2019;s authority to seize the nation&#x2019;s steel mills in the name
                                                        of national security. (The Justice Department cited the same analysis in 2006 in justifying President George W. Bush&#x2019;s power to order the National Security Agency surveillance program, as
                                                        did Judge Samuel A. Alito Jr. during his Supreme Court confirmation hearings.) The analysis notes that &#x201C;when the President takes measures incompatible with the expressed or implied will of
                                                        Congress,&#x201D; presidential &#x201C;power is at its lowest ebb&#x201D; and these circumstances should be rare.</p>
                                                </div>
                                                <div class="nytg-rightCol">
                                                    <p class="nytg-pullQuote">&#x201C;These occasions are rare and cannot be created or justified merely by a general invocation of the commander-in-chief power.&#x201D;</p>
                                                    <p class="nytg-caseAttribution">Solicitor general confirmation hearing, 2009</p>

                                            <h3>The Second Amendment</h3>
                                            <div class="nytg-columns clearfix">
                                                <div class="nytg-leftCol">
                                                    <p>In repsonse to questions about District of Columbia v. Heller, which struck down parts of the District of Columbia&#x2019;s gun control law and ruled that the Second Amendment protects an individual&apos;s
                                                        right to own guns, Ms. Kagan cited the court&#x2019;s analogy to the First Amendment, writing that it provided &#x201C;strong although not unlimited protection.&#x201D;</p>

                                                        for certain activities. Pregnancy care centers, she thought at the time, would not be able to counsel pregnant teenagers without injecting their religious beliefs. At her hearing, she said presuming
                                                        a religious organization would use money in an impermissible manner was incorrect.</p>
