It spreads from one person to another, either directly or indirectly. The variola virus infects only humans — other animals are unable to catch smallpox.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>There are two types of variola viruses — variola major and variola minor — with the former type being much more severe and deadly.</span></p><h3 class="h3-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="how-is-it-transmitted"><span>How is it transmitted?</span><a class="deep-link" aria-labelledby="how-is-it-transmitted" href="#how-is-it-transmitted"></a></h3><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>People can be infected with the variola virus if they inhale droplets, or come into contact with droplets, containing the virus from someone who is infected with it. Coughing and sneezing, for example, can lead to the virus being spread through droplets.</span><a href="#note-2" class="ref"><sup><span>2</span></sup></a></p><h2 class="h2-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="symptoms"><span>Symptoms</span><a class="deep-link" href="#symptoms"></a></h2><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>The classic symptom of smallpox is the rash — these are pustules covering a patient&#x27;s entire body. After being infected with the variola virus, patients usually have no symptoms for 10–14 days (which is called the incubation period) followed by symptoms like a fever and headache — which resemble a common cold — for 2–3 days. Since these symptoms are not specific to smallpox, it can be very difficult to tell from symptoms alone that they have been infected.</span><a href="#note-3" class="ref"><sup><span>3</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>On average, it then takes 24 hours for the rash to cover the body, and an additional three weeks for them to grow in size, form crusts, and eventually fall off. A surviving patient would be marked for life with depigmented skin and scars in places where pustules had formed.</span></p><h3 class="h3-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="age-profile-of-patients"><span>Age profile of patients</span><a class="deep-link" aria-labelledby="age-profile-of-patients" href="#age-profile-of-patients"></a></h3><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Smallpox could infect people of any age. However, in societies where it was endemic, it mainly affected children. Adults in an endemic population would have been infected with the virus at an earlier stage in their lives already and since the only two possible outcomes of an infection were death or survival and life-long immunity, the virus could only infect and be transmitted by individuals that had never been exposed before: children.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>In 17th century Britain, this led to children not being considered full members of the family until they had survived their smallpox infection.</span><a href="#note-4" class="ref"><sup><span>4</span></sup></a></p><h3 class="h3-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="death"><span>Death</span><a class="deep-link" aria-labelledby="death" href="#death"></a></h3><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Among people known to be infected with the variola major virus, around 30% are estimated to have died. In contrast, an infection of the variola </span><em><span>minor</span></em><span> virus was milder — it would lead to death in less than one percent of cases.</span><a href="#note-5" class="ref"><sup><span>5</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>The specific way a smallpox infection would lead to a patient&#x27;s death remains unclear.</span><a href="#note-2" class="ref"><sup><span>2</span></sup></a><span> The</span><em><span> Oxford Textbook of Medicine</span></em><span> notes that people infected may develop symptoms of prostration (total exhaustion), toxemia (toxins in the blood), and hypotension (low blood pressure), which all contribute, but the mechanism by which smallpox causes such symptoms are not clear.</span><a href="#note-6" class="ref"><sup><span>6</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Among those who died from smallpox, death usually occurred within 10 to 16 days of the beginning of their symptoms.</span><a href="#note-7" class="ref"><sup><span>7</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Patients who successfully fought off the infection and survived, would likely never catch smallpox again.</span><a href="#note-8" class="ref"><sup><span>8</span></sup></a><span> They would develop long-lasting immunity to the disease, through the production of memory cells that could quickly detect and activate killer cells against variola viruses that they encountered in the future.</span></p><h3 aria-labelledby="lack-of-treatment" href="#lack-of-treatment"></a></h3><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>While smallpox was endemic, there was never a treatment for it. Once a person was infected, it wasn’t possible to treat them — one could only helplessly let the disease run its course and hope they survived.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Modern antiviral drugs might now allow treatment of the disease if the disease still existed — the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)</span><a href="#note-9" class="ref"><sup><span>9</span></sup></a><span> now lists three antiviral drugs as potential treatment options.</span></p><h2 class="h2-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="origins-of-smallpox"><span>Origins of smallpox</span><a class="deep-link" href="#origins-of-smallpox"></a></h2><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>The origin of the variola virus and the time since when it infected humans are uncertain.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Classic symptoms of smallpox — the skin eruptions and pustules — have been identified in mummified individuals dating back to 1100–1500 BCE in Egypt.</span><a href="#note-10" class="ref"><sup><span>10</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>One of these was Pharaoh Ramses V who died in 1157 BCE. span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>During the following two years, WHO teams searched the African continent for further smallpox cases among people with This predated the WHO&#x27;s Intensified Smallpox Eradication Program, which was launched in 1966.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Countries across Sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America and Asia eliminated smallpox several decades later, in the 1960s and 1970s.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>In May 1980, the World Health Assembly, the governing body of the World Health Organization, officially certified the global elimination of smallpox, the first-ever eradication of a disease in human history.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>It further &quot;recommended that all countries cease vaccination, all laboratories should destroy their remaining stocks or transfer them&quot; to one of two certified high-security laboratories. eradication achieved?</span><a class="deep-link" href="#how-was-global-decline-eradication-achieved"></a></h1><h2 class="h2-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="variolation"><span>Variolation</span><a class="deep-link" href="#variolation"></a></h2><h3 class="h3-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="discovery-of-variolation"><span>Discovery of variolation</span><a class="deep-link" aria-labelledby="discovery-of-variolation" href="#discovery-of-variolation"></a></h3><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Progress against smallpox began with variolation, which refers to deliberately infecting people with weakened or milder versions of the smallpox virus. It is sometimes also inoculation.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Before the year 1000, people in India and China had already observed that children who survived smallpox infections were protected against any future outbreaks of the disease. As a consequence, they developed a procedure that involved the nasal inhalation of dried smallpox scabs by three-year-olds.</span><a href="#note-2" class="ref"><sup><span>2</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Another commonly practiced technique, whose origins are unknown, involved injecting the liquid from pustules of a smallpox patient into a healthy person, underneath their skin. This would usually result in a milder infection of smallpox, and the person would then develop immunity against the disease.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Both practices became known as variolation (inoculation) techniques.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>However, there were disadvantages of variolation. One was that, during the course of the mild infection, the person became a carrier of the disease and could infect other people. Additionally, it was difficult to control the severity of the infection — it could sometimes develop into a full-blown smallpox case that could lead to death.</span><a href="#note-23" class="ref"><sup><span>23</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>This meant that the practice </span><em><span>usually</span></em><span> reduced the severity of an infection and the likelihood of deaths, but was risky and couldn’t eliminate the virus.</span></p><h3 class="h3-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="institutionalized-variolation"><span>Institutionalized variolation</span><a class="deep-link" aria-labelledby="institutionalized-variolation" href="#institutionalized-variolation"></a></h3><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>A British ambassador&#x27;s wife, Lady Mary Wortley Montague (1689–1762) was the force that pushed for government-mandated variolation in England.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>She had suffered a smallpox infection when she was younger and had lost her younger brother to the disease at the age of 26. She first learned about variolation when she arrived in 1717 in Istanbul, where variolation was commonly practiced. She later had the embassy inoculate her two children.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>News about the practice spread among the royal family. After following trials, Charles Maitland, a physician, successfully inoculated the two daughters of the Princess of Wales with variolation in 1722. Thereafter, variolation became a common practice in Great Britain and became known in other European countries.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>It became an even more established practice when the French King Louis XV died of smallpox in May of 1776 and his successor and grandson Louis XVI was inoculated with the variola virus one month later.</span></p><h2 class="h2-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="vaccine-against-smallpox"><span>Vaccine against smallpox</span><a class="deep-link" href="#vaccine-against-smallpox"></a></h2><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>At the end of the 18th century, British surgeon and physician Edward Jenner (1749-1823) pioneered the first-ever vaccination against an infectious disease.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>He had been inoculated through variolation at the age of 8, and later as a surgeon, variolation was part of his work.</span><a href="#note-24" class="ref"><sup><span>24</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>He also observed that people who had suffered from </span><em><span>cowpox</span></em><span> would subsequently have a very mild, if at all visible, reaction to the smallpox variolation. This was because the cowpox and variola viruses were members of the same virus family, called orthopox viruses, and provided immunity against each other, which he had unknowingly observed.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>He hypothesized that variolation using the cowpox virus would protect children against smallpox as well. Since cowpox infections were much milder and never fatal, this would eliminate the problem of variolated children being carriers of smallpox and sometimes dying of the virus developing into a full-blown infection. In addition, it could reduce the pool of humans that the variola virus needed to survive, and it brought elimination, and eventually eradication, of smallpox into the realm of possibility.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>In May 1796, Jenner inoculated a boy with cowpox, and then a few months later, with the smallpox virus. When the boy did not develop any smallpox symptoms in response to being variolated, his hypothesis that cowpox offered protection from smallpox was confirmed, motivating his further research trials.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Initially, Jenner faced major barriers to spreading the word about his discovery. When he submitted a paper outlining his findings to the journal </span><em><span>Philosophical Transactions</span></em><span> edited by the Royal Society, it was rejected. They even advised him not to pursue his ideas any further, pointing to the detrimental impact on his career and reputation. Undeterred, he published his work with an increased number of trials at his own expense two years later, in 1798. He also went on to convince colleagues and supply them with vaccines in other British cities of his new procedure that became known as vaccination (derived from the Latin word for cow, </span><em><span>Vacca</span></em><span>).</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>By 1802, the British Parliament did acknowledge his important contribution and awarded him £30,000. Meanwhile, vaccination had spread to most of Europe and New England.</span><a href="#note-25" class="ref"><sup><span>25</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>His 1798 publication </span><em><span>Inquiry into the Variolae vaccinae known as the Cow Pox </span></em><span>had been translated into German, French, Spanish, Dutch, Italian, and Latin within three years. US President Thomas Jefferson was an important figure in the widespread application of vaccination throughout the United States, and in 1806, he thanked Edward Jenner in a letter for his discovery and famously predicted &quot;Future generations will know by history only that the loathsome smallpox existed and by you has been extirpated.&quot;</span><a href="#note-26" class="ref"><sup><span>26</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>The dramatic decline in smallpox fatalities in response to Jenner&#x27;s vaccine can be traced in the chart, which shows the number of deaths due to smallpox as a share of all deaths in London from 1629 to 1902. Before the introduction of a smallpox vaccine in 1796, around 7% of all deaths annually were caused by smallpox. Following the introduction of the vaccine, a clear decline in smallpox deaths is visible.</span></p><div class="article-block__chart col-start-4 span-cols-8 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2 full-width-on-mobile"><div class="GrapherWithFallback full-width-on-mobile"><figure class="grapherPreview GrapherWithFallback__fallback"><picture><source id="grapher-preview-source" srcSet="https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/deaths-from-smallpox-in-london.png?imWidth=850 850w, https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/deaths-from-smallpox-in-london.png?imWidth=1700 1700w" sizes="(max-width: 850px) 100vw, 850px"/><img class="GrapherImage" src="https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/deaths-from-smallpox-in-london.png" width="850" height="600" loading="lazy"/></picture></figure></div></div><h2 class="h2-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="smallpox-eradication-program"><span>Smallpox Eradication Program</span><a class="deep-link" href="#smallpox-eradication-program"></a></h2><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>It was only with the establishment of the World Health Organization (WHO) that international quality standards for the production of smallpox vaccines were introduced. This shifted the fight against smallpox from a national to an international agenda. It was also the first time that global data collection on the prevalence of smallpox was undertaken.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>By 1959, the World Health Assembly, the governing body of the World Health Organization (WHO), had passed a resolution to eradicate smallpox globally. But it was not until 1966, that funding was provided to the &quot;Intensified Smallpox Eradication Program&quot; to increase efforts for smallpox eradication.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>By 1966, the number of infections of smallpox had already substantially been reduced by national governments&#x27; efforts. Nonetheless, skepticism about the feasibility of eradication prevailed, as some previous efforts to eradicate diseases — such as malaria and yaws — had failed. Furthermore, funding for the Intensified Smallpox Eradication Programme was insufficient to meet global needs, which resulted in vaccine shortages.</span><a href="#note-27" class="ref"><sup><span>27</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>In addition, continued globalization and the growth of international air travel resulted in the continual re-introduction of the disease into countries that had previously managed to eliminate smallpox.</span></p><h2 class="h2-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="overcoming-the-last-mile-problem-ring-vaccination"><span>Overcoming the last mile problem: ring vaccination</span><a class="deep-link" href="#overcoming-the-last-mile-problem-ring-vaccination"></a></h2><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Smallpox&#x27;s eradication was greatly spurred by making use of the fact that smallpox transmission occurs via air droplets.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Initially, the WHO had pursued a strategy of mass vaccination which attempted to vaccinate as many people as possible, hoping that herd immunity (explained in our </span><a href="https://ourworldindata.org/vaccination#how-vaccines-work-herd-immunity-and-reasons-for-caring-about-broad-vaccination-coverage" class="span-link" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span>vaccine topic page</span></a><span>) would protect the whole population. Soon, however, vaccination efforts were targeted locally around smallpox cases, because smallpox was transmitted by sick patients&#x27; air droplets. This practice is known as “</span><strong><span>ring vaccination</span></strong><span>”.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>People who had been in direct contact with a smallpox patient over the last two weeks were quarantined and vaccinated. The downside of such an approach was that the virus could spread easily if it was re-introduced from overseas. This was the case in Bangladesh, for example, which had previously eliminated smallpox until 1972 when it was brought back from across its border with India.</span><a href="#note-28" class="ref"><sup><span>28</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Despite the risk of re-introductions, ring vaccination greatly reduced the cost of the eradication campaign. The number of administered vaccines declined and smallpox was increasingly brought under control.</span><a href="#note-2" class="ref"><sup><span>2</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>One of the last strongholds of the variola virus was India. While 58% of global reported smallpox cases were reported in India in 1973, this increased to 86% in 1974.</span><a href="#note-29" class="ref"><sup><span>29</span></sup></a><span> Major pushes in vaccination campaigns, however, successfully drove down the number of infections to zero in India in 1976.</span></p><h1 class="h1-semibold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="correlates-consequences"><span>Correlates &amp; consequences</span><a class="deep-link" href="#correlates-consequences"></a></h1><h2 class="h2-bold article-block__heading col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2" id="impact-on-life-expectancy"><span>Impact on life expectancy</span><a class="deep-link" href="#impact-on-life-expectancy"></a></h2><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Did smallpox variolation and vaccination against smallpox have a notable impact on life expectancy?</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Many have claimed that </span><em><span>inoculation</span></em><span> against smallpox was one of the first measures that had a positive effect on </span><a href="https://ourworldindata.org/life-expectancy" class="span-link" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span>life expectancy</span></a><span>. Angus Deaton (2013) makes this claim based on a book by Razzell (1977), which reviews existing birth, baptism and burial records in various counties of 18th century Britain.</span><a href="#note-30" class="ref"><sup><span>30</span></sup></a><span> </span><a href="#note-31" class="ref"><sup><span>31</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>It is hard to prove such claims because of the limited amount of high-quality data on both life expectancy and smallpox cases or deaths before the smallpox vaccine was developed in 1796 and adopted across countries.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>The high case fatality rate of approximately 30% (of the variola major virus) meant that smallpox shortened the lives of many. Since the virus </span><a href="https://ourworldindata.org/smallpox#smallpox-endemic-characterisation" class="span-link" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span>predominantly affected children</span></a><span>, smallpox deaths are likely to have had a disproportionate impact on average life expectancy.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>The chart below shows Sweden&#x27;s life expectancy versus deaths from smallpox from 1774 to 1900.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Large smallpox outbreaks were documented in the years 1779, 1784, 1789, 1795 and 1800. These years coincide with sharp declines in life expectancy, implying that smallpox endemicity did have a substantial effect on life expectancy.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Furthermore, the available data show dramatic declines in mortality in </span><a href="https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/deaths-from-smallpox-in-london" class="span-link"><span>London</span></a><span> as well as in Sweden (below) from 1800 onwards, after the vaccine was developed.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Jenner&#x27;s vaccine appears to have had a notable impact on increased life expectancy but this, to our knowledge, has not been studied in significant detail.</span><a href="#note-32" class="ref"><sup><span>32</span></sup></a></p><div href="#costs-of-smallpox-and-its-eradication"></a></h2><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>By the time the World Health Organization launched the Intensified Smallpox Eradication Program in 1967 many countries, most of them high income countries, had already eliminated smallpox. Therefore, the total cost of eradicating smallpox is very challenging to estimate — as individual country programs reach back to before records of public health expenditure existed.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Nonetheless, the Intensified Smallpox Eradication Program has been estimated to have cost $300 million in total from 1967 to 1977</span><a href="#note-33" class="ref"><sup><span>33</span></sup></a><span> with one-third of the funding provided by international donors and the remaining two-thirds financed by endemic country governments.</span><a href="#note-34" class="ref"><sup><span>34</span></sup></a></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Beyond direct program costs, smallpox incurred much higher indirect costs in the form of foregone economic performance.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>It has been estimated that smallpox cost low-to-middle href="#note-35" class="ref"><sup><span>35</span></sup></a><span> Industrialized countries incurred much lower costs: $350 million in 1968, which included vaccination programs and absence from work col-sm-start-2"><span>Overall, the Center for Global Development</span><a href="#note-37" class="ref"><sup><span>37</span></sup></a><span> estimated that direct and indirect costs of smallpox cost the world approximately $1.35 billion in the late 1960s.</span></p><p class="article-block__text col-start-5 span-cols-6 col-md-start-3 span-md-cols-10 span-sm-cols-12 col-sm-start-2"><span>Following its eradication, countries now do not have to spend money on vaccine development and administration.
