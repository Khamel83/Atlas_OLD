<p class="caption">Bobby Hosea decries head-first tackling.                            </p>

<div class="articleBody">
 <p>
&ldquo;Dip &rsquo;n&rsquo; rip, baby! Dip &rsquo;n&rsquo; rip!&rdquo; the coach said as players ducked under five-foot limbo sticks and exploded out the other side.        </p><p>
&ldquo;Head up! No! Head up!&rdquo;        </p><p>
&ldquo;Not chest up, chest out! Yeah!&rdquo;        </p><p>
Bobby Hosea, a 55-year-old former defensive back and longtime bit actor, has a singular passion: teaching young football players how to <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120720102048/http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GdKM7FnSSEM&amp;feature=player_embedded" title="Hoseaâs instruction video on USA Football site">protect their heads while tackling</a>. He has watched too many end up in wheelchairs, even coffins. He sees <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120720102048/http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/organizations/n/national_football_league/index.html?inline=nyt-org" title="More articles about the National Football League." class="meta-org">N.F.L.</a> defenders recklessly diving helmet-first and claiming it is too late to change. He hears youth coaches exhorting tacklers to &ldquo;lay a hat on him,&rdquo; a maneuver so neck-crushingly dangerous it could well be called Rushing Roulette.        </p><p>
So Hosea runs <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120720102048/http://www.dipnripsticks.com/" title="Hoseaâs camp Web site">camps that focus on one skill</a> &mdash; tackling with your head up instead of down, and away from contact &mdash; and gives individual instruction to players in and around Los Angeles. As football careens through its dark cloud of <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120720102048/http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/subjects/f/football/head_injuries/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier" title="More articles about the head injuries in football." class="meta-classifier">head injuries</a>, Hosea sees himself as saving more than the players&rsquo; ability to walk and think. He sees it as saving the sport, one youngster at a time.        </p><p>
USA Football, the governing body of Pop Warner and other leagues for players ages 6 to 14, recently hired Hosea as its tackling consultant and placed<a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120720102048/http://www.usafootball.com/pages/put-pride-aside/" title="USA Football Web site"> videos of his technique</a> on its Web site.        </p><p>
&ldquo;When a kid gets paralyzed or dies, it&rsquo;s not an accident &mdash; the injuries happen because people never teach kids how to tackle the right way,&rdquo; Hosea told about 20 rapt campers before a session this month. &ldquo;Everyone&rsquo;s talking about head injury awareness, awareness, awareness. What are you going to do about it? It drives me absolutely crazy. It&rsquo;s time for this to stop!&rdquo;        </p><p>
To lighten things up, Hosea could have amused the youngsters by reading the official definition of tackling, codified at Rule 3, Section 34 of the N.F.L. Rulebook: &ldquo;The use of hands or arms by a defensive player in his attempt to hold a runner or throw him to the ground.&rdquo; This quaint approach has evolved into the more gratifying and theatrical act of launching headfirst into a ball carrier&rsquo;s gut, chest or helmet.        </p><p>
A result has been a <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120720102048/http://www.unc.edu/depts/nccsi/" title="National Center for Catastrophic Injury Research">steady rise in concussions</a> &mdash; estimated at more than 500,000 each season among the 4.4 million children who play tackle football &mdash; as well as more rare but catastrophic injuries where vertebrae are crushed or fractured, leaving the player paralyzed.        </p><p>
&ldquo;A lot of youth coaches have no idea how to teach tackling &mdash; they say to just put a helmet in the numbers or light the other guy up,&rdquo; said Jeff Leets, whose seventh-grade son, Zack, is a defensive end and devoted Hosea pupil from nearby Torrance. &ldquo;They have the caveman element and don&rsquo;t want to be told their way is wrong or that their way is unsafe. Or they simply don&rsquo;t know. It&rsquo;s sad &mdash; you&rsquo;ve got babies in your hands, man.&rdquo;        </p><p>
On this Saturday, those babies ranged from a 9-year-old who weighed 70 pounds to a beefy high school senior eyeing junior-college ball. The players did not know Hosea from his playing days at <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120720102048/http://topics.nytimes.com/topics/reference/timestopics/organizations/u/university_of_california/index.html?inline=nyt-org" title="More articles about the University of California." class="meta-org">U.C.L.A.</a>, in the Canadian Football League or in the United States Football League, nor did they recognize him from <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120720102048/http://www.imdb.com/name/nm0395914/" title="Hoseaâs acting credits">recent parts on &ldquo;24&rdquo; or &ldquo;Bones.&rdquo;</a> To them,  he is the tackling guy &mdash; equal parts coach, pal and drill sergeant.        </p><p>
Hosea takes a tackler&rsquo;s most instinctual act &mdash; to dive toward a runner, head down and arms extended &mdash; and rebuilds it from the turf up. He keeps knees bent, backside out and chest up, bending the spine and forcing the chin and eyes up. Arms remain at the side until just before impact, when the hips and shoulders thrust up into the opponent, only then swinging forward to wrap up the runner and wrestle him down.        </p><p>
Hosea ran drills as unconventional as his method. The players lined up on their knees 10 at a time and flopped forward onto pads with their arms clasped behind their backs, looking like flying fish. In midair, they must call out the number of fingers a coach raises &mdash; to prove that their chins are up and eyes are alert. Elsewhere, they must run full speed under horizontal bars only 52 and 60 inches off the ground &mdash; Hosea&rsquo;s so-called Dip-&rsquo;n&rsquo;-Rip sticks &mdash; before hitting tackling dummies to ensure that they stay low enough with proper form.        </p><p>
Any dropping of the head resulted not just in dozens of push-ups, but also in spirited hooting from fellow students.        </p><p>
&ldquo;It gives me more confidence on the field &mdash; I feel like I&rsquo;m not going to hurt myself,&rdquo; said Michael Wilson, an eighth grader from Long Beach. &ldquo;Before, I didn&rsquo;t know what I was doing. When I was first taught, all the coach said was to put my head on the ball and knock it out.&rdquo;        </p><div id="pageLinks"><ul id="pageNumbers"><li> 1 </li><li> <a onclick="s_code_linktrack('Article-MultiPagePageNum2');" title="Page 2" href="/web/20120720102048/http://www.nytimes.com/2010/12/26/sports/football/26tackling.html?pagewanted=2&amp;_r=1">2</a> </li></ul><a class="next" onclick="s_code_linktrack('Article-MultiPage-Next');" title="Next Page" href="/web/20120720102048/http://www.nytimes.com/2010/12/26/sports/football/26tackling.html?pagewanted=2&amp;_r=1">Next Page &#x00bb;</a></div><nyt_correction_bottom>	<div class="articleCorrection">
