his third foul of the game. That's when the team knew its 7-foot passing savant was in trouble.</p><p>More than 5,000 miles away, Dejan Milojevic, Jokic's beloved Serbian coach, had already concluded that -- well in advance of the referee's whistle. Milojevic had tracked his former pupil carefully on his big-screen television as Jokic went through pregame warm-ups and noted, with some dismay, that the big man's mannerisms were uncharacteristically jittery.</p><p>"He was nervous," Milojevic said. "I can tell what he is thinking. I watched these guys several hours a day for many, many years. When I see that look, I know what it means."</p><aside class="inline float-r inline-track"><h2 class="img-title"><a href="https://www.espn.com/nba/story/_/id/29647539/nba-playoffs-2020-schedules-news-odds-latest-updates">2020 NBA Finals</a></h2><img data-lazyload="true" data-src="https://a.espncdn.com/photo/2020/1012/r759368_608x342_16-9.jpg" class="round lazyload"><p>The Los Angeles Lakers defeated the Miami Heat 4-2 in the 2020 NBA Finals to claim their 17th championship. </aside><p>It meant a <a href="/nba/story/_/id/29917078/nba-playoffs-dwight-howard-gives-lakers-game-1-jolt">Game 1 drubbing</a> in which Jokic logged just 25 foul-plagued minutes, followed by a heartbreaking Game 2 loss in which <a href="https://www.espn.com/nba/player/_/id/6583/anthony-davis">Anthony Davis</a> snuffed the Nuggets' upset intentions with <a href="/nba/story/_/id/29930520/nba-playoffs-biggest-takeaways-game-2-nuggets-lakers">a clutch jumper at the buzzer</a>. Although there was much hand-wringing over the miscommunication that gave Davis a clean look, Denver's woeful box-out skills and the missed free throws, Milojevic was buoyed by what was trending in the final minutes.</p><p>Jokic scored the final 11 points of the game for his team, and if not for Davis' game winner, the unorthodox center would have been the cause celebre du jour of the bubble.</p><p>"I know people are mad they lost," Milojevic said, "but they proved they can play with these Lakers. Their defense was excellent. They should have much confidence."</p><p>Milojevic coached Jokic from 2012 to '15 in Belgrade for Mega Basket (now Mega Soccerbet) and was a major influence in honing the center's skills so he could fulfill his NBA dream. Although the coach was certain that Jokic had the talent to succeed, there were many who were skeptical that he possessed the personal discipline to make it.</p><p>"I heard too much of, 'No, Jokic, he can't do it,'" Milojevic said. "I only started fighting if I was defending someone else," he said.</p><p>Milojevic was known for his exceptional footwork, a skill that he passed along to Jokic. "I showed him some things," his coach said, "and after a week, he had already passed me in ability."</p><p>Jokic's unusual skill set is a testament to his creativity. Yet Tim Connelly, Denver's head of basketball operations, insists that Milojevic's fingerprints are all over Jokic's game.</p><p>"I've spent so much time in countless gyms all over the world, but Dejan quickly jumps out as completely different," Connelly said. He's teaching his guys to become unique players."</p><p>Jokic exhibited patience on the court from the time he picked up a basketball, and he preferred to share the ball rather than dominate it. </aside><p>Although Milojevic admits that he had no idea that Jokic would develop into an All-NBA talent, he says he always believed that Jokic had a chance because of his willingness to try nearly anything on the basketball court. Naturally, that came with some bouts of indigestion. Early in his career, Jokic threw no-look cross-court bullets and tried to thread the needle with nearly impossible bounce passes, complete with backspin. His pass offerings came from between the legs, behind the back and even backward over his head -- a marvelous version of which was unveiled in the Nuggets' Game 7 win over the Clippers in the Western Conference semifinals.</p><p>Shortly after the Nuggets drafted Jokic, Connelly expressed to the big man his disappointment that Jokic was being so conservative. "I told Nikola that I was so transfixed by passing," Connelly said. "I said, 'So why are you just reversing the ball like everyone else?' He said, 'In Mega, I throw the ball off my head, and I still play. Here, it's a turnover, and I sit.'"</p><p>That early approach, Milojevic confirms, was completely accurate. "He made a lot of mistakes when he was younger," he said. "I had to swallow a lot of things. He was throwing all these ridiculous passes -- and it drove me crazy.</p><p>"But I saw something wonderful, so I didn't want to focus his mind on mistakes. I let these things go so he could grow and learn from them.</p><p>"And now he stands out.
