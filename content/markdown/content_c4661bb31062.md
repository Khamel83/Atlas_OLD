<div class="articleBody">
 <p>
ABOUT the only thing tanking faster than consumer confidence and the Greek economy would be the global compassion index, if such a measure existed.        </p><p>
Consider just a few recent news items: Americans are balking at extending unemployment benefits, and even disaster relief was in doubt for a time last week in another of Washington&rsquo;s budget skirmishes; Europeans are cutting payments to pensioners; and &ldquo;there&rsquo;s no mood for intervention&rdquo; to avert <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120103000834/http://www.nytimes.com/2011/09/16/world/africa/famine-hits-somalia-in-world-less-likely-to-intervene.htm">famine in Somalia</a>, according to one diplomat.        </p><p>
At a recent Republican presidential debate, the audience erupted into <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120103000834/http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VjKTHHTIXOU">cheers</a> upon hearing Texas&rsquo;s nation-leading rate of executions.        </p><p>
Behind such sentiments lie genuine concerns, be they for law and order or personal responsibility, not to mention limited resources and a struggling economy. After all, a lowering tide grounds a lot of rescue boats, literally and psychologically.        </p><p>
Yet psychologists and primatologists have been arguing for years that compassion is an evolved instinct, rooted in the brain&rsquo;s circuitry. In a new book, <a title="Steven Pinker book" href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120103000834/http://www.amazon.com/Better-Angels-Our-Nature-Violence/dp/0670022950">&ldquo;The Better Angels of Our Nature,&rdquo;</a> the psychologist <a title="Times articles about Steven Pinker" href="http://web.archive.org/web/20120103000834/http://topics.nytimes.com/topics/reference/timestopics/people/p/steven_pinker/index.html?scp=1-spot&amp;sq=steven%20pinker&amp;st=cse">Steven Pinker</a> calls empathy &ldquo;the latest fashion in human nature.&rdquo; Chimpanzees show evidence of compassion, as do some monkeys; even mice seem to feel the pain of close peers. But if current trends continue, rats might become a more appropriate subject of study.        </p><p>
Are people today &mdash; are societies &mdash; really becoming somehow more callous?        </p><p>
The answer is no, of course not &mdash; at least not in any fundamental sense. But compassion is a limited resource, a system rooted in cognitive networks that tire and need refueling. And it&rsquo;s not always rational.        </p><p>
Those in the so-called helping professions know as much about the limits of empathy as they do about its merits. Studies of oncology nurses, trauma workers and even marriage counselors, among others, have documented a common &ldquo;compassion fatigue&rdquo; that seems directly related to the amount of emotion shared. &ldquo;In particular, listening to people who are suffering and not being able to do enough for them puts a tremendous weight&rdquo; on caregivers, said Dr. Charles Figley, a psychologist at Tulane University.        </p><p>
In just the past decade, he said, professional organizations have begun to give guidelines to offset fatigue, like urging counselors to take time off, seek support from colleagues, even engage in therapy themselves.        </p><p>
Therapists quickly learn to recognize the signs.        </p><p>
Fatigue often results &ldquo;when you&rsquo;re seeing the same problems repeatedly, when they&rsquo;re chronic, and when the outcomes are not good,&rdquo; said Bret A. Moore, a former Army psychologist and co-author of &ldquo;Wheels Down: Adjusting to Life After Deployment.&rdquo; &ldquo;One sign that you&rsquo;re there is that you start hoping your appointments cancel.&rdquo;        </p><p>
The public has a similar reaction to mass joblessness and starving countries alike: the problems sap the imagination in part simply because they are daunting and have not responded well to previous efforts. We have already pumped billions into each, with little visible effect. If only they would cancel their next emergency.        </p><p>
Still, even when rested and ready, people generally find it far harder to extend empathetic concern to a nation than to a neighbor. The helping instinct evolved to protect the household, the clan. Some psychologists make a distinction between moral intuition, the physical horror at seeing someone hit by a car or the tears of a parent whose son is kidnapped; and moral reason, the more intellectual process of grasping larger tragedies, like floods and famine.        </p><p>
THE former is a stronger, more emotionally visceral reaction, which is why people often show far more compassion for an individual victim than for a dozen, or 100, or an entire region.        </p><p>
&ldquo;I sometimes make the analogy to vision,&rdquo; said Paul Slovic, a psychologist at the University of Oregon and president of a nonprofit company called Decision Research. &ldquo;We have this sophisticated capability that&rsquo;s rational and works well most of the time; but it can also be misleading&rdquo; &mdash; in the same way the eye is fooled by optical illusions.        </p><p>
In a recent study, Dr. Slovic and two colleagues, Daniel Vastfjall and Ellen Peters, tested this relationship directly.
