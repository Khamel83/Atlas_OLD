<div class="article_detail_body" style="font-size: 13px"><i></i></div><br/><div class="article_detail_body">
<p>The opponents of any regulation or restraint of any sort on campaign spending also include the three Republican commissioners of the FEC. Their leader, Donald S. McGahn, recently wrote self-righteously that he is free to ignore acts of Congress because, &ldquo;As an FEC Commissioner, I am bound by my oath to follow controlling precedent of the Supreme Court.&rdquo; But McGahn and his Stepford colleagues have consistently ignored the 8-1 precedent of the Court and moved to dilute or block any meaningful disclosure.</p>
<p>What to make of the turn away from support for disclosure? I can draw only one conclusion: The earlier support for disclosure was a fa&ccedil;ade. McConnell, McGahn, and the <i>Journal</i>, among others,don&rsquo;t actually want transparency or any regulation. At base, they are for a wholly unregulated, Gilded Age-style campaign system where big and secret money rules.</p>
<p>The motives and potential actions of the FCC, SEC, and White House remain under intense assault. Republican Representative Greg Walden, chairman of the House Telecommunications subcommittee, threatened to &ldquo;go nuclear&rdquo; if the FCC even tried to implement disclosure of political ad sponsors by TV stations, and McConnell and his allies are talking lawsuits and retaliation if the White House moves on its executive order. On the flip side, however, there is momentum to keep the push for disclosure alive. Notably, <a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20130119200358/http://blogs.wsj.com/washwire/2011/04/21/van-hollen-sues-fec-over-secret-donors/"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Democratic Representative Chris Van Hollen has sued the FEC</span></a> to require disclosure of donors.</p>
<p>The key now is for theWhite House and the regulatory agencies to ignore the threats from Congress and elsewhere, and move ahead with their efforts. After all, the sentiments of the 92 percent of the public that favors transparency, and the eight justices on the Supreme Court that have endorsed it, should outweigh the objections of the reborn anti-disclosure cabal.</p>
