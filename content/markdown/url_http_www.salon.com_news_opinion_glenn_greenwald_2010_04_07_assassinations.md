He also said there are criteria that must be met to authorize the killing of a U.S. citizen that include &#8220;whether that American is involved in a group that is trying to attack us, whether that American is a threat to other Americans. Those are the factors involved.&#8221; Although&#160;Blair emphasized that it requires &#8220;special permission&#8221; before an American citizen can be placed on the assassination list, consider from whom that &#8220;permission&#8221; is obtained:&#160;&#160;the President, or someone else under his authority within the Executive Branch.&#160; There are no outside checks or limits at all on how these &#8220;factors&#8221;&#160;are weighed.&#160; In last week&#8217;s post, I&#160;wrote about all the reasons why it&#8217;s so dangerous &#8212; as well as both legally and Consitutionally dubious &#8212; to allow the President to kill American citizens not on an active battlefield during combat, but while they are sleeping, sitting with their families in their home, walking on the street, etc.&#160; That&#8217;s basically giving the President the power to impose death sentences on his own citizens without any charges or trial. &#160;Who could possibly support that? But even if you&#8217;re someone who does want the&#160;President to have the power to order American citizens killed without a trial by decreeing that they are Terrorists (and it&#8217;s worth remembering that if you advocate that power, it&#8217;s going to be vested in all Presidents, not just the ones who are as Nice, Good, Kind-Hearted and Trustworthy as Barack Obama), shouldn&#8217;t there at least be some judicial approval required?&#160;&#160;Do we really want the President to be able to make this decision unilaterally and without outside checks?&#160; Remember when many Democrats were horrified (or at least when they purported to be)&#160;at the idea that Bush was merely eavesdropping on American citizens without judicial approval? &#160;Shouldn&#8217;t we be at least as concerned about the&#160;President&#8217;s being able to assassinate Americans without judicial oversight? &#160;That seems much more Draconian to me.&#160; It would be perverse in the extreme, but wouldn&#8217;t it be preferable to at least require the&#160;President to demonstrate to a court that probable cause exists to warrant the assassination of an American citizen before the&#160;President should be allowed to order it?&#160; That would basically mean that courts would issue &#8220;assassination warrants&#8221;&#160;or &#8220;murder warrants&#8221; &#8212; a repugnant idea given that they&#8217;re tantamount to imposing the death sentence without a trial &#8212; but isn&#8217;t that minimal safeguard preferable to allowing the President unchecked authority to do it on his own, the very power he has now claimed for himself? &#160;And if the Fifth Amendment&#8217;s explicit guarantee &#8212; that one shall not be deprived of life without due process &#8212; does not prohibit the U.S. Government from assassinating you without any process, what exactly does it prohibit?&#160; Noting Scott Brown&#8217;s campaign to deny accused Terrorists access to lawyers and a real trial, Adam&#160;Serwer wrote :&#160;&#160; This is the new normal for Republicans: You can be denied rights not through due process of law but merely based on the nature of the crime you are suspected of committing. That&#8217;s absolutely true, but that also perfectly describes this assassination program &#8212; as well as a whole host of other now-Democratic policies, from indefinite detention to denial of civilian trials. *&#160;*&#160;*&#160;*&#160;* The severe dangers of vesting assassination powers in the&#160;President are so glaring that even GOP&#160;Rep. Pete Hoekstra is able to see them&#160;(at least he is now that there&#8217;s a Democratic&#160;President).&#160;&#160;At yesterday&#8217;s hearing, Hoekstra asked Adm. Blair about the threat that the&#160;President might order Americans killed due to their Constitutionally protected political speech rather than because they were actually engaged in Terrorism. &#160;This concern is not an abstract one.&#160; The current controversy has been triggered by the Obama administration&#8217;s attempt to kill U.S. citizen Anwar al-Awlaki in Yemen.&#160; But al-Awlaki has not been accused (let alone convicted) of trying to attack Americans. &#160;Instead, he&#8217;s accused of being a so-called &#8220;radical cleric&#8221;&#160;who supports Al Qaeda and now provides &#8220;encouragement&#8221; to others to engage in attacks &#8211;&#160; a charge al-Awlaki&#8217;s family vehemently denies (al-Awlaki himself is in hiding due to fear that his own Government will assassinate him). The question of where First Amendment-protected radical advocacy ends and criminality begins is exactly the sort of question with which courts have long grappled.&#160; In the 1969 case of Brandenburg v. Ohio , the&#160;Supreme&#160;Court unanimously reversed a criminal conviction of a Ku&#160;Klux Klan leader who &#8212; surrounded by hooded indivduals holding weapons &#8212; gave a speech threatening&#160;&#8221;revengeance&#8221;&#160;against any government official who &#8220;continues to suppress the white, Caucasian race.&#8221;&#160;&#160;The Court held that the&#160;First Amendment protects advocacy of violence and revolution, and that the State is barred from punishing citizens for the expression of such views.&#160; The Brandenburg Court pointed to a long history of precedent protecting the First Amendment rights of Communists to call for revolution &#8212; even violent revolution &#8212; inside the U.S., and explained that the Government can punish someone for violent actions but not for speech that merely advocates or justifies violence&#160;(emphasis added): As we [395 U.S. 444, 448] said in Noto v. United States, 367 U.S. 290, 297 -298 (1961), &#8220; the mere abstract teaching . . . of the moral propriety or even moral necessity for a resort to force and violence, is not the same as preparing a group for violent action and steeling it to such action. &#8221; See also Herndon v. Lowry, 301 U.S. 242, 259 -261 (1937); Bond v. Floyd, 385 U.S. 116, 134 (1966). A statute which fails to draw this distinction impermissibly intrudes upon the freedoms guaranteed by the First and Fourteenth Amendments. It sweeps within its condemnation speech which our Constitution has immunized from governmental control. From all appearances, al-Awlaki seems to believe that violence by Muslims against the&#160;U.S. is justified in retaliation for the violence the U.S. has long brought (and continues to bring)&#160;to the Muslim world.&#160;&#160;But as an American citizen, he has the absolute Constitutional right to express those views and not be punished for them (let alone killed)&#160;no matter where he is in the world; it&#8217;s far from clear that he has transgressed the advocacy line into violent action.&#160; Obviously, there are those who justify such assassination powers on the ground that radical Islam is a grave threat, but that is what is always said to justify Constitutional abridgements (it was obviously said of Communists and war critics during World War I).&#160; Indeed, in light of episodes like the Timothy McVeigh bombing and the various attacks on abortion clinics, shouldn&#8217;t those who want the President to be able to assassinate American &#8220;radical clerics&#8221; without a trial also support the&#160;President&#8217;s targeting of Americans who advocate extremism or violence from a far right or extremist Christian perspective? &#160;What&#8217;s the principle that allows one but not the other?
