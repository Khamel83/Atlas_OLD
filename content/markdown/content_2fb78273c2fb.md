<html><body><div><div class="ct-rich-text-children font-cms-font-brand-text font-normal text-lg leading-7.75 [&amp;_&gt;p]:text-cms-story-body-color-text clearfix mb-10 md:max-w-170 md:mx-auto" data-element="story-body" data-subscriber-content=""> <p>During his brief stint as a film actor in Manila, David Bunevacz played a sexy kidnapper named Johnny in a rollicking crime escapade, “Tusong Twosome.”</p><p>In one scene, he lounges in bed with a woman in his arms as he haggles over ransom on the phone.</p><p>“Honey, they have 10 million to give us,” Johnny tells her. “What do you think?”</p><p>“Ten million?” she scoffs. “That’s peanuts. I want my 20 million.”</p><p>“Honey, 10 million is a lot of money,” he insists.</p><p>Acting, it turned out, was not Bunevacz’s calling; he abandoned show business in the Philippines soon after the movie’s 2001 release.</p><p>But <a class="link" href="https://www.bilibili.tv/en/video/2014613840" target="_blank">Johnny’s pillow talk</a> foreshadowed the brazen life of crime Bunevacz went on to lead back home in Los Angeles, where he put his charisma to work in swindles that made him tens of millions of dollars.</p><p>For more than a decade, Bunevacz, now 54, posed as a weed mogul cashing in on California’s booming marijuana trade.</p><p>With his self-confidence and gregariousness, he disarmed a long line of marks, dazzling them with stories of how they too could make a fortune in pot. A self-styled expert in psychology, he gauged their vulnerabilities and exploited them with devastating success.</p><p>Forging documents to make Holy Smokes Holdings LLC and other fake businesses look real, Bunevacz fleeced more than 100 people, including some of his best friends, a federal judge found. He ripped off his own dentist.</p><p>The loot fueled a lifestyle of boundless extravagance, richly detailed in court filings. Bunevacz tooled around L.A. in a yellow Lamborghini Urus. His wife, <a class="link" href="https://www.instagram.com/p/BErmQokxrIi/" target="_blank">Jessica R. Bunevacz</a>, author of the cheeky self-published “<a class="link" href="https://www.instagram.com/p/BItqn0tA719/" target="_blank">Date Like a Girl, Marry Like a Woman</a>,” drove a Bentley.</p><p>The family weathered much of the  COVID-19 pandemic in a Calabasas mansion that was once home to Kylie Jenner. He and Jessica sometimes chartered yachts and private jets and once paid for friends’ suites at the resort in the Bahamas where they renewed their wedding vows. Impressed, they lent still more to the shell companies he was siphoning for himself, his wife and their three children. Most investors got nothing back. A few lost their life savings.</p><p>The ruthlessness paid off, but also left many angry victims hounding him to retrieve their money. One of the bilked investors, according to a lawyer who spent years hunting for stolen proceeds, was so enraged that he grabbed Bunevacz and put him in a headlock.</p><p class="enhancement" data-align-center="" data-click="enhancement"> <hr class="divider"> </p><p>The <a class="link" href="https://www.instagram.com/p/xDinpyRrJy/" target="_blank">son of immigrants</a> from opposite sides of the world, Bunevacz grew up in the L.A. suburbs — the South Bay and Palmdale.</p><p>His mother, Filomena, was a young nurse when she moved from the Philippines to California in the late ‘60s. She fell in love with Joseph Bunevacz, a Hungarian in the restaurant business. They married, bought a two-bedroom house in Redondo Beach and had two kids, David and his younger sister, Desiree.</p><p>David emerged as a star athlete in high school. At track meets, he won <a class="link" href="https://media.hometeamsonline.com/photos/htosports/CLETETRACK/TF_101611_Paraclete_All_Time_Track___Field-2.pdf" target="_blank">first place</a> in high hurdles and high jump. At UCLA, he was a champion <a class="link" href="https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1992-04-10-sp-98-story.html">javelin thrower</a>.</p><p>Bunevacz (pronounced <i>boon-eh-vahtch</i>) went on to compete for the Philippines at the Southeast Asian Games in 1997, placing <a class="link" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20120226022737/http:/www.olympic.ph/pdf/SEAGamesMedallists.pdf" target="_blank">second</a> in the decathlon.</p><p>After some minor brushes with the law in L.A. (he and his friends had fun shoplifting clothes, he would later testify), Bunevacz settled for a time in Manila, where he met Jessica Rodriguez, the actor, model and talent manager he would marry.</p><p>He found gigs appearing in <a class="link" href="https://davidbunevacz.files.wordpress.com/2012/04/3.png" target="_blank">commercials</a> and broke into the country’s thriving entertainment industry — co-hosting a sports and travel TV show and landing a couple film parts.</p><p>His biggest role was in “Tusong Twosome.” At <a class="link" href="https://www.bilibili.tv/en/video/2014613840" target="_blank">the end</a>, Johnny gets beaten up in a warehouse, then blown to bits by a car bomb.</p><p>Around 2006, Bunevacz launched a cosmetic surgery clinic called Beverly Hills 6750 in an upscale Manila office tower. Its billboard slogan promised dramatic makeovers for romance seekers: “Miss Ugly No More.”</p><p>“It was a business that my wife wanted to put up,” Bunevacz explained years later.</p><p>In a sign of things to come, board members and investors soon accused Bunevacz of misusing clinic funds to fly first-class to California and buy luxury cars, according to civil lawsuits and <a class="link" href="https://www.pep.ph/lifestyle/14733/pep-scoop-david-bunevacz-and-jessica-rodriguez-booted-out-of-beverly-hills-6750" target="_blank">Filipino press reports</a>.</p><p>One of the cars was a BMW X5 that he gave his wife after she sang on the popular “Celebrity Duets” TV show. Jessica wept when she saw the big red ribbon taped to the SUV’s hood.</p><p>Bunevacz was later roughed up, threatened at gunpoint and forced to sign over his Porsche Cayenne Turbo, according to the celebrity press in Manila and a friend in California who heard him tell a version of the story that featured him leaping out a second-story window to flee. “A little mixture of ‘Jason Bourne’ and ‘Indiana Jones,’” the friend recalled.</p><p>Bunevacz testified that his business partners kidnapped him for three hours and left him “beat up pretty bad” before he finally escaped.</p><p>“I ran down the street, I got in a taxi all bloodied up,” he said. </p><p>He and his family quickly packed their belongings and left the Philippines for good, resettling at his parents’ place in Palmdale.</p><p class="enhancement" data-align-center="" data-click="enhancement"> <hr class="divider"> </p><p>Bunevacz resurfaced in Asia at the 2008 Summer Olympics in Beijing, where he set in motion an audacious ticket scam.</p><p>The deal seemed legitimate at first. </div> </figure> </div><p>The two of them met Gene Hammett, an Atlanta ticket broker, at a Beijing hotel. David took Hammett to a room with batches of tickets to various Olympics events over the next two weeks, Hammett said in an interview. </p><p>Hammett bought about 2,000 tickets and resold them at a markup, turning a quick profit of more than $300,000. He liked Bunevacz.</p><p>“We had a good relationship, and everything worked smoothly, and he was very connected,” Hammett recalled. </p><p>Bunevacz invited Hammett to join him in his suite at the Bird’s Nest stadium for track-and-field events; they watched Usain Bolt of Jamaica break a world record in the 100-meter sprint.</p><p>With such trappings of legitimacy,  Bunevacz lured Hammett into the con that would upend his life: He told him his connections could again supply tickets to the 2010  Winter Olympics in Vancouver. I never have.”</p><p>Months later, Elliott, who served on an FBI task force, got a search warrant to raid the Calabasas mansion, where detectives seized reams of documents they could use to prosecute Bunevacz. The FBI, Internal Revenue Service, and Securities and Exchange Commission joined the investigation.</p><p>In his request for an arrest warrant a year later, Elliott called Bunevacz a “peripatetic grifter” who forged financial records in layers and layers of schemes to steal millions of dollars.</p><p>In July, Bunevacz pleaded guilty to federal securities and wire fraud charges, admitting he stole as much as $35 million.</p><p>Efforts to interview Bunevacz in prison were unsuccessful, and his criminal lawyer, James S. Threatt, did not respond to a request for comment.</p><p>“Mr. Bunevacz, is there anything you would like to say?” U.S. District Judge Dale S. Fischer asked him at his sentencing in November.</p><p>“No, your honor,” Bunevacz, wearing a beige jail jumpsuit, told her.</p><p>Fischer invited his victims to speak. Hammett told her that Bunevacz “pillaged people who would fall prey to his charm.” Then Danford stepped up to the lectern and let loose.</p><p>“David and Jessica knew my family intimately,” he told the judge.</p><p>Recalling private conversations about him and Meredith struggling long ago to overcome substance abuse, he said David and Jessica knew their recovery and family were “the most important thing[s] in our lives next to our faith.”</p><p>“They preyed on that knowledge, and they manipulated us from the beginning of the relationship while pretending to care about us and the well-being of our children,” Tom said.</p><p>Looking back on the prayer groups that David and Jessica used to convene at their house, he added, “It was just another ruse to keep people believing that they were this morally centered family that truly cared about others and had a deep faith in Christ.” </p><p>Next came the dentist, who was tricked out of $800,000.</p><p>“Sorry, hands are shaking,” the dentist told Fischer. He remembered Bunevacz saying he could buy his parents a house with his earnings on the vape-pen deal. Expressing dismay at the “coldheartedness,” he suggested Bunevacz could be “sociopathic.”</p><p>“I think he’s a different level of crook,” he said.</p><p>Assistant U.S. Atty. Alexander Schwab recommended 11 years and three months in prison. Fischer decided Bunevacz deserved more. </p><p>“His purpose was to provide himself and his family with an extremely extravagant lifestyle, which they flaunted on social media,” she said.</p><p>She sentenced Bunevacz to 17 years and six months in prison. She also ordered him to pay victims $35 million in restitution, conceding it was unlikely he ever would.</p><p>Bunevacz had told her in a letter that he was “extremely sorry and will never forget the trail of damage I have left behind.” Fischer dismissed it as “probably the least convincing letter the court has ever received from a defendant.”</p><p>“I am not in the least persuaded,” she said, “that Mr. Bunevacz regrets anything other than he was caught.”</p> </div> </div></body></html>
