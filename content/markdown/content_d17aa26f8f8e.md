<h2>Vilfredo Pareto, 1848-1923 </h2>

<p><img src="image/pareto3.gif" , alt="Portrait of V. Pareto" WIDTH="234" HEIGHT="298"> <br>
<img src="image/paretosig.gif" WIDTH="185" HEIGHT="56"> </p>

<p>The Italian economist Vilfredo Pareto was one of the leaders of the <a href="../schools/lausanne.htm">Lausanne
School</a> and an illustrious member of the &quot;second generation&quot; of the
<a href="../essays/margrev/margrev.htm">Neoclassical revolution</a>.&nbsp;
Although only moderately influential during his lifetime, his
&quot;tastes-and-obstacles&quot; approach to <a href="../essays/paretian/paretocont.htm">general
equilibrium theory</a> were resurrected during the great &quot;<a href="../schools/paretian.htm">Paretian
Revival</a>&quot; of the 1930s and have guided much of economics since. </p>

<p>Vilfredo Pareto was born into an Italian aristocratic family in the year of people's revolutions, at its epicenter
-- Paris, 1848.&nbsp; His father, Raffaele Pareto, a Genoese <i>marchese</i> (marquis) and civil engineer, had fled to Paris in 1835
in self-imposed exile,&nbsp; following the example of Mazzini and other Italian
nationalists. Vilfredo was the third child (and first son) of his marriage to a
Frenchwoman.</p>
<p>The Pareto family returned to Genoa in 1852 where his father became a French 
teacher at a local school.&nbsp; Around 1859, they
moved to Casale Monferrato (Piedmont), where his father had another
teaching appointment in accounting and agriculture at the Istituto Leardi. In 1862, Pareto's father took a civil service job in the
ministry of agriculture, industry and trade in Turin, the Piedmontese (and now Italian) capital. Later that same year, when the
provisional capital was moved to Florence (Tuscany), the <i>marchese</i> was
forced to follow.&nbsp; But his son stayed behind in Turin to complete his
studies.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Vilfredo Pareto followed a conventional schooling, guided by his father. He 
obtained his <i>Licenza de maturita</i> from the Royal Technical Institute in 
Turin (1864), and proceeded to enroll at the University of Turin, where obtained a degree in mathematics and physics 
in 1867.&nbsp; His first publication (1866) was published in his father's 
journal.&nbsp;
After a year of military service, Pareto signed up to take an advanced degree in
civil engineering at the polytechnic institute of Turin. As his thesis on solid
bodies makes evident, it is here Pareto acquired his knowledge about mechanical equilibrium that
were to characterize his later contributions to economics.&nbsp;</p>
<p> After graduating
at the top of his class in 1870, Pareto moved to Florence to take up a job as an
engineer with the local branch of the Rome
Railway Company (Società Strade Ferrate Romane), which had just been created 
from a merger.&nbsp; The annexation of Rome -- the final step in the
unification of Italy -- happened that very year and, naturally, much debate
swirled around the political future of the new nation.&nbsp; A radical democrat
and &quot;<a href="../schools/manchester.htm">classical liberal</a>&quot;,
Pareto had been energized in his youth by the liberal ideals of Cavour -- and 
was consequently
horrified at the Bismarckian-style interventionist state that was being called for by
&quot;modern&quot; Italian politicians.&nbsp;&nbsp; The strongly-opinionated
Pareto was itching to get involved and speak his mind.&nbsp; In 1872, he
delivered his first address to the<i> <a href="http://www.georgofili.it/">Accademia
dei Georgofili</a>,</i> an agricultural economics research institute in
Florence, defending radical parliamentary democracy and universal suffrage,
while condemning Bismarckian paternalism.&nbsp; It was here that he made the
acquaintance of the celebrated Peruzzi family of Florence.&nbsp; Pareto began
attending the Peruzzi <i>salons</i> and imbibing in the political and
intellectual milieu of post-<i>risorgimento</i> Italy.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>In 1873, Pareto quit his humdrum job to and moved to San Giovanni Valdarno to take up a more 
responsible position with a local iron and steel company (Società per 
l'Industria del Ferro).&nbsp;&nbsp; It was in dealing with the demands and&nbsp; 
complaints of iron workers, that Pareto's interest in economics really picked
up.&nbsp; During this time, he took many business trips to Germany, France,
Belgium, Austria, etc. and soaked up not only the politics, but also the local
economic and sociological literature.&nbsp;He became a founding member of the &quot;Società 
Adamo Smith&quot; in Florence, launched by <a href="ferrara.htm">Ferrara</a> in 1874.</p>
<p>Pareto's first venture into economics came in 1877, in a celebrated address to the
<i>Accademia dei Georgofili</i>. Pareto lashed out at the <a href="../schools/historic.htm">German
Historical School</a> -- then enjoying a wave of 
<a href="../schools/italian.htm">popularity in Italy</a> after the
fall of the Cavourist liberal government in 1876 and the installation of a more
interventionist&nbsp; government under Agostino Depretis.&nbsp; The
paternalistic politics, State interventionism and anti-theoretical stance of the
Historicists rubbed against practically all of Pareto's instincts.&nbsp;
He also saved some nasty words for Auguste <a href="comte.htm">Comte</a>, whom
he regarded as a &quot;precursor&quot; to the Historicists, and defended the
scientific claims of <a href="../schools/ricardian.htm">Classical </a>economics.
Pareto invoked Herbert <a href="spencer.htm">Spencer</a>'s theory of social
evolution as a response to what he perceived to be their nihilistic &quot;historical
conditionality&quot;.</p>
<p>In 1880, the Valdano iron and steel concern was reorganized into the Società Ferriere d'Italia
and Pareto himself elevated to managing director.&nbsp; Released from menial
duties, Pareto had more time to reflect -- and, now as director, more issues to
reflect upon, particularly with regard to government regulation and worker
conditions.</p>
<p>Around this time, Pareto decided to get into politics himself.&nbsp; In 1877,
he had been elected to the municipal council of Valdano.&nbsp; Now, in 1880, 
Pareto
decided to run for national parliament as the radical party candidate for Montevarchi.&nbsp;
He lost.&nbsp; He tried again in 1882, this time as a deputy for Pistoia.&nbsp;
He lost again.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>Pareto's up-close-and-dirty experience with the mechanisms of political
double-dealing and patronage embittered him permanently.&nbsp; Henceforth, 
Pareto would take up the politics of resentment against the &quot;corrupt&quot; system.&nbsp; 
He derided the
Italian
parliamentary system as a sham, a &quot;pluto-democracy&quot;, a fig leaf for
the naked power of the nobility and the wealthy.&nbsp; In the newspapers and
magazines of the day, Pareto disgustedly&nbsp; identified and denounced the
vested interests that lay behind new waves of economic regulation, protectionism
and nationalization.&nbsp; In 1886, Pareto came out strongly in another lecture
to the <i>Accademia</i>, denouncing the hypocrisy of government for outlawing
worker initiatives, like a minimum wage and the right to strike, on the grounds
of <i>laissez-faire</i>, and yet happily putting up tariff walls and other
protections for the profits of the rich.&nbsp; Urged by his friends to run for a
seat again, Pareto would reply, &quot;I prefer to discover shame rather than be
a part of it.&quot;&nbsp;</p>
<p>In 1887, the Belgian economist Gustave <a href="molinari.htm">de Molinari</a> 
spotted Pareto's
tireless polemics in the press and invited him to submit articles on the Italian
situation to the prestigious <i>Journal d'economistes</i>. Pareto happily
acceded.&nbsp; Through Molinari and the <i>Journal</i>, Pareto came quickly into
the heady orbit of the <a href="../schools/frenchlib.htm">French Liberal</a>
school.</p>
<p>In 1889, after the death of his parents, Pareto changed his lifestyle
completely.&nbsp; He inherited the <i>marchese</i> title, but he never used it.&nbsp; Instead, he
quit his job, married a penniless girl from Venice, Alessandrina Bakunin (of
Russian descent, but apparently unrelated to the other <a href="bakunin.htm">Bakunin</a>), and,
in 1890, moved to a villa in Fiesole.&nbsp; His engineering career abandoned,
Pareto sat down to become an &quot;intellectual&quot;.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>
From his retreat, Pareto increased the frequency of his polemical articles --
which took on a particular urgency after the ascension of the openly Bismarckian
government of Crespi in 1887. His alarmist articles were followed up by public
lectures at a working man's institute and putting out feelers to the <a href="../schools/utopia.htm">socialist</a>
cause.&nbsp; He was quickly
targeted as a troublemaker by the authorities.&nbsp; Trailed by police,
intimidated by hired thugs, his
lectures were closed down and his applications for teaching posts
blocked.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Pareto's activities now brought him to the attention of Maffeo <a href="pantaleoni.htm">Pantaleoni</a>,
then Italy's leading Neoclassical economist, and a friendship sparked between the
two men..&nbsp; In 1891, Pareto published an article in the <i>Revue des deux
mondes</i>, which favorably cited an article by Pantaleoni.&nbsp; The content of
the article -- and the association between the serene academic and the revolted 
engineer --
came to the attention of the authorities and Pantaleoni was promptly dismissed
from his teaching job at Bari.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>Still, the two men only got closer.&nbsp; Pantaleoni introduced Pareto to economic
theory, particularly the <a href="../schools/lausanne.htm">Walrasian</a>
strand. Pareto, a quick learner with exceptionally good mathematical
aptitude, took to it immediately.&nbsp; Impressed, Pantaleoni invited Pareto to
contribute a monthly column, the &quot;Cronache&quot;, to his economics journal,
the <i>Giornale degli economisti</i>. There Pareto demonstrated his new mastery of
economics -- although he would also use the column to continue to take not-so-sly shots at
the Italian government.</p>
<p>In the meantime, Léon <a href="walras.htm">Walras</a> was looking for
someone to take over his chair in political economy at the University of <a href="../schools/lausanne.htm">Lausanne</a> in
Switzerland.&nbsp; Pantaleoni recommended Pareto to him -- "He is an engineer like you; he is an economist not like you, but wishing to
become like you, if you help him.&quot;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Although courteous and respectful to each other in public, Walras
and Pareto did not get along very well.&nbsp;&nbsp; Walras and Pareto disagreed on
many economic policy issues such as free trade and the role of the State.&nbsp;
They also had opposing temperaments -- Walras was a timid, bourgeois idealist
while Pareto remained a caustic, disputatious, aristocratic cynic.&nbsp;In
spite of this, Walras decided that Pareto ought to succeed him.&nbsp; Pareto was
appointed in 1893, and his position at Lausanne made permanent in
1894.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Doubtlessly, there were many people in Italy who were glad to see Pareto gone 
and safely
hidden away in Switzerland.&nbsp; But from his new academic perch, Pareto's
nerve only increased.&nbsp; His attacks on the Italian government continued in
his monthly columns to the <i>GdE</i> and in foreign journals.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In 1893,&nbsp; Pareto published an introduction to an abridged Italian
edition of Karl <a href="marx.htm">Marx</a>'s <i>Capital</i>.&nbsp; Pareto applauded
Marx's theory of class
struggle and even thought historical materialism was on the right track (albeit not
deep and general enough, in his view).&nbsp; But he deplored Marx's Oz-like
conclusion and wrapped it all up with a wholesale denunciation of the socialist
program. &quot;Any attack on economic freedom, from wherever it comes, is an
evil.&nbsp; Whether this freedom be violated in the name of bourgeois socialism
or that of the people's socialism, the effect is the same -- a destruction of
wealth which, in the end, falls on the poorer, more numerous part of the
population, increasing its sufferings.&quot; (Pareto, 1893).</p>
<p>Pareto was attentive to his academic duties and produced a superb
two-volume edition of his lecture
notes at Lausanne, the <i>Cours d'économie
politique</i> (1896-97).&nbsp; Pareto opens the first part, on &quot;pure
economics&quot;, immediately with a suggestion to drop the worn-out term
&quot;utility&quot; (which has connotations of &quot;well-being&quot;) and
replace it instead with &quot;ophelimity&quot; (which is simply meant to capture
purely subjective &quot;desire&quot;, which may or may not contribute to a
person's &quot;well-being&quot;).&nbsp; Preferences was what Pareto wanted to get at.&nbsp;&nbsp;For
&quot;marginal utility&quot;, Pareto substitutes the term &quot;elementary
ophelimity&quot;.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>In the <i>Cours</i>,<i> </i>Pareto relegates math and diagrams to footnotes. His first depiction of a
utility function is not additively separable, but the general one, U(x<sub>1</sub>,
x<sub>2</sub>, ..., x<sub>n</sub>) from <a href="edgeworth.htm">Edgeworth</a>.&nbsp;&nbsp;
He notes that a person is <i>only</i> conscious of the marginal, and never the
total, utility (§28; this argument was made earlier in a 1892 <i>GdE</i>
article).&nbsp;&nbsp; Pareto then steps back and adds some cautionary notes about scope and limits
of economic science, e.g.&nbsp; it does not cover &quot;motive&quot;, but only a
small facet of behavior in a social context and other motives and factors cannot
be ignored.&nbsp; He also repudiates any relationship with <a href="../schools/utilitarian.htm">utilitarian</a>
philosophy.&nbsp; From there, Pareto proceeds quickly to exchange.&nbsp; He assumes price-taking behavior
-- which he identifies with &quot;free competition&quot;. He then takes a
two-agent, two-good example and derives <a href="gossen.htm">Gossen</a>'s law
for exchange via the method of Lagrangian multipliers&nbsp; -- reminding us, in
the process, that utility is not comparable across people.&nbsp; No demand curve
is derived, although reference is made to the possibility of it (in a reference
to a 1892, <i>GdE</i> article).</p>
<p> This was more than merely an restatement of
the doctrines of the <a href="../schools/lausanne.htm">Lausanne
School</a>.&nbsp; Interspersed with his presentations of pure economic theory
were numerous asides on methodology and applied economics and extensive
sociological observations.&nbsp;&nbsp; His recent reading of Karl <a href="marx.htm">Marx</a>
and Social Darwinists like Herbert <a href="spencer.htm">Spencer</a> leaves its 
imprimatur.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>One of the contributions of the <i>Cours</i> was his exposition of &quot;Pareto's
Law&quot; of income distribution.&nbsp; He argued
that in all countries and times, the distribution of income and wealth follows a
regular logarithmic pattern that can be captured by the formula:</p>
<p>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; log N = log A + m log x&nbsp;<br>
<br>
where                                      where N is the number of income earners who receive incomes higher than x, and A and m are constants.&nbsp;
Over the years, Pareto's Law has proved remarkably resilient in empirical
studies.</p>
<p>Another contribution of the <i>Cours</i> was Pareto's criticism of the <a href="../essays/margrev/distrib.htm">marginal
productivity theory of distribution</a>. Pareto pointed out that it would fail in
situations where there is imperfect competition or limited substitutability
between factors.&nbsp; He'd repeat his criticisms in future writings.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p> Also
of importance was Pareto's observation that since the equilibrium is merely a
solution to a set of simultaneous equations, then it is at least
theoretically possible that a socialist or collectivist economy could
&quot;calculate&quot; this solution and so attain exactly the same outcome as in
a system guided by free markets.&nbsp;&nbsp; This proposition
was picked up and extended by Enrico <a href="barone.htm">Barone</a> and became the first shot of
the famous <a href="../essays/paretian/socialcalc.htm">socialist calculation debate</a>,
.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The Abyssinian debacle in 1896 and string of political corruption scandals
led to violent street demonstrations against the Italian government.&nbsp; In a
pre-emptive attempt to avoid a mass uprising, the authorities clamped down hard
on liberals, socialists any other potential troublemakers.&nbsp; Outspoken
journalists and academics, like Pantaleoni, were dismissed and exiled. Pareto's
&quot;Chronace&quot;&nbsp; were terminated in 1897.&nbsp; In Switzerland,&nbsp; Pareto assisted 
and even housed many Italian liberals and socialists that had been chased out of Italy 
(particularly after the 1898 May riots).&nbsp; When the Dreyfus affair broke out in 
France, Pareto put his pen to work against the anti-Semitic
French military authorities.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In a famous 1900 <i>Rivista</i> article, Pareto suddenly changed direction.&nbsp; Heretofore a radical democrat,
Pareto now decided to declare himself an <i>anti</i>-democrat.&nbsp; The disturbances of the 1890s in Italy and France led Pareto to
realize that, far from restoring true democracy, meritocracy and promoting
social welfare, the radical
movements were really just seeking to replace one <i>élite </i>with another <i>élite</i>,
the privileges and structures of power remaining intact.&nbsp; The struggle was not for a good
society, Pareto concluded, but a squabble among <i>élites </i>over whom exactly was to going to
govern.&nbsp; And the ideals and theories they claimed to fight for?&nbsp; Just
propaganda, Pareto declared, the way upwardly-mobile folks incite the helpless, hopeless mob to
take to the streets on their behalf.&nbsp; For Pareto, humanitarianism,
liberalism, socialism, communism, fascism, whatever, were all the same in the
end. All ideologies were just smokescreens foisted by &quot;leaders&quot; who
really only aspired to enjoy the privileges and powers of governing.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p> Pareto resumed his task as political polemicist -- and went on a crusade to expose the
sham of political ideology and doctrine.&nbsp; He condemned <a href="../schools/utopia.htm">socialists</a>
of all stripes roundly in a 1902 book, but took particular aim at logically
demolishing the &quot;new gospel&quot; of <a href="../schools/marxian.htm">Marxism</a>.&nbsp; 
Marx had sold a delusion, Pareto declared.&nbsp; The class struggle is eternal; the promised &quot;classless&quot; society
that would emerge under communism was
merely ideological fodder for socialist leaders to lay on their flock.&nbsp; Of
course, as a good Neoclassical, Pareto could not&nbsp; fathom the labor theory
of value either.&nbsp;</p>
<p>In 1906, Pareto published his <i>Manual of Political Economy</i>, his <i>magnum
opus</i> on pure economics, that moved him out from the shadow of <a href="walras.htm">Walras</a>.&nbsp; Unlike the <i>Cours</i>, the <i>Manual</i>
concentrates on presenting pure economics in an explicitly mathematical form (especially after it was heavily revised for the 1909
French edition).&nbsp; The Walrasian equations are
still there, but the focus is on formulating equilibrium in terms of solutions to
individual problems of &quot;<a href="../essays/paretian/paretequil.htm">objectives
and contraints</a>&quot;.&nbsp; To illustrate this, the indifference curves of <a href="edgeworth.htm">Edgeworth</a>
 (1881) are employed extensively -- both in his theory of the consumer and,
another great novelty, in his <a href="../essays/product/decision.htm"> theory of the
producer</a>.&nbsp; It is in the <i>Manual</i>
that we find the first representation of what has since become known (and
mislabeled) as the <a href="../essays/margrev/walrex.htm"> &quot;Edgeworth
box</a>&quot;.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Like Irving <a href="fisher.htm">Fisher</a> (1892), Pareto embraced the
idea that cardinal utility could be dispensed with.&nbsp; Preferences were the
primitive datum, and utility a mere representation of
preference-ordering.&nbsp;&nbsp; With this, Pareto not only inaugurated modern
microeconomics, but he also demolished the &quot;unholy alliance&quot; of
economics and <a href="../schools/utilitarian.htm">utilitarianism</a>.&nbsp; In its
stead, he introduced the notion of <a href="../essays/paretian/paretoptimal.htm">Pareto-optimality</a>,
the idea that a society is enjoying <i>maximum ophelimity</i> when no one can be
made better off without making someone else worse off.&nbsp;&nbsp; (for more
details, see our discussion of the <a href="../essays/paretian/paretocont.htm">Paretian
general equilibrium system</a>).</p>
<p>His <a href="../schools/optimist.htm#sociology"> sociological</a> observations also begin to indicate the future course of his
ideas.&nbsp; In 1900, Pareto had entered into a brief controversy in the <i>Giornale
degli economisti </i>with Benedetto Croce.&nbsp; Croce had criticized
economists' positivistic approach, particularly the assumption of &quot;rational
economic man&quot;.&nbsp;&nbsp; Pareto defended economists, but, at the same
time, realized that the conventional defense was not even convincing enough to
himself.&nbsp; Why
did the predictions of economics fail to correspond to reality?&nbsp; Why were
its policy recommendations, to him logically irrefutable, not adopted?&nbsp; The explanation, he
concluded, echoing Georges <a href="sorel.htm">Sorel</a>, was simply that much of human activity was driven not by logical
action, but rather by <i>non-logical</i> action.&nbsp; On this, of course,
economics has nothing to say -- which is why, ultimately, economics will always
fail empirically.&nbsp; Pareto realized that he had to move beyond economics to
look for his answer.</p>
<p>Pareto retired from his chair at Lausanne in 1907, gradually passing on his
teaching responsibilities to Pasquale <a href="boninsegni.htm">Boninsegni</a>.&nbsp;&nbsp;
He moved to Villa
Angora in
Céligny, near Lake Geneva.&nbsp; There he was nursing a heart disease,
surrounded by a dozen cats, his enormous personal library, a cellar full of superb
wines and a large cabinet of exquisite liquers.&nbsp; His wife ran off in 1901, but, as
an Italian citizen, he could not legally divorce her.&nbsp; A Frenchwoman, Jane
Régis moved in shortly afterwards, and they remained devoted companions for the
rest of his life. He only married her in 1923, after he became a citizen of
the city-state of Fiume and thus overcame the legal obstacles to divorcing his 
first wife.</p>
<p>Pareto used his time at
Céligny to write his <i>Trattato di sociologia generale</i>, which was finally
published, after wartime delays,&nbsp; in 1916.&nbsp; This was his great <a href="../schools/optimist.htm#sociology">
sociological</a> masterpiece.&nbsp;&nbsp; He explains how human action can be
neatly reduced
to <i>residue </i>and <i>derivation</i>.&nbsp; People act on the basis of
non-logical sentiments (residues) and invent justifications for them afterwards
(derivations). &nbsp; The derivation is thus just the content and form of the ideology itself.&nbsp; But the residues
are the real underlying problem, the particular cause of the squabbles that leads to the &quot;circulation of élites&quot;. The underlying
residue, he thought, was the only proper object of sociological enquiry.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>Residues are non-logical sentiments, rooted in the basic aspirations and
drives of people.&nbsp; He identifies six classes of residues, all of which are present but unevenly distributed across people -- so the population is always
a heterogeneous, differentiated mass of different psychic-types.&nbsp;The most
important residues are what he calls Class I
the &quot;instinct for combining&quot; (innovation) and Class II, the
&quot;persistence of aggregates&quot; (conservation).&nbsp;&nbsp; Class I types
rule by guile, and are calculating, materialistic and innovating.&nbsp; Class II types
rule by force and are more bureaucratic, idealistic and conservative.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p> Pareto's theory of
society claimed that there was a tendency to return to an equilibrium where a
balanced amount of Class I and Class II people are present in the governing élites. People are always
entering and leaving the élite thereby tending to restore the natural
balance.&nbsp; On occasion, when it gets too lopsided, an élite will be replaced <i>en
masse</i> by another&nbsp;&nbsp; If there are too many Class I people in a
governing élites, this means that violent, conservative Class II's are
in the lower echelons, itching and capable of taking power when the Class I's
finally make a mess of things by too much cunning and corruption (he regarded
Napoleon III's France and the Italian &quot;pluto-democratic&quot; system as an
example).&nbsp; If the
governing élite is composed mostly of Class II types, then it will fall
into a bureaucratic, inefficient and reactionary mess, easy prey for calculating
upwardly-mobile Class I's (e.g. Tsarist Russia).&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p>Pareto colored his sociological theory with numerous classical and
contemporary illustrations of his theory.&nbsp; He published two more
books (1920, 1921) expanding on the theme.&nbsp; His quasi-mystical arguments about the
non-logical motivations attracted many Italian Fascists (Mussolini himself
claimed to have attended his lectures at Lausanne).&nbsp; Pareto was
largely disdainful of the Fascist movement -- he never had patience for ideologies or
ideologues -- but he found them quite amusing.&nbsp; When Mussolini's small band
of Class II Fascists marched on Rome in 1922 and brought the whole Class I-dominated
Italian government tumbling down, Pareto mumbled triumphantly in his sick-bed,
&quot;I told you so!&quot;.&nbsp; He was not unhappy at the turn of events.</p>
<p>The Fascists showered Pareto with honors from afar, making him a
Senator of the Kingdom of Italy, inviting him to join the Italian delegation to the
Geneva Disarmament Conference, asking him to contribute to the Fascist party
periodicals, etc.&nbsp; He
declined most of the honors, but spoke favorably of certain early reforms
undertaken by the Fascists.&nbsp; However, he also warned them to avoid despotism,
censorship and economic corporatism.&nbsp; When the Fascists clamped down on
freedom of expression in Italian universities, Pareto managed to rouse himself
to write a protest.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p> Pareto died a mere ten months into Mussolini's reign -- before
the uglier aspects of Fascism became obvious.&nbsp;
The Fascists continued&nbsp; to use his name unreservedly to give intellectual
veneer to their movement.&nbsp; Writing in 1938 on the legacy of Pareto, the
economist (and Fascist) Luigi <a href="../schools/lausanne.htm#amoroso">Amoroso</a>
would have the gumption to write (and <i>Econometrica</i> the editorial lapse to
publish) the following:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Just as the weaknesses of the flesh delayed, but could not prevent, the triumph of Saint Augustine, so a rationalistic vocation
retarded but did not impede the flowering of the mysticism of Pareto. For that reason, Fascism, having become victorious,
extolled him in life, and glorifies his memory, like that of a confessor of its faith."&nbsp;
(Luigi <a href="../schools/lausanne.htm#amoroso">Amoroso</a>, &quot;Vilfredo Pareto", <i> Econometrica</i>, 1938: p.21)

</p>

</blockquote>
<p>Despite his posthumous association with Fascism, Pareto's sociological work has been taken
seriously, going through recurring phases of popularity and&nbsp; critical
scrutiny. Freudian psychology has given much weight to some of his
notions.&nbsp; It is not so much its main thrust, but its roughness, simplicity
and incompleteness that are the main sources of complaint.&nbsp;&nbsp;

</p>

<p>Pareto's economics have had a much greater impact.&nbsp; Pareto managed to
construct a proper school around himself at <a href="../schools/lausanne.htm">Lausanne</a>,
including G.B. <a href="gbantonelli.htm">Antonelli</a>, <a href="../schools/lausanne.htm#boninsegni">Boninsegni</a>,
<a href="../schools/lausanne.htm#amoroso">Amoroso</a> and others as disciples.&nbsp; Outside
this small group, his work also influenced W.E. <a href="../schools/engmath.htm#johnson">Johnson</a>,
Eugen <a href="slutsky.htm">Slutsky</a> and Arthur <a href="bowley.htm">Bowley</a>.&nbsp;
But Pareto's big break came posthumously in the 1930s and 1940s, a period which we have
decided to call the &quot;<a href="../schools/paretian.htm">Paretian Revival</a>&quot;.&nbsp;
His &quot;tastes-and-obstacles&quot; approach to demand were resurrected by John
<a href="hicks.htm">Hicks</a> and R.G.D. <a href="allen.htm">Allen</a>
 (1934) and extended and popularized by John <a href="hicks.htm">Hicks</a>
(1939), Maurice <a href="allais.htm">Allais</a> (1943) and Paul <a href="samuelson.htm">Samuelson</a>
(1947).&nbsp; Pareto's work on welfare were resurrected by Harold <a href="hotelling.htm">Hotelling</a>,
Oskar <a href="lange.htm">Lange</a> and the &quot;<a href="../essays/paretian/paretosocial.htm">New
Welfare Economics&quot;</a> movement.&nbsp; Finally, Pareto's ruminations on the potential
efficiency of a collectivist society were aired in the <a href="../essays/paretian/socialcalc.htm">Socialist
Calculation Debate</a> that broke out between the <a href="../schools/paretian.htm">Paretians</a>
and the <a href="../schools/austrian.htm">Austrians</a>.&nbsp;

</p>
